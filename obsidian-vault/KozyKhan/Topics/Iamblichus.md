Iamblichus (c. 245–325 CE) was a Syrian Neoplatonist philosopher and mystic who played a significant role in the development of late antique philosophy. He was a student of [[Porphyry]], who was himself a disciple of [[Plotinus]], the founder of [[Neoplatonism]]. Iamblichus is best known for his efforts to synthesize Platonic philosophy with religious and theurgical practices, emphasizing the importance of ritual and divine intervention in the pursuit of spiritual enlightenment.

### Key Contributions and Ideas:
1. **[[Theurgy]]**: Iamblichus is particularly famous for his defense and elaboration of theurgy, a form of ritual practice aimed at invoking divine powers to achieve union with the divine. He believed that philosophical reasoning alone was insufficient for attaining the highest levels of spiritual realization and that theurgical practices were necessary.

2. **Hierarchy of Beings**: He elaborated a complex metaphysical system that included a detailed hierarchy of divine beings, ranging from the ultimate One (the ineffable source of all reality) down through various levels of gods, angels, demons, and human souls. This hierarchy was integral to his theurgical practices.

3. **Commentaries on [[Plato]] and [[Aristotle]]**: Iamblichus wrote extensive commentaries on the works of Plato and Aristotle, although many of these have been lost. His interpretations often sought to harmonize the teachings of these two great philosophers within a Neoplatonic framework.

4. **Influence on Later Thought**: Iamblichus had a profound influence on later Neoplatonists, such as Proclus and Damascius, and through them, on the development of medieval and Renaissance thought. His ideas also had an impact on Christian, Islamic, and Jewish mystical traditions.

### Major Works:
- **On the Mysteries (De Mysteriis)**: This is one of his most famous works, where he defends theurgy and responds to criticisms from his teacher Porphyry.
- **The Pythagorean Life (Vita Pythagorica)**: A biography of [[Pythagoras]] that also serves as a vehicle for Iamblichus's own philosophical and religious ideas.
- **Various Commentaries**: Although many are lost, fragments and references to his commentaries on Plato's dialogues and Aristotle's works survive.

Iamblichus's work represents a significant shift in Neoplatonism towards a more religious and ritualistic approach, distinguishing him from the more contemplative and philosophical focus of his predecessors like Plotinus and Porphyry. His ideas continued to influence philosophical and religious thought long after his death.
