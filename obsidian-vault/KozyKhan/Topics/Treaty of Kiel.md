**The Treaty of Kiel: A Comprehensive Overview**

  

**I. Introduction**

  

The **Treaty of Kiel** (Danish: Kielerfreden) was a pivotal peace treaty signed on **January 14, 1814**, in the city of Kiel, then part of the Duchy of Holstein under Danish control. This treaty marked the end of the **Napoleonic Wars** for Denmark-Norway and resulted in significant territorial and political changes in Scandinavia and Northern Europe. The Treaty of Kiel had profound implications for the sovereignty of Norway, ultimately leading to its brief independence and subsequent union with Sweden.

  

**II. Historical Context**

  

**A. Denmark-Norway Union**

1. **Formation and Structure:**

• **Denmark-Norway** was a dual monarchy formed in 1380, uniting the Kingdom of Denmark and the Kingdom of Norway (which included territories like Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, and Norwegian overseas possessions).

• The union was characterized by shared monarchs and centralized administration, though Norway maintained some distinct legal and administrative systems.

2. **Napoleonic Era Involvement:**

• During the **Napoleonic Wars** (1803–1815), Denmark-Norway initially attempted to remain neutral.

• In 1807, following the British attack on Copenhagen to prevent the Danish fleet from falling into French hands, Denmark-Norway joined the **Third Coalition** against Britain, aligning with Napoleon.

  

**B. The Napoleonic Wars and Their Impact**

1. **Alliance with France:**

• Denmark-Norway’s alliance with Napoleon led to economic blockades, naval conflicts, and strain on the country’s resources.

• The British Royal Navy’s dominance and blockades severely impacted Danish trade and economy.

2. **Defeat and Occupation:**

• In 1813, Denmark-Norway faced military defeats against the British and their allies.

• The loss of the Danish fleet and territories weakened the union, making peace negotiations inevitable.

  

**C. The Treaty of Kiel Negotiations**

1. **Participants:**

• **Denmark:** Represented by King Frederick VI and his ministers.

• **Sweden:** Represented by Crown Prince Charles John (formerly Jean Baptiste Bernadotte, a Marshal of France adopted by Sweden).

• **Prussia and Russia:** Allied with Sweden as part of the coalition against Napoleon.

2. **Objectives:**

• To formally end hostilities between Denmark-Norway and the coalition powers.

• To reorganize territorial control in Scandinavia and Northern Germany.

  

**III. Provisions of the Treaty**

  

**A. Territorial Changes**

1. **Cession of Norway to Sweden:**

• **Denmark ceded Norway** to the **Kingdom of Sweden**, marking the dissolution of the Denmark-Norway union.

• This transfer was based on the principle of compensation for Sweden’s losses to Napoleon.

2. **Retention by Denmark:**

• **Norwegian Pomerania:** The region of **Norwegian Pomerania** (a Danish possession in Northern Germany) was ceded to **Prussia**.

• **Faroe Islands, Greenland, and Iceland:** Denmark retained sovereignty over these territories.

3. **Territorial Adjustments:**

• **Trøndelag:** Norway was allowed to retain the province of Trøndelag and other northern territories, maintaining a degree of territorial integrity.

• **Jan Mayen:** This Arctic island remained under Norwegian control.

  

**B. Political Implications**

1. **Union with Sweden:**

• Norway entered into a **personal union** with Sweden, where both countries shared the same monarch but maintained separate constitutions, laws, and institutions.

• This union lasted until **1905**, when Norway peacefully gained full independence.

2. **Constitutional Developments:**

• Following the Treaty of Kiel, Norway sought to assert its sovereignty by drafting its own constitution, leading to the brief establishment of an independent Norwegian state in 1814.

  

**C. Economic and Social Impacts**

1. **Economic Strain on Denmark:**

• Loss of Norway weakened Denmark’s position in Northern Europe, both economically and politically.

• The cession exacerbated Denmark’s post-war economic challenges, leading to financial instability.

2. **Norwegian Nationalism:**

• The forced cession ignited a surge in **Norwegian nationalism**, culminating in the drafting of the **1814 Norwegian Constitution** at Eidsvoll and the declaration of independence.

  

**IV. Immediate Aftermath**

  

**A. The 1814 Norwegian Independence Movement**

1. **Constituent Assembly at Eidsvoll:**

• In response to the Treaty of Kiel, **Norway convened a constituent assembly** at Eidsvoll from April to May 1814.

• The assembly drafted and signed the **Norwegian Constitution** on **May 17, 1814**, declaring Norway an independent constitutional monarchy.

2. **Declaration of Independence:**

• **Prince Christian Frederik** of Denmark-Norway became the provisional King of Norway, seeking international recognition for Norway’s independence.

  

**B. The Swedish Invasion and Union Negotiations**

1. **Swedish Military Response:**

• Sweden, unwilling to accept the cession of Norway without compensation, **invaded Norway** in July 1814 to enforce the Treaty of Kiel.

2. **Convention of Moss:**

• Faced with military pressure and lack of widespread support, Norway entered negotiations with Sweden.

• The **Convention of Moss** (August 14, 1814) resulted in a **personal union** between Norway and Sweden.

• Norway retained its constitution and a significant degree of internal autonomy, while Sweden managed foreign affairs and defense.

  

**C. Union with Sweden (1814–1905)**

1. **Personal Union Characteristics:**

• Shared monarch, but separate constitutions and institutions.

• Norway maintained its own parliament (**Stortinget**) and laws, fostering a distinct national identity.

2. **Path to Full Independence:**

• Over the 19th century, Norway increasingly pushed for greater autonomy.

• The peaceful dissolution of the union in **1905** led to Norway’s full sovereignty and the establishment of its own monarchy.

  

**V. Significance and Legacy**

  

**A. National Identity and Democracy**

1. **1814 Constitution:**

• The Norwegian Constitution remains one of the oldest in the world still in use, embodying principles of democracy, individual rights, and separation of powers.

• It laid the foundation for modern Norwegian governance and national identity.

2. **Constitution Day:**

• **May 17**, celebrated as Norway’s **National Day**, commemorates the signing of the constitution and is marked by parades, traditional costumes (**bunad**), and various cultural festivities.

  

**B. Territorial Reconfiguration in Scandinavia**

1. **Denmark’s Shift:**

• Loss of Norway marked the end of Denmark’s significant influence in Northern Europe.

• Denmark focused on rebuilding and maintaining control over remaining territories like the Faroe Islands, Greenland, and Iceland.

2. **Sweden’s Enhanced Position:**

• The acquisition of Norway expanded Sweden’s influence and territorial holdings.

• The personal union with Norway solidified Sweden’s role as a major power in Scandinavia.

  

**C. Influence on European Politics**

1. **Model for National Unification:**

• The Treaty of Kiel and the subsequent Norwegian independence movement influenced other regions seeking self-determination and unification.

2. **Diplomatic Precedents:**

• Demonstrated the complexities of post-war treaties and the balance of power in Europe, highlighting the challenges of enforcing territorial changes.

  

**D. Historical Reflection**

1. **Balancing Histories:**

• The Treaty of Kiel is often examined in the context of Norwegian resilience and Denmark’s strategic concessions during wartime.

• It underscores the interplay between force, diplomacy, and national aspirations in shaping modern Europe.

2. **Educational Significance:**

• The treaty and its consequences are central topics in Norwegian and Danish historical studies, symbolizing the transition from medieval unions to modern nation-states.

  

**VI. Key Figures Associated with the Treaty of Kiel**

  

**A. King Frederick VI of Denmark-Norway**

1. **Role:**

• The reigning monarch during the signing of the Treaty of Kiel.

2. **Actions:**

• Attempted to maintain the Denmark-Norway union but ultimately ceded Norway under the treaty’s pressures.

  

**B. Crown Prince Charles John of Sweden (Jean Baptiste Bernadotte)**

1. **Role:**

• Former Marshal of Napoleon, adopted by the Swedish crown.

2. **Actions:**

• Led Swedish forces during the invasion of Norway and negotiated the terms of the personal union.

  

**C. Prince Christian Frederik of Denmark-Norway**

1. **Role:**

• The Danish prince who became the provisional King of Norway during its brief independence in 1814.

2. **Actions:**

• Played a central role in drafting the Norwegian Constitution and leading the independence movement before the union with Sweden was established.

  

**D. Georg Sverdrup and Wilhelm Frimann Koren Christie**

1. **Role:**

• Key delegates at the Eidsvoll assembly who contributed significantly to the drafting of the Norwegian Constitution.

2. **Actions:**

• Advocated for democratic principles and the preservation of Norwegian autonomy within the new union framework.

  

**VII. Analysis of the Treaty’s Terms**

  

**A. Fair Compensation?**

1. **Sweden’s Gains:**

• The cession of Norway was seen as favorable to Sweden, compensating for its own losses during the Napoleonic Wars.

2. **Denmark’s Losses:**

• Ceding Norway was a significant blow to Denmark-Norway’s territorial integrity and regional power.

  

**B. Norwegian Resistance**

1. **Nationalist Sentiment:**

• The forced cession galvanized Norwegian nationalism, leading to the swift and organized movement for independence and constitutionalism.

2. **Diplomatic Maneuvering:**

• Norway’s ability to negotiate favorable terms within the Convention of Moss demonstrated effective diplomacy despite military pressures.

  

**C. Long-Term Implications**

1. **Union Dynamics:**

• The personal union with Sweden allowed Norway to maintain substantial autonomy, preserving its constitutional traditions and fostering a unique national identity.

2. **Foundation for Modern Norway:**

• The Treaty of Kiel indirectly set the stage for Norway’s eventual full independence in 1905, solidifying the principles of self-governance and constitutionalism.

  

**VIII. Comparison with Other Treaties**

  

**A. Treaty of Kiel vs. Treaty of Kiel (different contexts)**

1. **Clarification:**

• Ensure no confusion with other treaties named after Kiel; this overview focuses solely on the 1814 Treaty of Kiel affecting Denmark-Norway and Sweden.

  

**B. Similar Treaties in European History**

1. **Congress of Vienna (1814-1815):**

• The Treaty of Kiel was part of the broader post-Napoleonic settlement orchestrated by the Congress of Vienna, which sought to restore balance and redraw the map of Europe after Napoleon’s defeat.

2. **Impact on National Movements:**

• Similar to the Treaty of Kiel, other treaties during this period often resulted in forced territorial changes that fueled nationalist movements across Europe, such as in Italy and Germany.

  

**IX. Criticisms and Controversies**

  

**A. Sovereignty Issues**

1. **Forced Cession:**

• Norway was forced to cede its sovereignty under duress, leading to debates about the legitimacy and fairness of the treaty’s terms.

  

**B. Historical Narrative**

1. **Denmark’s Position:**

• Denmark-Norway’s decision to align with Napoleon and the subsequent loss of Norway are often subjects of critical historical analysis regarding leadership and strategic choices.

  

**C. Legacy Debates**

1. **Union with Sweden:**

• While the union preserved Norwegian autonomy, some view it as a compromise that temporarily delayed full independence, raising questions about the effectiveness of the union arrangement.

  

**X. Conclusion**

  

The **Treaty of Kiel** was a transformative moment in Scandinavian history, effectively dissolving the longstanding Denmark-Norway union and reshaping the political landscape of Northern Europe. By ceding Norway to Sweden, Denmark faced significant territorial and political losses, while Norway embarked on a path toward constitutionalism and eventual full independence. The treaty underscored the complexities of post-war settlements and the enduring aspirations for national self-determination. Its legacy is deeply ingrained in Norwegian national identity, symbolized by the continued celebration of the 1814 Constitution and the enduring principles of democracy and sovereignty that emerged from this historical event.
