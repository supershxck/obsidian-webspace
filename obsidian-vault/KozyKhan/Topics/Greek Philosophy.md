## Introduction to Greek Philosophy

### Overview
Greek philosophy, spanning from the 6th century BCE to the Hellenistic period, laid the foundational framework for Western thought. It explored fundamental questions about reality, knowledge, ethics, and human existence.
### Key Periods

#### Pre-Socratic Philosophy (6th–5th centuries BCE)
- **Focus**: Natural phenomena, cosmology, and the nature of existence.
- **Notable Philosophers**:
  - **[[Thales]]**: Considered the first philosopher, proposed that water is the primary substance of all things.
  - **[[Heraclitus]]**: Known for the idea that change is the essence of the universe, famously stated, "You cannot step into the same river twice."
  - **[[Parmenides]]**: Argued that change is an illusion and reality is unchanging and indivisible.
  - **[[Pythagoras]]**: Introduced mathematical principles into the understanding of the cosmos, emphasizing the importance of numbers.

#### Classical Philosophy (5th–4th centuries BCE)
- **Focus**: Ethics, politics, epistemology, and metaphysics.
- **Socrates**:
  - **Method**: Socratic method, a form of dialectical questioning to stimulate critical thinking and illuminate ideas.
  - **Contribution**: Emphasized the importance of ethics and self-knowledge. His ideas are known through the writings of his students, particularly Plato.
- **Plato**:
  - **Theory of Forms**: Proposed that non-material abstract forms, or ideas, are the highest and most fundamental kind of reality.
  - **Republic**: Explored justice, the ideal state, and the philosopher-king concept.
  - **Academy**: Founded the first institution of higher learning in the Western world.
- **Aristotle**:
  - **Empiricism**: Emphasized observation and experience as the basis for knowledge.
  - **Works**: Contributions across multiple fields, including metaphysics, ethics, politics, logic, and biology.
  - **Lyceum**: Established a school in Athens that rivaled Plato's Academy.

#### Hellenistic Philosophy (3rd century BCE–3rd century CE)
- **Focus**: Practical ethics, personal happiness, and how to live a good life.
- **Major Schools**:
  - **Stoicism**: Founded by Zeno of Citium, taught that virtue is the highest good and that we should be indifferent to external circumstances.
  - **Epicureanism**: Founded by Epicurus, advocated for the pursuit of pleasure and avoidance of pain through simple living and intellectual pursuits.
  - **Skepticism**: Questioned the possibility of certain knowledge, promoting a life of inquiry and suspended judgment.
  - **Cynicism**: Emphasized living in accordance with nature and rejecting conventional desires for wealth, power, and fame.

### Broader Implications
- **Influence on Western Thought**: Greek philosophy profoundly influenced Roman thought, early Christian theology, and the development of Western philosophy.
- **Scientific Inquiry**: Laid the groundwork for scientific thinking and methodology by emphasizing reason and empirical observation.
- **Ethical Frameworks**: Provided diverse perspectives on ethics and the best way to live, influencing subsequent philosophical and religious traditions.
### Key Concepts
- **Metaphysics**: Study of the nature of reality and existence.
- **Epistemology**: Study of knowledge and belief.
- **Ethics**: Study of morality and how individuals should live.
- **Political Philosophy**: Study of government, justice, rights, and the role of the state.
