**Kepler’s Three Laws of Planetary Motion**

  

[[Johannes Kepler]]’s laws, formulated between 1609 and 1619, revolutionized our understanding of celestial motion by replacing centuries‑old circular orbits with precise mathematical descriptions. These three empirical laws describe how planets move around the Sun and laid the groundwork for Newtonian mechanics.

---

**1. First Law – The Law of Ellipses**

  

**“Every planet’s orbit is an ellipse with the Sun at one focus.”**

  

• **Ellipse:** A flattened circle defined by two focal points.

• **Semi‑major axis (a):** Half the longest diameter of the ellipse.

• **Focus:** One of two fixed points; the Sun occupies one focus in planetary orbits.

---

**2. Second Law – The Law of Equal Areas**

  

**“A line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times.”**

  

• **Implication:** Planets move faster when closer to the Sun (perihelion) and slower when farther away (aphelion).

• **Conservation of Angular Momentum:** Underpins this behavior in modern physics.

---

**3. Third Law – The Harmonic Law**

  

**“The square of a planet’s orbital period (T²) is proportional to the cube of its semi‑major axis (a³).”**

  

• **Mathematical form:**

\frac{T^2}{a^3} = \text{constant}

• **Consequence:** Enables comparison of orbital characteristics across all planets, linking distance to orbital period.

**Key Vocabulary**

• **Ellipse:** A conic section describing planetary orbits.

• **Perihelion/Aphelion:** Points of closest/farthest approach to the Sun.

• **Orbital Period (T):** Time required to complete one orbit.

• **Semi‑Major Axis (a):** Half the longest diameter of an ellipse.

• **Angular Momentum:** Quantity conserved in orbital motion, underlying Kepler’s second law.
