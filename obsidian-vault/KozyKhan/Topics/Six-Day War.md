# Chapter: The Six‑Day War — Rapid Conflict, Lasting Consequences

## Introduction  
The **Six‑Day War** (5–10 June 1967) was a brief but pivotal conflict in Middle Eastern history. Israel fought Egypt, Jordan, and Syria, dramatically altering regional borders and shaping the Arab–Israeli conflict’s trajectory.

---

## Historical Context  
- Post‑1948 Arab–Israeli tensions; unresolved refugee crisis  
- Suez Crisis legacy (1956); Israeli security concerns  
- Pan‑Arab nationalism under Egypt’s Gamal Abdel Nasser  
- Closure of Straits of Tiran (May 1967) and Egyptian troop buildup in Sinai

## Causes & Drivers  
- Israeli security doctrine: pre‑emptive strike doctrine  
- Arab nationalism and territorial claims  
- Superpower rivalry: U.S. support for Israel; Soviet backing of Arabs  
- Miscalculations and intelligence failures

## Outcomes & Legacy  
| Dimension | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| Territorial | Israel occupies Sinai, Gaza, West Bank, East Jerusalem, Golan Heights |
| Political | Rise of Palestinian nationalism; OPEC oil embargo (1973) |
| Legal | UN Security Council Resolution 242 calls for “land for peace” |
| Social | 300,000+ new Palestinian refugees; settlement expansion |

## Psychological & Philosophical Reflection  
The Six‑Day War illustrates the “Preemptive Paradox”: achieving swift security gains while sowing long‑term conflict. Psychologically, collective trauma and triumph shape national narratives; philosophically, it raises enduring questions about just war, sovereignty, and self‑determination.
