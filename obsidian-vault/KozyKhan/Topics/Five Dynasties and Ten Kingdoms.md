**The Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms Period (907–979 CE)**

  

The **Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period** was an era of political fragmentation and chaos that followed the collapse of the **[[Tang Dynasty]] (618–907 CE)**. It lasted from **907 CE to 979 CE** and is characterized by a succession of short-lived dynasties in northern China (the Five Dynasties) and a cluster of independent kingdoms in southern and western China (the Ten Kingdoms). Despite its instability, this period also saw significant cultural and economic development, laying the groundwork for the unification under the **[[Song Dynasty]] (960–1279 CE)**.

  

**1. Historical Context**

  

**a. Collapse of the Tang Dynasty**

• **Internal Decline**: The Tang Dynasty, weakened by the **[[An Lushan Rebellion]] (755–763 CE)** and increasing decentralization, saw its imperial authority diminish as military governors (_jiedushi_) gained autonomy.

• **Warlordism**: By the late Tang period, local warlords effectively ruled their territories, contributing to the empire’s fragmentation.

• **End of the Tang**: In **907 CE**, the last Tang emperor, **Emperor Ai**, was deposed by the warlord **Zhu Wen**, who established the **Later Liang Dynasty**.

  

**2. The Five Dynasties (907–960 CE)**

  

The Five Dynasties were a series of five short-lived regimes that controlled northern China, particularly the **Yellow River Basin**, during this chaotic period.

  

**a. Later Liang (907–923 CE)**

• **Founder**: Zhu Wen (Emperor Taizu of Later Liang), a former Tang general.

• **Territory**: Controlled much of northern China, with its capital at **Kaifeng**.

• **Decline**: Overthrown by **Li Cunxu**, who established the Later Tang Dynasty.

  

**b. Later Tang (923–937 CE)**

• **Founder**: Li Cunxu (Emperor Zhuangzong of Later Tang), a descendant of the Tang imperial family.

• **Territory**: Expanded control over northern China and some southern regions.

• **Cultural Revival**: Attempted to restore Tang traditions.

• **Decline**: Internal strife and rebellion led to its overthrow.

  

**c. Later Jin (936–947 CE)**

• **Founder**: Shi Jingtang, who sought support from the **Khitan Liao Dynasty** in exchange for territorial concessions.

• **Dependency on Liao**: Often regarded as a puppet state of the Khitan.

• **Fall**: Overthrown by the Liao forces, who briefly occupied northern China.

  

**d. Later Han (947–951 CE)**

• **Founder**: Liu Zhiyuan, a general who ousted the Liao and established a new dynasty.

• **Short Reign**: Internal weaknesses led to its quick downfall.

  

**e. Later Zhou (951–960 CE)**

• **Founder**: Guo Wei, a former military leader.

• **Reforms**: Introduced military and administrative reforms aimed at strengthening central authority.

• **End**: The Later Zhou was overthrown by one of its own generals, **Zhao Kuangyin**, who founded the **Song Dynasty**.

  

**3. The Ten Kingdoms (907–979 CE)**

  

While the Five Dynasties ruled northern China, the south and west were divided among ten independent kingdoms. These states were generally more stable and prosperous than the northern dynasties, benefiting from trade, agriculture, and relative peace.

  

**a. Key Kingdoms**

1. **Wu (902–937 CE)**:

• Located in the lower Yangtze River region.

• Capital: Yangzhou.

• Prosperity through trade and agriculture.

2. **Southern Tang (937–975 CE)**:

• Successor state to Wu, centered in the Yangtze Delta.

• Known for its cultural achievements, particularly in painting and poetry.

• Capital: Nanjing.

3. **Wuyue (907–978 CE)**:

• Located in modern Zhejiang and southern Jiangsu.

• Renowned for its economic prosperity and maritime trade.

• Capital: Hangzhou.

4. **Min (909–945 CE)**:

• Located in modern Fujian Province.

• Known for fostering regional independence and distinct cultural traditions.

5. **Chu (907–951 CE)**:

• Centered in modern Hunan and parts of Guangdong and Guangxi.

• Relatively stable and focused on regional development.

6. **Southern Han (917–971 CE)**:

• Located in modern Guangdong and Guangxi.

• Capital: Guangzhou.

• Controlled important maritime trade routes.

7. **Former Shu (907–925 CE)**:

• Located in modern Sichuan.

• Capital: Chengdu.

• Prosperous and culturally vibrant.

8. **Later Shu (934–965 CE)**:

• Successor to Former Shu, maintaining similar boundaries and cultural prominence.

9. **Jingnan (924–963 CE)**:

• A small kingdom centered around modern Hubei.

• Played a minor role in regional politics.

10. **Northern Han (951–979 CE)**:

• Located in modern Shanxi.

• Maintained a tenuous existence under the protection of the Khitan Liao Dynasty.

  

**4. Cultural and Economic Developments**

  

**a. Cultural Achievements**

• **Literature and Poetry**:

• Despite political fragmentation, the period witnessed a flourishing of poetry, particularly in the southern kingdoms.

• Prominent poets such as **Li Yu**, the last ruler of the Southern Tang, left enduring works.

• **Art and Architecture**:

• The development of regional artistic styles, including ceramics, calligraphy, and Buddhist art.

• Wuyue was renowned for the construction of Buddhist temples and pagodas.

  

**b. Economic Growth**

• **Southern Prosperity**:

• The southern kingdoms, particularly **Wuyue** and **Southern Tang**, became economic powerhouses, leveraging maritime trade and fertile agricultural lands.

• **Urbanization**:

• Cities like **Hangzhou**, **Nanjing**, and **Guangzhou** grew into major commercial and cultural hubs.

  

**5. Key Themes and Impacts**

  

**a. Political Fragmentation**

• **Regionalism**: The decentralization of power led to strong regional identities, which influenced Chinese history even after reunification.

• **Warlordism**: The reliance on military governors (_jiedushi_) during the Tang Dynasty set the stage for the fragmentation of this period.

  

**b. Prelude to the Song Dynasty**

• **Reunification**:

• The Song Dynasty (960–1279 CE) emerged out of the Later Zhou and successfully reunified China, ending the Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period.

• **Centralization**:

• The Song rulers adopted policies to reduce the power of regional warlords, centralizing administration and fostering long-term stability.

  

**c. Economic and Cultural Foundations**

• The economic prosperity and cultural advancements of the southern kingdoms influenced the Song Dynasty’s “golden age.”

• Maritime trade and urbanization during this period foreshadowed the Song’s later dominance in commerce and innovation.

  

**6. Legacy**

  

The Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period is often seen as a turbulent but transformative era in Chinese history. While the political fragmentation posed challenges, it also spurred regional cultural developments and economic growth that shaped China’s trajectory. The lessons learned from this period, particularly the dangers of decentralization, influenced the administrative reforms and centralized governance of the Song Dynasty.

  

**7. Timeline**

  

**Year** **Event**

**907** Tang Dynasty falls; Later Liang established as the first of the Five Dynasties.

**923** Later Tang overthrows Later Liang.

**937** Southern Tang replaces Wu.

**960** Song Dynasty founded by Zhao Kuangyin, ending the Five Dynasties.

**979** Song reunifies most of China, ending the Ten Kingdoms period.
