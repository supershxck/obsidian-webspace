**Chapter: The Etymology of “Bless” and “Blessing”**

  

**Introduction**

  

The English words _bless_ and _blessing_ carry connotations of consecration, bestowal of favor, and holiness. Yet their origins in early Germanic languages reveal an older, more concrete sense rooted in sacrificial rites. Examining the etymology provides insight into how their meaning shifted—especially under the influence of Christian usage.

**1. Old English Roots and the Connection to Blood**

1. **Old English blēdsian / bletsian**

• Earliest forms of _bless_ appear in Old English as _blēdsian_, _bletsian_, or _bledsian_.

• These words likely derive from the Proto-Germanic root *blōþisōn (or similar forms), which is related to the word for “blood” (_blōð_ in Old English).

2. **Sacrificial Connotation**

• Originally, _blēdsian_ meant “to consecrate or make holy by marking with blood,” harking back to the practice of sprinkling blood on altars or objects in a sacred rite.

• This connection is visible in other Germanic cultures, where the act of sacrifice or _blót_ involved offerings—often of blood—to gods or spirits.

**2. Shift in Meaning Under Christian Influence**

1. **From Blood-Sprinkling to Divine Favor**

• As Christianity became dominant in Anglo-Saxon England, the word _blēdsian_ evolved away from literal sacrifice.

• Its religious sense of “consecration” or “sanctification” remained, but the role of blood sacrifice receded in Christian contexts, replaced by ideas of bestowing divine grace or favor.

2. **Comparison to Latin-Based Terminology**

• In Latin, _benedīcere_ (to speak well of, to bless) contributed to words like _benediction_.

• Over time, English “bless” took on connotations more similar to “benediction,” focusing on spiritual favor or grace, rather than ritual blood rites.

**3. The Emergence of “Blessing”**

1. **Noun Form**

• _Blessing_ as a noun follows a similar trajectory: from the act of consecrating (originally with blood) to the state or gift of divine favor.

• By the Middle English period, “blessing” referred to both a prayer asking for favor/protection and the favor or benefit itself.

2. **Extended Meanings**

• Today, “blessing” can mean anything from an invocation of God’s help and goodness, to a simple expression of gratitude (e.g., “count your blessings”), to permission or approval (“you have my blessing”).

**4. Broader Linguistic and Cultural Significance**

1. **Echoes in Ritual Language**

• While the literal practice of blood-sprinkling is no longer central in mainstream Christianity, the etymological roots highlight how deeply sacrificial rituals shaped early religious vocabularies.

• The transformation from “blood-consecration” to “divine favor” reflects broader shifts in worship and theology as European cultures transitioned from pagan traditions to Christianity.

2. **Modern Usage**

• Contemporary expressions like “God bless you” often carry no overt reference to blood or sacrifice. They stand as vestiges of a sacred concept that has evolved significantly over time.

• In secular speech, “blessing” may mean a beneficial circumstance or a positive outcome (“It was a blessing in disguise”), continuing the sense of grace or good fortune.

**Conclusion**

  

Etymologically, the English _bless_ (Old English _blēdsian_) once pointed directly to the act of making holy through a blood ritual—an important practice in pre-Christian Germanic religion. Through centuries of Christianization, the notion of “blessing” shifted to emphasize divine grace, sanctification, and favor, shedding its explicit link to blood sacrifice. Yet the original sense survives in the very root of the word, reminding us that our modern idea of “blessing” has ancient, deeply symbolic origins.
