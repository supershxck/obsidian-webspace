### [[Ancient Armenia]]
- **Urartu (860–590 BC)**: The kingdom of Urartu is considered the precursor to Armenia. It was located in the Armenian Highlands and its capital was Tushpa (modern Van).
- **Early Armenians**: The Armenians are believed to have settled in the region around the 7th century BC, merging with the native Urartians.

### Classical Period
- **[[Orontid Dynasty]] (6th–3rd century BC)**: The Orontid dynasty ruled Armenia under the influence of the Achaemenid Empire.
- **[[Artaxiad Dynasty]] (190 BC–1 AD)**: The Artaxiad dynasty saw Armenia rise as a significant regional power.
  - **[[Tigranes the Great]] (95–55 BC)**: Under Tigranes II, also known as Tigranes the Great, Armenia reached its greatest territorial extent, encompassing parts of modern Turkey, Syria, and Lebanon.

### Roman and Sassanian Periods
- **Roman-Armenian Relations**: Armenia became a client state of [[Rome]] in the 1st century AD. The Kingdom of Armenia was often caught between the Roman Empire and the [[Sasanian Empire]].
- **Christianization (301 AD)**: Armenia became the first nation to adopt [[Christianity]] as its state religion under King Tiridates III, influenced by [[Saint Gregory the Illuminator]].

### Medieval Period
- **Bagratid Armenia (885–1045)**: The [[Bagratid dynasty]] established a kingdom in Armenia, with Ani as its capital. This period is often considered a golden age of Armenian culture and architecture.
  - **Ashot I (885–890)**: Ashot I was recognized by both the Abbasid Caliphate and the Byzantine Empire as the King of Armenia, marking the official establishment of the Bagratid Kingdom.
  - **Ashot III (953–977)**: Ashot III moved the capital to Ani, which became a cultural and economic hub.
- **Seljuk Invasions (11th century)**: The [[Seljuk Turks]] invaded and occupied large parts of Armenia, leading to the decline of the Bagratid Kingdom.

### [[Topics/Cilician Armenia]] (1080–1375)
- **Foundation**: Armenians established a new kingdom in Cilicia, southwest of the Armenian Highlands, after fleeing the Seljuk invasions.
- **Rubenid and Hethumid Dynasties**: The Rubenids and later the Hethumids ruled Cilician Armenia.
  - **Levon I (1198–1219)**: Under Levon I, Cilician Armenia became a significant power, recognized by European states and the Papacy.
- **Fall of Cilicia**: The Mamluks of Egypt captured the Cilician capital, Sis, in 1375, ending the Armenian Kingdom of Cilicia.

### Ottoman and Persian Rule
- **[[Ottoman Empire]]**: Large parts of Armenia fell under Ottoman control in the 16th century, following the Ottoman-Persian Wars.
- **Persian Influence**: Eastern Armenia was contested between the Ottomans and Safavid Persia, with the [[Treaty of Zuhab]] in 1639 dividing the region between the two empires.

### Modern Period
- **Russian Armenia (1828–1917)**: Eastern Armenia was annexed by the Russian Empire following the [[Russo-Persian War]] (1826–1828).
- **[[Armenian Genocide]] (1915–1917)**: During World War I, the Ottoman government systematically exterminated 1.5 million Armenians in the Armenian Genocide.
- **First Republic of Armenia (1918–1920)**: Armenia briefly gained independence after the Russian Revolution but was soon invaded by Turkish and Soviet forces.

### [[Soviet Armenia]] (1920–1991)
- **Soviet Rule**: Armenia became a part of the [[Soviet Union]] in 1920, forming the Armenian Soviet Socialist Republic.
- **[[Nagorno-Karabakh Conflict]]**: In the late 1980s, the conflict over the Nagorno-Karabakh region between Armenians and Azerbaijanis escalated, leading to violence and displacement.

### Republic of Armenia
- **Independence (1991)**: Armenia declared independence from the [[Soviet Union]] on September 21, 1991.
- **Modern Challenges**: Armenia has faced significant economic, political, and social challenges since independence, including the Nagorno-Karabakh War with Azerbaijan and the ongoing blockade by [[Turkey]] and [[Azerbaijan]].

### Conclusion
Armenia's history is marked by periods of independence and subjugation, cultural flourishing, and tragic loss. From ancient kingdoms to modern republics, Armenia's resilience and cultural heritage have endured through millennia.
