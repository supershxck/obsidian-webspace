**3rd Century Influential Figures: A Global Outline (50 Figures)**

  

The 3rd century was a time of transition and transformation as the Roman Empire faced internal strife and external pressures, early Christianity grew in influence, and the foundations of East Asian states were being laid during the tumultuous Three Kingdoms period. Below is an outline featuring 50 influential figures from the 3rd century. Each name is enclosed in double brackets to emphasize its significance during this formative era.

---

**I. Western Europe & the Roman Empire (25 Figures)**

1. **[[Septimius Severus]]**

• Roman Emperor whose reign (193–211) stabilized the empire and expanded its frontiers.

2. **[[Caracalla]]**

• Emperor known for the Constitutio Antoniniana, which granted Roman citizenship to many free inhabitants.

3. **[[Geta]]**

• Co-emperor and son of Septimius Severus, whose brief rule was marked by familial strife.

4. **[[Elagabalus]]**

• Controversial emperor (218–222) noted for his religious and cultural eccentricities.

5. **[[Alexander Severus]]**

• Emperor (222–235) whose relatively peaceful reign contrasted with the later crisis.

6. **[[Maximinus Thrax]]**

• Military emperor (235–238) whose ascent reflected the increasing role of the army in politics.

7. **[[Gordian I]]**

• Proclaimed emperor in 238 during a brief but turbulent revolt.

8. **[[Gordian II]]**

• Co-emperor with Gordian I, whose short reign ended in conflict.

9. **[[Philip the Arab]]**

• Emperor (244–249) known for his administrative reforms and for presiding over a period of relative stability.

10. **[[Decius]]**

• Emperor (249–251) remembered for his persecution of Christians and military campaigns.

11. **[[Valerian]]**

• Emperor (253–260) whose capture by the Persians marked a major crisis for Rome.

12. **[[Gallienus]]**

• Co-emperor with Valerian and later sole ruler, notable for his attempts at military and administrative reform.

13. **[[Claudius Gothicus]]**

• Emperor (268–270) celebrated for his victory over the Goths.

14. **[[Aurelian]]**

• Emperor (270–275) who restored the empire’s eastern and western provinces and introduced the coinage reform.

15. **[[Probus]]**

• Emperor (276–282) known for his efforts to reestablish order in a fragmented empire.

16. **[[Carus]]**

• Emperor (282–283) whose short reign was marked by successful campaigns in the east.

17. **[[Numerian]]**

• Son of Carus and emperor (283–284), whose mysterious death contributed to the empire’s instability.

18. **[[Carinus]]**

• Rival emperor (283–285) whose rule epitomized the challenges of the late 3rd century.

19. **[[Origen]]**

• Early Christian theologian whose extensive writings laid the groundwork for later Christian thought.

20. **[[Tertullian]]**

• Influential early Christian apologist and writer, known for his sharp critiques and theological insights.

21. **[[Lactantius]]**

• Christian author whose works offered moral guidance during times of crisis.

22. **[[St. Agnes]]**

• Young martyr whose story of faith and sacrifice inspired early Christian communities.

23. **[[St. Sebastian]]**

• Martyr revered for his steadfastness in the face of persecution.

24. **[[St. Perpetua]]**

• Early Christian martyr whose recorded visions and resolve became a symbol of Christian courage.

---

**II. Eastern Mediterranean & Sassanid Empire (10 Figures)**

26. **[[Ardashir I]]**

• Founder of the Sassanid Empire (224–242), who established a new Persian state and reformed administration.

27. **[[Shapur I]]**

• Sassanid Emperor (240–270) renowned for his military conquests and cultural patronage.

28. **[[Hormizd I]]**

• Briefly reigned Sassanid emperor (270–271) during a transitional period.

29. **[[Bahram I]]**

• Sassanid ruler (271–274) whose reign marked the consolidation of power in Persia.

30. **[[Bahram II]]**

• Emperor (274–293) noted for his military successes and administrative reforms.

31. **[[Aphrahat]]**

• Syriac Christian writer known as “the Persian Sage,” whose homilies influenced Eastern Christian thought.

32. **[[Aelius Aristides]]**

• Greek orator and writer whose speeches provide insight into 3rd‑century intellectual life.

---

**III. East Asia – The Three Kingdoms of China (10 Figures)**

36. **[[Cao Cao]]**

• Warlord whose strategic acumen laid the groundwork for the state of Cao Wei.

37. **[[Liu Bei]]**

• Charismatic leader and founder of the Shu Han state, revered for his benevolence.

38. **[[Sun Quan]]**

• Ruler who established the Eastern Wu state and maintained a stable regime.

39. **[[Zhuge Liang]]**

• Renowned strategist and chancellor of Shu Han, whose innovations in warfare and administration are legendary.

40. **[[Sima Yi]]**

• Military general and strategist of Wei, whose maneuvers paved the way for his family’s future rule.

41. **[[Cao Pi]]**

• Successor to Cao Cao and first emperor of the Cao Wei state.

42. **[[Liu Shan]]**

• Emperor of Shu Han following Liu Bei, known for his long but often criticized reign.

43. **[[Guan Yu]]**

• Legendary general celebrated for his loyalty and martial prowess.

44. **[[Zhang Fei]]**

• Fierce general of Shu Han, remembered for his bravery and strength.

45. **[[Sun Ce]]**

• Early warlord whose conquests laid the foundation for Eastern Wu’s eventual stability.

---

**IV. Africa & Other Regions (5 Figures)**

46. **[[King Ezana of Axum]]**

• Ruler of the Axumite Kingdom, whose conversion to Christianity marked a pivotal shift in East African history.

47. **[[St. Dionysius of Alexandria]]**

• Influential bishop in North Africa whose leadership helped shape early Christian doctrine.


50. **[[Plotinus]]**

• Neoplatonist philosopher whose ideas bridged classical thought and emerging Christian theology.

---

**Concluding Reflections**

  

The 3rd century was a time of immense upheaval and transformation. From the turbulent reigns of Roman emperors and the fervent development of early Christian thought to the dynamic political landscape of China’s Three Kingdoms and the foundational changes in the Sassanid Empire, these 50 figures—each encapsulated in double brackets—offer a glimpse into a world in flux. Their legacies continue to inform our understanding of how ancient societies navigated crisis, redefined power, and laid the groundwork for future cultural and religious developments.
