Proclus (c. 412–485 CE) was one of the last major philosophers of antiquity and a central figure in the Neoplatonic tradition. Born in [[Constantinople]], he spent most of his life in [[Athens]], where he became the head (scholarch) of the Platonic Academy. Proclus is renowned for his systematic and comprehensive synthesis of [[Neoplatonism]], building on the works of his predecessors, including [[Plotinus]], [[Porphyry]], and [[Iamblichus]].

### Key Contributions and Ideas:
1. **Metaphysics and Theology**: Proclus developed an elaborate metaphysical system that described the structure of reality as a series of emanations from the One, the ultimate principle of unity and goodness. His system included a hierarchy of divine beings, including the Henads (divine unities), the Intelligible Gods, the Intellectual Gods, and the Cosmic Gods, each playing a role in the procession and return of all things to the One.

2. **Theurgy and Ritual**: Like Iamblichus, Proclus emphasized the importance of theurgy (ritual practices aimed at invoking divine powers) as a means of achieving union with the divine. He believed that philosophical contemplation alone was insufficient and that ritual practices were necessary for the soul's ascent.

3. **Commentaries on Plato**: Proclus wrote extensive commentaries on several of Plato's dialogues, including the *Timaeus*, *Parmenides*, and *Republic*. These commentaries are invaluable for understanding both Plato's thought and Proclus's own philosophical ideas. His commentary on the *Timaeus*, in particular, is a major source for his cosmological views.

4. **Elements of Theology**: One of Proclus's most influential works is the *Elements of Theology*, a systematic exposition of Neoplatonic metaphysics. This work presents a series of propositions and proofs that outline the structure of reality and the relationships between different levels of being. It had a significant impact on later medieval and Renaissance thought.

5. **Influence on Later Thought**: Proclus's works were highly influential in the development of later Neoplatonism and had a lasting impact on medieval and Renaissance philosophy. His ideas were transmitted to the Latin West through translations and commentaries by figures such as Thomas Aquinas and Marsilio Ficino. Proclus's thought also influenced Islamic philosophy and mysticism.

### Major Works:
- **Elements of Theology**: A systematic presentation of Neoplatonic metaphysics, consisting of 211 propositions and proofs.
- **Commentary on the Timaeus**: An extensive commentary on Plato's *Timaeus*, exploring cosmological and metaphysical themes.
- **Commentary on the Parmenides**: A detailed commentary on Plato's *Parmenides*, focusing on the nature of the One and the principles of reality.
- **Commentary on the Republic**: A commentary on Plato's *Republic*, discussing ethical, political, and metaphysical issues.
- **Platonic Theology**: A comprehensive work that outlines Proclus's theological and metaphysical system, emphasizing the role of the divine in the structure of reality.

### Legacy:
Proclus's work represents the culmination of the Neoplatonic tradition in antiquity. His systematic approach to metaphysics, his integration of philosophical and religious practices, and his extensive commentaries on Plato ensured that his ideas would continue to influence philosophical and theological thought for centuries. Proclus's emphasis on the hierarchical structure of reality and the importance of theurgy had a profound impact on both pagan and Christian thinkers, making him a key figure in the history of Western philosophy.
