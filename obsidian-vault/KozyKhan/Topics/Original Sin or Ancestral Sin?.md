- Excursus on Pelagianism

The difference between original sin and ancestral sin primarily lies in how Western Christianity (mainly through Augustine's teachings) and Eastern Christianity understand the consequences of Adam and Eve's sin in the Garden of Eden and its effects on humanity.

**Original Sin**
- A concept more prevalent in Western Christianity.
- It posits that the guilt of Adam's sin is transmitted to his descendants through generation. This means all human beings are born in a state of sin, sharing not just the consequences but the guilt of Adam's sin.
- Original sin explains the universal propensity to sin in human beings and the necessity of baptism for its remission, including in infants.
- It emphasizes the need for divine grace through Jesus Christ to redeem humanity from this inherited guilt and restore the relationship between God and humans.

**Ancestral Sin**
- A concept found in Eastern Orthodoxy.
- It focuses on the transmission of death and a corrupted nature (a propensity or inclination to sin) as the consequences of Adam's sin, rather than the guilt of the sin itself.
- Humanity inherits the consequences of the first sin, which are death and a tendency to sin, but not the guilt. Each person is only guilty of their own sins, not Adam's.
- The concept underscores the importance of Christ's incarnation, death, and resurrection, which conquer death and offer salvation to humanity. It emphasizes the necessity of personal participation in salvation through cooperation with God's grace (synergy).

In summary, the key difference lies in the understanding of what is transmitted through Adam's sin: 
Western Christianity teaches that both guilt and a corrupted nature are inherited (original sin), while Eastern Christianity teaches that only the consequences of sin—death and a tendency towards sin—are inherited, without the guilt of Adam's original transgression (ancestral sin).

**Pelagianism**
- Named after Pelagius, a British monk active in the 4th and 5th centuries.
- Denies the concept of original sin as it is traditionally understood in Western Christianity. According to Pelagianism, Adam's sin affected only himself, and people are born innocent without the stain of original sin.
- Argues that human beings have the inherent capacity to choose good or evil without the necessity of divine grace. Thus, salvation is achievable through one's efforts and choices.
- Was condemned as heresy by several councils in the early Church because it was seen to undermine the necessity of grace for salvation.
**Semi-Pelagianism**
- A term developed later in theological debates, it seeks a middle ground between Pelagianism and the teachings of Augustine on grace and original sin.
- Accepts that original sin affects human nature but maintains that human beings have the initial and ongoing capacity to seek God and make a first step towards salvation on their own, after which divine grace comes to assist in their efforts.
- Like Pelagianism, it was criticized for underestimating the necessity of grace for beginning and continuing the Christian life of faith and was condemned at the Second Council of Orange in 529.

**Synergism (Eastern Orthodox View)**
- The Eastern Orthodox Church does not align with Pelagianism or Semi-Pelagianism as defined by Western Christianity but rather teaches a view often described as synergism.
- Synergism emphasizes that salvation is the result of cooperation between divine grace and human free will. God offers grace freely, which enables human beings to believe and seek God, but human response and cooperation with that grace are necessary.
- This view holds that while human nature is fallen and inclined towards sin, God's grace is always preceding, accompanying, and fulfilling human freedom, allowing individuals to participate in their salvation through faith and works.
- This perspective avoids the extremes of asserting human capability without grace (Pelagianism) and negating human freedom by emphasizing predestination or irresistible grace.

The Eastern Orthodox perspective, therefore, provides a nuanced understanding of salvation that affirms the necessity of grace while also maintaining the importance of human freedom and responsibility in the process of salvation.

The Eastern Orthodox Church, while not directly involved in the Western Church's specific debates over Pelagianism, has its theological perspectives that address the underlying issues of human freedom, divine grace, and salvation. The Eastern Church's approach to these issues can be characterized by a balance between recognizing human free will and the indispensable role of divine grace.

The Eastern Orthodox Church emphasizes the concept of synergy between divine grace and human free will. This means that salvation is seen as a cooperative effort between God's grace and human freedom. Humans are expected to participate actively in their salvation through cooperation with God's grace.

While the Western Church, influenced by Augustine, developed the doctrine of original sin, emphasizing the transmission of guilt from Adam to his descendants, the Eastern Church speaks more often of ancestral sin. Ancestral sin refers to the consequences of Adam's sin for humanity (death and a propensity to sin), rather than a guilt inherited by all humans. Thus, while recognizing the fallen state of humanity, the Eastern Orthodox perspective does not deny the ability of humans to choose good with the help of divine grace.

The Eastern Orthodox Church strongly affirms the necessity of divine grace for salvation. Grace is not only required for the beginning of the Christian life but is also essential for its continuation and completion. Salvation is impossible without God's grace, which heals, transforms, and enables humans to grow in likeness to God.

Christ is seen as the New Adam who, through His obedience, life, death, and resurrection, reversed the curse of ancestral sin. By uniting themselves to Christ through faith, baptism, and a life in the Church, believers participate in the new life He offers, a process that is both a gift of grace and a call to personal cooperation.

In summary, the Eastern Orthodox Church navigates the issues surrounding human will, divine grace, and salvation with a nuanced approach that emphasizes both the need for divine grace and the active role of human cooperation in the process of salvation. While not engaging directly with Pelagianism as a named controversy, the principles underlying the Eastern Orthodox understanding of salvation address the concerns that the controversy raised.
