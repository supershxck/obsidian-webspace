### Background
- **Context**: The [[Battle of Vienna]] took place on September 11-12, 1683, and was a pivotal moment in European history. It marked the end of the Ottoman Empire's expansion into Europe.
- **Ottoman Ambitions**: The [[Ottoman Empire]] sought to expand its territory into Central Europe, aiming to capture Vienna, the capital of the [[Habsburg Monarchy]] and a key strategic point.

### Lead-up to the Battle
- **Ottoman Preparations**: The Ottoman army, led by Grand Vizier [[Kara Mustafa Pasha]], launched a massive campaign to besiege Vienna. The siege began in July 1683, with the Ottomans deploying a large force of approximately 90,000-150,000 men.
- **Vienna's Defenses**: The city was defended by a smaller force of around 15,000 soldiers and 8,700 civilians capable of bearing arms, under the command of Count Ernst Rüdiger von Starhemberg. The defenders prepared extensive fortifications to withstand the siege.

### Formation of the Holy League
- **Allied Forces**: The Habsburg Monarchy called for aid from its allies. The [[Holy Roman Empire]] and the [[Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth]] formed a coalition known as the [[Holy League]] to counter the Ottoman threat.
- **Key Leaders**: The coalition was led by King [[John III Sobieski]] of Poland, who brought a combined relief force to Vienna, including Polish, Austrian, and German troops.

### The Battle
- **Relief Force Arrives**: On September 6, 1683, the relief force arrived near Vienna. By September 11, they had positioned themselves on the Kahlenberg hill overlooking the city.
- **Attack Begins**: On September 12, the battle commenced. The allied forces launched a multi-pronged attack against the Ottoman besiegers. The Polish winged hussars, led by Sobieski, played a crucial role in the cavalry charge that broke the Ottoman lines.
- **Ottoman Defeat**: The combined assault overwhelmed the Ottoman forces, leading to a decisive victory for the Holy League. Kara Mustafa Pasha's army was forced to retreat in disarray, abandoning their siege equipment and camps.

### Aftermath and Significance
- **Turning Point**: The Battle of Vienna was a turning point in the Ottoman-Habsburg wars. It halted the Ottoman advance into Europe and marked the beginning of a steady decline in Ottoman power.
- **Christian Europe's Victory**: The victory bolstered the confidence and unity of Christian European states. It also led to further military campaigns against the Ottomans, known as the Great Turkish War (1683-1699), which culminated in the Treaty of Karlowitz, significantly reducing Ottoman territories in Europe.
- **Legacy**: King John III Sobieski was hailed as the savior of Vienna and Western Christendom. The battle is remembered as a key moment in the struggle between the Ottoman Empire and European powers.
