The Vichy regime, officially known as the French State (État Français), refers to the authoritarian government that ruled the unoccupied portion of France during World War II, from July 1940 to August 1944.1 Here's a breakdown of its key characteristics:

- **Context:**
    - It was established after France's defeat by Nazi Germany in 1940.2
    - The Franco-German Armistice divided France into two zones: one under German occupation, and the other, the "free zone," which was governed by the Vichy regime.3
    - The regime was named after Vichy, a town in central France, which served as its capital.4
- **Leadership and Ideology:**
    - It was led by [[Marshal Philippe Pétain]].5
    - The regime pursued a policy of collaboration with Nazi Germany.6
    - Its ideology was characterized by traditionalism, authoritarianism, and anti-republicanism. The slogan "Liberty, equality, fraternity" of the French Republic was replaced with "Work, family, fatherland".7
    - It also enacted anti-semetic laws.
- **Collaboration:**
    - The Vichy government actively collaborated with the German occupiers in various ways, including assisting in the deportation of Jews.
    - This collaboration remains a highly controversial aspect of French history.
- **End of the Regime:**
    - The Vichy regime ended with the Allied liberation of France in 1944.8
    - After the war, Pétain and other Vichy officials were tried for treason.

In essence, the Vichy regime represents a dark period in French history, marked by collaboration with Nazi Germany and the abandonment of republican principles.9
