Absolute divine simplicity is a theological concept primarily associated with Western Christian thought, particularly with the work of [[Thomas Aquinas]]. It posits that God is entirely without parts, composition, or complexity; His essence and attributes are identical, meaning that God’s essence is His existence, and all His attributes (like omnipotence, omniscience, and omnipresence) are one and the same with His essence.

Orthodox Christians do not hold to a concept of divine simplicity that is absolute in this way. Instead, they maintain a distinction between God’s essence and His energies, a framework that allows for both the transcendence and immanence of God. Here are the key reasons why Orthodox Christians do not adhere to absolute divine simplicity, elaborated with references to the Church Fathers:
### 1. **[[Essence-Energies Distinction]]**
One of the most crucial theological distinctions in Orthodox Christianity is between God’s essence (ousia) and His energies (energeia). God’s essence is completely transcendent and unknowable, while His energies are the manifestations of His presence and activity in the world, through which humans can participate in the divine life.
#### [[St. Basil the Great]]:
St. Basil the Great, in his letter *Ad Amphilochium*, articulates this distinction:
"We know the essence through the energies. We do not assert that we participate in the essence, but that we participate in the energies. For this reason, we say that we are partakers of the divine nature, not by participation in the essence but by participation in the energies."
### 2. **Relational and Participatory Theology**
Orthodox theology emphasizes the relational and participatory nature of humanity's relationship with God. The essence-energies distinction preserves the transcendence of God’s essence while allowing for real participation in His divine life through His energies.
#### [[St. Gregory Palamas]]:
St. Gregory Palamas, in his defense of the Hesychast tradition, asserts:
"The divine essence is imparticipable, but the divine energies are participable, manifesting the dynamic presence of God in the world and in the hearts of the faithful. Through these energies, we experience the living and active God."
### 3. **[[Apophatic Theology]]**
Orthodox theology heavily relies on apophatic (negative) theology, which emphasizes the ineffability and incomprehensibility of God. Absolute divine simplicity tends to define God in more positive, philosophical terms, which can conflict with the apophatic approach.
#### [[St. Gregory of Nyssa]]:
St. Gregory of Nyssa, in his *Life of Moses*, emphasizes the unknowability of God:
"The true knowledge and vision of God consist in this—in seeing that He is invisible, because what we seek lies beyond all knowledge, being wholly separated by the darkness of incomprehensibility."
### 4. **Avoidance of Monistic Tendencies**
Orthodox Christianity is cautious of any theological framework that might lead to a monistic view of God, where distinctions within the Godhead are collapsed into a single undifferentiated essence. Absolute divine simplicity can risk such a monism by making all attributes of God indistinguishable from His essence.
#### [[St. Maximus the Confessor]]:
St. Maximus the Confessor, in his *Ambigua*, elaborates on the differentiation within God:
"The divine nature is simple, yet not without distinctions. We must affirm the real distinctions within the divine operations while maintaining the unity of the divine essence."
### 5. **Biblical and Patristic Foundation**
Orthodox theology is deeply rooted in the biblical revelation and the teachings of the Church Fathers, who frequently speak of the manifold operations (energies) of God. This patristic witness supports the understanding of God as both transcendent in His essence and immanent in His energies.
#### [[St. Athanasius of Alexandria]]:
St. Athanasius, in *On the Incarnation*, reflects this balance:
"God is beyond all being, yet He comes down to us in His energies. He remains in His essence wholly transcendent, yet in His operations, He is fully present and active in the world."
### Conclusion
From an Orthodox Christian perspective, absolute divine simplicity is problematic because it tends to negate the crucial distinction between God's essence and His energies. This distinction allows for a more relational and experiential understanding of God, which is central to Orthodox spirituality and theology. The essence-energies framework preserves God's transcendence while affirming His immanence and the possibility of genuine participation in His divine life. The teachings of the Church Fathers, particularly those involved in the defense of the essence-energies distinction, provide a robust theological foundation for this understanding, emphasizing the importance of maintaining both the unity and the distinctions within the Godhead.
