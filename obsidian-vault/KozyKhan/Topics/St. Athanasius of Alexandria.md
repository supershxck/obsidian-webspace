St. Athanasius the Great, a pivotal figure in early Christianity known for his defense against Arianism, was a staunch supporter of the decisions made at the First Council of Nicaea in 325 AD. This council was the first ecumenical council and is most famous for producing the Nicene Creed, which articulated the Church's belief in the Trinity and explicitly affirmed the divinity of Jesus Christ against Arian teachings that questioned it.

While Athanasius was a vocal proponent of the Nicene Creed and worked tirelessly to promote its teachings, the concept of "infallibility" as it is understood today in ecclesiastical terms was not explicitly articulated in his writings in the way the modern Church—especially the Roman Catholic Church—defines it. The development of the theological concept of infallibility, particularly regarding ecumenical councils and the papacy, occurred over centuries and was not fully defined until the First Vatican Council in 1870.

Athanasius regarded the decisions of the First Council of Nicaea as authoritative and binding for all Christianity because they reflected the apostolic faith and were confirmed by the consensus of the bishops gathered in council, who were guided by the Holy Spirit. His defense of the Council's decrees, especially against Arianism, was based on his conviction that the Council faithfully transmitted the true faith handed down from the Apostles.

Therefore, while Athanasius might not have used the term "infallible" in the way it is used today, he clearly viewed the decrees of the First Council of Nicaea as authoritative and grounded in divine truth, acting as a key champion of its teachings throughout his life.
