**Introduction**
The decolonization of [[Africa]] and [[Asia]] refers to the process by which countries in these regions gained independence from European colonial powers. This movement, which primarily took place in the mid-20th century, resulted in the emergence of numerous new sovereign states and had profound political, economic, and social impacts.

**Background**
- **Colonialism**: By the early 20th century, European powers such as [[Britain]], [[France]], [[Portugal]], [[Belgium]], and the [Netherlands] had established vast colonial empires in Africa and Asia. These colonies were exploited for their resources and were often subject to oppressive rule.
- **World War II**: The war significantly weakened European colonial powers, both economically and militarily. The war also inspired anti-colonial sentiments as colonized peoples contributed to the war efforts of the colonial powers, fueling their demands for independence.

**Decolonization of [[Asia]]**
- **[[India]]**: One of the earliest and most significant examples of decolonization in Asia. India gained independence from Britain in 1947, following a long non-violent struggle led by figures like Mahatma Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru.
- **[[Indonesia]]**: Declared independence from the Netherlands in 1945, but it wasn't officially recognized until 1949 after a violent struggle.
- **[[Vietnam]]**: Declared independence from [[France]] in 1945, leading to the First Indochina War, which resulted in the division of Vietnam and eventually full independence after the [[Vietnam War]] in 1975.
- **[[Philippines]]**: Gained independence from the [[U.S.]] in 1946.

**Decolonization of Africa**
- **North Africa**: Countries like [[Egypt]] (1952) and [[Libya]] (1951) gained early independence. [[Algeria]], after a brutal war of independence, became free from French rule in 1962.
- **Sub-Saharan Africa**: [[Ghana]] became the first sub-Saharan African country to gain independence from [[Britain]] in 1957. This was followed by a wave of decolonization across the continent in the 1960s.
- **Belgian Congo**: Gained independence in 1960, marking a significant and tumultuous decolonization process.
- **Portuguese Colonies**: Angola and Mozambique gained independence in 1975 following protracted wars.

**Factors Leading to Decolonization**
- **Economic Weakness**: Post-war economic challenges made it difficult for European powers to maintain and control their colonies.
- **Nationalism**: The rise of nationalist movements and leaders who demanded self-determination and independence.
- **International Pressure**: The establishment of the United Nations and the support for decolonization by superpowers like the United States and the Soviet Union.

**Impact**
- **Political**: The creation of new states and the re-drawing of national boundaries, often leading to political instability and conflict.
- **Economic**: Many newly independent countries struggled with economic development and the legacy of colonial exploitation.
- **Social**: Decolonization brought significant social changes, including efforts to reclaim indigenous cultures and identities.

**Conclusion**
The decolonization of Africa and Asia was a monumental process that reshaped the global political landscape. While it ended centuries of colonial rule, the legacy of colonialism continues to affect the political, economic, and social dynamics of many countries in these regions.
