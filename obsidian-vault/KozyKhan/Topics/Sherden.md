**The Sherden: An Overview**

  

**Introduction**

  

The **Sherden** were one of the groups of the **Sea Peoples**, a confederation of maritime raiders who appeared in the eastern Mediterranean during the **Bronze Age collapse** (around the 12th century BCE). These groups played a significant role in the widespread upheaval and destruction of several ancient civilizations, particularly the **Hittite Empire**, **Egyptian Kingdom**, and the **Levantine coast**. While much of the precise history of the Sherden remains obscure due to the limited nature of historical sources, they are known for their involvement in **raiding**, **warfare**, and their eventual settlement in the Mediterranean.

  

The Sherden are often identified as one of the most prominent and aggressive groups among the Sea Peoples and are particularly remembered for their role in the destruction of key Bronze Age civilizations and their connection to the Egyptian military.

**1. Origins of the Sherden**

  

**1.1 The Sea Peoples and Their Migrations**

  

The **Sea Peoples** were a collection of maritime raiders from various regions, likely from the Aegean, Anatolia, and the **central Mediterranean**, who attacked coastal cities and kingdoms during the late **Bronze Age** (c. 1200 BCE). The Sherden were one of the prominent tribes within this confederation.

• **Where They Came From**: The exact origins of the Sherden are uncertain, but scholars generally believe that they hailed from regions around the **Aegean Sea** or **Anatolia**, with some speculating that they could have been **Aegean islanders**, **Carians**, or **Luwians**. Other theories suggest that they might have been **nomadic Anatolian tribes** who were part of the broader migration movements of the Sea Peoples.

• **Name and Identity**: The term “Sherden” appears in ancient Egyptian texts, where the group is referred to as “**Sherden**,” but it is unclear if they were a distinct, cohesive ethnic or political group, or rather a term used to describe a collection of similarly allied peoples. Some scholars suggest that the name **Sherden** might have been a designation for a group of warriors, rather than an entire society or ethnic identity.

**2. The Sherden and Their Role in the Bronze Age Collapse**

  

**2.1 The Sea Peoples and Their Attacks on Egypt**

  

The Sherden, like the other Sea Peoples, are most famously mentioned in the context of their raids against **Egypt** during the reign of **Ramesses III** in the late 12th century BCE. The Egyptian records describe them as invaders who contributed to the collapse of several major Bronze Age civilizations. The **Great Sea Peoples Invasion** took place around **1177 BCE**, during which the Sherden are listed as one of the groups that attacked Egypt’s borders and settlements.

• **The Battle of the Delta (1175 BCE)**: The Sherden, along with other groups, attempted to invade Egypt by sea and land. They are depicted as **warriors with shields** and **distinctive weapons**, including **bronze helmets** and **swords**, possibly representing them as professional soldiers. According to Egyptian sources, the Sherden were heavily involved in the Battle of the Nile Delta, where they were defeated by the Egyptian forces of **Ramesses III**. This battle is one of the key events that defines their role in the broader **Sea Peoples’ invasions**.

• **Egyptian Depictions**: In Egyptian records, particularly in the **Medinet Habu temple**, the Sherden are depicted as a group of **heavily armed warriors**, often shown wearing **horned helmets** and armed with **long swords**. The images suggest they were feared and respected as formidable soldiers. Some interpretations suggest that the Egyptians may have even employed Sherden warriors as mercenaries after their defeat.

  

**2.2 Aftermath of the Sea Peoples’ Raids**

  

While the Sea Peoples, including the Sherden, did not succeed in permanently conquering Egypt, their raids contributed to the collapse of several major civilizations in the **eastern Mediterranean**.

• **The Hittite Empire**: The Sherden were part of the larger group that caused the **fall of the Hittite Empire**. The **Hittite capital**, **Hattusa**, fell around 1180 BCE, and the invasion of the Sea Peoples is believed to have played a key role in this collapse. The Sherden may have been involved in the raids on Hittite cities along the Mediterranean coast.

• **The Levant and Canaan**: The Sherden, along with the other Sea Peoples, were also involved in the destruction of several cities along the Levantine coast, including **Canaan** and the **Philistine cities**. Some of the Sea Peoples, including the Sherden, eventually settled in these areas, forming part of the population of the **Philistine** cities.

**3. The Sherden as Mercenaries in Egypt**

  

**3.1 Integration into the Egyptian Military**

  

After the failure of the Sea Peoples’ invasions, the Sherden were eventually absorbed into the Egyptian military as mercenaries. This was a common practice for the Egyptians, who often employed foreign soldiers and used them to bolster their own armies.

• **Mercenaries in Egyptian Service**: The Sherden were incorporated into the **Egyptian army**, and evidence suggests that they served as elite troops in the pharaoh’s personal guard. They were granted land and status within Egyptian society in exchange for their service.

• **The Role of the Sherden as Elite Warriors**: In Egyptian depictions, Sherden soldiers are often shown as part of elite units, reflecting their high status within the army. Their role in defending Egypt against future invasions further cemented their position as key military figures.

  

**3.2 The Sherden in Egyptian Art**

  

The Sherden are frequently depicted in **Egyptian art**, particularly in **battle scenes** and **military processions**. They are often shown as **heavily armored**, with **distinctive horned helmets** and **swords**, suggesting their important role as **warriors** and their **fearsome reputation**.

• **Iconography**: In Egyptian iconography, the Sherden are often paired with other groups of the Sea Peoples, such as the **Philistines**, **Tjeker**, and **Lukka**. These representations provide insights into their military appearance and their role in the broader conflicts of the period.

**4. Legacy and Impact of the Sherden**

  

**4.1 Historical Significance**

  

The Sherden’s role in the **Bronze Age collapse** and their subsequent integration into the **Egyptian military** has left a lasting legacy on the ancient world. They were part of a larger wave of migrations and invasions that led to the end of the **Late Bronze Age** and the transition to the **Iron Age**.

• **Contributions to Egyptian Military**: The Sherden’s legacy as mercenaries in the Egyptian army likely influenced the way Egypt interacted with other neighboring civilizations in the following centuries. They were part of Egypt’s **elite military forces**, and their experience as **warriors** helped Egypt defend its borders.

• **Cultural Influence**: The Sherden were not only involved in military conflicts but also contributed to the cultural exchange between Egypt and the various peoples of the eastern Mediterranean. They likely brought with them their own traditions, warfare tactics, and cultural practices, which would have had some influence on the Egyptian society.

  

**4.2 Enduring Mystery**

  

While much of the history of the Sherden tribe remains a mystery due to the limited historical records, their mention in Egyptian sources and their role in the **Sea Peoples’ invasions** has ensured their place in the historical narrative of the ancient Mediterranean world. Scholars continue to study their origins, migration patterns, and cultural legacy, though many details about their identity and history remain elusive.

**5. Conclusion**

  

The **Sherden** were an important tribe within the **Sea Peoples** who played a key role in the **Bronze Age collapse** of the **12th century BCE**. Their raids and invasions contributed to the downfall of several ancient civilizations, including the **Hittites** and parts of **Egypt**. However, after their defeat, the Sherden were integrated into the Egyptian military, where they became **elite mercenaries** and contributed to Egypt’s defense. Despite the gaps in historical knowledge about their exact origins and fate, the Sherden’s impact on the ancient Mediterranean world remains significant, and their legacy as formidable warriors endures through Egyptian depictions and historical accounts.
