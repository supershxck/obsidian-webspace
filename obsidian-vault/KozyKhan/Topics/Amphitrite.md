**Amphitrite (Ἀμφιτρίτη)** is a **sea goddess** in [[Greek mythology]], best known as the **consort of [[Poseidon]]**, the god of the sea. She is counted among the **[[Nereids]]**, the fifty daughters of the ancient sea god **[[Nereus]]** and the Oceanid **Doris**. Below is a concise overview of her mythological background and significance:

  

**1. Family and Lineage**

• **Parents**: Amphitrite’s father is **Nereus**, sometimes called the “Old Man of the Sea,” who represents the benevolent and bountiful aspect of ocean waters. Her mother, **Doris**, is a daughter of the Titan Oceanus.

• **Nereid Sisters**: She has numerous sisters, including Thetis (the mother of Achilles) and Galatea. All are collectively known for their beauty and their roles as attendants of Poseidon’s realm.

  

**2. Marriage to Poseidon**

• **Consort of the Sea God**: Amphitrite became Poseidon’s wife, effectively making her the **Queen of the Sea**. The marriage symbolizes a unification of the sea’s diverse powers, personified by Poseidon’s dominion and Amphitrite’s gentler, nurturing influence.

• **The Courtship**: In some versions of the myth, she was initially wary of Poseidon’s advances and fled to the far ends of the ocean. A dolphin—acting as a go-between—convinced her to return, and as a reward, Poseidon placed the dolphin in the sky as a constellation (Delphinus).

  

**3. Role and Iconography**

1. **Sea Queen**: Amphitrite is often portrayed enthroned beside Poseidon in his underwater palace, surrounded by sea creatures.

2. **Attendants**: Depictions of her court often include sea nymphs and various marine creatures such as dolphins, hippocamps (mythical sea-horse hybrids), and fish.

3. **Attributes**: Like other sea deities, she can be shown wielding a trident, though this emblem is more commonly associated with Poseidon.

  

**4. Offspring**

• **Triton**: Their most famous child is Triton, a merman who serves as Poseidon’s herald. Some myths mention additional children, but Triton is consistently recognized in literature and art.

  

**5. Worship and Cultural Presence**

• **Maritime Significance**: While Poseidon received the majority of large-scale worship among Greek seafarers, Amphitrite’s role as his queen afforded her reverence in local cults, especially in coastal and island regions.

• **Artistic Representations**: Amphitrite appears in classical art—on vases, friezes, and mosaics—often depicted amidst marine motifs, symbolizing the beauty and fertility of the sea.

  

**Conclusion**

  

**Amphitrite** embodies the serenity and life-sustaining aspects of the ocean, balancing Poseidon’s more turbulent power. As **Queen of the Sea**, she complements his dominion, contributing to the rich tapestry of Greek maritime mythology and underscoring the ancients’ reverence for the ocean’s vast, mysterious depths.
