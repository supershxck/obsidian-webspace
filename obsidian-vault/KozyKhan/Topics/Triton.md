**Triton (Τρίτων)** is a sea deity in [[Greek mythology]], commonly described as a **merman** with the upper body of a man and the tail of a fish. He is **the son of Poseidon**, god of the sea, and **Amphitrite**, queen of the sea. Below is an overview of Triton’s character and role:

  

**1. Parentage and Realm**

• **Divine Offspring**: As the child of Poseidon and Amphitrite, Triton resides in his parents’ undersea palace—often said to be at the bottom of the Aegean or near the coast of Boeotia (depending on the mythic variant).

• **Messenger of the Sea**: Triton serves as a herald for Poseidon, using his conch shell to **calm or stir the waves**.

  

**2. Iconography and Attributes**

1. **Conch Shell**: Triton famously blows a large conch shell. His blasts can **pacify** or **provoke** the seas at will.

2. **Hybrid Form**: Typically depicted with the upper body of a human male and the tail of a fish, Triton stands out among the Greek pantheon as one of the earliest merman figures.

  

**3. Myths and Appearances**

• **Argonauts and Lake Tritonis**: In some stories, Jason and the Argonauts encounter Triton on the shores of a large lake, and he assists them by guiding their ship to the open sea.

• **Multiple Tritons**: Later writers sometimes refer to a group of Tritons—**Tritones**—depicting them as a race or retinue of minor sea gods, all shaped like their namesake.

  

**4. Cultural Depictions**

• **Art and Sculpture**: Triton was a popular subject in ancient Greek and Roman art, often shown blowing the conch.

• **Influence on Later Traditions**: The image of Triton helped shape the broader Western concept of “mermen” or marine spirits, influencing medieval bestiaries and later cultural works.

  

**Conclusion**

  

Triton is a **lesser but significant sea god** in Greek mythology—**Poseidon’s messenger** whose conch shell commands the roiling waters. His hybrid form and role as herald of the deep made him an enduring symbol of the sea’s power and mystery.
