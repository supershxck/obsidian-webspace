#### Overview
- **Established:** January 10, 1920
- **Purpose:** To maintain world peace and prevent future conflicts following World War I
- **Headquarters:** Geneva, [[Switzerland]]
- **Disbanded:** April 18, 1946 (succeeded by the United Nations)

#### Founding and Goals
The League of Nations was founded as part of the [[Treaty of Versailles]], which ended World War I. The League's primary goals included:
- **Preventing Wars:** Through collective security and disarmament.
- **Settling Disputes:** Via negotiation and arbitration.
- **Improving Global Welfare:** Addressing issues such as labor conditions, human and drug trafficking, and the status of women and children.

#### Key Components
1. **The Assembly:**
   - Comprised representatives from all member states.
   - Met annually to discuss and make decisions on international issues.
   - Each member had one vote.

2. **The Council:**
   - Consisted of permanent and non-permanent members.
   - Permanent members included major powers such as [[Britain]], [[France]], [[Italy]], and [[Japan]].
   - Non-permanent members were elected by the Assembly for three-year terms.
   - Addressed specific issues and crises as they arose.

3. **The Secretariat:**
   - Headed by a Secretary-General.
   - Managed the League’s day-to-day operations and administrative functions.

4. **The Permanent Court of International Justice:**
   - Established in 1921.
   - Aimed to resolve disputes between nations through legal means.
   - Provided advisory opinions on international legal issues.

#### Major Achievements
1. **Settling Disputes:**
   - Successfully mediated conflicts such as the Åland Islands dispute between [[Sweden]] and [[Finland]], and the Greco-Bulgarian border conflict.

2. **Humanitarian Efforts:**
   - Addressed issues such as refugee crises, particularly following World War I and the collapse of [[The Ottoman Empire]].
   - Combated human trafficking and worked to improve labor conditions worldwide.

3. **Health and Social Issues:**
   - Established the Health Organization, a precursor to the [[World Health Organization]] (WHO).
   - Worked to control the spread of diseases and improve global health standards.

#### Challenges and Criticisms
1. **Lack of Universal Membership:**
   - [[The United States]] never joined, weakening the League’s authority and global reach.
   - Key powers like [[Germany]] and the [[Soviet Union]] were initially excluded, and later membership was inconsistent.
2. **Ineffectiveness in Preventing Aggression:**
   - Failed to prevent invasions and acts of aggression by Italy in [[Ethiopia]], [[Japan]] in Manchuria, and [[Germany]] in the lead-up to World War II.
   - Lacked a standing military force to enforce its resolutions and decisions.
3. **Decision-Making Inefficiencies:**
   - Required unanimous decisions for major actions, leading to gridlock and inefficiency.
#### Dissolution and Legacy
The League of Nations was unable to prevent the outbreak of World War II, which highlighted its limitations. It was officially dissolved on April 18, 1946, and its functions were transferred to the newly established [[United Nations]] (UN), which aimed to address the shortcomings of the League and provide a more robust framework for international cooperation and conflict resolution.
### Conclusion
The League of Nations was a pioneering effort to create a global organization dedicated to maintaining peace and promoting cooperation among nations. Despite its many challenges and ultimate failure to prevent World War II, the League's legacy lives on through the United Nations, which continues to strive toward the ideals initially set forth by the League.
