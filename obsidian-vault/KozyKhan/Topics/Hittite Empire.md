### History of the Hittite Empire and People

#### Origins and Early History (ca. 2000-1650 BCE)
- **Indo-European Origins**
  - The Hittites were an Indo-European people who migrated to Anatolia (modern-day Turkey) around 2000 BCE.
  - They settled in the central part of the region, known as Hatti, and assimilated the indigenous Hattian culture.

- **Old Hittite Kingdom (ca. 1650-1500 BCE)**
  - **[[Hattusili I]] (ca. 1650-1620 BCE)**
    - Established the Hittite capital at Hattusa.
    - Expanded the kingdom through military campaigns, securing dominance in Anatolia.
  - **[[Mursili I]] (ca. 1620-1590 BCE)**
    - Conducted a successful raid on Babylon, leading to its temporary decline.
    - After Mursili’s assassination, the kingdom experienced internal strife and decentralization.

#### Hittite Empire (ca. 1500-1180 BCE)
- **New Kingdom or Empire Period (ca. 1400-1180 BCE)**
  - **[[Suppiluliuma I]] (ca. 1350-1322 BCE)**
    - Rebuilt the Hittite state after a period of decline.
    - Expanded the empire into Syria, securing vassal states and clashing with Egypt.
  - **[[Muwatalli II]] (ca. 1295-1272 BCE)**
    - Fought against the Egyptian pharaoh [[Ramses II]] at the Battle of Kadesh (ca. 1274 BCE), resulting in one of the earliest known peace treaties.
  - **[[Hattusili III]] (ca. 1267-1237 BCE)**
    - Continued diplomatic relations with Egypt, maintaining stability through alliances.
  - **[[Tudhaliya IV]] (ca. 1237-1209 BCE)**
    - Faced internal revolts and external threats, including from the Assyrians.

- **Decline and Fall (ca. 1200-1180 BCE)**
  - The Hittite Empire began to fragment under pressure from external invasions, including the Sea Peoples.
  - By 1180 BCE, the capital Hattusa was abandoned, marking the end of the Hittite Empire.

#### Neo-Hittite Kingdoms (ca. 1180-700 BCE)
- After the fall of the empire, small Neo-Hittite states emerged in southeastern Anatolia and northern Syria.
- These states maintained Hittite cultural traditions and political structures.

#### Cultural and Scientific Contributions
- **Legal and Political Innovations**
  - Developed one of the earliest legal codes, influencing subsequent Near Eastern legal traditions.
  - Pioneered a feudal system, where vassal states pledged loyalty to the Hittite king.

- **Literature and Language**
  - Hittite is the oldest known Indo-European language, documented in cuneiform tablets.
  - Produced significant mythological and historical texts, preserving a blend of Hattian, Hurrian, and Indo-European traditions.

- **Military Tactics**
  - Known for their use of chariots in warfare, influencing military tactics in the ancient Near East.

- **Art and Architecture**
  - Developed distinctive art and architectural styles, including monumental sculptures and fortified cities.

### Summary
The Hittite Empire, a powerful Indo-European state centered in Anatolia, rose to prominence through military prowess and strategic diplomacy. From its early beginnings and establishment by kings like Hattusili I and Mursili I, the Hittites expanded their territory and influence, clashing with major powers such as Egypt. The empire’s zenith during the New Kingdom saw significant achievements in law, literature, and military tactics. Despite their decline and eventual fall due to internal and external pressures, the legacy of the Hittites continued through the Neo-Hittite states, preserving their cultural and political heritage in the ancient Near East.
