**The Septuagint: The Greek Old Testament and Its Impact on Christianity**

  

The **Septuagint (LXX)** is the **Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible (Old Testament)**, produced in the **3rd–2nd century BC**. It was the **first major translation of Jewish Scriptures** and became the **primary Bible of the early Christian Church**. Many of the **Old Testament quotations in the New Testament come from the Septuagint**, making it a **foundational text for both Judaism and Christianity**.

**1. Origins and Translation Process**

  

**The Name and Meaning**

  

The word **“Septuagint”** (from the Latin _septuaginta_, meaning “seventy”) refers to the tradition that **seventy (or seventy-two) Jewish scholars** translated the Hebrew Scriptures into Greek. This story is recorded in the **Letter of Aristeas** (c. 2nd century BC), which claims that **King Ptolemy II Philadelphus (285–247 BC) of Egypt** commissioned the translation for the famous **[[Library of Alexandria]]**.

  

**The Translation Timeline**

• The **Pentateuch (Torah)** (Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy) was translated **first**, around **250 BC**.

• The **rest of the Hebrew Scriptures** (Prophets, Psalms, Wisdom books, etc.) were translated over the next **100–150 years**.

• Some books, like **Daniel and Esther**, have **Greek additions** that are not in the Hebrew text.

**2. The Textual Differences Between the Septuagint and Hebrew Bible**

  

The **Septuagint (LXX) differs from the Masoretic Text (MT)** (the standard Hebrew Bible used in modern Judaism) in several key ways:

  

**1. Expanded Canon (Deuterocanonical Books)**

  

The LXX includes books **not found in the Hebrew Bible**, known as the **Deuterocanonical books** (or “Apocrypha” in Protestant traditions):

• Tobit

• Judith

• Wisdom of Solomon

• Sirach (Ecclesiasticus)

• Baruch

• 1 & 2 Maccabees

• Additional sections in **Daniel and Esther**

  

These books were **included in early Christian Bibles** and are still part of the **Catholic and Orthodox** canons, though they were **removed from Protestant Bibles** during the Reformation.

  

**2. Differences in Textual Readings**

• **Psalm 151** is included in the Septuagint but missing from the Hebrew Bible.

• **Isaiah 7:14** in the LXX says **“A virgin (παρθένος) shall conceive”**, while the Hebrew text says **“a young woman (עלמה, ‘almah’) shall conceive.”**

• This difference **directly influenced Christian theology**, as the Gospel of **Matthew (1:23)** quotes the LXX to support the **virgin birth of Christ**.

• **Jeremiah is shorter** in the LXX than in the Hebrew Bible by about **one-eighth of its length**.

• **Genesis 5 (Genealogy of Adam)** has different ages for the **patriarchs**.

**3. The Septuagint in Early Christianity**

  

The LXX was the **Bible of the early Church** because:

• Greek was the **dominant language** of the Mediterranean world.

• Most **Jews outside of Israel** (the Jewish Diaspora) spoke **Greek, not Hebrew**.

• The **New Testament writers** (especially Paul) frequently quoted from the Septuagint rather than the Hebrew Bible.

  

For example:

• **Hebrews 1:6** quotes **Deuteronomy 32:43** from the LXX, but this phrase does not exist in the Hebrew Bible.

• **Acts 7:14** (Stephen’s speech) follows LXX numbers for Joseph’s family (75 people), rather than the Hebrew (70 people).

  

This led to **conflict between Jews and Christians**, as **rabbinic Judaism later rejected the LXX in favor of the Masoretic Text**.

**4. Jewish Rejection of the Septuagint**

  

After Christianity spread, Jewish leaders **distanced themselves from the Septuagint** because:

1. **Christians used it to prove that Jesus was the Messiah**.

2. **Textual differences favored Christian interpretations**.

3. By the **2nd century AD**, Jewish scholars (like the **Masoretes**) sought to **preserve the Hebrew text in a standardized form**.

  

The Jewish rejection of the LXX led to **later Greek translations** by:

• **Aquila** (a strict, word-for-word translation)

• **Symmachus** (a smoother, more readable version)

• **Theodotion** (a mix of both approaches)

  

By the **4th century AD**, the Jewish community primarily used the **Masoretic Text**, while **Christians continued to use the Septuagint**.

**5. The Septuagint’s Role in Christian Theology**

  

**1. Christology and Prophecy**

• The **LXX’s use of “virgin” (παρθένος) in Isaiah 7:14** was a key **messianic prophecy** in early Christian thought.

• The **Deuterocanonical books** (like Wisdom and Sirach) shaped early Christian views on **Jesus as the Logos** (Word of God).

• **Typology** (viewing Old Testament events as foreshadowing Christ) was easier in the LXX than in the Hebrew text.

  

**2. Influence on Church Fathers**

• **Origen** (184–253 AD) compiled the **Hexapla**, comparing the LXX with Hebrew and other Greek versions.

• **Athanasius, Augustine, and Cyril of Alexandria** all defended the LXX as **the inspired Old Testament**.

• **The Eastern Orthodox Church still uses the Septuagint as its official Old Testament**.

  

**3. Influence on the Latin Vulgate**

• **St. Jerome (347–420 AD)** originally used the **Septuagint** for his **Latin Vulgate**, but later switched to translating from the **Hebrew Masoretic Text**.

• Despite Jerome’s preference for Hebrew, the **Western Church continued using the Septuagint-based Old Testament**.

**6. The Septuagint Today**

• The **Eastern Orthodox Church** still considers the LXX to be the **authoritative Old Testament**.

• The **Roman Catholic Church** accepts the Deuterocanonical books from the LXX.

• **Protestants** largely reject the Deuterocanon and rely on the Hebrew-based **Masoretic Text**.

  

Modern translations like the **New English Translation of the Septuagint (NETS)** and the **Brenton Septuagint** help scholars study the LXX today.

**Conclusion: Why the Septuagint Matters**

  

The **Septuagint is more than just a translation**—it is **a bridge between Judaism and Christianity**, influencing **New Testament theology, early Church doctrine, and biblical scholarship**. It remains **central in Eastern Orthodox Christianity** and is a crucial text for **understanding biblical history**.
