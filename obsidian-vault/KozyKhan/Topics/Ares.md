**Ares: The God of War, Bloodlust, and Chaos**

  

**Ares** (_Ἄρης_) is the **Greek god of war**, representing the **brutal, violent, and chaotic side of battle**. Unlike Athena, who embodies **strategic warfare and wisdom**, Ares is the **personification of raw aggression, bloodlust, and destruction**. He is both **feared and despised by gods and mortals alike**, yet he remains one of the **most powerful and primal Olympians**.

**I. Origins and Family**

  

**1. Divine Parentage**

• **Father:** [[Zeus]], king of the gods.

• **Mother:** [[Hera]], queen of the gods.

• **Siblings:**

• **[[Hephaestus]]** (god of the forge)

• **[[Eris]]** (goddess of strife and discord)

• **[[Enyo]]** (goddess of war)

• Many others, depending on the myth.

  

**2. His Children and Affairs**

  

Ares had many children, most of whom were **violent warriors or monstrous figures**:

• **With [[Aphrodite]] (goddess of love):**

• **[[Phobos]] (Fear) and [[Deimos]] (Terror)** – His **twin sons** who accompanied him in battle.

• **[[Eros]] (Love/Desire)** – Sometimes said to be his son instead of Aphrodite’s usual partner, [[Hephaestus]].

• **[[Harmonia]]** – Goddess of harmony (ironically, given her father’s chaotic nature).

• **Other Offspring:**

• **The Amazons** – The fierce warrior women were said to be his daughters.

• **Kyknos** – A brutal warrior who tried to kill Hercules.

• **Diomedes of Thrace** – A ruthless king who fed people to his flesh-eating horses.

**II. Ares in Mythology: The Warrior Who Lost Battles**

  

**1. The Iliad: Ares Humbled by Athena**

• In _The Iliad_, **Ares fights for the Trojans**, but his involvement is **reckless and destructive**, causing unnecessary bloodshed.

• **[[Athena]] (his rival) outsmarts him**, knocking him down **with a massive boulder**.

• **Zeus scolds Ares**, calling him **the most hated of all the gods** because of his **mindless destruction**.

  

**2. Affair with Aphrodite and the Trap of Hephaestus**

• Ares was **Aphrodite’s lover**, despite her being married to **Hephaestus**, the lame god of smithing.

• When **Hephaestus discovered their affair**, he created an **unbreakable golden net** and **trapped them in bed together**.

• He then **invited all the gods to witness their humiliation**, making Ares **a laughingstock**.

• Though **Ares was powerful**, this myth shows that **brute force is useless without intelligence**.

  

**3. Captured by the Aloadae Giants**

• The twin giants, **Otus and Ephialtes**, captured Ares and **stuffed him into a bronze jar**.

• He was trapped for **13 months**, only escaping when **Hermes rescued him**.

• Again, this myth highlights **Ares’ lack of strategy and reliance on raw strength alone**.

**III. Symbolism and Worship**

  

**1. Ares as the Embodiment of Chaos and Bloodlust**

• Unlike **Athena, who represents noble war**, Ares represents **senseless violence, slaughter, and the dark side of battle**.

• He is **fierce but unpredictable**, symbolizing **war’s destructive consequences**.

  

**2. His Sacred Animals and Symbols**

• **Animals:**

• **Vulture** – A symbol of death and carnage.

• **Dog** – Linked to bloodthirst and wild aggression.

• **Serpent** – Sometimes associated with his **hidden wisdom and unpredictable nature**.

• **Weapons & Symbols:**

• **Spear and shield** – Representing relentless battle.

• **Flaming torch** – Symbolizing destruction and war’s uncontrollable nature.

  

**3. Temples and Cults**

• Unlike other Olympians, **Ares had very few temples** because Greeks saw him as **dangerous and destructive**.

• However, **the Spartans and Thracians** worshipped him **fiercely**, offering **human sacrifices** before battles.

• In **Rome, Ares became Mars**, a more **respected and disciplined god of war**.

**IV. Ares vs. Mars: The Greek vs. Roman War Gods**

|**Aspect**|**Ares (Greek)**|**Mars (Roman)**|
|---|---|---|
|**Personality**|Brutal, reckless, chaotic|Strategic, disciplined, respected|
|**View by People**|Feared and disliked|Honored and worshipped|
|**Role in Society**|War as destruction|War as necessary for empire|
|**Mythology**|Often humiliated or outmatched|Seen as a noble warrior and protector|

**V. The Legacy of Ares**

  

**1. In Philosophy and Literature**

• **Homer’s Iliad** portrays him as **a destructive force rather than a noble god**.

• **Plato and later philosophers saw Ares as the embodiment of unchecked aggression**, a cautionary figure.

  

**2. In Modern Culture**

• Ares appears in **movies, video games, and literature**, often as a **villainous war deity**.

• In DC Comics, he is **a major enemy of Wonder Woman**.

• In video games like _God of War_, he is a **bloodthirsty antagonist**.

**VI. Conclusion: The Warrior Who Was Feared but Not Loved**

  

Ares is the **primordial force of war in its rawest form**—a god of **bloodlust, violence, and destruction**. Unlike the strategic **Mars of Rome**, Ares is **unpredictable, chaotic, and often outmatched**. Though he was **strong and feared**, he was also **hated by his own divine family**. His myths serve as a **warning about the dangers of unchecked aggression**, reminding us that **war without wisdom leads only to downfall**.
