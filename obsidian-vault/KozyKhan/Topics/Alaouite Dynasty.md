The **Alaouite dynasty** (Arabic: العلويون, al-ʿAlawiyyūn) is the current ruling dynasty of Morocco, in power since 1666, making it one of the world’s oldest continuous monarchies. Like the Saadians before them, the Alaouites claim sharifian descent—tracing their lineage to the [[Prophet Muhammad]] through his grandson [[Hasan ibn Ali]] via Ali ibn Abi Talib and Fatima. Originating from the Tafilalt region in southeastern Morocco, they rose to prominence by unifying a fragmented country after the Saadian decline, establishing a stable, enduring rule that blends religious legitimacy with political adaptability.

### Origins and Rise to Power
The Alaouites hail from the oasis of Sijilmasa in Tafilalt, a historic trade hub. Their founder, **Moulay Ali Sharif** (d. 1659), was a sharif who settled there in the 13th century, but it was his descendant, **Moulay Muhammad ibn Sharif**, who began their ascent by governing Tafilalt in the early 17th century. His brother, **[[Moulay Rashid]]**, seized Fez in 1664 and, after defeating rival factions like the Zawiya of Dila, proclaimed himself sultan in 1666. By 1668, he had taken Marrakesh from the last Saadian holdouts, ending their dynasty and consolidating Alaouite control.

### Consolidation Under Moulay Ismail
The dynasty’s true foundation was laid by **[[Moulay Ismail ibn Sharif]]** (r. 1672–1727), Rashid’s half-brother and one of Morocco’s most formidable rulers:
- **Military Strength:** Ismail built a professional army, the **Abid al-Bukhari** (Black Guard), composed of enslaved Sub-Saharan Africans and their descendants, ensuring loyalty and crushing tribal rebellions.
- **Centralization:** He moved the capital to Meknes, constructing a vast palace complex and imposing order on a previously chaotic realm.
- **Foreign Policy:** Ismail repelled Ottoman incursions and negotiated with European powers, famously ransoming captives from Barbary piracy while resisting colonial encroachment.
- His 55-year reign—exceptionally long for the era—stabilized Morocco but left a succession crisis after his death, as his numerous sons (hundreds, by some accounts) vied for power.

### Evolution and Challenges
The Alaouites faced ups and downs over centuries:
- **18th–19th Century:** Rulers like **Moulay Sulayman** (r. 1792–1822) focused on internal reform and isolationism, while **Moulay Abd al-Rahman** (r. 1822–1859) dealt with French and Spanish pressure, losing Algeria to France in 1830.
- **Colonial Era:** Under **Moulay Yusuf** (r. 1912–1927) and **Mohammed V** (r. 1927–1961), Morocco became a French protectorate (1912–1956), with Spain controlling the north and south. Mohammed V’s alliance with nationalists led to independence in 1956, cementing his legacy as a unifying figure.

### Modern Era
Since independence, the Alaouites have adapted to a constitutional monarchy:
- **Hassan II (r. 1961–1999):** Survived coups and ruled with an iron fist during the “Years of Lead,” balancing modernization with authoritarianism. He also launched the Green March in 1975, reclaiming Western Sahara.
- **Mohammed VI (r. 1999–present):** As of April 2025, he remains king, known for economic reforms, women’s rights advancements (e.g., the 2004 Mudawana family code), and infrastructure projects like high-speed rail. His reign has faced criticism over human rights and Western Sahara, but he retains broad support as “Commander of the Faithful.”

### Key Features
- **Religious Authority:** The Alaouites’ sharifian status gives them spiritual clout, reinforced by their role as protectors of Morocco’s Maliki Sunni Islam and Sufi traditions.
- **Longevity:** Their ability to navigate tribal politics, European colonialism, and modern governance has kept them in power for over 350 years.
- **Cultural Impact:** They’ve fostered a distinct Moroccan identity, seen in landmarks like the Hassan II Mosque in Casablanca, completed in 1993.

### Legacy
The Alaouites transformed Morocco from a post-Saadian patchwork into a centralized state, weathering foreign domination and internal strife. Today, they symbolize continuity and resilience, with Mohammed VI steering the kingdom through globalization, regional instability (e.g., the Arab Spring), and economic challenges. Their rule blends tradition with cautious modernization, maintaining Morocco as a stable outlier in a turbulent region.
