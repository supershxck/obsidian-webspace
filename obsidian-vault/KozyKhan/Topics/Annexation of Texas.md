**The Annexation of Texas (1845): Expansion, Controversy, and Consequence**

---

**Introduction**

  

The **Annexation of Texas** on December 29, 1845 transformed the Republic of Texas into the 28th state of the United States. Fueled by **Manifest Destiny**, it marked a pivotal expansion of U.S. territory—and intensified sectional tensions over slavery.

---

**Historical Context**

  

**Texas Independence (1836–1845)**

• Texas seceded from Mexico in 1836 after the Battle of San Jacinto

• Functioned as an independent republic facing economic hardship, Native American raids, and Mexican threats

  

**U.S. Expansionism**

• Growing belief in Manifest Destiny urged westward growth

• Southern planters eyed Texas’s fertile land for cotton cultivation

---

**Political Process & Debate**

|**Actor**|**Position**|**Motivation**|
|---|---|---|
|President John Tyler|Advocated annexation via joint resolution|Secure southern support, strengthen presidency|
|Congress|Narrow vote (House: 120–98; Senate: 27–25)|Split between expansionist Democrats and anti‑slavery Whigs|
|Northern Opposition|Fought expansion fearing spread of slavery|Prevent sectional imbalance in Senate|
|Texas Government|Sought U.S. protection from Mexico|Economic stability and security|

  

---

**Mechanism of Annexation**

• **Joint Resolution (March 1, 1845):** Lower legislative threshold (simple majority) bypassed two‑thirds Senate treaty requirement

• **Statehood Admission:** Texas accepted on December 29, 1845 under conditions preserving public lands and permitting future division into multiple states

---

**Consequences**

| **Domain**   | **Outcome**                                                                   |
| ------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Territorial  | Added ~389,000 sq mi; precipitated [[Mexican-American War]] (1846–1848)       |
| Slavery      | Expanded slaveholding territory, deepening North-South divide                 |
| Diplomacy    | Strained U.S.-Mexico relations; led to Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo            |
| Demographics | Stimulated Anglo-American migration; displaced Native and Mexican communities |

**Key Vocabulary**

• **Manifest Destiny:** Belief in America’s ordained expansion

• **Joint Resolution:** Legislative measure requiring simple majority

• **Sectionalism:** Regional loyalty intensifying political conflict

• **Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo:** 1848 treaty ending Mexican-American War
