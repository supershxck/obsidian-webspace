The Norman Kingdom of Sicily was a remarkable medieval state in southern Italy and Sicily, established by Norman adventurers in the 11th century and lasting until 1194 CE (formally until 1266 in some reckonings). It emerged from the conquests of the Hauteville (or Altavilla) family, blending Norman, Latin, Greek, and Arab influences into a unique multicultural realm. Here’s an overview of its history, character, and legacy:

---

### Origins and Conquest (c. 1017–1130)
- **Norman Arrival**: The [[Normans]], descendants of Viking settlers in northern France, began infiltrating southern Italy as mercenaries in the early 11th century. Invited by Lombard princes to fight Byzantine and Muslim forces, they soon turned opportunists.
- **Key Figures**: The Hauteville brothers—sons of Tancred of Hauteville—led the charge:
  - **Robert Guiscard**: By 1059, he was named Duke of Apulia and Calabria by Pope Nicholas II, legitimizing Norman control over former Lombard and Byzantine lands.
  - **Roger I**: Younger brother of Robert, he spearheaded the conquest of Sicily, a Muslim emirate since the 9th century.
- **Sicily’s Conquest**:
  - **1061**: Roger I began the campaign, capturing Messina with Robert’s aid.
  - **1071**: After Manzikert weakened Byzantium, the Normans took Bari, ending Byzantine rule in Italy.
  - **1091**: Palermo fell, completing the Norman takeover of Sicily after 30 years of intermittent war against the Kalbid emirs and local resistance.
- **Establishment**: In 1130, Roger II, son of Roger I, unified these territories—Sicily, Apulia, Calabria, and parts of Naples—into a single kingdom.

---

### Foundation of the Kingdom (1130)
- **Coronation**: On Christmas Day 1130, Roger II was crowned King of Sicily in Palermo, with papal approval from the antipope Anacletus II (a contested blessing due to the schism with Pope Innocent II). This marked the birth of the Norman Kingdom of Sicily.
- **Territory**: At its peak, it spanned Sicily, southern Italy up to Abruzzo, and briefly parts of North Africa (e.g., Tunis, Tripoli, 1140s–1150s).

---

### Key Rulers and Reigns
1. **Roger II (1130–1154)**:
   - **Consolidation**: Centralized power, creating a strong monarchy with a sophisticated bureaucracy inherited from Arab and Byzantine predecessors.
   - **Multiculturalism**: Roger embraced Sicily’s diversity—Greek administrators, Arab scholars, and Norman knights coexisted. He spoke Arabic and patronized all three cultures.
   - **Military**: Defeated rebellions in Italy and expanded into North Africa, challenging Byzantine naval power.
   - **Legacy**: His court at Palermo became a cultural hub, blending East and West.
2. **William I “the Bad” (1154–1166)**:
   - **Reign**: Faced baronial revolts and Byzantine attacks (e.g., 1155–1156). His harsh rule earned his nickname, though his admiral Maio of Bari stabilized the kingdom.
   - **Challenges**: Lost African holdings to the Almohads by 1160.
3. **William II “the Good” (1166–1189)**:
   - **Golden Age**: A peaceful, prosperous reign. He built the Monreale Cathedral and aligned with the Holy Roman Empire via marriage to Joan of England.
   - **Diplomacy**: Avoided major wars, focusing on trade and culture.
   - **End**: Died childless, sparking succession crises.
4. **Tancred (1189–1194)** and **William III (1194)**:
   - **Decline**: Tancred, an illegitimate cousin, faced opposition from Holy Roman Emperor Henry VI, husband of Constance (Roger II’s daughter). After Tancred’s death, William III briefly ruled as a puppet until Henry VI seized the kingdom.

---

### Governance and Society
- **Administration**: The Normans adopted and refined existing systems:
  - **Arab Influence**: Retained the *diwan* (financial office) and Arabic record-keeping.
  - **Byzantine Model**: Used Greek officials and a hierarchical bureaucracy.
  - **Feudalism**: Imposed Norman-style land tenure, with barons owing military service.
- **Law**: Roger II’s *Assizes of Ariano* (1140) codified a legal system blending Roman, Norman, and local traditions.
- **Religion**: Officially Latin Christian, but tolerant of Orthodox Greeks, Muslims, and Jews. Mosques and synagogues operated alongside churches.
- **Economy**: Thrived on Mediterranean trade—grain, silk, citrus—linking Europe, North Africa, and the Levant. Palermo and Messina were bustling ports.

---

### Culture and Achievements
- **Multicultural Fusion**: The kingdom was a melting pot:
  - **Architecture**: The Cappella Palatina and Monreale Cathedral mix Norman arches, Byzantine mosaics, and Arab stucco work.
  - **Science and Learning**: Arab scholars like Al-Idrisi (who created the *Tabula Rogeriana* world map for Roger II) worked alongside Greek and Latin thinkers.
  - **Language**: Latin, Greek, and Arabic were official; Sicilian dialects emerged as a vernacular.
- **Art**: Known for opulent mosaics and the *coronation mantle* of Roger II, inscribed with Arabic blessings.

---

### Decline and End (1194–1266)
- **Hohenstaufen Takeover**: In 1194, Henry VI of the Holy Roman Empire, claiming the throne via Constance, conquered Sicily after Tancred’s death. The Norman dynasty ended, and the kingdom became part of the Hohenstaufen realm.
- **Formal End**: The [[Hohenstaufen]] ruled until 1266, when Charles of Anjou defeated Manfred (Henry VI’s illegitimate son) at Benevento. The subsequent Angevin period (1266–1282) ended with the Sicilian Vespers revolt, transferring Sicily to Aragon.
- **Legacy Continuation**: The title “Kingdom of Sicily” persisted under various rulers, but the Norman era closed in 1194.

---

### Significance
- **Bridge of Cultures**: The kingdom fused Norman militarism, Byzantine administration, and Arab intellectualism, influencing medieval Europe’s cultural evolution.
- **Power Projection**: It challenged Byzantium, the Papacy, and Muslim powers, briefly dominating the central Mediterranean.
- **Precedent**: Its centralized monarchy and tolerance foreshadowed later Mediterranean states like Aragon and the Ottoman Empire.
- **Historical Echo**: The Norman Kingdom inspired romanticized views of chivalry and multiculturalism in later European literature.
