### **Overview of Indian Philosophy**

Indian philosophy spans thousands of years and includes a diverse array of schools of thought. These schools can broadly be divided into two categories: **orthodox (Āstika)** and **heterodox (Nāstika)** systems, based on whether they accept the authority of the **Vedas**.

---

### **I. Orthodox (Āstika) Schools**

These schools accept the Vedas as authoritative and often align with Hindu religious and philosophical thought.

#### **1. Nyāya (Logic and Epistemology)**
- **Founder:** Sage Gautama (Akṣapāda).
- **Focus:** Logical reasoning, epistemology (pramāṇas), and the pursuit of truth.  
- **Key Ideas:**
  - **Pramāṇas:** Sources of valid knowledge (perception, inference, comparison, testimony).
  - Emphasis on systematic logic to understand the world and attain liberation (mokṣa).  

#### **2. Vaiśeṣika (Atomism and Metaphysics)**
- **Founder:** Sage Kaṇāda.  
- **Focus:** Study of the nature of reality through categories (**padārthas**).  
- **Key Ideas:**
  - The world is composed of eternal **atoms** (paramāṇu).
  - Padārthas include substances, qualities, actions, and universals.

#### **3. Sāṃkhya (Dualism of Matter and Spirit)**
- **Founder:** Sage Kapila.  
- **Focus:** Analysis of reality as dualistic: **Prakṛti (matter)** and **Puruṣa (consciousness)**.  
- **Key Ideas:**
  - Liberation occurs by disentangling Puruṣa from Prakṛti through knowledge.
  - Prakṛti evolves into the material world, governed by three **guṇas** (qualities): sattva, rajas, and tamas.

#### **4. Yoga (Practical Discipline for Liberation)**
- **Founder:** Sage Patañjali (author of the *Yoga Sūtras*).  
- **Focus:** Union of individual consciousness with the universal.  
- **Key Ideas:**
  - **Aṣṭāṅga Yoga (Eightfold Path):** Ethical principles, physical postures, breath control, meditation, and samādhi (absorption).  
  - Closely linked with Sāṃkhya philosophy.

#### **5. Pūrva Mīmāṃsā (Ritualism and Vedic Interpretation)**
- **Founder:** Sage Jaimini.  
- **Focus:** Analysis of Vedic texts, emphasizing **karma (ritual action)**.  
- **Key Ideas:**
  - Rituals (yajñas) are central to maintaining cosmic order (ṛta).
  - Dharma is derived from the injunctions of the Vedas.

#### **6. Vedānta (Philosophy of the Upaniṣads)**
- **Founder:** Based on the *Upaniṣads*, interpreted by later thinkers like Śaṅkara, Rāmānuja, and Madhva.  
- **Focus:** Nature of Brahman (ultimate reality) and the self (ātman).  
- **Key Schools:**
  - **Advaita Vedānta (Non-Dualism):** Śaṅkara’s philosophy that Brahman and ātman are identical.
  - **Viśiṣṭādvaita (Qualified Non-Dualism):** Rāmānuja’s idea that Brahman is the personal God with attributes.
  - **Dvaita (Dualism):** Madhva’s view that Brahman and individual souls are distinct.

---

### **II. Heterodox (Nāstika) Schools**

These schools reject the authority of the Vedas and often align with alternate religious traditions like Jainism and Buddhism.

#### **1. Cārvāka (Materialism and Skepticism)**
- **Focus:** Materialism and rejection of metaphysics, rituals, and the afterlife.  
- **Key Ideas:**
  - Sensory perception is the only valid source of knowledge.
  - Pursuit of pleasure (kāma) is the primary goal of life.

#### **2. Jain Philosophy**
- **Founder:** Mahavira (24th Tīrthaṅkara).  
- **Focus:** Liberation through non-violence (ahiṃsā) and austerity.  
- **Key Ideas:**
  - Reality is composed of **jīva (soul)** and **ajīva (non-soul)**.
  - Strict ethical practices and avoidance of karma accumulation lead to liberation (mokṣa).

#### **3. Buddhist Philosophy**
- **Founder:** Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha).  
- **Focus:** Overcoming suffering (duḥkha) through the **Four Noble Truths** and the **Eightfold Path**.  
- **Key Schools:**
  - **Theravāda:** Focuses on early teachings of the Buddha.
  - **Madhyamaka (Middle Way):** Nāgārjuna’s philosophy of emptiness (śūnyatā).
  - **Yogācāra:** Emphasis on mind-only (citta-mātra) philosophy.

---

### **III. Later Developments and Syntheses**

#### **1. Bhakti Movements (Devotion)**
- Emphasis on personal devotion to God (e.g., Vishnu, Shiva, Devi).
- Saints like **Kabir, Mirabai, Tulsidas**, and **Chaitanya** combined philosophical insights with emotional devotion.

#### **2. Sufism and Indo-Islamic Philosophy**
- Mystical traditions from Islam influenced Indian thought, focusing on divine love and unity (tawḥīd).

#### **3. Modern Indian Philosophy**
- Thinkers like **Swami Vivekananda, Rabindranath Tagore, Aurobindo Ghose, and Mahatma Gandhi** synthesized traditional Indian ideas with Western philosophy.
- Focus on spirituality, social reform, and global unity.
