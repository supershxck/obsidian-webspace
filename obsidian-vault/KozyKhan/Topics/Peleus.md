**Peleus: The Mortal King and Father of Achilles**

  

**Peleus** (_Πηλεύς_) is a **heroic king of Greek mythology**, best known as the **father of Achilles**, the greatest warrior of the Trojan War. He was one of the **Argonauts**, a participant in the **Calydonian Boar Hunt**, and the **husband of the sea nymph Thetis**. His life was marked by **glory, divine favor, tragedy, and loss**, making him a figure of both **great triumph and sorrow**.

**I. Origins and Early Life**

  

**1. Noble Lineage**

• **Father:** **Aeacus**, king of Aegina, known for his **justice and wisdom**.

• **Mother:** **Endeïs**, a mountain nymph.

• **Brother:** **Telamon**, who later became the father of **Ajax the Great**.

  

Peleus and Telamon were **exiled from Aegina** after they **killed their half-brother, Phocus**, either out of jealousy or by accident. They sought refuge in **Phthia**, where Peleus was purified by **King Eurytion**.

**II. Heroic Deeds of Peleus**

  

**1. The Calydonian Boar Hunt**

• Peleus joined the famous hunt to kill the **Calydonian Boar**, a monstrous creature sent by Artemis.

• During the battle, he **accidentally killed King Eurytion with a stray spear**, leading to **more exile and guilt**.

  

**2. The Argonauts and the Quest for the Golden Fleece**

• Peleus was one of the **Argonauts**, sailing with **Jason, Heracles, and Orpheus** to **Colchis**.

• He played a minor role in the adventure but remained a **trusted warrior and ally**.

  

**3. Escape from Acastus and the Treacherous Queen**

• Peleus sought **purification for his accidental killing of Eurytion** from **King Acastus of Iolcus**.

• Acastus’ wife, **Astydameia**, **fell in love with Peleus**, but he rejected her.

• **Enraged, she falsely accused him of trying to seduce her**, causing Acastus to **plot his murder**.

• Acastus **abandoned Peleus unarmed on Mount Pelion**, where **centaurs** lived.

• However, the wise centaur **Chiron saved him**, gifting him the famous **sword of Hephaestus**, a divine weapon.

**III. The Divine Marriage of Peleus and Thetis**

  

**1. Thetis: The Sea Nymph Destined to Bear a Great Son**

• Thetis was a **beautiful Nereid (sea nymph)** desired by both **Zeus and Poseidon**.

• A prophecy warned that **her son would be greater than his father**, leading the gods to **marry her off to a mortal**—Peleus.

  

**2. The Struggle to Win Thetis**

• Thetis **rejected Peleus**, transforming into **fire, water, a lion, and a serpent** to escape him.

• **Guided by the centaur Chiron**, Peleus **held onto her** despite her shape-shifting, proving his worth.

• Defeated, Thetis **agreed to marry him**.

  

**3. The Wedding of Peleus and Thetis**

• Their **wedding was held on Mount Pelion**, attended by **all the gods**.

• **Hephaestus gifted Peleus a magical sword**, and **Poseidon gave him immortal horses**, **Balius and Xanthus**.

• However, **Eris (the goddess of discord) was not invited**, leading her to **throw the Golden Apple of Discord**, causing the feud between **Hera, Athena, and Aphrodite**—the event that eventually **led to the Trojan War**.

**IV. Peleus as the Father of Achilles**

  

**1. Thetis’ Attempt to Make Achilles Immortal**

• Thetis tried to **make Achilles immortal** by:

1. **Dipping him in the River Styx**—but **his heel remained mortal**.

2. **Burning away his mortality** in a fire ritual—Peleus **interrupted her**, causing her to leave him forever.

  

**2. Achilles’ Fate and Peleus’ Sorrow**

• Achilles was raised by **Chiron**, becoming **the greatest Greek warrior**.

• **Peleus did not fight in the Trojan War** but **lived to see Achilles die**.

• **He later lost his grandson Neoptolemus**, leaving him **alone and sorrowful in his old age**.

**V. Symbolism and Esoteric Meaning**

  

**1. The Mortal Tied to the Divine**

• Peleus represents **the struggle of mortals who touch the divine but remain bound to fate**.

• His **marriage to Thetis** symbolizes the **union of earth and water**, yet **mortal limits prevent true harmony**.

  

**2. The Tragic Fate of Heroes**

• Despite being **brave and noble**, Peleus’ life was full of **exile, loss, and grief**.

• His story echoes **the inevitable tragedy of Greek heroes**, who **rise to greatness but cannot escape sorrow**.

**VI. Worship and Legacy**

  

**1. Peleus as a Legendary Ancestor**

• He was **honored as the father of Achilles** but was **not widely worshipped as a hero**.

• His lineage was **celebrated in [[Thessaly]] and Sparta**, where Achilles’ memory was strongest.

  

**2. Peleus in Later Literature and Mythology**

• His tragic tale appears in:

• **Homer’s Iliad** – where he sends **Achilles to war, knowing his fate**.

• **Ovid’s Metamorphoses** – which describes **his marriage to Thetis**.

• **Post-Trojan War legends**, where he is left **mourning and forgotten**.

**VII. Conclusion: The Sorrowful King and Forgotten Hero**

  

Peleus was a **warrior, king, and father of the greatest hero of Greece**, yet his own **life was filled with sorrow and exile**. He touched **divine power** but remained **mortal, bound by fate**. His **greatest legacy was Achilles**, yet he **lived long enough to see him die**, making Peleus a **symbol of tragic heroism, lost glory, and the limits of mortal ambition**.
