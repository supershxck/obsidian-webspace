The **Toltec civilization** was a influential Mesoamerican culture that flourished between approximately **900–1150 CE** during the Postclassic period. Centered in **Tula** (modern Hidalgo, Mexico), the Toltecs are often regarded as cultural and political precursors to the Aztecs, who revered them as a model of wisdom, artistry, and power. While historical details are intertwined with myth, the Toltecs left a lasting legacy in art, architecture, and ideology across Mesoamerica.

---

### **Key Aspects of the Toltec Civilization:**

1. **Geography and Capital**:
   - The Toltec heartland was in **central Mexico**, with their capital at **Tollan** (Tula), a city renowned for its grandeur and influence.
   - Tula’s strategic location allowed control over trade routes connecting the Valley of Mexico to northern and southern regions.

2. **History and Timeline**:
   - **Rise**: The Toltecs emerged after the collapse of **Teotihuacan** (c. 650 CE), filling a power vacuum. Their golden age began under the semi-legendary ruler **Ce Acatl Topiltzin Quetzalcoatl** (a priest-king associated with the feathered serpent god).
   - **Decline**: By the 12th century, Tula fell due to drought, internal strife, or invasions by nomadic groups like the **Chichimecs**. Survivors migrated south, influencing Maya cities such as **Chichen Itza**.

3. **Political and Military Power**:
   - The Toltecs established a **militaristic state** that expanded through conquest and alliances. Their warriors (called *Tolteca-Chichimeca*) were feared and respected.
   - They created a network of tributary states, spreading their cultural and religious practices across Mesoamerica.

4. **Economy**:
   - Agriculture (maize, beans, squash) and **trade** were central. Toltec artisans produced obsidian tools, pottery, textiles, and metals (copper and gold).
   - Tula became a hub for exchanging goods like feathers, jade, and cacao, linking regions from the American Southwest to Central America.

5. **Religion and Mythology**:
   - The Toltecs worshipped a pantheon of gods, including **Quetzalcoatl** (feathered serpent, god of wisdom) and **Tezcatlipoca** (smoking mirror, god of destiny and conflict). Their rivalry is central to Toltec myths.
   - **Human sacrifice** and ritual warfare were practiced, later adopted and intensified by the Aztecs.
   - The Toltecs promoted the cult of Quetzalcoatl, whose exile from Tula (due to Tezcatlipoca’s scheming) became a foundational myth.

6. **Art and Architecture**:
   - **Tula’s architecture** featured pyramid-temples, colonnaded halls, and ball courts. The **Temple of Tlahuizcalpantecuhtli** (Morning Star) is famed for its 4.6-meter-tall **Atlantean figures** (carved warrior columns).
   - **Chacmools** (reclining stone figures holding offering bowls) and **tzompantli** (skull racks) first appeared in Toltec art, later adopted by the Aztecs.
   - Their art blended realism and symbolism, depicting warriors, jaguars, eagles, and serpents.

7. **Cultural Influence**:
   - The Toltecs profoundly influenced later civilizations. The **Aztecs** claimed descent from them, calling their own era the "Fifth Sun" in Toltec tradition.
   - **Chichen Itza** (in Yucatán) shows strong Toltec architectural and artistic traits, suggesting a Toltec-Maya hybrid culture after Toltec migrants arrived there.

8. **Legacy and Controversy**:
   - The term **Toltec** (Nahuatl for "artisan") became synonymous with "wise craftsman" or "noble sage" in Aztec lore. The Aztecs idealized Toltec achievements, blurring history with legend.
   - Scholars debate the extent of Toltec political control versus their cultural influence. Some view them as a **hegemonic empire**, others as a confederation of city-states.

---

### **Decline and Historical Impact**:
The Toltec collapse around 1150 CE marked the end of their dominance, but their legacy endured. Their innovations in warfare, urban planning, and art shaped the Aztec worldview, while their myths about Quetzalcoatl influenced the Spanish conquest (as the Aztecs initially mistook Cortés for the returning god). Today, Tula’s ruins and the enduring symbolism of Quetzalcoatl attest to the Toltecs’ pivotal role in Mesoamerican history.
