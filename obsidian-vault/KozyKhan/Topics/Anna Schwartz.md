**Anna J. Schwartz (1915–2012): Co‑Architect of Monetarist History**

---

**Introduction**

  

Anna Jacobson Schwartz was an American economist renowned for her groundbreaking empirical analysis of monetary history. Her collaboration with [[Milton Friedman]] produced **A Monetary History of the United States, 1867–1960**, fundamentally shaping [[Monetarism]] and modern central‑bank policy.

---

**Early Life & Education**

• **Born:** November 11, 1915, New York City

• **Education:** BA (Hunter College, 1937); PhD in Economics (Columbia University, 1946)

• Mentored by Wesley Mitchell at the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER)

---

**Major Contributions**

|**Work**|**Year**|**Contribution**|
|---|---|---|
|_A Monetary History of the United States_ (with Friedman)|1963|Empirical evidence linking money supply contractions to the Great Depression; monetarist paradigm|
|Numerous NBER studies|1940s–1980s|Statistical reconstruction of U.S. money supply series; analysis of banking crises|

  

---

**Key Insights & Impact**

|**Insight**|**Significance**|
|---|---|
|Fed’s Contraction Deepened Depression|Challenged Keynesian interpretation; elevated role of central banks in macro stabilization|
|Long‑Run Neutrality of Money|Reinforced view that stable money growth underpins price stability|
|Historical Data Rigor|Set gold standard for empirical economic research|

  

---

**Philosophical & Psychological Dimensions**

|**Dimension**|**Insight**|
|---|---|
|Collective Shadow|Exposed how policy neglect can exacerbate societal suffering during crises (Jungian)|
|Ethical Imperative|Emphasized accountability and transparency in monetary governance|

  

---

**Legacy & Influence**

• Standard reference for central bankers worldwide

• Influenced U.S. Federal Reserve policy framework toward inflation targeting

• Inspired generations of economic historians and quantitative researchers

---

**Practical Framework: Applying Schwartz’s Methodology Today**

|**Step**|**Action**|**Outcome**|
|---|---|---|
|1|Historical Data Reconstruction|Robust understanding of financial cycles|
|2|Empirical Policy Evaluation|Evidence-based monetary policy decisions|
|3|Transparent Reporting|Enhanced public trust in central banks|
|4|Crisis Diagnostics|Early identification of liquidity crunches|

  

---

**Key Vocabulary**

• **Monetarism:** Theory linking money supply to macroeconomic outcomes

• **Money Supply Series:** Statistical measures (M1, M2) of currency and deposits

• **Great Contraction:** Sharp decline in money supply during the early 1930s

• **Empirical Economics:** Data-driven analysis of economic phenomena
