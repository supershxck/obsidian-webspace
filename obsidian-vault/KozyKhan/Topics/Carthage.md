**Carthage: The Great Rival of Rome**

  

**Carthage (814–146 BC)** was an ancient **Phoenician city-state** and **maritime empire** that became the greatest **trading power of the Western Mediterranean**. Founded by the **Phoenicians of Tyre** (modern Lebanon), it grew into a **superpower** that controlled vast territories in **North Africa, Spain, Sicily, and Sardinia**.

  

Carthage is best known for:

• **Its powerful navy and trade network.**

• **The Punic Wars (264–146 BC) against Rome.**

• **Hannibal’s legendary invasion of Italy (218 BC).**

• **Its destruction by Rome in 146 BC.**

**1. The Founding of Carthage (814 BC)**

  

According to legend, **Queen Dido (Elissa)**, a Phoenician princess from **Tyre**, fled to North Africa and founded **Carthage (“Kart-Hadasht,” meaning “New City”)**.

• **Located in modern Tunisia**, Carthage had a **strategic position** on the Mediterranean.

• It became a **trading hub** connecting **Phoenicia, Africa, and Europe**.

• By the **6th century BC**, Carthage surpassed **Tyre** and became **independent**.

**2. Carthage’s Power and Society**

  

**A. Economy: The Trading Superpower**

  

Carthage was **the richest city in the Western Mediterranean**, thanks to:

• **Phoenician trade networks** across the Mediterranean.

• **Control of major trade routes** in **North Africa, Spain, and Sicily**.

• A vast **merchant fleet** and powerful **navy**.

  

**B. Government and Politics**

• **Ruled by an oligarchy** of **wealthy merchant families** (not a monarchy).

• **Two elected suffetes (judges) ruled**, advised by a powerful **Senate**.

• **Generals, like Hannibal, had influence**, but politicians controlled the state.

  

**C. Religion and Culture**

• Worshiped **Phoenician gods** like **Baal Hammon and Tanit**.

• Practiced **child sacrifices (according to Greek and Roman sources)** at the **Tophet of Carthage**.

• Blended **Phoenician, African, and Mediterranean cultures**.

**3. The Punic Wars: Rome vs. Carthage (264–146 BC)**

  

Carthage’s greatest conflict was with **Rome**, leading to three **Punic Wars** that decided control of the Mediterranean.

  

**First Punic War (264–241 BC) – War for Sicily**

• Rome and Carthage fought for **Sicily**.

• **Rome built its first navy** and **defeated Carthage**.

• Carthage **lost Sicily** and had to pay **Rome war reparations**.

  

**Second Punic War (218–201 BC) – Hannibal’s Revenge**

• **Hannibal Barca**, Carthage’s greatest general, **invaded Italy via the Alps**.

• **Won major battles** (Trebia, Lake Trasimene, Cannae), nearly defeating Rome.

• **Rome counterattacked**, forcing Hannibal back to Africa.

• **Battle of Zama (202 BC)** – Roman general **Scipio Africanus defeated Hannibal**.

• Carthage **lost Spain and became a Roman vassal**.

  

**Third Punic War (149–146 BC) – The Destruction of Carthage**

• Rome, fearing Carthage’s resurgence, **declared war again**.

• After a **three-year siege**, Roman general **Scipio Aemilianus burned Carthage to the ground**.

• **Carthage was completely destroyed**, and its people were **killed or enslaved**.

• Rome **salted the earth** (possibly a myth) to prevent rebuilding.

**4. The Legacy of Carthage**

  

**A. Carthage’s Influence on Rome**

• Rome **adopted Carthaginian naval technology**, improving its **fleet**.

• Hannibal’s tactics influenced **Roman generals like Scipio and Julius Caesar**.

• The **Latin term “Punic” (Punicus) means “Carthaginian,” reflecting their Phoenician origin**.

  

**B. Roman Carthage and Later History**

• Rome **rebuilt Carthage as a colony** in **146 BC**, making it a major city again.

• Under **Augustus (1st century AD), Carthage became one of Rome’s largest cities**.

• It became a center of **Christianity**, producing **Tertullian, St. Cyprian, and St. Augustine**.

**5. Why Carthage Matters**

  

✅ **Rivaled Rome for Mediterranean supremacy.**

✅ **Produced one of the greatest generals in history (Hannibal).**

✅ **Major influence on Mediterranean trade, warfare, and culture.**

✅ **Rome’s destruction of Carthage symbolized the birth of Roman dominance.**
