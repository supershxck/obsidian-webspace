**The 1814 Norwegian Constitution: A Comprehensive Overview**

  

**I. Introduction**

  

The **1814 Norwegian Constitution** is a landmark document in Norway’s history, representing the nation’s quest for self-determination and democratic governance. Drafted and signed at Eidsvoll on May 17, 1814, the constitution established Norway as an independent constitutional monarchy. Although its immediate impact led to a brief period of independence before entering a union with Sweden, the 1814 Constitution remains a foundational symbol of Norwegian democracy and national identity, celebrated annually on Norway’s Constitution Day, **May 17**.

  

**II. Historical Context**

  

**A. Denmark-Norway Union**

1. **Union Dynamics:**

• From 1380 to 1814, Norway was in a union with Denmark, often referred to as **Denmark-Norway**. During this period, Denmark exerted significant control over Norwegian affairs, including its foreign policy and governance.

2. **Napoleonic Wars Influence:**

• The early 19th century was marked by the **[[Napoleonic Wars]]** (1803–1815), which reshaped European political boundaries. Denmark-Norway initially remained neutral but eventually sided with Napoleon after the British attack on Copenhagen in 1807.

  

**B. Treaty of Kiel and Its Aftermath**

1. **[[Treaty of Kiel]] (January 14, 1814):**

• As part of the peace settlement after Denmark-Norway’s alliance with Napoleon, the **Treaty of Kiel** ceded Norway to the King of Sweden, **Charles XIII**, while Denmark retained control over the territories of **Norwegian Pomerania** (now part of Germany).

2. **Norwegian Response:**

• Norwegians opposed the forced union with Sweden, sparking a movement for independence. This led to the convening of a national assembly to draft a constitution and declare independence.

  

**III. The Road to the Constitution**

  

**A. The Constituent Assembly at Eidsvoll**

1. **Selection of Representatives:**

• In the spring of 1814, **60 delegates** from various parts of Norway convened at **Eidsvoll**, a historic estate near Oslo, to draft a constitution. These delegates are often referred to as the **“Eidsvollsmenn”** (Men of Eidsvoll).

2. **Influences and Inspirations:**

• Enlightenment ideas, as well as the American Declaration of Independence (1776) and the French Revolution (1789), significantly influenced the constitutional framers. The desire for democratic governance, separation of powers, and individual rights were paramount.

  

**B. Drafting the Constitution**

1. **Process:**

• The drafting process involved extensive debates and deliberations over the course of several weeks. Key issues included the structure of government, the role of the monarchy, and the protection of individual freedoms.

2. **Key Figures:**

• Notable delegates included **Christian Magnus Falsen**, often called the “Father of the Norwegian Constitution,” **Johan Caspar Herman Wedel Jarlsberg**, and **Count Wedel-Jarlsberg**.

  

**IV. Key Features of the 1814 Constitution**

  

**A. Sovereignty and the Nation**

1. **Popular Sovereignty:**

• The constitution declared that **sovereignty resided with the Norwegian people**, establishing the principle that the government’s authority derived from the consent of the governed.

2. **National Identity:**

• Emphasized Norwegian identity and cultural distinctiveness, fostering a sense of unity and independence.

  

**B. Separation of Powers**

1. **Legislative, Executive, and Judicial Branches:**

• Clearly delineated the separation of powers among the **Storting** (parliament) as the legislative body, the **monarch** as the executive authority, and an independent judiciary.

2. **Checks and Balances:**

• Established mechanisms to prevent the concentration of power, ensuring that each branch could limit the others.

  

**C. Constitutional Monarchy**

1. **Role of the Monarch:**

• Defined the monarchy as a constitutional institution with limited powers. The king’s role was largely ceremonial, with real political power vested in elected representatives.

2. **Succession and Rights:**

• Outlined rules for royal succession and the rights of the monarch, ensuring continuity and stability.

  

**D. Individual Rights and Freedoms**

1. **Bill of Rights:**

• Included provisions safeguarding **freedom of speech**, **religion**, **assembly**, and **press**. Protected property rights and the right to a fair trial.

2. **Equality Before the Law:**

• Established legal equality, prohibiting privileges based on birth or social status.

  

**E. Legislative Framework**

1. **Stortinget:**

• Created a **unicameral parliament** known as the **Storting**, responsible for making laws, approving budgets, and overseeing the government.

2. **Decision-Making Process:**

• Introduced democratic processes, including elections and representative governance, allowing citizens to participate in the legislative process.

  

**V. Implementation and Challenges**

  

**A. Declaration of Independence**

1. **April 17, 1814:**

• Norway declared independence from Denmark and adopted the newly drafted constitution, with Prince Christian Frederik (later King Christian VIII of Denmark) as its provisional king.

  

**B. The Swedish Invasion and Union Negotiations**

1. **Swedish Military Response:**

• Sweden, asserting its claim over Norway as per the Treaty of Kiel, launched a military campaign to enforce the union.

2. **Convention of Moss (August 14, 1814):**

• Faced with military pressure and limited international support, Norway entered into negotiations with Sweden. The **Convention of Moss** allowed Norway to maintain its constitution and a significant degree of internal autonomy within a **personal union** with Sweden.

  

**C. The Union with Sweden**

1. **Union Terms:**

• Norway retained its constitution, parliament, and internal governance structures. However, foreign policy and certain other areas were managed jointly with Sweden.

2. **Monarchical Compromise:**

• Prince Christian Frederik renounced his claim to the Norwegian throne, allowing Charles XIII of Sweden to become King of Norway as well, thus forming the **Swedish-Norwegian Union** (1814–1905).

  

**VI. Significance and Legacy**

  

**A. Foundation of Modern Norwegian Democracy**

1. **Democratic Principles:**

• The 1814 Constitution laid the groundwork for Norway’s democratic institutions and governance structures, emphasizing popular sovereignty and the rule of law.

2. **Continuity and Adaptation:**

• While the union with Sweden altered some aspects, the constitution remained a living document, subject to amendments and evolving with societal changes.

  

**B. National Identity and Independence Movement**

1. **Symbol of Sovereignty:**

• The constitution became a powerful symbol of Norwegian independence and national pride, inspiring future generations in the pursuit of full sovereignty.

2. **Path to Full Independence:**

• The 1814 Constitution was instrumental in the eventual peaceful dissolution of the union with Sweden in 1905, leading to Norway’s full independence and the establishment of a purely Norwegian monarchy.

  

**C. Annual Celebration**

1. **Constitution Day (May 17):**

• Celebrated as Norway’s National Day, marked by parades, traditional costumes (**bunad**), speeches, and various cultural events, commemorating the signing of the constitution and the spirit of freedom.

  

**D. International Influence**

1. **Model for Other Nations:**

• Norway’s emphasis on democratic governance, separation of powers, and individual rights influenced constitutional developments in other countries.

  

**VII. Key Figures Associated with the 1814 Constitution**

  

**A. Christian Magnus Falsen**

1. **Role:**

• A leading constitutionalist and jurist, Falsen is often credited as the principal author of the Norwegian Constitution.

2. **Contributions:**

• Advocated for liberal and democratic principles, ensuring the constitution’s progressive nature.

  

**B. Prince Christian Frederik**

1. **Role:**

• The Danish prince who became the provisional King of Norway during the brief period of independence in 1814.

2. **Contributions:**

• Played a central role in the declaration of independence and the establishment of the constitution, later relinquishing his claim to facilitate the union with Sweden.

  

**C. Johan Caspar Herman Wedel Jarlsberg**

1. **Role:**

• A noble and statesman who was instrumental in the negotiations leading to the Convention of Moss.

2. **Contributions:**

• Advocated for the preservation of the Norwegian constitution and significant autonomy within the union with Sweden.

  

**D. Georg Sverdrup and Wilhelm Frimann Koren Christie**

1. **Role:**

• Key delegates at Eidsvoll who contributed to the drafting and promotion of the constitution.

2. **Contributions:**

• Helped articulate the democratic and nationalistic ideals embedded in the constitution.

  

**VIII. The Constitution in Practice**

  

**A. Amendments and Revisions**

1. **Adaptability:**

• The Norwegian Constitution has undergone numerous amendments to adapt to changing political, social, and economic circumstances, ensuring its relevance and resilience.

2. **Notable Revisions:**

• Significant amendments include the establishment of parliamentary democracy, the introduction of women’s suffrage, and modernization of civil rights.

  

**B. Judicial Interpretation**

1. **Supreme Court Role:**

• The Norwegian judiciary interprets and enforces the constitution, ensuring that laws and government actions comply with constitutional principles.

2. **Protection of Rights:**

• Courts uphold individual rights and freedoms as enshrined in the constitution, acting as a check on legislative and executive powers.

  

**C. Educational and Cultural Impact**

1. **Constitutional Education:**

• The constitution is a central subject in Norwegian education, fostering an understanding of democratic principles and civic responsibility.

2. **Cultural Symbolism:**

• Represented in national symbols, literature, and public discourse, the constitution embodies Norway’s commitment to democracy, equality, and human rights.

  

**IX. Comparative Perspectives**

  

**A. Influence of Other Constitutions**

1. **American and French Models:**

• Elements of the Norwegian Constitution reflect influences from the American Constitution and the French Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, particularly in the emphasis on individual rights and democratic governance.

  

**B. Scandinavian Constitutionalism**

1. **Regional Similarities:**

• The Norwegian Constitution shares similarities with other Scandinavian constitutions, emphasizing democratic institutions, social welfare, and the rule of law.

2. **Cooperative Union:**

• The union with Sweden was characterized by mutual respect for each country’s constitutional frameworks, setting a precedent for peaceful cooperation among Scandinavian nations.

  

**X. Conclusion**

  

The **1814 Norwegian Constitution** stands as a cornerstone of Norway’s national identity and democratic heritage. Drafted in a time of political upheaval and inspired by Enlightenment ideals, it established foundational principles of governance, individual rights, and national sovereignty. Despite the subsequent union with Sweden, the constitution’s enduring legacy facilitated Norway’s path to full independence and continues to guide its democratic institutions today. Celebrated annually on May 17, the constitution remains a symbol of Norway’s resilience, unity, and commitment to democratic values.
