#### Overview
- **Name:** Milner Plan
- **Date:** Proposed in 1920
- **Context:** British administration of Mandatory [[Palestine]]
#### Background
The Milner Plan, named after British politician Alfred Milner, was proposed to address the administrative and political challenges faced by the British Mandate in Palestine following World War I. The mandate was established by [[The League of Nations]], with Britain assigned to oversee the territory.
#### Objectives
The main goals of the Milner Plan were:
1. **Stabilize Governance:** Improve the effectiveness of British administration in Palestine.
2. **Balance Interests:** Address the conflicting interests and tensions between Jewish and Arab populations in the region.
3. **Implement Balfour Declaration:** Facilitate the establishment of a national home for the Jewish people, while ensuring the rights of non-Jewish communities.
#### Key Proposals
1. **Administrative Reforms:**
   - **Governor System:** Replace the existing military administration with a civilian government led by a High Commissioner.
   - **Local Autonomy:** Establish local councils to allow greater self-governance for both Jewish and Arab communities.
   - **Economic Development:** Promote economic initiatives to improve living standards and reduce tensions.
2. **Jewish Immigration:**
   - **Controlled Immigration:** Allow Jewish immigration to continue but with restrictions to prevent overwhelming the local Arab population.
   - **Land Purchases:** Regulate Jewish land purchases to avoid significant displacement of Arab farmers.
3. **Political Representation:**
   - **Advisory Council:** Create an advisory council with representation from both Jewish and Arab communities to assist the High Commissioner in decision-making.
   - **Equal Rights:** Ensure civil and political rights for all inhabitants, irrespective of ethnicity or religion.
#### Implementation and Impact
- **High Commissioner:** Herbert Samuel was appointed as the first High Commissioner of Palestine, implementing the civilian administration aspect of the Milner Plan.
- **Tensions:** The plan faced opposition from both Jewish and Arab communities. Jews were concerned about limitations on immigration and land purchases, while Arabs opposed the implementation of the Balfour Declaration and the establishment of a Jewish national home.
- **Modifications:** The plan underwent various modifications and faced significant challenges in achieving its objectives. The conflicting national aspirations of Jews and Arabs continued to create friction.
#### Legacy
The Milner Plan highlighted the complexities of administering a diverse and conflict-prone territory like Mandatory Palestine. It reflected early British attempts to balance competing interests and manage tensions through administrative and political reforms. Despite its limited success, the plan set a precedent for subsequent efforts to address the conflicting nationalisms in Palestine.
### Conclusion
The Milner Plan was an early British effort to stabilize and effectively administer Mandatory Palestine by addressing the needs and aspirations of both Jewish and Arab populations. Although it faced significant challenges and opposition, its proposals for administrative reform, controlled immigration, and political representation were foundational in shaping British policies in the region during the mandate period.
