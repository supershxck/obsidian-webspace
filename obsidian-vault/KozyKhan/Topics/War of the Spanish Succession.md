The War of the Spanish Succession (1701–1714) was a major European conflict sparked by the death of the childless Spanish King Charles II in 1700, triggering a struggle over who would inherit the vast Spanish Empire. It pitted two coalitions—one led by France and Spain under Bourbon rule, the other by the Habsburgs, Britain, the Dutch Republic, and their allies—over the balance of power in Europe. The war reshaped alliances, curbed French ambitions, and marked Britain’s rise as a global power. Here’s a detailed explanation:

---

### Background
- **Spanish Succession Crisis**: Charles II, the last Habsburg king of Spain, died on November 1, 1700. Physically frail and heirless, he left a will naming Philip of Anjou, grandson of French King Louis XIV, as his successor, uniting the Spanish and French crowns under the Bourbon dynasty.
- **Habsburg Claim**: The Austrian Habsburgs, led by Emperor Leopold I, contested this, backing Archduke Charles (Leopold’s younger son) as a rival claimant, fearing French domination of Europe if the Bourbon succession stood unchallenged.
- **European Concerns**: Louis XIV’s aggressive expansionism (e.g., Nine Years’ War, 1688–1697) alarmed Britain, the Dutch Republic, and smaller states like Savoy and Portugal. A Bourbon Spain risked encircling the Habsburgs and dominating trade routes, especially in the Americas.
- **Grand Alliance**: In 1701, Britain, the Dutch Republic, and the Habsburgs formed the Grand Alliance (later joined by Prussia, Savoy, and Portugal) to prevent French hegemony and install Charles as king.

---

### Major Players
- **Bourbon Coalition**:
  - **France**: Louis XIV sought to secure Philip V’s throne and expand French influence.
  - **Spain**: Philip V (r. 1700–1746), supported by Castilian loyalists, though parts of Spain (e.g., Catalonia) backed the Habsburgs.
  - **Bavaria**: Elector Maximilian II Emanuel allied with France for territorial gains.
- **Grand Alliance**:
  - **Habsburg Austria**: Leopold I (then Joseph I after 1705) aimed to reclaim Spain for the Habsburgs.
  - **Britain**: Under Queen Anne (after 1702), led militarily by John Churchill, Duke of Marlborough, to protect trade and curb France.
  - **Dutch Republic**: Sought to safeguard its borders and commerce against French encroachment.
  - **Savoy and Portugal**: Switched sides mid-war (1703) to the Alliance, seeking strategic advantages.

---

### Course of the War
1. **Opening Moves (1701–1704)**:
   - **Italy**: Fighting began in 1701 when Habsburg forces under Prince Eugene of Savoy invaded Spanish-held Milan, defeating the French at Carpi and Chiari.
   - **Low Countries**: Marlborough took command in 1702, capturing Liège and pushing French forces back from the Rhine.
   - **Bavarian Threat**: Bavaria joined France, threatening Vienna. Eugene’s victory at Höchstädt (1703) checked this advance.
2. **Battle of Blenheim (August 13, 1704)**:
   - **Context**: A Franco-Bavarian army under Marshal Tallard and Maximilian II Emanuel marched to link with French forces near Vienna.
   - **Battle**: Marlborough and Eugene combined 52,000 Allied troops to crush 56,000 enemies near Blindheim (Blenheim), Bavaria. A daring river crossing and flanking maneuvers killed or captured 20,000–30,000, ending Bavaria’s role and saving Vienna.
   - **Impact**: A turning point, shifting momentum to the Alliance.
3. **Middle Phase (1705–1709)**:
   - **Spain**: Allied invasions faltered—Charles briefly held Madrid (1706) but faced resistance from Castile. Catalonia and Valencia backed him, splitting Spain.
   - **Low Countries**: Marlborough’s victories at Ramillies (1706) and Oudenarde (1708) expelled France from the Spanish Netherlands (modern Belgium).
   - **Italy**: Eugene secured Savoy and Milan, driving French forces out by 1707.
   - **France Invaded**: The Allies reached French soil, winning at Malplaquet (1709), though at heavy cost (20,000 Allied casualties).
4. **Endgame (1710–1714)**:
   - **War Weariness**: Heavy losses and economic strain eroded Allied resolve. British Tories, gaining power in 1710, sought peace.
   - **Archduke Charles’ Ascension**: Joseph I’s death (1711) made Charles Holy Roman Emperor (Charles VI), reducing Allied enthusiasm—his rule over both Austria and Spain risked a Habsburg monopoly as feared as a Bourbon one.
   - **Philip V’s Resilience**: Despite losses (e.g., Almansa, 1707), Philip held Spain with French support.

---

### Treaties of Utrecht and Rastatt (1713–1714)
- **Utrecht (April 1713)**:
  - **Britain and Allies**: Recognized Philip V as King of Spain, but Spain ceded the Spanish Netherlands, Milan, Naples, and Sardinia to Austria; Gibraltar and Minorca to Britain; and trade rights (Asiento) in Spanish America to Britain.
  - **France**: Ceded Newfoundland, Acadia, and Hudson Bay to Britain; recognized Protestant succession in Britain.
- **Rastatt and Baden (1714)**: Between France and Austria, confirmed Utrecht’s terms and gave Austria the Spanish Netherlands and Italian territories.
- **Outcome**: Spain’s empire was partitioned, France was contained, and the Bourbons kept Spain but renounced union with France.

---

### Why the Allies Won
- **Leadership**: Marlborough and Eugene’s brilliance—coordination, bold offensives, and adaptability—outmatched French generals like Villars and Tallard.
- **Coalition Strength**: The Grand Alliance’s resources and naval power (Britain’s control of the seas) wore down France and Spain.
- **French Overreach**: Louis XIV’s ambitions strained his economy and military, especially after Blenheim.
- **Spanish Division**: Internal support for Charles in Catalonia diluted Philip V’s control, though not enough to unseat him.

---

### Aftermath
- **Balance of Power**: The war prevented a single dynasty from dominating Europe, establishing a multipolar system upheld until the Napoleonic era.
- **British Ascendancy**: Britain gained colonial and naval supremacy, laying foundations for its 18th-century empire.
- **Habsburg Gains**: Austria became a major power in Italy and the Low Countries, though it lost Spain.
- **French Decline**: Louis XIV’s prestige waned; France faced debt and exhaustion, dying in 1715.
- **Spain’s Loss**: Philip V retained the throne but lost European territories, focusing Spain inward.

---

### Significance
- **Global Scope**: Fought across Europe, North America (Queen Anne’s War), and the Caribbean, it was an early “world war.”
- **Dynastic Shift**: Ended Habsburg rule in Spain, entrenching the Bourbons (still reigning today).
- **Diplomatic Precedent**: Utrecht’s treaties shaped modern international law and power-sharing.
