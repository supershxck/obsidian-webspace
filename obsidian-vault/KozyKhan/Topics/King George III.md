**King George III (1738–1820): Monarch of Transformation and Turmoil**

  

**I. Introduction**

  

King **George III** ruled Great Britain and Ireland from October 25, 1760 until his death on January 29, 1820. His 59‑year reign—the third longest in British history—saw seismic political, social, and intellectual shifts: the loss of America, the rise of parliamentary democracy, and the dawn of the Industrial Revolution.

---

**II. Early Life & Accession**

• **Born:** June 4, 1738 at [[Norfolk House]], London

• **Education:** Classical curriculum emphasizing morality, religion, and languages

• **Ascended Throne:** At age 22 following his grandfather George II’s death, pledging to rule “according to law and custom” rather than ministerial factions.

---

**III. Major Events of His Reign**

| **Event**                             | **Date**                | **Significance**                                               |
| ------------------------------------- | ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| [[Seven Years’ War]]                  | 1756–1763               | Expanded British empire but accrued massive debt               |
| [[American Revolutionary War]]        | 1775–1783               | Loss of thirteen colonies; redefined empire’s global strategy  |
| Regency Crisis                        | 1788–1789               | Episodes of mental illness led to temporary transfer of powers |
| [[Napoleonic Wars]]                   | 1803–1815               | Mobilized Britain into European coalition defeating Napoleon   |
| Agricultural & Industrial Revolutions | Late 18th–Early 19th c. | Accelerated economic and social transformation                 |

  

---

**IV. Psychological & Philosophical Dimensions**

• **King’s Madness:** Likely bipolar disorder or porphyria, his mental health shaped public perceptions of monarchy and informed debates on constitutional limits.

• **Sovereign Archetype:** Embodied Jung’s **King archetype**—source of order and stability—yet his illness revealed the shadow risks of concentrated authority.

---

**V. Governance & Constitutional Legacy**

• Transitioned Britain toward **constitutional monarchy**, diminishing direct royal intervention in favor of ministerial responsibility.

• Supported agricultural innovation (Corn Laws) yet resisted early calls for political reform, highlighting tension between tradition and progress.

---

**VI. Cultural & Scientific Patronage**

• Avid amateur astronomer and botanist; founded the Royal Academy of Arts (1768).

• His support for science fostered Enlightenment ideals of reason and discovery.

---

**VII. Vocabulary & Key Terms**

• **Regency:** Temporary rule by a substitute when monarch incapacitated

• **Corn Laws:** Protectionist tariffs on grain imports

• **Constitutional Monarchy:** System limiting sovereign’s power via law

• **King’s Madness:** Historical term for George’s recurrent mental illness episodes

---

**VIII. Legacy & Reflection**

  

King George III’s reign epitomizes a transformative era: his steadfastness preserved Britain through upheaval, yet his failings underscored the need for accountable governance. His legacy teaches that leadership demands both resilience and humility—balancing authority with adaptability, and vision with ethical restraint.
