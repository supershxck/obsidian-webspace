**What is Lent?**
Lent in the Eastern Orthodox Church, often referred to as Great Lent, is a sacred period of 40 days leading up to Pascha (Easter), the celebration of the Resurrection of Jesus Christ. It mirrors the 40 days Jesus spent fasting in the desert and is a time of spiritual preparation, reflection, and renewal for Orthodox Christians. Great Lent begins on Clean Monday, the day after Forgiveness Sunday, and ends on the Friday before Lazarus Saturday and Palm Sunday, leading directly into Holy Week.

During Lent, Orthodox Christians follow a strict fast, avoiding meat, dairy, and sometimes oil and fish, while also striving to refrain from sinful actions and thoughts. This season is marked by increased prayer, both personally and through special services like the Liturgy of the Pre-Sanctified Gifts and the Akathist Hymn, aiming to foster spiritual growth and a closer relationship with God. Charity is also a key focus, with believers encouraged to help those in need and embody Jesus' teachings on love. Lent is a time of repentance and self-examination, with practices like the Rite of Forgiveness helping believers to seek reconciliation with God and others.

The Rite of Forgiveness, held on Forgiveness Sunday before Lent begins, is a key ceremony in the Eastern Orthodox Church focusing on reconciliation and purifying relationships. Taking place during the Vespers service, it involves congregation members, both clergy and laity, asking each other for forgiveness through a structured exchange that includes a prostration or bow and an embrace, symbolizing mutual forgiveness, humility, and love. This practice, which echoes Jesus's teachings on forgiveness, aims to prepare believers for Lent by promoting a clean heart, reconciliation with God and others, and readiness for the season's fasting, prayer, and almsgiving. The Rite of Forgiveness is an emotional and powerful expression of the Gospel's call to forgiveness and unity, setting a spiritual tone for Lent focused on personal growth, repentance, and deepening community bonds.

Lent is a time when Orthodox Christians focus on getting closer to God and getting ready for Easter. They do this through fasting, prayer, giving to others, and repenting of their sins. This helps them clean their minds and bodies and prepare to celebrate Jesus's Resurrection. Lent in the Eastern Orthodox Church is more than just following traditions; it's a chance for people to really connect with their faith, become more holy, and refresh their spiritual lives. 

Lent lasts for 40 days before Palm Sunday, followed by Holy Week, ending with Easter. During this time, people pray more, fast by not eating certain foods like meat and dairy, and help others more. This is all done to get ready for Easter. During the weekdays of Lent, the usual church service isn't held because it's too _joyful_ for such a serious time. Instead, a special service called the _Liturgy of the Pre-Sanctified Gifts_ is done, where people can take Communion with bread and wine that were blessed the previous Sunday. Early in Lent, _there's also a reading of the Great Canon, a long hymn that makes people think deeply about sin, forgiveness, and God's mercy._

The Akathist Hymn is a special prayer song in the Eastern Orthodox Church, praising the Virgin Mary, also known as the Theotokos. The name "Akathist" means people stand up while it's sung, showing respect for the Virgin Mary and the special events the hymn talks about. It's known for its beautiful poetry, deep religious meaning, and its celebration of the Virgin Mary's role in Jesus's birth and Christian salvation. The hymn is made up of different parts that tell a story about Jesus's birth and honors the Virgin Mary for her part in it. It's usually sung during a specific time in Lent and can also be part of personal prayers or other religious services. People find it comforting and inspiring. Thought to have been created in the 6th century and possibly written by Romanos the Melodist, a famous hymn writer, its popularity grew after a miraculous event in 626 when the Virgin Mary was believed to have saved Constantinople from an attack. It has been a key part of Orthodox Christian worship and tradition ever since.

Lazarus Saturday marks the end of Great Lent and begins the transition to Holy Week. It commemorates the resurrection of Lazarus by Jesus as a prefiguration of His own Resurrection. Palm Sunday, the following day, celebrates Jesus' triumphant entry into Jerusalem, with churchgoers often receiving blessed palms or willow branches. Holy Week is the climax of the Lenten season, with each day commemorating specific events leading up to the crucifixion and resurrection of Christ. Services include Bridegroom Matins, the Service of the Holy Unction (anointing with oil for healing), the 12 Gospels reading on Holy Thursday, the Lamentations on Holy Friday, and the rush procession during the Paschal Vigil on Saturday night. 

The Lenten journey culminates in the celebration of Pascha, the Resurrection of Jesus Christ. This is the most joyous and significant event in the Christian calendar, marked by the Paschal Vigil, which starts late in the evening and continues into the early hours of Sunday. The service transitions from darkness to light, symbolizing the Resurrection, and is followed by the Paschal Divine Liturgy. The greeting "Christ is Risen!" and the response "Indeed, He is Risen!" are exchanged among the faithful, often in multiple languages, reflecting the universal nature of Christ's victory over death.

Lent in the Eastern Orthodox Church is a time of introspection, growth, and spiritual renewal. Attend the services, participate in the fasting to the extent that you are able, and take time for personal prayer and reading of the Scriptures and spiritual books. This is a unique opportunity to deepen your faith and experience the richness of the Orthodox spiritual tradition.
