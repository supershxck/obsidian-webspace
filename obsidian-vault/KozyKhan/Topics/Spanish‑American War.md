**The Spanish‑American War (1898): America’s Entry into Imperial Power**

---

**Introduction**

  

The **Spanish‑American War** (April–August 1898) was a brief conflict between the United States and Spain that marked America’s emergence as a global power. Triggered by humanitarian outrage and expansionist ambition, it resulted in U.S. acquisition of overseas territories and reshaped international geopolitics.

---

**I. Historical Context & Causes**

|**Factor**|**Description**|
|---|---|
|Cuban Independence|Cuban rebellion against Spanish colonial rule (1895–1898)|
|Yellow Journalism|Sensational press fueled American sympathy for Cuban insurgents|
|USS Maine Explosion|Battleship exploded in Havana Harbor (Feb 15, 1898); blamed on Spain without conclusive evidence|
|Manifest Destiny & Expansionism|Ideological drive for overseas markets and strategic naval bases|

  

---

**II. Declaration of War**

• **Date:** April 25, 1898

• **Congressional Resolution:** Cited protection of U.S. citizens and support for Cuban independence

---

**III. Major Campaigns & Battles**

|**Theater**|**Key Engagement**|**Outcome**|
|---|---|---|
|Caribbean|Battle of Santiago de Cuba (July 3)|Destruction of Spanish fleet; Cuban liberation accelerated|
|Pacific|Battle of Manila Bay (May 1)|U.S. Asiatic Squadron under Commodore Dewey defeated Spanish Pacific fleet|
|Puerto Rico|Invasion (July 1898)|Rapid U.S. occupation with minimal resistance|

  

---

**IV. Treaty of Paris (Dec 10, 1898)**

|**Term**|**Result**|
|---|---|
|Cuban Independence|Spain relinquished control; U.S. occupation until 1902|
|Territorial Cessions|U.S. acquired Puerto Rico, Guam, Philippines (for $20 million)|
|Platt Amendment|Allowed U.S. intervention in Cuban affairs post‑independence|

  

---

**V. Psychological & Philosophical Reflections**

|**Dimension**|**Insight**|
|---|---|
|Collective Shadow|War revealed tension between professed ideals of liberty and imperial conquest (Jungian projection of national ambition)|
|Ethical Paradox|Framed as liberation yet established colonial rule over new territories|

  

---

**VI. Consequences & Legacy**

|**Domain**|**Impact**|
|---|---|
|Imperial Status|U.S. became colonial power with Pacific and Caribbean holdings|
|Philippine–American War|Insurrection against U.S. rule (1899–1902) with significant casualties|
|Military Reform|Highlighted need for modern navy and expeditionary forces|
|Domestic Debate|Sparked anti‑imperialist movement led by Mark Twain, Andrew Carnegie, and others|

  

---

**VII. Lessons & Modern Framework**

|**Challenge**|**Principle**|**Strategy**|
|---|---|---|
|Military Intervention|Rigorous cause‑benefit analysis|International law compliance; clear exit strategy|
|Colonial Governance|Prioritize self‑determination|Transition planning; inclusive institution-building|
|Historical Memory|Acknowledge past injustices|Support reconciliation and educational initiatives|

  

---

**VIII. Key Vocabulary**

• **Yellow Journalism:** Sensationalist media influencing public opinion

• **Platt Amendment:** U.S. leverage over Cuban sovereignty post‑war

• **Imperialism:** Policy of extending national power through acquisition

• **Anti‑Imperialist League:** Group opposing U.S. colonial expansion
