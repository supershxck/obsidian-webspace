RS-232 (Recommended Standard 232) is a **serial communication standard** that defines how data is transmitted between devices. It was originally introduced in 1960 by the **Electronic Industries Association (EIA)** and is primarily used for **point-to-point** communication between computers and peripherals like **modems, printers, industrial equipment, and embedded systems**.

  

**Key Features of RS-232**

1. **Voltage Levels**: Uses **-12V to -3V for logical “1” (MARK) and +3V to +12V for logical “0” (SPACE)**.

2. **Asynchronous Communication**: Data is transmitted in frames with a **start bit, data bits (5-8), optional parity bit, and stop bits (1 or 2)**.

3. **Baud Rate**: Supports various speeds like **9600, 115200, 38400**, etc.

4. **Full Duplex**: Allows simultaneous sending and receiving of data.

5. **Connector Types**: Typically uses **DB9 (9-pin) or DB25 (25-pin) connectors**.

  

**Common Uses of RS-232**

• **Industrial automation**: Connecting sensors and controllers.

• **Embedded systems**: Communication between microcontrollers and PCs.

• **Networking**: Legacy modem and router communication.

• **Point-of-sale (POS) systems**: Barcode scanners and receipt printers.

  

Although **USB and Ethernet** have largely replaced RS-232 in consumer applications, it is still widely used in **industrial, scientific, and legacy systems** where stable, long-distance serial communication is required.
