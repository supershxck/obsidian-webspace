The **International Organization for Standardization (ISO)** is an independent, non-governmental international body that develops and publishes standards to ensure quality, safety, efficiency, and interoperability across industries and countries. Founded on February 23, 1947, and headquartered in Geneva, Switzerland, ISO is not a "who" in the sense of a person but a global network of national standards bodies. Its name comes from the Greek word *isos* (meaning "equal"), reflecting its mission to standardize practices worldwide. Here’s a detailed explanation:

---

### Structure and Membership
- **Composition**: ISO is a federation of 169 member organizations (as of 2025), each representing a country’s standards authority. Examples include:
  - **ANSI** (American National Standards Institute) for the U.S.
  - **BSI** (British Standards Institution) for the UK.
  - **DIN** (Deutsches Institut für Normung) for Germany.
- **Membership Types**:
  - **Full Members** (129): Have voting rights and influence standards development (e.g., U.S., Japan).
  - **Correspondent Members** (36): Observe but don’t vote (e.g., smaller nations like Barbados).
  - **Subscriber Members** (4): Minimal participation (e.g., Antigua and Barbuda).
- **Governance**: Run by a General Assembly, a Council, and technical committees. The President (e.g., Sung Hwan Cho of South Korea, 2025–2026) and Secretary-General (e.g., Sergio Mujica, as of recent years) lead operations.

---

### Purpose and Function
- **Mission**: ISO creates voluntary standards to facilitate global trade, improve safety, and promote innovation by ensuring products, services, and systems are consistent and reliable.
- **How It Works**: Experts from member countries collaborate in technical committees to draft standards. Once approved, they’re published for adoption by businesses, governments, or organizations.
- **Scope**: Covers nearly every sector—technology, manufacturing, healthcare, agriculture, environmental management, and more.

---

### Key Standards
ISO has published over 24,000 standards (as of 2023, with more by 2025). Some famous examples:
- **ISO 9001**: Quality management systems—ensures consistent product/service quality (e.g., a car manufacturer proves reliability).
- **ISO 14001**: Environmental management—helps organizations reduce ecological impact.
- **ISO 27001**: Information security—protects data (e.g., cybersecurity for banks).
- **ISO 3166**: Country codes (e.g., “US” for United States, “DE” for Germany).
- **ISO 8601**: Date/time format (e.g., 2025-03-13 for March 13, 2025).

---

### History
- **Predecessors**: Born from the International Federation of the National Standardizing Associations (ISA, 1926–1942) and the United Nations Standards Coordinating Committee (UNSCC) during WWII.
- **Founding**: Post-war, 65 delegates from 25 countries met in London in 1946, launching ISO in 1947 to unify industrial standards disrupted by global conflict.
- **Growth**: From 67 technical committees in 1947, it now has over 800, reflecting its expanding role in a globalized economy.

---

### Why It Matters
- **Trade**: Standards like ISO container sizes (e.g., ISO 668) enable seamless shipping worldwide—90% of global trade uses them.
- **Safety**: ISO 22000 ensures food safety from farm to table.
- **Innovation**: Standards provide a baseline for new tech (e.g., ISO/IEC 17025 for lab testing).
- **Trust**: Certification (e.g., “ISO 9001 certified”) signals quality to customers.

---

### How It Operates
- **Voluntary**: ISO standards aren’t legally binding—adoption depends on industries or governments (though some, like ISO 13485 for medical devices, become regulatory requirements).
- **Funding**: Supported by member fees, publication sales, and partnerships (e.g., with the IEC for electrotechnical standards).
- **Collaboration**: Works with bodies like the UN, WTO, and IEC to align global efforts.

---

### Impact
- **Daily Life**: From the ISO metric system (bolts, screws) to USB standards (ISO/IEC), ISO touches everything.
- **Economy**: Harmonized standards cut costs—e.g., a phone charger works globally.
- **Sustainability**: ISO 50001 drives energy efficiency, tackling climate change.

---

### Fun Facts
- **Name**: “ISO” isn’t an acronym—it’s a deliberate choice for consistency across languages (not “IOS” or “OIN” in French).
- **Logo**: A simple globe-and-text design, symbolizing universality.
- **Scale**: Over 1 million companies worldwide are ISO 9001 certified (2023 data).
