**Outline of Babylonian Mythology**

  

**1. Foundations and Influences**

• Babylonian mythology evolved from earlier **Sumerian myths**, with heavy influences from Akkadian culture.

• Central to their worldview was a belief in a **polytheistic pantheon**, where the gods governed natural and cosmic forces.

• Mythology served religious, political, and cultural functions, with texts written in **cuneiform** on clay tablets.

  

**2. The Babylonian Pantheon**

• **[[Marduk]]**: The chief deity of Babylon, especially during the **Neo-Babylonian period**. Known as a creator god and the victor in the myth of the _Enuma Elish_.

• **[[Anu]] (An)**: The sky god and head of the pantheon, inherited from Sumerian mythology.

• **[[Enlil]]**: The god of wind, air, and storms, often a mediator between divine and human realms.

• **[[Ea]]/[[Enki]]**: The god of water, wisdom, magic, and creation.

• **[[Ishtar]] ([[Inanna]])**: Goddess of love, fertility, and war, central to many myths of transformation and descent.

• **[[Nergal]]**: The god of the underworld and plague.

• **[[Tiamat]]**: The chaos dragon and primordial goddess in creation mythology.

• **[[Shamash]]**: The sun god associated with justice and truth.

• **[[Sin]] ([[Nanna]])**: The moon god, tied to cycles of time and divination.

  

**3. Creation Myths**

• **The** **_Enuma Elish_** (Babylonian Creation Epic):

• Central myth that elevates Marduk as the supreme deity.

• Marduk battles and defeats **Tiamat**, the primordial goddess of chaos, creating the world from her body.

• Establishes divine order and the role of humans as servants to the gods.

• This myth was recited during the **Akitu (New Year Festival)** to reaffirm Babylonian cosmology and Marduk’s kingship.

  

**4. Flood Myths**

• **The Epic of Atrahasis**:

• Explains the creation of humanity to relieve the gods from labor.

• Humanity’s overpopulation leads to divine anger, resulting in a great flood.

• Atrahasis, warned by the god Ea, builds a boat to save himself and other life forms, similar to the later story of **Utnapishtim** in the _Epic of Gilgamesh_.

• Babylonian flood myths influenced **Hebrew** and **later Western traditions** (e.g., Noah’s Ark).

  

**5. Heroic Myths**

• **The Epic of Gilgamesh**:

• Originally a Sumerian myth, expanded during the Babylonian period.

• Tells the story of **Gilgamesh**, king of Uruk, and his quest for immortality.

• Includes the flood narrative and emphasizes themes of mortality, friendship, and human limits.

• **Etana and the Eagle**:

• The tale of a king (Etana) who seeks the **Plant of Birth** to ensure his lineage, involving his flight to the heavens with the help of an eagle.

  

**6. Underworld Myths**

• **Ishtar’s Descent to the Underworld**:

• Ishtar descends to the realm of **Ereshkigal**, queen of the underworld, to gain power or rescue a loved one.

• She is stripped of her divine powers and held captive, causing fertility to cease on Earth.

• Eventually released after an exchange, often involving her lover **Tammuz (Dumuzi)**, symbolizing the cycle of death and rebirth.

  

**7. Cosmic Order and Chaos**

• Babylonian mythology focuses on the **struggle between order (cosmos)** and **chaos**:

• **Marduk vs. Tiamat**: Represents cosmic order defeating primordial chaos.

• The gods’ frequent quarrels and alliances reflect the instability of the natural world.

• The belief in divine hierarchy mirrored the social and political order of Babylonian society.

  

**8. Astrological and Astronomical Myths**

• The gods were associated with celestial bodies, giving rise to **Babylonian astrology**:

• **Marduk**: Linked to **Jupiter**.

• **Ishtar**: Represented by **Venus**.

• **Shamash**: The **Sun**.

• **Sin**: The **Moon**.

• Astrology and divination were central to their understanding of the gods’ will.

  

**9. Rituals and Festivals**

• **Akitu Festival**: Celebrated the renewal of the cosmos, the recitation of the _Enuma Elish_, and the reaffirmation of Marduk’s supremacy.

• Temples (ziggurats) served as houses of the gods and centers for ritual practices, linking divine and earthly realms.

  

**10. Legacy of Babylonian Mythology**

• Babylonian myths influenced **Hebrew**, **Greek**, and later **Western traditions**:

• Creation and flood myths influenced biblical texts like **Genesis**.

• Themes of cosmic struggle shaped later stories of divine conflict.

• Their advancements in astrology and numerology remain foundational in esoteric traditions.
