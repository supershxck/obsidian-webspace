**Free Interactive Learning Resources for Calculus, Physics, and Electromagnetism**

  

To build a strong foundation for engineering (especially computer engineering) from a computer science background, it’s best to follow a modular learning path. Below are **free, high-quality, and interactive** resources – including courses, textbooks, and simulations – for each major topic. These resources emphasize both theoretical depth and practical applications, and they can be combined into a self-paced curriculum.

  

**1. Introductory Calculus (Differential & Integral Calculus)**

  

Understanding limits, derivatives, and integrals is the first step. These resources cover single-variable calculus from basics through engineering applications:

• **Khan Academy – Calculus:** A comprehensive _interactive_ course with video lessons, step-by-step exercises, and progress tracking. It spans limits, differentiation, integration, and more, from basic concepts up to advanced calculus . Khan Academy’s platform is free and self-paced, with instant feedback on practice problems .

• **MIT OpenCourseWare – Single Variable Calculus:** Free lecture notes, video lectures, and problem sets from MIT’s Calculus courses . For example, _MIT 18.01 (Calculus I)_ covers differentiation and integration of functions of one variable, and _18.02 (Calculus II)_ extends to multivariable calculus. These materials, taught by renowned professors, include engineering-oriented examples and applications .

• **Engineer4Free – Calculus I Tutorials:** A free online calculus course with 46 short tutorials covering a typical university Calculus I syllabus . It includes applied problems (related rates, optimization, etc.) relevant to engineering (e.g. maximizing volumes, projectile motion). Each tutorial breaks down concepts like the chain rule or integrals into digestible videos, making it easy to follow in sequence .

  

_Supporting tool:_ As you learn calculus, tools like **Desmos** (graphing calculator) or **Wolfram Alpha** can visualize functions and check work. These complement the above courses by providing interactive plotting and symbolic integration capabilities .

  

**2. Fundamental Physics: Mechanics (Kinematics & Dynamics)**

  

Classical mechanics introduces how objects move and interact – crucial for understanding forces and energy in engineering systems. The following resources use calculus to derive physical laws:

• **Khan Academy – Physics (Classical Mechanics):** Free conceptual videos and practice problems covering Newtonian mechanics, kinematics, dynamics, work/energy, and momentum. While Khan Academy’s physics content is largely algebra-based, it includes calculus-based **AP Physics C** material for mechanics. This is great for building intuition about forces and motion, with problem sets to test understanding.

• **MIT OCW – Physics I: Classical Mechanics:** An undergraduate course (e.g. _MIT 8.01_) with video lectures (Walter Lewin’s lectures are legendary), assignments, and exams. It introduces core concepts – space, time, mass, force, momentum, torque, angular momentum – and uses calculus to solve motion problems . Conservation laws (energy, momentum) are taught alongside force laws , giving a deep theoretical framework. The course materials are free and self-paced, allowing you to work through problem sets that mirror real-world scenarios (like planetary motion and orbits).

• **OpenStax University Physics Volume 1 (Mechanics):** A free, peer-reviewed textbook covering classical mechanics, waves, and oscillations. Volume 1 “covers mechanics, sound, oscillations, and waves” and _“emphasizes connections between theory and application.”_ Concepts are explained clearly with examples (e.g. using calculus to derive projectile trajectories or pendulum motion). End-of-chapter problems provide practice, and an online version includes interactive diagrams. This is well-suited for engineering students since it aligns with calculus-based physics courses .

  

_Tip:_ Start mechanics once you have a grasp of basic differentiation/integration, since you’ll apply calculus to compute velocities from positions, forces from potentials, etc. Mechanics will reinforce your calculus skills by showing how mathematical formulas describe real motion.

  

**3. Electricity (Electrostatics and Circuits)**

  

Next, build understanding of electrical principles – essential for computer engineering. Key topics include electric charge, electric fields, voltage, current, resistance, Ohm’s Law, and circuit analysis (Kirchhoff’s laws):

• **Khan Academy – Electrical Engineering (Circuit Basics):** An introductory course on electricity and circuits that is very accessible. Khan Academy’s _Intro to Electrical Engineering_ module covers fundamental electrical quantities (charge, current, voltage) and basic circuit laws in an interactive format . Through videos and quizzes it teaches Ohm’s law, resistor networks, and simple RC circuits, as well as practical concepts like how amplifiers and semiconductor devices work . This helps connect physics theory to the components in computers (wires, resistors, transistors).

• **All About Circuits – Online Textbook & Exercises:** _All About Circuits_ is an open online resource offering **comprehensive electronics textbooks**, interactive examples, and worksheets . The textbooks (by Tony R. Kuphaldt) cover everything from basic DC circuits to AC theory and beyond, and are updated by a community of engineers . For foundational topics, start with chapters on **Basic DC Electricity**: you’ll learn about charge, electric fields, current, and voltage in a rigorous way. The site also provides **interactive circuit simulations and calculators** to apply concepts . Worksheets with practice problems allow you to test your understanding of Ohm’s and Kirchhoff’s laws in real circuit scenarios .

• **OpenStax University Physics Volume 2 (Electricity and Magnetism):** The first half of this free textbook covers electrostatics and circuits in depth. _Volume 2 covers thermodynamics, electricity, and magnetism_, again emphasizing theory-application connections . You can read chapters on Coulomb’s law, electric fields, Gauss’s law, electric potential, capacitors, and DC circuits. Engineering examples (like electric fields in capacitors or using Kirchhoff’s rules to analyze electrical networks) are included, bridging theory with practical calculations.

  

_Hands-on component:_ Try building simple circuits virtually with **Circuit simulators** (like the free _PhET Circuit Construction Kit_ or EveryCircuit). These tools let you place batteries, resistors, etc., and observe voltages/currents, reinforcing the laws of electricity in an interactive way.

  

**4. Magnetism**

  

Magnetism is the flip side of electricity. Key concepts include magnetic fields, magnetic forces on charges or currents, and how materials respond (ferromagnetism). Historically, Oersted’s and Faraday’s experiments revealed the link between electricity and magnetism, which is crucial for the next step (electromagnetism). Resources to learn magnetism:

• **Saylor Academy – (within) PHYS102: Introduction to Electromagnetism (Magnetism Units):** Saylor’s free course on E&M has units dedicated to magnetostatics and induction. It recounts the _historical discoveries_ – for instance, how **Hans Christian Oersted** in 1820 found that _“an electric current could deflect a nearby compass needle,”_ revealing that moving charges produce magnetic fields . You’ll study magnetic field concepts (fields around wires, forces on moving charges, the Biot–Savart law, etc.) and then Faraday’s Law of induction (how a changing magnetic field creates an electric current). The course includes readings (from free texts like OpenStax), lecture videos, and problems. By the end of the magnetism section, you’ll see qualitatively and quantitatively how electricity and magnetism interplay (e.g. a changing current in a coil induces a magnetic field and vice versa).

• **PhET Interactive Simulations – Faraday’s Electromagnetic Lab:** An excellent interactive tool to **experiment with magnetism virtually**. In this free simulation, you can _“move a bar magnet near one or two coils to make a light bulb glow”_ and _“view the magnetic field lines”_, exploring Faraday’s law in real time . This hands-on simulation shows the concepts of magnetic fields and induction: when the magnet moves, you see field lines cutting through a coil and observe the induced current lighting a bulb. PhET also offers a **Bar Magnet** simulation to visualize field lines around a magnet. These interactive visuals make abstract concepts (like invisible magnetic fields) more concrete and are a fun way to reinforce what you learn from the textbooks.

• **CK-12 or OpenStax (Magnetism chapters):** Free science textbooks like CK-12’s **“Discovery of Electromagnetism”** or OpenStax’s magnetism chapters provide concise explanations of Oersted’s and Faraday’s experiments in context. They explain how a current-carrying wire creates a circular magnetic field (Oersted’s finding), how loops of wire experience torque in magnetic fields (principle of electric motors), and how changing magnetic flux induces EMFs (Faraday’s law). These texts often include simulations or videos (CK-12 integrates some interactives) to illustrate the historical experiments.

  

**5. Electromagnetism (Maxwell’s Equations and EM Waves)**

  

With the foundations of electricity and magnetism in place, the next module is **electromagnetism** – the unified theory that electric and magnetic fields are interrelated and propagate as electromagnetic waves (light, radio waves, etc.). This is where calculus (especially vector calculus and differential equations) plays a big role in describing Maxwell’s equations. Resources for this advanced topic:

• **Saylor Academy – PHYS102: Introduction to Electromagnetism (Maxwell’s Equations & Beyond):** The culmination of Saylor’s E&M course covers Maxwell’s four equations which elegantly unify electricity and magnetism . In the course, _“in Maxwell’s equations, we finally unify electric and magnetic effects and discover electromagnetic radiation”_, putting radio waves on the same footing as light . You will learn each of Maxwell’s equations (Gauss’s laws for electric _and_ magnetic fields, Faraday’s law, and Ampère’s law with Maxwell’s addition), and see how they predict electromagnetic waves. The course then explores how **light is an electromagnetic wave** and even touches on related applications like optics (light as EM radiation) and an introduction to special relativity . Saylor’s material is self-paced and pulls from open textbooks and lecture notes – it provides a structured path through what can be a mathematically heavy subject.

• **MIT OpenCourseWare – Physics II: Electricity and Magnetism:** This is MIT’s calculus-based E&M course (e.g. _MIT 8.02_). It includes video lectures, demonstrations, and problem sets on all facets of E&M. The latter part of the course focuses on Maxwell’s Equations and electromagnetic waves, often with engaging demonstrations (like creating electromagnetic waves in a coax cable, etc.). Studying MIT’s lecture notes or watching the lectures can give you a deep theoretical understanding. You’ll see how Maxwell’s equations lead to the wave equation for fields and learn about the speed of light and wave phenomena like polarization.

• **Feynman Lectures on Physics Vol. II (Electromagnetism):** As a supplemental resource, this famous volume (available free online) offers insightful explanations of E&M theory. While not interactive, Feynman’s lectures connect theory with real examples (e.g. how radio antennae work, the beauty of Maxwell’s equations) in an accessible way. It’s a great reference if you want more intuition or an alternate explanation for topics you encounter in courses.

  

_Why this matters:_ Mastering electromagnetism is crucial for engineering – Maxwell’s equations underpin how **electromagnetic waves** carry signals (Wi-Fi, radio, fiber-optic communications) and how fields behave in circuits (high-speed PCB design, signal integrity, etc.). They even explain why a changing current in one wire induces voltage in another (cross-talk in computer buses). The above resources will help you gain both the **mathematical formalism** and the physical intuition for these phenomena.

  

**6. Real-World Applications in Engineering (Especially Computer Engineering)**

  

Learning is reinforced by seeing how abstract principles apply in real systems. After or alongside the theoretical modules, explore these application-focused resources:

• **MIT OpenCourseWare / edX – Circuits and Electronics (MITx 6.002):** This intro electrical engineering course applies physics and math to real engineering problems. It covers circuit design techniques foundational to microchips and modern electronic devices. For example, you learn to analyze RC/RL circuits (which requires solving differential equations) – directly applying calculus to design timing circuits in computers. You also study how transistors switch and amplify signals, which connects back to physics (semiconductor theory) and is the basis of all digital logic. The edX version is free to audit and features homework and labs; it’s described as teaching _“techniques foundational to the design of microchips used in smartphones, self-driving cars, computers, and the Internet.”_ (i.e. how theoretical laws enable real-world tech).

• **All About Circuits – Practical Articles & Projects:** In addition to theory, AllAboutCircuits provides **practical engineering articles and webinars** . These include real-world topics like PCB design basics, signal processing, and power systems. Reading how Ohm’s law and Kirchhoff’s laws are used to design a computer’s power supply, or how Maxwell’s laws explain electromagnetic interference, will cement your understanding. For instance, one can explore how _“all electronics apply various features of electromagnetism, so that computers, HDTVs, smartphones, motors, etc., are applied electromagnetic devices”_ . Seeing that your laptop’s Wi-Fi antenna, its CPU clock signal, and even its cooling fan rely on these physics principles can be eye-opening.

• **Case Studies / Projects:** Engage in a small project to apply what you’ve learned. For example, use an Arduino (microcontroller) to build a simple sensor circuit – this touches on electricity (circuits), magnetism (if using a magnetic sensor or motor), and requires calculus-based formulas to tune sensor response. Resources like **Arduino project guides** (free online) or **electronic DIY tutorials** let you apply Kirchhoff’s laws and Faraday’s law in a tangible way. Similarly, a physics simulation or coding a simple physics engine (for a game, perhaps) can apply mechanics. While these are not formal courses, they are interactive and will solidify your learning through practice.

• **Additional Applied Courses:** If interested in communication systems or fields in computing, look for free courses like **“Electromagnetics for Engineers”** or **RF Engineering basics** (sometimes available on NPTEL or other open course platforms). These show how Maxwell’s equations are used to design antennas and high-speed interconnects – directly relevant to wireless networks and computer hardware communications.

  

By engaging with applied content, you ensure the abstract math and physics truly _click_ as tools for solving engineering problems.

  

**7. Mathematical Formalism (Vector Calculus and Differential Equations)**

  

To fully succeed in advanced physics and engineering, you should also study the math that underlies electromagnetic theory: **multivariable calculus (vector calculus)** and **differential equations**. These are often the “language” of physical laws , so a solid grasp will enhance your understanding and ability to derive results.

• **Coursera (HKUST) – Vector Calculus for Engineers:** A highly recommended free (audit) course focusing on vector and multivariable calculus with engineering applications. It covers gradient, divergence, curl, line integrals, surface integrals, and the fundamental theorems (Gradient Theorem, Divergence (Gauss’s) Theorem, Stokes’ Theorem) . These topics are _“essential for subjects in engineering such as electromagnetism and fluid mechanics.”_ The course includes concise lecture videos and practice quizzes. By learning here how to compute flux integrals or apply Stokes’ theorem, you’ll be well-prepared to tackle Maxwell’s equations in differential form (which rely on those theorems). The practical problems (53 problem sets in total) tie the math to real engineering scenarios .

• **MIT OpenCourseWare – Multivariable Calculus (18.02) & Differential Equations (18.03):** These courses provide free notes, videos, and assignments to master advanced math. _18.02 Multivariable Calc._ will teach you 3D calculus, partial derivatives, multiple integrals, vector fields, and line/surface integrals. _18.03 Differential Equations_ covers how to solve ODEs (ordinary differential equations) and touches on Fourier series and Laplace transforms – tools used in circuit analysis and wave equations. MIT’s course description emphasizes that _“Differential Equations are the language in which the laws of nature are expressed”_ and understanding their solutions is _fundamental to science and engineering_ . These materials often include interactive Java applets (“Mathlets”) demonstrating solutions of differential equations visually (e.g. oscillations of a spring with varying parameters).

• **Khan Academy – Multivariable Calculus & Differential Equations:** As an alternative, Khan Academy also offers modules on multivariable calculus (including vectors, gradients, and simple divergence/curl concepts) and on differential equations (focusing on basic solution techniques for first-order and second-order ODEs). Khan’s strength is step-by-step problem solving practice – you can solidify techniques like solving $\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)$ or finding eigenvalues for systems of differential equations through exercise sets.

  

Strengthening these math skills will make the physics far more approachable. For instance, when you reach Maxwell’s equations, you’ll recognize the divergence and curl operators and appreciate results like “$\nabla \cdot B = 0$” (no magnetic monopoles) on a deeper level. Similarly, in circuit engineering, you’ll solve differential equations for RC and RLC circuits (which model computer power supplies or signal filters).

  

**8. Structured Learning Path for a CS Background**

  

Finally, here is a **suggested learning path** combining the above modules into a coherent sequence. This path is tailored for someone with a computer science background (familiar with logic and programming, but seeking the continuous math and physics skills for engineering). You can adjust the pace as needed, but the order ensures prerequisites are covered:

1. **Single-Variable Calculus →** Begin with differential and integral calculus. (Use Khan Academy or Engineer4Free for quick feedback, and MIT/OpenStax for depth.) This provides the mathematical toolkit (derivatives for rates, integrals for areas/accumulations) used in physics formulas.

2. **Classical Mechanics (Physics I) →** With basic calculus in hand, study mechanics (Khan Academy Physics or Saylor PHYS101/MIT 8.01). You’ll apply derivatives to motion (velocity as $dx/dt$) and integrals to compute quantities like work and center of mass. Mechanics builds physical intuition and shows how math models reality .

3. **Multivariable Calculus (Vector Calculus) →** As you progress in mechanics, start learning multivariable calculus in parallel or next. Understanding 3D vectors and fields is important before diving into full electromagnetism. Courses like HKUST’s Vector Calculus for Engineers will prepare you by teaching divergence, curl, and integrals over fields – concepts that Maxwell’s equations are built on .

4. **Electricity & Magnetism (Physics II) →** Now take on E&M fundamentals (Saylor PHYS102 or MIT 8.02/OpenStax Vol. 2). Tackle electrostatics first (Coulomb’s law, fields, potentials, circuits) then magnetostatics (magnetic fields, forces) . This module will use your calculus skills (e.g. line integrals for electric potential, simple differential equations for circuit transients) and your new vector calculus knowledge (e.g. Gauss’s law uses flux integrals). Resources like Saylor and MIT gradually introduce Maxwell’s laws as you explore Faraday’s and Oersted’s discoveries, culminating in the unified view .

5. **Differential Equations →** Either alongside E&M or right before Maxwell’s equations, study ODEs. Knowing how to solve $L\frac{dI}{dt} + RI = V(t)$ or wave equations will be invaluable. MIT 18.03 or Khan’s Differential Equations module can be taken concurrently with the early part of E&M, since circuit equations and certain motion equations will motivate the need for ODE solutions.

6. **Maxwell’s Equations & Electromagnetic Waves →** With E&M basics and advanced math in place, dive into Maxwell’s equations and electrodynamics (the latter part of Saylor PHYS102 or specialized resources on electromagnetism). Work through how Maxwell’s four equations are formulated (in integral or differential form) and how they predict electromagnetic wave propagation. Use simulations (PhET, etc.) to visualize fields and waves. This part is math-heavy, but also incredibly rewarding – it ties together all that you’ve learned in calculus and physics into one elegant framework. You’ll also learn that light is an EM wave and see the connection to optics and modern physics .

7. **Applications and Projects →** Finally, consolidate your knowledge through practical application. Take the MITx Circuits course or experiment with electronics kits to apply electricity and magnetism in a hands-on way. For instance, analyzing an RC filter in the circuits course will use both your calculus (exponential charging curves from a differential equation) and your physics (capacitor theory from electrostatics) knowledge. Similarly, exploring how a computer’s memory or CPU clock works might lead you to topics like semiconductor physics or signal integrity, which rely on E&M principles. At this stage, you might also circle back to any weak spots – e.g., revisit a calculus concept in the context of its use in an engineering problem.

  

Each step builds on the previous, and all resources mentioned are free and accessible online. By following this path, you’ll gain a deep theoretical foundation **and** see how each concept is applied in real engineering, especially in computing technology. The modular format means you can tackle one topic at a time (e.g. spend a few weeks on calculus, then a few on mechanics, etc.), and the interactive nature of these resources will keep you engaged through videos, problem-solving, and virtual labs.

  

**In summary:** Starting from calculus and classical physics, and progressing through electricity, magnetism, and electromagnetism – supported by the necessary math – will equip you with the knowledge to understand and innovate in engineering fields. The recommended resources (Khan Academy, MIT OCW, Saylor Academy, OpenStax texts, interactive simulations, and more) provide a rich, self-paced learning experience . By leveraging these free tools and following the structured path, you will develop both the **analytical skills** and the **practical insights** needed to bridge computer science with physical engineering applications. Good luck on your learning journey!
