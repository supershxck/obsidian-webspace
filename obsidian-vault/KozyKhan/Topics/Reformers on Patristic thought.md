**Did the Reformers properly understand and use Patristic thought?**

Martin Luther and John Calvin did not entirely discard patristic thought. Both reformers engaged with the Church Fathers, though their approaches to patristic thought and how they incorporated it into their theological frameworks differed from the established practices of the Roman Catholic Church at the time. Their critiques were more focused on the Church's practices and doctrines that they believed had strayed from biblical teachings and the early Christian tradition. Here are some considerations on their engagement with the Church Fathers:

**Martin Luther**
Luther did not reject the Church Fathers outright. He valued them, especially when their writings supported his theological perspectives, such as justification by faith. For instance, he often cited Augustine, who he saw as an ally in understanding grace and salvation. Luther's principle of sola scriptura (Scripture alone) meant that the Bible was the ultimate authority in matters of faith and practice. When he felt that the Church Fathers' writings were in harmony with Scripture, he embraced their insights. However, when there was a **perceived** conflict, he prioritized the biblical text. Luther's intent was not to discard the tradition entirely but to reform the church and return it t**o what he believed was the true gospel message** found in Scripture and the early Christian church. This involved critiquing later church practices and teachings that, in his view, had no foundation in the Bible or early Christianity.

**John Calvin**
Calvin had a high regard for the Church Fathers and frequently referenced them in his works. He saw himself as part of the catholic (universal) church and sought continuity with the early church tradition. He engaged critically with the Church Fathers, acknowledging their contributions to theology while also not hesitating to critique them when he believed their teachings deviated from Scripture. Like Luther, Calvin's ultimate authority was the Bible. Calvin often used patristic thought to support his theological arguments, especially when disputing with opponents or seeking to show that his reforms were in line with early Christian teachings. Luther and Calvin did not simply throw off Patristic thought; instead, they engaged with it critically, valuing it when it aligned with their scriptural interpretations and critiquing it when it did not. Their reformative work sought to correct what they perceived as the excesses and errors of the medieval Church, _aiming for a return to what they believed were the biblical and early Christian roots of the faith._

The question of whether Martin Luther and John Calvin _fully understood_ Patristic thought in an "Orthodox" way (presumably meaning in a way that aligns with the Eastern Orthodox Church's understanding and tradition) requires a nuanced answer. Both reformers were well-educated and had access to many writings of the Church Fathers. However, their interpretations and uses of patristic sources were influenced by several factors. Luther and Calvin _lived in a Western European context, deeply shaped by Latin Christianity. Their primary influences were from the Western Church Fathers, such as Augustine, rather than Eastern Fathers_ like John Chrysostom or Basil the Great, although they were aware of these figures. The theological and ecclesial debates that formed the backdrop of their work were different from those in the Eastern Orthodox tradition.

Their theological concerns and priorities were shaped by the issues of their time, particularly those related to abuses in the church and the question of how salvation is attained. **This focus influenced how they read and applied the writings of the Church Fathers, seeking support for their theological positions like sola scriptura (Scripture alone) and sola fide (faith alone).**

Luther and Calvin approached the Church Fathers through _their own interpretive lenses_, shaped by their critical engagement with the Scriptures and their desire for reform in the church. This means that even when they engaged with patristic thought, their understanding and application of it were mediated by their own theological and hermeneutical frameworks.

The availability of patristic texts in the 16th century, especially in their original languages, was limited compared to today. _While both reformers were proficient in Latin (the language of Western theological and ecclesiastical discourse), their access to Greek Fathers was often through Latin translations, which could affect their understanding of certain nuances._

Given these considerations, while Luther and Calvin were knowledgeable about patristic writings and respected the Church Fathers, their engagement with these sources was _selective and aimed at supporting their theological reforms_. Their interpretations were not in line with how the Orthodox Church understands and utilizes the Fathers within its own theological and liturgical life. The differences in theological emphasis, ecclesiastical context, and historical situation mean that their understanding and use of patristic thought were shaped by their particular aims and contexts, _rather than a straightforward continuation of the Orthodox tradition._

The Reformers indeed sought to compare and align their reforms with what they understood to be the beliefs, practices, and organizational structures of the early Church. This endeavor was part of their broader effort to correct what they saw as deviations and corruptions that had crept into the Church over the centuries, particularly under the Roman Catholic Church's auspices. Their call to return to the sources (ad fontes) included a strong emphasis on the early Church as a model. Here are some key ways in which the Reformers engaged with the early Church.

The principle of sola scriptura (Scripture alone) was foundational to the Reformation. The Reformers _argued that the Bible should be the ultimate authority in all matters of faith and practice_, **a position they believed was in line with the practices of the early Church**, which relied on the Apostolic writings and the Old Testament.

Martin Luther, John Calvin, and other Reformers emphasized justification by faith alone (sola fide) as a doctrine they believed was taught by the Apostles and _obscured over time by the Church's institutional development_. They saw themselves as recovering a central tenet of the Apostle Paul's teachings, particularly as found in Romans and Galatians.

In terms of church governance and structure, the Reformers sought to establish models they believed were closer to those of the early Church, _emphasizing the priesthood of all believers, the importance of congregational involvement, and a less hierarchical church structure than that which characterized the late medieval Catholic Church._

The Reformers also _revisited the nature and number of the sacraments, eventually recognizing only those they believed_ were instituted by Christ and practiced by the early Church: Baptism and the Eucharist (Lord's Supper). They critiqued what they saw as the sacramental system's abuses and sought to restore a more authentic understanding and practice.

The Reformers engaged with the writings of the Church Fathers, though their interpretations and applications of these sources varied. They looked to the Fathers both for support for their positions and as models for theological reflection and church life. **However, their engagement with patristic sources was selective and subordinate to their interpretation of Scripture.**

In reforming worship practices, the Reformers aimed to strip away what they considered later medieval accretions, _such as certain sacramentals and the use of Latin in the Mass, which they felt obscured the gospel and the early Church's simplicity._ **They sought to make worship more accessible and focused on the preaching of the Word, in line with what they perceived to be early Christian practice.**

In sum, the Reformers did try to compare their reforms to the early Church, seeking to recover and restore what they understood to be the original purity and simplicity of Christian faith and practice. This effort was part of their broader critique of the Catholic Church of their time, which they believed had strayed far from its roots. However, their interpretations of the early Church and Scripture were shaped by their historical context, theological disputes, and the pressing pastoral concerns of their era.
