**The Reform Act of 1832: Britain’s First Major Democratic Breakthrough**

---

**Introduction**

  

The **Reform Act of 1832**—also known as the Representation of the People Act 1832—was the landmark legislation that dismantled Britain’s archaic electoral system, expanded the franchise, and reallocated parliamentary seats. It marked the first major step toward modern representative democracy in [[British Liberalism]].

---

**I. Historical Context & Catalysts**

|**Factor**|**Description**|
|---|---|
|Rotten Boroughs|Tiny constituencies with disproportionate influence (e.g., Old Sarum)|
|Industrial Revolution|Rise of urban middle class demanding political voice|
|Economic Dislocation|Post‑Napoleonic War hardship fueling protest (e.g., Swing Riots)|
|Philosophical Shift|Enlightenment ideals of equality and consent of the governed|

  

---

**II. Key Provisions**

|**Reform**|**Detail**|**Impact**|
|---|---|---|
|Redistribution of Seats|Abolished 56 rotten boroughs; created 67 new urban seats|Balanced representation toward industrial towns|
|Franchise Expansion|Lowered property threshold: householders worth £10/year|Electorate grew from ~400,000 to ~650,000 (≈7% of adult males)|
|Uniform Voting Qualification|Standardized borough and county qualifications|Simplified and legitimized electoral process|

  

---

**III. Political & Social Significance**

  

**Short‑Term Effects**

• Pacified moderate reformers; defused radical unrest

• Strengthened Whig (Liberal) political ascendancy

  

**Long‑Term Legacy**

• Paved way for subsequent Reform Acts (1867, 1884)

• Established principle that Parliament must reflect demographic realities
