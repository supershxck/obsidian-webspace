The **Saadian dynasty** (Arabic: السعديون, al-Saʿdiyyūn) was a ruling dynasty of Morocco that governed from 1549 to 1659, marking a significant resurgence of Moroccan power after the weaker Wattasid period. Originating from the southern Draa Valley, the Saadians were of Arab descent, claiming sharifian lineage—descent from the [[Prophet Muhammad]] through his grandson [[Hasan ibn Ali]]—which bolstered their legitimacy among Morocco’s religious and tribal communities. They rose to prominence by unifying the country, resisting European colonial ambitions, and establishing a golden age of prosperity and cultural achievement.

### Origins and Rise to Power
The Saadians emerged in the early 16th century from Tagmadert in the Draa Valley, where they were local notables tied to the Qadiriyya Sufi order. Their ascent began under Ahmad al-Araj, who, with his brother Muhammad al-Shaykh, rallied southern tribes against the Wattasids and Portuguese incursions. By 1524, they had captured Marrakesh, making it their base. Their big break came with military successes: al-Araj expelled the Portuguese from Agadir in 1537, and Muhammad al-Shaykh, after becoming sole ruler in 1544, overthrew the Wattasids at the Battle of Tadla in 1554, seizing Fez and ending their dynasty. Muhammad al-Shaykh thus became the first Saadian sultan to rule a unified Morocco.

### Height of Power
The Saadian dynasty peaked under **Ahmad al-Mansur** (r. 1578–1603), known as "the Victorious" or "the Golden." His reign is considered Morocco’s early modern high point:
- **Military Triumph:** Al-Mansur’s defining moment was the **Battle of Alcácer Quibir** (Battle of the Three Kings) in 1578, where his forces crushed a Portuguese invasion, killing King Sebastian I and securing Morocco’s independence from European domination. This victory earned him international prestige.
- **Songhai Conquest:** In 1591, al-Mansur launched a daring trans-Saharan campaign, conquering the Songhai Empire in West Africa (modern Mali and Niger). His army, equipped with firearms, captured Timbuktu and Gao, securing control of gold and salt trade routes. This enriched Morocco and showcased its military reach.
- **Diplomacy:** Al-Mansur maintained strategic ties with England (notably Queen Elizabeth I) against Spain, trading sugar and saltpeter for weapons, and kept the Ottoman Empire at bay despite their nominal claim over Morocco.

### Cultural and Economic Flourishing
Al-Mansur’s wealth funded a cultural renaissance:
- **Architecture:** He built the lavish **El Badi Palace** in Marrakesh, a symbol of Saadian opulence, using materials like Italian marble and gold from Sudan.
- **Economy:** Sugar exports and trans-Saharan trade fueled prosperity, with Marrakesh becoming a hub of commerce and learning.
- **Society:** The dynasty welcomed Andalusian refugees (Muslims and Jews expelled from Spain), whose skills enriched Moroccan crafts, music, and scholarship.

### Decline and Fall
After al-Mansur’s death in 1603 from plague, the Saadians faltered. His sons—Zidan, Abu Faris, and Muhammad al-Shaykh al-Ma’mun—fought over succession, splitting the realm into rival factions based in Marrakesh and Fez. This civil war weakened central authority, and regional powers, like the Zawiya of Dila (a Sufi brotherhood), gained influence. The dynasty’s end came in 1659 when the last Saadian ruler, Ahmad al-Abbas, was overthrown by the Alaouites, a sharifian family from Tafilalt who capitalized on the chaos to establish Morocco’s current ruling dynasty.

### Legacy
The Saadians left a lasting mark:
- **National Unity:** They restored Morocco as a centralized state after centuries of fragmentation.
- **Resistance:** Their defiance of Portugal and Spain preserved Moroccan sovereignty during a vulnerable era.
- **Monuments:** The **Saadian Tombs** in Marrakesh, rediscovered in 1917, remain a testament to their artistic peak, housing al-Mansur’s mausoleum.

In essence, the Saadian dynasty transformed Morocco from a fractured, threatened land into a unified, prosperous power. Their rule bridged the medieval Marinid era and the modern Alaouite period, with al-Mansur’s reign standing out as a pinnacle of Moroccan ambition and achievement.
