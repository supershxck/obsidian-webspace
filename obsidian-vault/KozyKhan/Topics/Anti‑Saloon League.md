**The Anti‑Saloon League: A Structured Chapter**

  

**I. Introduction**

  

Founded in 1893, the Anti‑Saloon League (ASL) was America’s foremost single‑issue pressure group advocating for nationwide prohibition of alcohol. It transformed temperance activism into a powerful political machine that culminated in the 18th Amendment.

---

**II. Historical Context ([[history]])**

• **Temperance Movement Roots:** Emerged from 19th‑century religious revivalism and social reform.

• **Founding:** Established in Oberlin, Ohio by Howard Hyde Russell to focus exclusively on legislative prohibition.

---

**III. Ideological Foundations ([[philosophy]])**

• **Moral Reform:** Framed alcohol as source of poverty, crime, and family breakdown.

• **Social Purity:** Viewed prohibition as essential to national uplift and moral order.

---

**IV. Organizational Structure & Strategies**

|**Element**|**Description**|**Impact**|
|---|---|---|
|Single‑Issue Focus|Sole dedication to banning alcohol|Laser‑sharp lobbying effectiveness|
|Political Pressure|“Wets vs. Drys” voter guides, candidate endorsements|Shifted party platforms toward prohibition|
|Grassroots Network|Local chapters in every state|Mobilized churches, civic groups|
|Propaganda|Broadsides, pamphlets, mass mailings|Shaped public opinion through fear and moral appeal|

  

---

**V. Psychological & Social Dynamics ([[psychology]])**

• **Moral Certainty:** Created in‑group solidarity among “drys.”

• **Fear Appeals:** Emphasized alcohol’s dangers to children and family stability.

---

**VI. Impact & Legacy ([[economics]])**

• **Legislative Success:** Key architect of the 18th Amendment and Volstead Act.

• **Institutional Precedent:** Model for single‑issue political lobbying in American politics.

• **Long-Term Influence:** Tactics adopted by modern advocacy movements (e.g., public health campaigns).

**IX. Conclusion**

  

The Anti‑Saloon League demonstrates how focused advocacy, moral rhetoric, and political strategy can enact sweeping constitutional change. By dissecting its methods and impacts, we learn to discern the balance between social reform and individual autonomy—and apply these lessons to contemporary policy debates.
