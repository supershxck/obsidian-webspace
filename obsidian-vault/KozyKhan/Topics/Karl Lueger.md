### Richard Wagner

#### Early Life and Career
- **Birth**: Wilhelm Richard Wagner was born on May 22, 1813, in Leipzig, Germany.
- **Musical Beginnings**: Wagner showed early interest in music, influenced by composers like Ludwig van Beethoven. He began composing operas in his twenties.
- **Paris and Dresden**: After struggling in Paris, Wagner found success in Dresden with his opera "Rienzi" in 1842, followed by "The Flying Dutchman" in 1843.

#### Major Works
- **Operas**: Wagner is best known for his operas, including "The Flying Dutchman," "Tannhäuser," "Lohengrin," "Tristan und Isolde," "Die Meistersinger von Nürnberg," "Parsifal," and the epic four-opera cycle "Der Ring des Nibelungen" (The Ring of the Nibelung), which includes "Das Rheingold," "Die Walküre," "Siegfried," and "Götterdämmerung."
- **Innovation**: Wagner revolutionized opera with his concept of the Gesamtkunstwerk, or "total work of art," integrating music, poetry, and stagecraft. He expanded the role of the orchestra and used leitmotifs (recurring musical themes associated with characters or ideas).

#### Anti-Semitic Views
- **Writings**: Wagner's 1850 essay "Das Judenthum in der Musik" ("Jewishness in Music") expressed his anti-Semitic beliefs, criticizing Jewish composers and musicians and asserting that Jews were incapable of true artistic creativity.
- **Influence on Hitler**: Wagner's anti-Semitic ideas and his portrayal of heroic, Germanic myths influenced Adolf Hitler, who admired Wagner's music and saw him as a precursor to his own ideology.

#### Bayreuth Festival
- **Festival**: Wagner established the Bayreuth Festival in 1876 to showcase his operas. The festival continues to be an important cultural event, dedicated to performing Wagner's works.
- **Legacy**: Despite his controversial views, Wagner remains one of the most influential composers in Western music. His innovations in harmony, orchestration, and operatic structure had a lasting impact on music.

#### Death
- **Death**: Wagner died on February 13, 1883, in Venice, Italy. He was buried in Bayreuth, Germany.

### Karl Lueger

#### Early Life and Career
- **Birth**: Karl Lueger was born on October 24, 1844, in Vienna, Austria.
- **Education**: He studied law at the University of Vienna and became a lawyer, entering politics as a member of the Christian Social Party.

#### Political Career
- **Vienna City Council**: Lueger was elected to the Vienna City Council in 1875, where he gained a reputation as a champion of the middle class and a critic of liberal policies.
- **Mayor of Vienna**: He became the mayor of Vienna in 1897, a position he held until his death in 1910. His tenure was marked by significant urban improvements, including the expansion of public transportation, water supply, and social services.

#### Anti-Semitic Views
- **Political Strategy**: Lueger used anti-Semitic rhetoric as a political tool to gain support from the lower and middle classes, who felt threatened by economic changes and blamed Jews for their difficulties.
- **Influence**: Lueger's anti-Semitic policies and rhetoric had a significant impact on young Adolf Hitler, who lived in Vienna during Lueger's mayoralty. Hitler admired Lueger's ability to use populist and anti-Semitic rhetoric to gain political power.

#### Death
- **Death**: Karl Lueger died on March 10, 1910, in Vienna. Despite his controversial views, he is remembered for his contributions to the modernization of Vienna.

### Conclusion
Richard Wagner and Karl Lueger were influential figures whose anti-Semitic views left a lasting impact on history. Wagner's contributions to music and opera were profound, while Lueger's political career showcased the dangers of using prejudice as a tool for political gain. Both men significantly influenced Adolf Hitler, shaping his ideologies and strategies.
