The Celts were a collection of Indo-European peoples who shared linguistic, cultural, and artistic traits, originating in Central Europe and spreading across much of Europe between roughly 1200 BCE and the Roman era. Here's a detailed look at who they were:

### Historical Context
- **Origins**: The Celts emerged during the late Bronze Age, with their early culture tied to the **Urnfield culture** (c. 1300–750 BCE) in Central Europe. By the Iron Age, they evolved into the **Hallstatt culture** (c. 1200–500 BCE) and later the **La Tène culture** (c. 500 BCE–1st century CE), which is considered the peak of Celtic civilization.
- **Time Period**: They flourished from around 800 BCE until Roman conquests and Germanic migrations diminished their dominance, though their influence persisted in some regions (e.g., Ireland and Scotland) into the Middle Ages.

### Culture and Society
- **Language**: The Celts spoke Celtic languages, a branch of Indo-European, which split into two main groups:
  - **Continental Celtic**: E.g., Gaulish, spoken in mainland Europe (now extinct).
  - **Insular Celtic**: E.g., Irish Gaelic, Scottish Gaelic, Welsh, and Breton (some still spoken today).
- **Society**: Celtic society was tribal, organized into clans and kingdoms led by chieftains or kings. Druids—priests, political advisors, and scholars—played a central role in religion and governance.
- **Religion**: They practiced a polytheistic religion with gods tied to nature (e.g., Lugus, Cernunnos). Rituals included offerings at sacred sites like rivers and groves. Druids were key spiritual figures, and some evidence suggests human sacrifice in early periods.
- **Art**: Celtic art is renowned for its intricate designs—spirals, knots, and animal motifs—seen in metalwork (e.g., the Gundestrup Cauldron) and stone carvings. The La Tène style is particularly iconic.

### Geographic Spread
- **Core Areas**: The Celts originated in what’s now southern Germany, Austria, and Switzerland.
- **Expansion**: By 400 BCE, they spread widely:
  - **Gaul**: Modern France and Belgium (e.g., the Parisii tribe gave Paris its name).
  - **Britain and Ireland**: Including tribes like the Britons and Gaels.
  - **Iberian Peninsula**: The Celtiberians, a mix of Celtic and Iberian peoples.
  - **Central Europe**: Parts of modern Czech Republic, Slovakia, and Hungary.
  - **Asia Minor**: The Galatians, a Celtic group that settled in modern Turkey around 278 BCE.
- **Decline**: Roman conquests (e.g., Gaul by Julius Caesar in 58–50 BCE) and later Germanic invasions reduced their territories, though Celtic culture survived in the British Isles.

### Interactions with Other Cultures
- **Greeks**: The Celts traded with Greek colonies and occasionally clashed with them (e.g., the sack of Delphi in 279 BCE).
- **Romans**: Rome’s expansion absorbed much of Celtic Europe. Gaul became Romanized, but Britain and Ireland retained more Celtic identity until later invasions (e.g., Anglo-Saxons).
- **Germans**: Germanic tribes displaced Celts in Central Europe, pushing them westward.

### Notable Traits
- **Warfare**: Celts were skilled warriors, known for iron weapons, chariots (in Britain), and a reputation for ferocity. They often fought naked or painted with woad to intimidate enemies.
- **Economy**: They were farmers, herders, and metalworkers, trading goods like salt, tin, and amber across Europe.
- **Oral Tradition**: Lacking a widespread writing system (though they used Ogham in Ireland later), Celts relied on oral history, preserved by bards and druids.

### Modern Legacy
- **Surviving Cultures**: Celtic identity endures in Ireland, Scotland, Wales, Brittany, and the Isle of Man, where languages like Irish Gaelic and Welsh are still spoken.
- **Genetic Impact**: Modern Europeans, especially in Western Europe, carry Celtic ancestry, though it’s mixed with Roman, Germanic, and other influences.
- **Cultural Revival**: The “Celtic” label was romanticized in the 18th–19th centuries, leading to a modern cultural movement (e.g., Celtic music, festivals).

### Celts vs. Iberians
Since you asked about Iberians earlier, the Celts and Iberians overlapped in the Iberian Peninsula, where the Celtiberians emerged as a hybrid culture. The Celts brought Indo-European languages and ironworking, blending with the non-Indo-European Iberians. Elsewhere, their histories diverged, with Celts dominating northern and western Europe while Iberians remained rooted in the southwest.
