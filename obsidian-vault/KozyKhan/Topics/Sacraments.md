**Sacraments**
**St. Isidore of Seville on the meaning of sacrament**
«A _sacrament_ takes place in a particular liturgical rite when an action is performed in such a way that it is understood to signify something that ought to be received in a holy way. Sacraments, then, are baptism and unction, and the body and blood of the Lord. These things are called sacraments (sacramentum) for this reason, that under the covering of corporeal things the divine virtue very secretly brings about the saving power of those same sacraments — whence from their secret (secretus) or holy (sacer) power they are called sacraments. Sacraments are fruitfully performed under the aegis of the Church because of the Holy Spirit dwelling in the Church in a hidden way brings about the aforesaid effect of the sacraments. Hence, although they may be dispensed through the Church of God by good or by bad ministers, nevertheless because the Holy Spirit mystically vivifies them — that Spirit that formerly in apostolic times would appear in visible works — these gifts are neither enlarged by the merit of good ministers nor diminished by the bad, for, _neither he that planteth is any thing, nor he that watereth; but God that giveth the increase_ (1 Cor. 3:7). For this reason in Greek a sacrament is called a _mystery_, because it has a secret and recondite character.»

The Orthodox Church recognizes the following seven sacraments, also known as [[Holy Mysteries]]:
1. **[[Baptism]]**: The sacrament of Christian initiation, where one is immersed in water, symbolizing death to the old life of sin and rebirth into a new life in Christ. It is usually performed once in a person's life.
2. **[[Chrismation]] (Confirmation)**: Immediately following Baptism, this sacrament involves the anointing with chrism (holy oil), signifying the gift of the Holy Spirit to the newly baptized. It's seen as a personal Pentecost for the believer, equipping them with the gifts necessary for their life in the Church.
3. **[[Eucharist]] (Holy Communion)**: Considered the central sacrament of the Church, the Eucharist is the partaking of the Body and Blood of Jesus Christ, under the forms of bread and wine, for the remission of sins and eternal life. Orthodox Christians believe in the real presence of Christ in the Eucharist.
4. **[[Confession]] (Penance)**: This sacrament involves the confession of sins to a priest and the receiving of absolution, through which one's sins are forgiven, and they are reconciled to God and the Church. It's a practice encouraged to be done regularly, not just when in mortal sin.
5. **Holy Unction ([[Anointing of the Sick]])**: This sacrament, often associated with healing, involves anointing the sick with holy oil blessed by the Church. It's meant for the healing of both physical and spiritual ailments and can also be administered as a preparation for passing.
6. **Holy Orders ([[Ordination]])**: Through this sacrament, men are ordained as bishops, priests, or deacons through the laying on of hands by the bishop. It is through this sacrament that the apostolic succession of the Church is continued.
7. **[[Matrimony]] (Marriage)**: This sacrament blesses the union between a man and a woman, making their relationship a symbol of the unity between Christ and His Church. It is intended to be indissoluble and a means of mutual sanctification.
These sacraments are seen as [[means of grace]], through which God sanctifies [[the faithful]], incorporating them more fully into [[the life of the Church]] and into [[the life of Christ]] Himself.
