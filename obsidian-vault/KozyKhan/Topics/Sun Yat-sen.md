**Sun Yat-sen (1866–1925): The Father of Modern China**

  

Sun Yat-sen is a pivotal figure in Chinese history, revered as the **“Father of Modern China”** for his leadership in overthrowing the Qing Dynasty and establishing the Republic of China. His political ideology, known as the **[[Three Principles of the People]]**, has had a lasting impact on Chinese politics and continues to influence both mainland China and Taiwan.

  

**1. Early Life and Education**

  

**a. Birth and Family Background**

• **Born**: November 12, 1866, in **Canton (Guangzhou)**, **Guangdong Province**, China.

• **Family**: Sun was born into a **Hakka** family, a Han Chinese subgroup with distinct cultural and linguistic traits. His father was a farmer, and his mother managed the household.

  

**b. Education Abroad**

• **Move to Hawaii (1879)**:

• Sun moved to the United States with his family to escape political unrest and seek better opportunities.

• He attended **Iolani School** in Honolulu, where he was exposed to Western ideas and education.

• **Further Studies**:

• Sun continued his education in **Hong Kong** and **Taiwan**, where he became involved in anti-Qing activities and revolutionary circles.

  

**2. Revolutionary Activities and the Quest to Overthrow the Qing Dynasty**

  

**a. Early Revolutionary Efforts**

• **Founding of the Revive China Society (1894)**:

• Sun established the **Revive China Society** in Hong Kong, aimed at overthrowing the Qing Dynasty and establishing a republican government.

• **Failed Uprisings**:

• Sun led several unsuccessful uprisings against the Qing, including the **Shanghai Uprising (1900)** and the **Huanghuagang Uprising (1911)**.

  

**b. Exile and Organization**

• **Exile**:

• Sun spent significant periods in exile in **Japan**, **the United States**, and **Europe**, where he garnered international support for his revolutionary cause.

• **Tongmenghui (1905)**:

• In Tokyo, Sun founded the **Tongmenghui (Chinese Revolutionary Alliance)**, uniting various anti-Qing factions and laying the groundwork for a nationwide revolution.

  

**3. The Xinhai Revolution and the Establishment of the Republic of China**

  

**a. The Xinhai Revolution (1911)**

• **Outbreak**:

• The revolution was triggered by the **Wuchang Uprising** on October 10, 1911, which quickly spread across China, leading to the collapse of the Qing Dynasty.

• **Role of Sun Yat-sen**:

• Although Sun was not present in China during the initial stages, he was recognized as the leader and the driving force behind the revolutionary movement.

  

**b. Provisional Government**

• **Founding of the Republic**:

• On January 1, 1912, Sun Yat-sen was inaugurated as the **Provisional President** of the **Republic of China** in Nanjing.

• **Collaboration with Yuan Shikai**:

• Sun ceded the presidency to **Yuan Shikai**, a powerful Qing general, in exchange for Yuan’s support in consolidating the republic. This move was controversial but aimed at unifying the fragmented nation.

  

**4. Political Philosophy: The Three Principles of the People**

  

Sun Yat-sen’s political ideology is encapsulated in the **Three Principles of the People (三民主義, Sānmín Zhǔyì)**:

  

**a. Nationalism (民族主義, Mínzú Zhǔyì)**

• **Objective**: To end foreign domination and unify all Chinese people, including various ethnic groups.

• **Focus**: Promoting national sovereignty, cultural identity, and resisting imperialist encroachments.

  

**b. Democracy (民主主義, Mínzhǔ Zhǔyì)**

• **Objective**: To establish a government based on the will of the people.

• **Focus**: Implementing a republican form of government with elected representatives, civil liberties, and the rule of law.

  

**c. Livelihood (民生主義, Mínshēng Zhǔyì)**

• **Objective**: To ensure economic well-being and social welfare for all citizens.

• **Focus**: Addressing issues like land reform, industrialization, infrastructure development, and equitable distribution of resources.

  

**5. Later Political Career and Challenges**

  

**a. Struggles for Power**

• **Factionalism**:

• After stepping down as Provisional President, Sun faced challenges from regional warlords and rival political factions.

• **Provisional Constitution**:

• Sun attempted to draft a provisional constitution to stabilize the republic but faced opposition from warlords like **Yuan Shikai**.

  

**b. Formation of the Kuomintang (KMT)**

• **Return to Power**:

• Sun reorganized the **Kuomintang (Nationalist Party)** in **1919**, aiming to reunify China and eliminate warlordism.

• **Alliance with Soviet Union**:

• In the early 1920s, the KMT formed a **United Front** with the **Chinese Communist Party (CCP)** to strengthen the national movement.

  

**c. The Northern Expedition (1926–1928)**

• **Unification Effort**:

• Although Sun Yat-sen was more of a symbolic leader at this point, the KMT launched the **Northern Expedition** to unify China under a central government.

• **Death**:

• Sun Yat-sen passed away on **March 12, 1925**, in **Moscow**, leaving the KMT to continue his legacy.

  

**6. Legacy and Influence**

  

**a. Father of Modern China**

• **Symbol of Nationalism**:

• Sun is celebrated for his unwavering commitment to national rejuvenation and resistance against imperialism.

• **Inspiration for Future Leaders**:

• His ideas influenced both the **Nationalist Government** (led by Chiang Kai-shek) and the **Communist Movement** in China.

  

**b. Influence in Taiwan**

• **Republic of China**:

• After the Chinese Civil War, the KMT retreated to **Taiwan**, where Sun Yat-sen is revered as the **“National Father”** and his Three Principles remain foundational in Taiwanese political ideology.

  

**c. Memorials and Honors**

• **Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum**:

• Located in **Nanjing**, it is a major historical site commemorating his contributions.

• **Statues and Memorials**:

• Numerous statues, streets, and institutions across China, Taiwan, and Southeast Asia honor his legacy.

  

**7. Timeline of Key Events**

  

**Year** **Event**

**1866** Sun Yat-sen is born in Guangzhou, Guangdong Province.

**1894** Founds the Revive China Society in Hong Kong.

**1905** Establishes the Tongmenghui (Chinese Revolutionary Alliance) in Tokyo.

**1911** Xinhai Revolution begins; Qing Dynasty collapses.

**1912** Sun Yat-sen becomes Provisional President of the Republic of China.

**1919** Reorganizes the Kuomintang (KMT) to continue the revolution.

**1925** Passes away in Moscow, Russia.

**1926–1928** Northern Expedition aims to unify China under KMT leadership.

**1949** KMT retreats to Taiwan after losing the Chinese Civil War.

**1965** Taiwan officially honors Sun Yat-sen as the “National Father.”

  

**8. Key Figures Associated with Sun Yat-sen**

  

**Name** **Role**

**Chiang Kai-shek** Leader of the KMT who succeeded Sun in unifying parts of China.

**Mao Zedong** Leader of the CCP who was influenced by Sun’s ideas in the early stages.

**Liang Qichao** Influential reformist and associate of Sun Yat-sen.

**Yuan Shikai** Qing general and later President who collaborated briefly with Sun.

  

**9. Conclusion**

  

Sun Yat-sen’s visionary leadership and revolutionary zeal were instrumental in dismantling imperial rule and laying the groundwork for modern China. Despite facing numerous challenges, including internal factionalism and external pressures, his enduring legacy as a unifying figure and proponent of nationalism, democracy, and livelihood continues to resonate in contemporary Chinese and Taiwanese societies. Sun’s life exemplifies the struggle for national rejuvenation and the complex path toward modern statehood in East Asia.
