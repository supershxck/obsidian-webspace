**The Union of Krewo: An Overview**

  

**Introduction**

  

The **Union of Krewo** was a **dynastic union** between the **[[Kingdom of Poland]]** and the **[[Grand Duchy of Lithuania]]**, signed on **August 14, 1385**, at **Krewo** (now in modern-day **Lithuania**). This agreement marked the beginning of a long-lasting political and military alliance between the two powers, which would eventually form the **Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth** in 1569. The union was a crucial moment in both Polish and Lithuanian history, as it not only solidified an alliance between two powerful states but also set the stage for the Christianization of the pagan Grand Duchy of Lithuania.

**1. Background and Context**

  

**1.1 The Situation in Lithuania**

  

Before the Union of Krewo, the **Grand Duchy of Lithuania** was one of the largest and most powerful states in Europe. Despite its size and military strength, Lithuania was still largely a **pagan** society, while its neighbors, particularly Poland, were already **Christian**. This religious divide created a diplomatic and military challenge for Lithuania, as much of **Western Europe** viewed paganism with hostility, and the pressures of external threats, especially from the **Teutonic Order** (a powerful Christian military order), made the situation more precarious.

  

**1.2 The Polish Context**

  

In Poland, the **Piast dynasty** had been weakened after the death of **Casimir III the Great** in 1370. Casimir had no male heir, and the **Polish throne** passed to **Louis I of Hungary**, whose rule over Poland was increasingly challenged by internal divisions. In Poland, there was a growing desire for a strong and stable monarchy, which could address both internal problems and external threats, particularly the growing influence of the **Teutonic Knights**.

  

**1.3 The Teutonic Order**

  

The **Teutonic Order** had long been a threat to both Poland and Lithuania, particularly after their defeat of the **Prussian tribes** and their expansion into the region. The Teutonic Knights sought to bring both Poland and Lithuania under their influence by **Christianizing** the pagan lands, and they became a significant political and military challenge for both states.

**2. The Union of Krewo: Terms and Key Figures**

  

**2.1 The Agreement**

  

The **Union of Krewo** was primarily a **dynastic union**, which was agreed upon when **Jadwiga of Poland**, the 11-year-old queen of Poland, married **Jogaila**, the Grand Duke of Lithuania. This union would have profound political and religious implications for both countries.

• **Jadwiga** was the daughter of **Louis I of Hungary**, the king of Poland. She became queen at a young age, and after Louis’s death, there was a need for a strong and politically advantageous marriage to secure Poland’s future.

• **Jogaila**, also known as **Władysław II Jagiełło**, was a powerful ruler in Lithuania. He agreed to marry Jadwiga under the condition that he and his people would convert to **Christianity**.

  

**2.2 Key Provisions of the Union**

  

The key elements of the **Union of Krewo** were:

• **Conversion of Lithuania to Christianity**: One of the most significant outcomes of the union was **Lithuania’s conversion** to Christianity. Jogaila and many of his nobles agreed to be baptized, marking the end of Lithuania’s status as the last pagan state in Europe. This was a key requirement of the union, as Poland was a Christian kingdom, and the union would not have been possible without Lithuania adopting Christianity.

• **Marriage of Jadwiga and Jogaila**: The union was also marked by the **marriage** between **Jadwiga** and **Jogaila**. This marriage solidified the dynastic union between Poland and Lithuania, bringing the two states closer together.

• **Recognition of Polish Sovereignty**: Jogaila was required to formally recognize **Poland’s sovereignty** under the crown of **Poland**, and he pledged to unite his Grand Duchy of Lithuania with Poland through a common monarch. Although Lithuania and Poland would remain separate political entities, the union would effectively create a shared rule by the same monarch.

  

**2.3 Political and Military Alliance**

  

The union also led to a **military alliance** between Poland and Lithuania, which was vital in combating external threats, particularly from the Teutonic Order. The combined forces of Poland and Lithuania proved to be a powerful defense against the **Teutonic Knights** and other potential invaders.

**3. Impact of the Union of Krewo**

  

**3.1 Christianization of Lithuania**

  

The conversion of Lithuania to Christianity was a crucial moment in European history. Lithuania, having resisted Christianization for centuries, was finally brought into the Christian fold through the marriage of Jadwiga and Jogaila. This decision had long-lasting effects on the culture, religion, and politics of the region. The conversion helped solidify the relationship between Poland and Lithuania, as both were now Christian kingdoms, and it opened the door for further diplomatic and military cooperation between the two states.

  

**3.2 Strengthening of Polish-Lithuanian Relations**

  

The Union of Krewo laid the foundation for the eventual **Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth**, a powerful political entity that would last for several centuries. The dynastic union established a shared monarchy, which helped to strengthen the ties between the two countries and promoted cooperation in military, economic, and political affairs.

• **Royal Union**: The personal union between the crowns of Poland and Lithuania lasted for several centuries, culminating in the **Union of Lublin** in **1569**, which created the **Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth**, a federative state with joint political institutions, a common monarch, and shared military forces.

  

**3.3 Political Stability and Defense Against the Teutonic Order**

  

The union helped to stabilize both Poland and Lithuania, especially in terms of defense. The Teutonic Order had been a consistent threat to both regions, and the combined forces of Poland and Lithuania were able to effectively combat them. One of the most notable battles in this context was the **Battle of Grunwald (1410)**, where the Polish-Lithuanian army, led by **Jogaila (Władysław II Jagiełło)**, decisively defeated the **Teutonic Knights**, significantly weakening their influence in the region.

**4. Long-Term Effects and Legacy**

  

**4.1 Creation of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth**

  

While the Union of Krewo was initially a personal and dynastic union, it paved the way for the eventual creation of the **Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth** in **1569**, which united the two countries into a powerful and influential European state. This commonwealth lasted until the late 18th century and played a major role in the politics of **Eastern Europe**.

• **Shared Institutions**: The Commonwealth shared a **common monarchy**, but it also developed shared political institutions, such as a **parliament** and a **joint military**. The Commonwealth remained an important political force in Europe for centuries, although it eventually weakened and was partitioned in the late 18th century.

  

**4.2 Cultural and Religious Exchange**

  

The Union of Krewo also fostered significant **cultural and religious exchange** between Poland and Lithuania. Lithuania, which had been a center of **pagan traditions**, began to absorb **Polish Catholic** customs, values, and religious practices, which eventually played a role in shaping the broader identity of both countries.

**5. Conclusion**

  

The **Union of Krewo** was a pivotal moment in the history of both Poland and Lithuania. It brought two powerful states together, helping to secure their political and military strength, especially in the face of external threats like the Teutonic Order. It also marked the **Christianization** of Lithuania, uniting the two countries under a shared religious and political framework. The union laid the groundwork for the eventual creation of the **Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth**, which became one of the most important political entities in early modern Europe.
