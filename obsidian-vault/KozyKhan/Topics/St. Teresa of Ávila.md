**St. Teresa of Ávila (1515–1582): Mystic Doctor of the Church**

---

**Introduction**

  

**St. Teresa of Ávila** (also Teresa Sánchez de Cepeda y Ahumada) was a Spanish Carmelite nun, mystic, and reformer whose visionary writings profoundly shaped [[Catholic mysticism]] and Christian contemplative practice. Canonized in 1622 and declared a Doctor of the Church in 1970, her legacy endures as a model of interior transformation and spiritual leadership.

---

**Early Life & Historical Context**

• **Born:** March 28, 1515 in Ávila, Spain

• Raised amid the Spanish Golden Age—an era of religious fervor, Inquisition tensions, and Renaissance humanism

• Entered the Carmelite convent of the Incarnation at age 20

---

**Reform & Founding of Discalced Carmelites**

• Frustrated by lax observance, Teresa initiated a return to austere, contemplative life

• Founded the first reformed (“discalced”) Carmelite convent at Ávila in 1562, emphasizing poverty, silence, and prayer

• Ultimately established 17 monasteries across Spain

**Legacy & Influence**

• Pioneered female leadership in the Counter‑Reformation Church

• Inspired contemplative orders worldwide (Discalced Carmelites, Benedictines)

• Influences modern mindfulness, psychotherapy, and interfaith spirituality

**Key Vocabulary**

• **Discalced:** “Barefoot,” symbolizing poverty and humility

• **Interior Castle:** Metaphor for the soul’s progressive chambers toward God

• **Dark Night of the Soul:** Period of spiritual desolation refining faith

• **Contemplatio:** Wordless, receptive prayer beyond intellectual meditation
