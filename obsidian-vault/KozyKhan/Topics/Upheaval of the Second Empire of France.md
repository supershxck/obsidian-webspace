**Upheaval of the Second Empire (1852–1870) and the Birth of the Early Third Republic (1870–1875)**

---

**Introduction**

  

The collapse of France’s [[Second Empire]] under Napoleon III and the tumultuous establishment of the [[Third Republic]] mark a pivotal transition from authoritarian rule to fragile republicanism — a period defined by military defeat, social revolution, and ideological realignment.

---

**I. The Second Empire: Zenith to Crisis**

  

**Authoritarian Modernization**

• **Founding (1852):** Louis‑Napoléon Bonaparte crowns himself Emperor Napoleon III, promising order and prosperity

• **Achievements:** Industrial expansion, Haussmann’s Paris renovations, railroad network growth

  

**Growing Discontent**

• **Political Repression:** Censorship, limited suffrage, suppression of dissent

• **Economic Inequality:** Rapid modernization widened gap between bourgeoisie and working classes

• **Intellectual Opposition:** Critics like [[Émile Zola]] decried social injustice and materialist excess

---

**II. Catalysts of Collapse**

| **Event**                      | **Date**           | **Impact**                                              |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| [[Franco-Prussian War]]        | July 1870–Jan 1871 | Crushing defeat; capture of Napoleon III at Sedan       |
| Siege of Paris                 | Sept 1870–Jan 1871 | Starvation, radicalization of urban poor                |
| Proclamation of Third Republic | Sept 4, 1870       | End of imperial rule; provisional republican government |

  

---

**III. The Paris Commune: Radical Interlude (March–May 1871)**

  

**Origins & Ideology**

• Reaction against conservative Versailles government

• Influenced by socialist, anarchist, and utopian currents

  

**Outcome**

• Brutal suppression (“La Semaine Sanglante”) — ≈20,000 killed

• Deep social trauma; entrenched political polarities

---

**IV. Early Third Republic: Stabilization Amid Turmoil**

  

**Institutional Formation**

• **Constitutional Laws (1875):** Parliamentary bicameral legislature; weak presidency

• **Political Factions:** Monarchists vs Republicans; secularists vs Catholic conservatives

  

**Social & Cultural Shifts**

• Expansion of public education (Jules Ferry laws)

• Rise of laïcité (state secularism) as republican bedrock
