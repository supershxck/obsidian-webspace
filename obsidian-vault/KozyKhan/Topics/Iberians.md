The Iberians were an ancient people who inhabited the Iberian Peninsula, which today encompasses modern Spain and Portugal. They are primarily known from historical records dating back to around 1200 BCE through the period of Roman conquest, which began in the 3rd century BCE. Here's a breakdown of who they were:

### Historical Context
- **Origins**: The Iberians were likely indigenous to the Iberian Peninsula, with their culture emerging during the Bronze Age. Their exact origins are debated, but they are thought to be a mix of local Neolithic populations and possibly some influence from migrating groups.
- **Time Period**: They flourished between approximately 1200 BCE and the Roman conquest (starting with the Second Punic War, 218–201 BCE), after which their distinct identity largely merged into Roman culture.

### Culture and Society
- **Language**: The Iberians spoke a non-Indo-European language (or languages), which remains only partially deciphered. Their script, known as the Iberian script, was used to write their language, but much of it is still untranslated due to limited surviving texts.
- **Settlements**: They lived in fortified hilltop settlements called *oppida*. Notable examples include Ullastret and Castulo. These sites indicate a tribal society with some level of urban organization.
- **Economy**: They were skilled in agriculture, animal husbandry, and metallurgy (especially iron and bronze). Trade with Phoenicians, Greeks, and later Carthaginians brought wealth, particularly through the exchange of metals like silver and tin.
- **Art**: Iberian art is famous for its sculptures, such as the *Lady of Elche* and *Lady of Baza*, which suggest a sophisticated artistic tradition, possibly linked to religious or elite status symbols.

### Interactions with Other Cultures
- **Phoenicians and Greeks**: From around 1000 BCE, Phoenician traders established coastal colonies (e.g., Gadir, modern Cádiz), influencing Iberian culture with trade and technology. Greeks followed, founding cities like Emporion (Empúries).
- **Carthaginians**: By the 6th century BCE, Carthage dominated parts of southern Iberia, using it as a base during the Punic Wars against Rome.
- **Romans**: The Roman conquest (completed by 19 BCE under Augustus) integrated the Iberians into the Roman Empire, leading to the Romanization of their culture. Many Iberians adopted Latin, and their distinct identity faded over time.

### Ethnic and Regional Diversity
- The term "Iberian" doesn't refer to a single unified people but rather a collection of tribes with shared cultural traits. Major groups included:
  - **Tartessians**: In the southwest, possibly a distinct early Iberian culture tied to the biblical Tarshish, known for wealth and trade.
  - **Celtiberians**: In the central and northern interior, a blend of Iberian and Celtic influences.
  - **Turdetani**: Successors to the Tartessians in the south, heavily influenced by Phoenician contact.
  - **Bastetani, Edetani, and others**: Various tribes along the eastern coast.

### Modern Connection
Today, "Iberian" refers geographically to the peninsula or linguistically to the Romance languages (Spanish, Portuguese, Catalan) that evolved from Latin in the region. The ancient Iberians are ancestors, in part, to the modern populations of Spain and Portugal, though their genetic and cultural legacy is mixed with Celtic, Roman, Visigothic, and later influences.
