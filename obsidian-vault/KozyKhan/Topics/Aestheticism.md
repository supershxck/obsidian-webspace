**Aestheticism: “Art for Art’s Sake” and the Cult of Beauty**

---

**Introduction**

  

**Aestheticism** was a late‑19th‑century cultural and artistic movement celebrating beauty, sensory experience, and formal perfection above moral or social purpose. Coined by Walter Pater, its rallying cry—“art for art’s sake”—rejected [[Utilitarianism]] and moral didacticism in favor of pure aesthetic enjoyment.

---

**Historical Origins & Context**

|**Period**|**Catalyst**|**Significance**|
|---|---|---|
|1850s–1860s|Reaction against Victorian moral earnestness|Shift toward individual sensibility|
|1870s–1890s|Flourishing in Britain, France|Integrated literature, visual arts, interior design|

  

---

**Core Tenets**

| **Principle**   | **Description**                                                 |
| --------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| [[Formalism]]   | Emphasis on form, colour, style over narrative or message       |
| [[Sensualism]]  | Prioritizing sensory perception and aesthetic pleasure          |
| Autonomy of Art | Art exists independently of social, political, or moral agendas |
| [[Exoticism]]   | Fascination with unusual motifs and non‑Western aesthetics      |

**Key Vocabulary**

• **Art for Art’s Sake:** Philosophy that art needs no justification beyond its beauty

• **Formalism:** Focus on structure and composition over content

• **Decadence:** Cultural movement overlapping aestheticism, exploring excess and decline

• **Exoticism:** Adoption of foreign motifs to evoke novelty
