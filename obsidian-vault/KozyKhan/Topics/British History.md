## Outline of British History
### Prehistoric and Ancient Britain

### Prehistoric Britain
- **Early Inhabitants**: The earliest human presence in Britain dates back to around the most famous prehistoric site is Stonehenge, constructed between 3000 and 2000 BC.
- **Bronze Age and Iron Age**: From around 2500 BC, Bronze Age culture emerged, followed by the Iron Age around 800 BC. The Iron Age is marked by the construction of hillforts and the development of tribal societies.

### Roman Britain (43–410 AD)
- **Roman Conquest**: Britain was invaded by the Romans in 43 AD under Emperor Claudius. The province was named Britannia.
- **Roman Rule**: The Romans established towns, roads, and baths, introducing Roman law and culture. Hadrian’s Wall was built in the north to protect against the Picts.
- **Decline**: Roman control weakened in the 4th century due to internal strife and external pressures. The Romans withdrew from Britain in 410 AD.

### Anglo-Saxon Period (410–1066)
- **Migration and Settlement**: After the Romans left, the Anglo-Saxons, composed of Germanic tribes such as the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes, settled in Britain. They established several kingdoms, including Wessex, Mercia, and Northumbria.
- **Christianization**: The conversion to Christianity began in the late 6th century, notably with the mission of St. Augustine of Canterbury in 597 AD.
- **Heptarchy**: The Anglo-Saxon period is often referred to as the Heptarchy due to the seven main kingdoms that existed.

### Viking Invasions (8th–11th centuries)
- **Raids and Settlements**: Viking raids began in the late 8th century, with significant attacks on monasteries like Lindisfarne in 793 AD. Vikings eventually settled in parts of Britain, particularly the Danelaw in the north and east.
- **Alfred the Great**: The King of Wessex who successfully defended his kingdom against Viking invasions and promoted education and legal reform.
- **Unification**: By the early 10th century, the kingdom of England began to emerge under the rule of the West Saxon kings.

### Norman Conquest (1066)
- **Battle of Hastings**: In 1066, William the Conqueror, Duke of Normandy, defeated King Harold II at the Battle of Hastings. This led to the Norman Conquest of England.
- **Feudal System**: The Normans introduced the feudal system, transforming English society and governance. The Domesday Book was compiled in 1086 to record landholdings and resources.

### Plantagenet Dynasty (1154–1485)
- **Angevin Empire**: The Plantagenet dynasty began with Henry II, who controlled vast territories in France and England.
- **Magna Carta**: In 1215, King John signed the Magna Carta, limiting royal power and laying the foundation for parliamentary democracy.
- **Hundred Years’ War**: A series of conflicts between England and France from 1337 to 1453, largely over territorial claims. The war saw major battles like Agincourt and figures like Joan of Arc.

### Wars of the Roses (1455–1487)
- **Dynastic Conflict**: The Wars of the Roses were a series of civil wars fought between the House of Lancaster (red rose) and the House of York (white rose) for control of the English throne.
- **Henry VII**: The conflict ended with the victory of Henry Tudor (Henry VII) at the Battle of Bosworth Field in 1485, marking the start of the Tudor dynasty.

### Tudor Dynasty (1485–1603)
- **Henry VIII**: Known for his six marriages and the break with the Roman Catholic Church, leading to the establishment of the Church of England.
- **Elizabeth I**: The last Tudor monarch, her reign is known as the Elizabethan Era, marked by the defeat of the Spanish Armada in 1588 and a flourishing of English culture and exploration.

### Conclusion
Before the rise of the British Empire, the history of Britain was marked by invasions, cultural transformations, and the gradual unification of various kingdoms. From prehistoric times to the end of the Tudor period, each era laid the foundations for the later emergence of Britain as a global power.

### Early Modern Period

#### Tudor Dynasty (1485-1603)
- **Henry VII**: End of the Wars of the Roses, establishment of the Tudor dynasty.
- **Henry VIII**: Break with the Roman Catholic Church, establishment of the Church of England.
- **Elizabeth I**: Elizabethan Era, defeat of the Spanish Armada (1588).

#### Stuart Dynasty (1603-1714)
- **James I and Charles I**: Conflicts with Parliament, leading to the English Civil War (1642-1651).
- **Oliver Cromwell**: Commonwealth and Protectorate (1649-1660).
- **Restoration**: Monarchy restored under Charles II in 1660.
- **Glorious Revolution (1688)**: Overthrow of James II, establishment of constitutional monarchy under William III and Mary II.

### Modern Period

#### Georgian Era (1714-1837)
- **Hanoverian Succession**: George I becomes king, beginning the Hanoverian dynasty.
- **Industrial Revolution**: Transformation of economy and society through industrialization.

#### Victorian Era (1837-1901)
- **Queen Victoria**: Long reign marked by British imperial expansion.
- **Social and Political Reform**: Movements for workers' rights, women's suffrage, and abolition of slavery.
#### 20th Century
- **World War I (1914-1918)**: Major involvement, significant loss of life.
- **Interwar Period**: Economic challenges, rise of the Labour Party.
- **World War II (1939-1945)**: Key role in Allied victory, leadership of Winston Churchill.
- **Post-War Era**: Welfare state establishment, decolonization, and formation of the United Nations.

#### Late 20th Century to Present
- **Thatcher Era (1979-1990)**: Economic liberalization, decline of industrial sector.
- **European Union**: Joining (1973) and Brexit (2016 referendum, 2020 departure).
- **Contemporary Issues**: Devolution in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland; challenges of multiculturalism and globalization.
### Summary
British history spans from prehistoric settlements to modern times, marked by significant events such as the Roman occupation, Norman Conquest, establishment of a constitutional monarchy, and major global conflicts. The evolution of Britain from a collection of feudal kingdoms to a modern democratic state reflects its complex and influential past.
