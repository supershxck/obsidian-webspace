The **Aztec civilization** (also known as the **Mexica**) was one of the most powerful and influential cultures in Mesoamerica, flourishing from the **14th to the 16th centuries**. Centered in the Valley of Mexico, the Aztecs built a vast empire through military conquest, sophisticated governance, and cultural innovation. Their capital, **Tenochtitlán**, was a marvel of engineering and urban planning, rivaling the largest cities of the world at the time. The Aztec civilization came to an abrupt end with the Spanish conquest in 1521, but its legacy continues to shape Mexican identity and culture.

---

### **Key Aspects of the Aztec Civilization:**

1. **Geography and Capital**:
   - The Aztecs settled in the **Valley of Mexico**, a highland basin surrounded by mountains and volcanoes. Their capital, **Tenochtitlán**, was founded in 1325 on an island in **Lake Texcoco** (modern-day Mexico City).
   - The city was connected to the mainland by causeways and featured canals, aqueducts, and floating gardens (**chinampas**) for agriculture.

2. **History and Timeline**:
   - **Origins**: The Aztecs (or Mexica) were a nomadic tribe from northern Mexico, possibly originating from **Aztlán** (a mythical homeland). They migrated south and settled in the Valley of Mexico.
   - **Rise to Power**: After serving as mercenaries for more powerful city-states, the Aztecs founded Tenochtitlán and formed the **Triple Alliance** with **Texcoco** and **Tlacopan** in 1428. This alliance became the foundation of the Aztec Empire.
   - **Peak**: By the early 16th century, the Aztec Empire controlled much of central and southern Mexico, encompassing over 400 city-states and 5–6 million people.
   - **Fall**: The empire collapsed in 1521 after the Spanish conquistador **Hernán Cortés**, allied with indigenous groups hostile to the Aztecs, defeated the Aztec ruler **Moctezuma II** and sacked Tenochtitlán.

3. **Political and Social Structure**:
   - The Aztec Empire was a **hegemonic state**, allowing conquered regions to retain local rulers in exchange for tribute (goods, crops, and captives).
   - Society was highly stratified:
     - **Nobles** (*pipiltin*): Rulers, priests, and warriors.
     - **Commoners** (*macehualtin*): Farmers, artisans, and merchants.
     - **Slaves** (*tlacotin*): Often prisoners of war or debtors.
   - The emperor (*huey tlatoani*) was both a political and religious leader, believed to be a representative of the gods.

4. **Economy**:
   - The Aztec economy was based on **agriculture**, **tribute**, and **trade**. Key crops included maize, beans, squash, and chili peppers.
   - **Chinampas** (floating gardens) were used to grow food in the swampy lake environment.
   - The Aztecs had a vast trade network, with markets like **Tlatelolco** (in Tenochtitlán) attracting thousands of traders daily. They used **cacao beans** and **quachtli** (cotton cloth) as currency.

5. **Religion and Mythology**:
   - The Aztecs were deeply religious, worshipping a pantheon of gods associated with natural forces, agriculture, and warfare. Key deities included:
     - **Huitzilopochtli** (god of sun and war, patron of Tenochtitlán).
     - **Tlaloc** (god of rain and fertility).
     - **Quetzalcoatl** (feathered serpent, god of wisdom and wind).
   - **Human sacrifice** was central to Aztec religion, believed to nourish the gods and maintain cosmic balance. Captives from wars (*Flowery Wars*) were often sacrificed.
   - The Aztecs believed in a cyclical view of time and the universe, with the world having gone through four previous eras (*suns*) before the current one.

6. **Art and Architecture**:
   - **Tenochtitlán** was a masterpiece of urban planning, with temples, palaces, and plazas laid out in a grid. The **Templo Mayor** (Great Temple) was the religious heart of the city, dedicated to Huitzilopochtli and Tlaloc.
   - Aztec art included **stone sculptures**, **featherwork**, **pottery**, and **codices** (painted books). Their art often depicted gods, warriors, and animals.
   - The Aztecs were skilled engineers, building aqueducts, causeways, and dykes to manage water in their lake environment.

7. **Military and Warfare**:
   - The Aztecs were formidable warriors, expanding their empire through conquest and intimidation. Elite warriors belonged to orders like the **Eagle** and **Jaguar** knights.
   - Warfare was not only for territorial expansion but also to capture prisoners for sacrifice (*Flowery Wars*).

8. **Legacy and Impact**:
   - The Aztec civilization left a profound legacy in Mexico, influencing language (Nahuatl is still spoken today), cuisine (e.g., tamales, chocolate), and cultural traditions.
   - The Spanish conquest destroyed much of Aztec culture, but elements survived through syncretism with Spanish colonial culture.
   - Archaeological sites like the **Templo Mayor** in Mexico City and the **National Museum of Anthropology** preserve and showcase Aztec artifacts and history.

---

### **Decline and Historical Significance**:
The Aztec Empire fell in 1521 due to a combination of Spanish military technology, disease (smallpox), and alliances with indigenous groups resentful of Aztec rule. Despite their downfall, the Aztecs remain a symbol of resilience and cultural richness. Their achievements in governance, urban planning, and art continue to inspire admiration and study, making them one of the most iconic civilizations of the Americas.
