# Chapter: Woodrow Wilson — Architect of Modern American Idealism

## Introduction  
Thomas Woodrow Wilson (1856–1924) served as the 28th President of the United States (1913–1921). A scholar‑turned‑statesman, he reshaped American domestic policy through progressive reforms and redefined global order by championing the League of Nations.

---

## Early Life & Intellectual Formation  
- **Born:** December 28, 1856 in Staunton, Virginia  
- **Education:** Princeton University (BA), Johns Hopkins University (PhD in Political Science) — first U.S. president with a doctorate  
- **Academic Career:** Professor and President of Princeton (1902–1910); developed theories on governance, public administration, and moral leadership

---

## Political Philosophy & Core Principles  
| Dimension | Principle | Application |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Progressivism | Government as agent of social justice | Federal Reserve Act; Clayton Antitrust Act |
| Moral Internationalism | Democracies foster peace | “Make the world safe for democracy” speech |
| Self‑Determination | National sovereignty rights | Fourteen Points framework |

---

## Presidency (1913–1921)  

### Domestic Achievements  
- **Progressive Reforms:** Federal Reserve System (1913), Federal Trade Commission (1914), income tax (16th Amendment)  
- **Labor & Social Policy:** Child labor restrictions, eight‑hour workday for railroad workers  

### Foreign Policy  
- **World War I Leadership:** Declared U.S. entry (1917) to defend democratic ideals  
- **Fourteen Points (1918):** Blueprint for postwar peace emphasizing open diplomacy, free trade, and self‑determination  
- **League of Nations:** Principal architect — U.S. Senate refusal to ratify Treaty of Versailles prevented U.S. membership

---

## Psychological & Philosophical Analysis  
Wilson embodies the “Visionary Idealist” archetype: driven by moral conviction yet constrained by political realities. Psychologically, his progressive zeal coexisted with paternalism and racial segregation policies — illustrating cognitive dissonance between egalitarian ideals and prevailing prejudices.

- **Contested Legacy:** Advocacy for racial segregation undermines progressive record
