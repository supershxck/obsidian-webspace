Here’s an **outline of Chinese philosophy**, which has profoundly shaped China's culture, politics, and worldview over millennia. It primarily revolves around three major traditions: **Confucianism**, **Daoism**, and **[[Buddhism]]**, though other schools also played significant roles.

---

### **1. Early Chinese Thought and Mythological Origins**  
- Rooted in **mythology** and **cosmology**, emphasizing harmony between **Heaven (Tian)**, **Earth**, and **Humanity**.  
- Early texts like the *[[I Ching]] (Book of Changes)* introduced **yin-yang dualism** and the concept of **Qi (vital energy)** as foundational principles of reality.  

---

### **2. Hundred Schools of Thought (770–221 BCE)**  
A vibrant intellectual period during the **Spring and Autumn** and **Warring States Periods**, featuring multiple schools of thought:  

#### **a. Confucianism (Ruism)**  
- **Founder**: **Confucius (Kong Fuzi)** (551–479 BCE).  
- **Core Texts**: *Analects*, *Mencius*, *Book of Rites*.  
- Emphasized **virtue ethics**, the importance of **relationships** (e.g., filial piety, loyalty), and the role of the **Junzi** (ideal gentleman).  
- Focus on creating a harmonious society through **education**, **rituals**, and proper governance.  
- Later developed by thinkers like **Mencius** (human nature is good) and **Xunzi** (human nature is bad but can be improved).  

#### **b. Daoism (Taoism)**  
- **Founders**: **Laozi** (*Tao Te Ching*) and **Zhuangzi*.  
- Emphasis on living in accordance with the **Dao (The Way)**, the ineffable principle underlying all existence.  
- Advocated **wu wei** (non-action or effortless action), simplicity, and harmony with nature.  
- Contrasted with Confucian activism by prioritizing **spiritual freedom** and retreat from worldly concerns.

#### **c. Legalism**  
- **Founders**: **Han Feizi**, **Shang Yang**, and **Li Si**.  
- Advocated for a **strong central authority** and strict laws to maintain order.  
- Viewed human nature as inherently selfish, requiring discipline and harsh punishments.  
- Became the ideological foundation of the **Qin Dynasty**.

#### **d. Mohism**  
- **Founder**: **Mozi** (470–391 BCE).  
- Advocated for **universal love** (*jian ai*) and rejection of warfare.  
- Valued meritocracy, pragmatism, and simple living.  

#### **e. Other Schools**  
- **School of Yin-Yang**: Focused on cosmology and the balance of **yin and yang**.  
- **School of Names (Logicians)**: Early exploration of logic and paradoxes (e.g., Gongsun Long).  

---

### **3. Synthesis of Philosophical Traditions (Han Dynasty, 206 BCE–220 CE)**  
- **Confucianism** became the state ideology, blending with **Legalist political structure** for governance.  
- **Daoism** influenced spiritual practices, cosmology, and Chinese medicine.  
- Emergence of **Dong Zhongshu**’s Confucian synthesis, combining Confucianism with **yin-yang** and **Five Elements** theory.

---

### **4. Introduction and Development of Chinese Buddhism (1st Century CE)**  
- **Origin**: Introduced from India via the Silk Road.  
- Adapted to Chinese culture by integrating Confucian and Daoist ideas.  
- Key schools of Chinese Buddhism:  
  - **Chan (Zen)**: Emphasized meditation and intuition over scripture.  
  - **Pure Land**: Focused on devotion to Amitabha Buddha for rebirth in the Pure Land.  
  - **Tiantai**: Systematized Buddhist teachings, focusing on the *Lotus Sutra*.  
  - **Huayan**: Explored interdependence and the infinite connections of all things.

---

### **5. Neo-Confucianism (Song and Ming Dynasties, 960–1644 CE)**  
- Revival and reinterpretation of Confucianism, incorporating **Buddhist metaphysics** and **Daoist cosmology**.  
- Key figures:  
  - **Zhu Xi**: Synthesized Confucian canon (e.g., *Four Books*) and emphasized the principle of **Li (universal order)**.  
  - **Wang Yangming**: Advocated **innate moral knowledge** (*liangzhi*) and individual introspection.  

---

### **6. Late Imperial and Modern Developments (1644–present)**  
#### **Late Qing Dynasty (1644–1912)**:  
- Confrontation with Western ideologies (e.g., Christianity, democracy, Marxism).  
- Reformists like **Kang Youwei** and **Sun Yat-sen** blended Confucian ideals with modernity.  

#### **Communist Era (1949–present)**:  
- Suppression of traditional philosophies during the **Cultural Revolution**.  
- Rediscovery and reinterpretation of Confucianism, Daoism, and Buddhism in recent decades as **cultural heritage** and sources of moral guidance.

---

### **7. Core Concepts Across Traditions**  
- **Dao (The Way)**: The ultimate principle of existence (central in Daoism but also present in Confucianism and Buddhism).  
- **Qi (Vital Energy)**: The life force that flows through the universe and living beings.  
- **Yin-Yang**: The dynamic interplay of opposites in maintaining balance and harmony.  
- **Wu Wei**: Effortless action or acting in accordance with the natural flow of life.  
- **Ren (Humaneness)**, **Li (Ritual/Propriety)**, **Xiao (Filial Piety)**: Ethical pillars of Confucianism.
