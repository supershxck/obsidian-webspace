**Outline of the Sumerian Language**

  

**1. Introduction to the Sumerian Language**

• **Sumerian** is a **language isolate**, meaning it has no known relatives or linguistic descendants.

• Spoken in **southern Mesopotamia** (modern-day Iraq) by the Sumerians, the creators of one of the world’s earliest civilizations.

• Predates and influenced the **Akkadian language**, making it foundational for Mesopotamian culture.

• Became a **dead language** around 2000 BCE but remained in use as a **liturgical, literary, and scholarly language** for centuries.

  

**2. Historical Development**

• **Early Sumerian (c. 3100–2600 BCE)**:

• Early inscriptions appear during the **Uruk period**, often administrative in nature.

• Written in early pictographs that later evolved into **cuneiform** script.

• **Classical Sumerian (c. 2600–2000 BCE)**:

• The **Golden Age of Sumerian literature**, including hymns, epics, and administrative texts.

• Language of the city-states of Ur, Uruk, and Lagash.

• Widely used for administration and culture even as Akkadian became the spoken language.

• **Post-Sumerian (c. 2000–100 CE)**:

• After the **Akkadian conquest**, Sumerian was no longer spoken but persisted as a written language for religious, legal, and scholarly purposes.

• Last surviving texts written in Sumerian date to the **Parthian period** (c. 1st century CE).

  

**3. Script: Cuneiform**

• Sumerian was the first language written in **cuneiform**.

• Originally a **pictographic** script, it evolved into a syllabic and logographic system by 2600 BCE.

• Required scribal training, making writing an elite skill.

  

**4. Grammar and Structure**

• **Language Type**:

• **Agglutinative**: Words are formed by combining a root with affixes, each adding specific grammatical meaning.

• No evidence of gender in nouns, unlike later Semitic languages.

• **Nouns**:

• Declined for **case** (e.g., subject, object, locative), **number** (singular, plural), and **possessive** (e.g., “my house”).

• Cases include nominative, genitive, dative, locative, and ablative.

• **Verbs**:

• Complex verbal system with **prefixes** and **suffixes** encoding subject, object, tense, mood, and other grammatical information.

• Often incorporate both grammatical and semantic information (e.g., who is doing the action, to whom, and where).

• **Syntax**:

• Typically **subject-object-verb (SOV)** word order.

• Flexible structure, heavily reliant on grammatical markers.

  

**5. Vocabulary and Lexicon**

• Vocabulary reflects the **agricultural and urban society** of Sumer:

• Words for irrigation, farming, trade, and governance.

• Extensive terminology for religious and mythological concepts, reflecting a **polytheistic worldview**.

• Borrowed heavily from **Akkadian** as the two languages coexisted for centuries, with many Akkadian loanwords appearing in later Sumerian texts.

  

**6. Literary and Scholarly Use**

• **Religious Texts**:

• Used in hymns, prayers, and myths, such as the stories of **Inanna** and the **creation of humanity**.

• **Epic Literature**:

• Early versions of the _Epic of Gilgamesh_ were written in Sumerian.

• Other famous works include the **Lamentation for Ur** and the **Instructions of Shuruppak**.

• **Administrative Texts**:

• Extensively used for legal and economic documents, including tax records and trade agreements.

• **Scholarly Context**:

• Retained as a language of scholarship and ritual even after it ceased to be spoken.

  

**7. Relationship with Akkadian**

• **Language Shift**:

• By the 3rd millennium BCE, Akkadian (a Semitic language) replaced Sumerian as the spoken language.

• Sumerian influenced Akkadian grammar, vocabulary, and writing.

• **Bilingualism**:

• Scribes were often bilingual, producing **Sumerian-Akkadian bilingual texts** for study and preservation.

• The **lexical lists** created to translate Sumerian into Akkadian became the world’s first dictionaries.

  

**8. Decline and Legacy**

• By **2000 BCE**, Sumerian ceased to be a spoken language.

• Persisted as a **liturgical and scholarly language** in Mesopotamian schools for over 1,500 years.

• Its influence on Akkadian, Babylonian, and Assyrian languages ensured its ideas and culture survived.

• Rediscovered in the 19th century through archaeological excavations, particularly in **Uruk** and **Ur**.

  

**9. Legacy in Modern Study**

• Sumerian is key to understanding Mesopotamian history and culture.

• Modern linguists study Sumerian through thousands of surviving **cuneiform tablets**.

• Its innovations in writing and literature are considered foundational to human civilization.
