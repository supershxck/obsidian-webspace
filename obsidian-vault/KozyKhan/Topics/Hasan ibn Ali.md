**Hasan ibn Ali** (Arabic: الحسن بن علي, al-Ḥasan ibn ʿAlī) (c. 625–670 CE) was a pivotal figure in Islamic history, revered as the eldest son of Ali ibn Abi Talib (the fourth Rashidun caliph and first Shia Imam) and Fatima, daughter of the Prophet Muhammad. As the grandson of Muhammad, Hasan is a central figure in both Sunni and Shia traditions, known for his piety, leadership, and role in early Muslim politics. His life bridged the Prophet’s era with the tumultuous succession disputes that shaped Islam’s sectarian divide.

### Early Life
- **Birth:** Born in Medina around 625 CE (3 AH), likely in the month of [[Ramadan]], though exact dates vary in sources.
- **Family:** His mother, Fatima, was Muhammad’s only surviving child from his first wife, Khadija. His father, Ali, was Muhammad’s cousin and son-in-law, raised in the Prophet’s household. Hasan’s younger brother was Husayn ibn Ali, another key figure in Islamic history.
- **Upbringing:** Hasan and Husayn were doted on by Muhammad, who reportedly said, “Hasan and Husayn are the leaders of the youth of Paradise” (a hadith widely accepted in Sunni and Shia traditions). He grew up during the Prophet’s final years, witnessing the early Muslim community’s expansion.

### Role in the [[Rashidun Caliphate]]
After Muhammad’s death in 632, Hasan lived through the reigns of the first four caliphs:
- **Under Ali (656–661):** When Ali became caliph, Hasan supported his father during the [[First Fitna]] (civil war), including the Battle of Siffin (657) against Muawiya ibn Abi Sufyan, governor of Syria. Ali’s assassination in 661 thrust Hasan into leadership.

### Caliphate and Abdication
- **Succession:** After Ali’s death, Hasan was acclaimed caliph in Kufa (Iraq) in 661 by Ali’s followers, marking him as the fifth Rashidun caliph in Sunni tradition and the second Imam in Shia belief. His rule lasted about six months, a period known as the “Year of Unity” (Jamāʿa).
- **Conflict with Muawiya:** Muawiya, a powerful Umayyad leader, challenged Hasan’s authority, amassing an army. Facing internal dissent and a war-weary populace, Hasan negotiated peace rather than fight.
- **Treaty of 661:** Hasan abdicated to Muawiya, ending the First Fitna. The treaty stipulated that Muawiya would rule without naming a successor during his lifetime (a condition later broken), and Hasan would retire to Medina with a pension. Shia sources claim he acted to preserve Muslim unity, while some Sunni narratives praise his pragmatism.

### Later Life and Death
- **Retirement:** Hasan lived quietly in Medina, focusing on family and religious devotion. He married multiple times (accounts vary widely on the number of wives) and had numerous children, founding a lineage that includes many of Islam’s sharifs (descendants of Muhammad).
- **Death:** He died in 670 CE (50 AH) at around age 45, reportedly poisoned by his wife Ja’da bint al-Ash’ath at Muawiya’s instigation, according to Shia tradition. Sunni accounts are less definitive, often attributing his death to illness. He was buried in Medina’s Baqi Cemetery.

### Significance
- **Sunni View:** Hasan is one of the Rightly Guided Caliphs, admired for his wisdom and peacemaking. His abdication is seen as a noble sacrifice for stability.
- **Shia View:** As the second Imam, Hasan is a martyr and infallible leader. His treaty with Muawiya is interpreted as a tactical retreat, preserving the Imamate’s spiritual legacy for his brother Husayn.
- **Legacy:** His descendants, the Hasanids, include dynasties like Morocco’s Alaouites and Idrisids, as well as countless sharifs across the Muslim world. His life symbolizes the tension between spiritual authority and political power.

### Character
Hasan was known for his gentle demeanor, generosity, and resemblance to Muhammad in appearance and character. Stories depict him as a mediator, avoiding bloodshed where possible, contrasting with Husayn’s later martyrdom at Karbala.
