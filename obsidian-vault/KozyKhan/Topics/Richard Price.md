**Richard Price: The Moral Philosopher Who Championed Liberty and Reason**

  

**1. Introduction: A Luminary of the Enlightenment**

  

**Richard Price** (1723–1791) was a Welsh moral philosopher, Nonconformist minister, and mathematician whose intellectual pursuits and political activism significantly influenced the course of the 18th century.  His work traversed the realms of ethics, economics, and political theory, positioning him as a pivotal figure in the Enlightenment era.

  

**2. Early Life and Intellectual Formation**

  

Born on February 23, 1723, in Tynton, Glamorgan, Wales, Price was raised in a devout Nonconformist family.  His education emphasized both religious studies and the burgeoning scientific knowledge of the time, laying the foundation for his interdisciplinary approach to philosophy and mathematics.

  

**3. Contributions to Moral Philosophy**

  

Price’s seminal work, _A Review of the Principal Questions in Morals_ (1758), critiques and builds upon contemporary ethical theories. He argued for the existence of an innate moral sense, asserting that individuals possess an inherent ability to discern right from wrong through reason alone. This rationalist perspective contributed to the broader Enlightenment discourse on human nature and morality.

  

**4. Advocacy for Political Reform and Support for Revolutions**

  

A fervent proponent of civil liberties, Price actively supported both the American and French Revolutions. His 1776 pamphlet, _Observations on Civil Liberty and the Justice and Policy of the War with America_, defended the American colonies’ quest for independence, emphasizing the principles of self-governance and natural rights.

  

**5. Contributions to Economics and Actuarial Science**

  

Beyond philosophy and politics, Price made significant strides in economics and actuarial science. His expertise in finance led him to advise the British government on matters of national debt and public credit. Notably, his work on life expectancy and probability laid the groundwork for modern actuarial practices, influencing the development of insurance and pension systems.

  

**6. Legacy and Influence**

  

Price’s interdisciplinary approach and commitment to reason and liberty left an indelible mark on Enlightenment thought. His ideas resonated with contemporaries and successors alike, fostering dialogues that bridged moral philosophy, political theory, and economic science.

  

**7. Vocabulary and Key Concepts**

• **Nonconformist Minister**: A clergy member who does not conform to the established church’s practices, particularly within the context of 18th-century England.

• **Moral Sense Theory**: The philosophical concept that humans have an innate ability to perceive moral truths.

• **Actuarial Science**: A discipline that applies mathematical and statistical methods to assess risk in insurance, finance, and other industries.

• **Civil Liberties**: Fundamental individual rights protected by law, such as freedom of speech and religion.

  

**8. Conclusion**

  

Richard Price epitomized the Enlightenment’s spirit of inquiry and advocacy for human rights. His multifaceted contributions continue to be recognized as foundational in various fields, underscoring the enduring relevance of his work in contemporary discussions on ethics, governance, and economic policy.
