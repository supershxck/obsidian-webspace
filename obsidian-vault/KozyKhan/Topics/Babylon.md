### Early History

#### Mesopotamian Beginnings
- **Origins**: The Babylonian Empire was located in [[Mesopotamia]], an ancient region between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, often referred to as the cradle of civilization.
- **Early Inhabitants**: Before the rise of Babylon, the region was dominated by the [[Sumerians]] and the [[Akkadian Empire]], who developed early forms of writing, complex societies, and significant architectural achievements.

### First Babylonian Empire (c. 1894–1595 BC)

#### Foundation and Rise
- **Amorite Dynasty**: The city of Babylon rose to prominence under the Amorite dynasty, which began around 1894 BC.
- **Hammurabi**: The most famous ruler of this period was [[King Hammurabi]] (reigned c. 1792–1750 BC). He is best known for creating the [[Code of Hammurabi]], one of the earliest and most complete written legal codes.

#### Expansion and Prosperity
- **Conquests**: Under Hammurabi, Babylon expanded its territory, conquering neighboring city-states such as Larsa, Eshnunna, and Mari, and uniting much of southern Mesopotamia.
- **Cultural Achievements**: The period saw significant advances in law, literature, and science, with Babylon becoming a major cultural and economic center.

### Decline and Kassite Dynasty (c. 1595–1155 BC)

#### Hittite Invasion
- **Fall of Babylon**: Around 1595 BC, Babylon was sacked by the Hittites, leading to a period of decline.
- **Kassite Rule**: The [[Kassite Dynasty]] took over Babylon around 1531 BC and ruled for nearly four centuries. They stabilized the region and maintained Babylon’s cultural heritage.

#### Achievements and Stability
- **Cultural Continuity**: Despite foreign rule, Babylon remained a center of learning and culture, with continued development in astronomy, mathematics, and literature.
- **Economic Prosperity**: The Kassite period saw economic growth, with Babylon engaging in extensive trade.

### Neo-Babylonian Empire (626–539 BC)

#### Revival and Expansion
- **Nabopolassar**: The [[Neo-Babylonian Empire]] began with Nabopolassar (ruled 626–605 BC), who successfully revolted against the Assyrian Empire and established Babylonian independence.
- **Nebuchadnezzar II**: His son, [[Nebuchadnezzar II]] (ruled 605–562 BC), expanded the empire further, conquering Jerusalem in 587 BC and leading the Babylonian Captivity of the Jews.

#### Cultural Flourishing
- **Architectural Achievements**: Nebuchadnezzar II is known for his extensive building projects, including the Hanging Gardens of Babylon, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, and the Ishtar Gate.
- **Cultural Renaissance**: The period saw a resurgence in Babylonian art, literature, and science, with significant advancements in astronomy and mathematics.

### Fall of Babylon

#### Persian Conquest
- **Cyrus the Great**: In 539 BC, Babylon fell to the [[Persian Empire]] under [[Cyrus the Great]]. The city surrendered without a fight, and Cyrus declared himself the liberator of Babylon.
- **Integration into the Persian Empire**: Babylon became an important administrative center within the Persian Empire, retaining its cultural significance.

#### Legacy
- **Cultural Impact**: The legacy of the Babylonian Empire is profound, influencing subsequent civilizations in areas such as law, literature, architecture, and science.
- **Historical Significance**: Babylon's contributions to human history, particularly during the reigns of Hammurabi and Nebuchadnezzar II, continue to be studied and admired for their impact on the development of civilization.

### Conclusion
The history of the Babylonian Empire is marked by periods of significant cultural and scientific achievement, as well as periods of conquest and decline. From the establishment of one of the earliest legal codes to the grandeur of the Neo-Babylonian period, Babylon played a crucial role in shaping the ancient world.
