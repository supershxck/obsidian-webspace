The **Avesta** is the central collection of sacred texts in **[[Zoroastrianism]]**, one of the world’s oldest continuously practiced religions. Zoroastrianism was founded by the Prophet **Zoroaster** (also known as Zarathustra) in ancient Persia, roughly between the 18th and 6th centuries BCE. The Avesta serves as the foundation for Zoroastrian theology, liturgy, and moral philosophy.

  

**Structure of the Avesta**

  

The Avesta is composed of several distinct sections, each serving different religious functions:

1. **Yasna**: This is the primary liturgical text, which includes the **Gathas**—hymns believed to be composed by Zoroaster himself. The Yasna is used in ceremonial worship and includes rituals, prayers, and hymns dedicated to various deities.

2. **Visperad**: An extension of the Yasna, the Visperad contains additional invocations and praises for use in more elaborate religious ceremonies. It elaborates on the themes found in the Yasna and is often recited during major Zoroastrian festivals.

3. **Vendidad** (also known as the **Videvdat**): This text focuses on purity laws, rituals, and the moral code expected of Zoroastrians. It includes stories, mythological narratives, and guidelines for maintaining spiritual and physical cleanliness.

4. **Yashts**: A collection of hymns dedicated to various deities, each Yasht is devoted to a specific divine figure or concept, such as Mithra, Anahita, or the divine aspects of nature.

5. **Khordeh Avesta**: Meaning “Small Avesta,” this section comprises shorter prayers and liturgical texts intended for daily use by laypeople. It serves as a practical guide for personal devotion and routine worship.

  

**Language and Composition**

  

The Avesta was originally composed in the **Avestan language**, an ancient Iranian language closely related to Old Persian. Due to historical invasions, particularly by Islamic conquerors in the 7th century CE, much of the Avesta was lost or destroyed. The surviving texts represent only a fraction of the original corpus, preserved primarily by Zoroastrian communities in regions like Iran and India (where they are known as Parsis).

  

**Historical Context and Preservation**

  

Zoroastrianism was the dominant religion of the Persian Empire before the advent of Islam. The Avesta played a crucial role in shaping the religious and cultural identity of the Persian people. Following the Islamic conquest, Zoroastrianism faced significant persecution, leading to the migration of many adherents to the Indian subcontinent. This diaspora community worked diligently to preserve their sacred texts, including the Avesta, ensuring their transmission to future generations.

  

**Theological Themes**

  

The Avesta encapsulates key Zoroastrian beliefs, including:

• **Monotheism**: Worship of **Ahura Mazda**, the supreme god representing truth and order.

• **Dualism**: The ongoing struggle between **Ahura Mazda** (good) and **Angra Mainyu** (destructive spirit representing evil).

• **Moral Responsibility**: Emphasis on individual choice, encouraging followers to live righteously, promote good thoughts, words, and deeds, and oppose evil.

• **Afterlife and Judgment**: Belief in a final judgment where souls are evaluated based on their earthly actions, leading to reward or punishment.

  

**Influence and Legacy**

  

The Avesta has had a profound impact on religious thought, particularly in shaping ethical frameworks that emphasize individual responsibility and the moral dualism between good and evil. Its influence can be seen in later religious traditions and philosophical systems.

  

Despite the challenges in preserving the Avesta, ongoing scholarly efforts continue to study and translate its texts, making its rich theological and philosophical content accessible to a broader audience. Zoroastrian communities around the world remain dedicated to maintaining their religious heritage, with the Avesta at the heart of their spiritual life.

  

**Modern Relevance**

  

Today, Zoroastrianism is a minority religion, with communities primarily in India and Iran, and diaspora populations worldwide. The Avesta remains central to religious ceremonies, rituals, and the preservation of Zoroastrian identity. Contemporary scholars and practitioners alike continue to explore its teachings, ensuring that the wisdom of Zoroaster remains relevant in the modern world.
