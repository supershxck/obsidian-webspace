The **Celtic tribes** of Iron Age Britain (roughly **800 BC to AD 43**) represent a fascinating and dynamic era of history, characterized by tribal societies, distinct artistic styles, and a rich cultural heritage. These tribes, often referred to as **Britons**, were part of the wider Celtic cultural sphere that stretched across much of Europe. Here’s an overview of their society, culture, and legacy.

  

**Origins and Identity**

  

The Britons were descended from the Indo-European-speaking **Celtic peoples** who spread into Britain during the late Bronze Age and early Iron Age. They were not a single unified people but instead organized into **tribes**, each with its own territory, leadership, and cultural identity. These tribes shared common linguistic, artistic, and religious traits with other Celtic groups in Europe, particularly in **Gaul (modern France)** and **Ireland**.

  

**Key Characteristics of Celtic Tribes in Britain**

  

**1. Tribal Organization**

• Celtic Britain was divided into numerous tribes, each controlling a distinct territory.

• Prominent tribes included:

• **[[Iceni]]**: In modern Norfolk, famous for their Queen **Boudica**, who led a revolt against the Romans.

• **[[Brigantes]]**: The largest tribe, dominating northern England.

• **[[Catuvellauni]]**: A powerful tribe in the southeast, known for resisting Roman incursions.

• **[[Cornovii]]**: In the West Midlands, their territory included hillforts like The Wrekin.

• **[[Silures]]**: In south Wales, known for their fierce resistance to Roman conquest.

• **[[Dumnonii]]**: Occupying Devon and Cornwall, with strong ties to the tin trade.

• Each tribe was governed by a **chieftain** or **king**, with a warrior aristocracy that held significant power.

  

**2. Economy and Trade**

• The economy was based on **agriculture**, with wheat, barley, and livestock as staples.

• Tribes engaged in local and long-distance trade, exchanging goods like **tin**, **iron**, **salt**, and **amber**.

• They used a form of early **coinage**, inspired by Greek and Roman designs, as a sign of their growing sophistication.

  

**3. Hillforts**

• Hillforts were a hallmark of Celtic society, serving as **defensive strongholds**, **political centers**, and **symbols of tribal power**.

• Notable examples include:

• **Maiden Castle** (Dorset): One of the largest hillforts in Europe.

• **Danebury** (Hampshire): A well-excavated example of a Celtic tribal center.

• These forts were heavily fortified with ditches, ramparts, and wooden palisades.

  

**4. Art and Culture**

• Celtic art is renowned for its **intricate designs**, including spirals, knots, and animal motifs.

• The **La Tène style**, originating in central Europe, heavily influenced Celtic art in Britain, as seen in objects like the **Snettisham torcs** (gold neck rings) and decorative weapons.

• Music, storytelling, and oral traditions were central to their culture, preserved by **bards** and **druids**.

  

**5. Religion and Druids**

• The Celts were **polytheistic**, worshipping a pantheon of gods and goddesses linked to nature, fertility, and war.

• Sacred sites included rivers, forests, and hilltops, and ritual offerings were often made in these places.

• **Druids**, a powerful priestly class, acted as religious leaders, judges, and keepers of knowledge. They held significant influence in society and were key to the resistance against Roman conquest.

  

**Interaction with Rome**

  

When the Romans invaded Britain in **AD 43**, the Celtic tribes responded in various ways:

• Some tribes, like the **Catuvellauni**, fiercely resisted Roman expansion, leading to significant battles.

• Others, like the **Iceni**, initially allied with Rome but later revolted, most famously under **Boudica’s leadership**.

• The Romans gradually subdued the tribes, incorporating their territories into the province of **Britannia** and transforming tribal societies through urbanization and Roman governance.

  

**Legacy**

  

The Celtic tribes left an enduring legacy in Britain:

• Many tribal territories formed the basis of later **regional identities** and kingdoms in the post-Roman period.

• Celtic languages, such as **Welsh**, **Cornish**, and **Gaelic**, are direct descendants of the languages spoken by the Britons.

• The rich artistic and cultural traditions of the Celts continue to inspire modern art, literature, and folklore.
