**[[the Holy Trinity]]**
The [[EOC]] believes in [[the Holy Trinity]] as described in [[Nicene Creed]] at [[the First Council of Nicaea]]:
- God [[the Father]],
- God [[the Son]] ([[Jesus Christ]]),
- and God [[the Holy Spirit]].
God is both transcendent (existing beyond the material world) 
and immanent (actively present and involved within the world).

[[Jesus Christ]] is both fully God and fully human in one person. This is the [[hypostatic union]]. [[The Incarnation]] refers to [[God the Son]] taking on human nature in the person of Jesus Christ. This was for the purpose of [[Salvation]]. Unlike the [[Protestant]] concept of [[Penal Substitutionary Atonement]], the [[EOC]] sees salvation as less of a transaction satisfying a legal requirement of [[God's wrath]] and more about healing the broken relationship between God and humanity. 

[[Theosis]] 
A central concept is [[Theosis]], or [[deification]]. This is the process of humans becoming more like God, participating in His divine nature through transformation and communion with Him. [[Salvation]] isn't simply a passive reception, but involves active participation in the life of the Church, the [[Sacraments]], [[repentance]], and [[Good works]].

**[[the Church]]**
The [[EOC]] is the one true Church founded by Jesus Christ and passed down through the Apostles. It is the [[One Holy Catholic and Apostolic Church]]. Through honoring both [[Holy Scripture]] and the [[Apostolic Tradition]], the true faith and traditions of Christianity have been preserved through the continuous and [[unbroken lineage of Bishops]] from [[the Apostles]] to the present day Church. There are a number of [[Autocephalous]] or self-governing churches with some diversity in practices, but these core beliefs and liturgical practices are fundamental across them, reflecting the unity and [[Catholicity]] of the faith. [[Worship]] in the EOC is highly liturgical and follows a structured format. [[The Divine Liturgy]], particularly that of [[St. John Chrysostom]], is central to the communal worship where the [[Sacraments]] may be partaken in. Personal prayer is encouraged through a [[prayer rule]], with a strong emphasis on [[Jesus Prayer]]: "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner." The [[Communion of the Saints]] is clear in Orthodoxy. Intercession to [[Our Lady]] and the [[Saints]] are taught and encouraged as role models and aids in prayer. [[Holy Icons]], [[Relics]] and even [[the Lifegiving Cross]] are venerated, not worshipped, and are seen as windows into the heavenly realm, visual sermons, etc. [[Monasticism]] plays a significant role with monastic communities serving as centers of spiritual life, prayer, and theological study. See [[Desert Fathers]] 

The EOC has a rich tradition of fasting and feasting, observing several fasting periods (such as [[Great Lent]]) and numerous feast days celebrating events in the life of Christ, the Theotokos, and the saints.

**[[Sacraments]]**
The [[EOC]] recognizes seven major sacraments:
- [[Baptism]]
- [[Holy Chrism]] (Confirmation)
- [[Eucharist]]
- [[Confession]]
- [[Holy Unction (Anointing of the Sick)]]
- [[Holy Orders]]
- [[Marriage]]
These are seen as [[Sacred Mysteries]] through which God works in the world.

**[[The Ecumenical Councils]]**
The EOC places a strong emphasis on the decisions of [[the Seven Ecumenical Councils]], which it views as guided by [[the Holy Spirit]], and considers the teachings and writings of the [[Church Fathers]] as authoritative for understanding and living out the Orthodox faith. Through this unbroken historical and spiritual lineage, the EOC believes it faithfully preserves the original teachings and sacramental life instituted by Christ and His Apostles.

**[[Eschatology]]**
The [[EOC]] has a rich view of eschatology (beliefs about the end times) including belief in the Second Coming of Christ, bodily resurrection of the dead, and [[the Final Judgement]].
