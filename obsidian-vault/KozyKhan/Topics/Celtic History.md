### Origins and Early Migration
#### Indo-European Roots
- **Proto-Indo-Europeans**: The Celts are believed to have originated from the Proto-Indo-European peoples who lived on the steppes north of the Black Sea around 3000 BCE.
- **Early Migration**: Around 2000 BCE, these groups began migrating westward into Europe.
### Hallstatt Culture (c. 1200-500 BCE)
#### Early Celtic Society
- **Central Europe**: [[The Hallstatt culture]], centered in modern-day [[Austria]], [[Germany]], [[Switzerland]], and Eastern [[France]], is considered the early Iron Age Celtic culture.
- **Economic Activities**: The Hallstatt Celts engaged in farming, metalworking, and trade, with a strong warrior aristocracy.
- **Artifacts**: Rich grave goods and elaborate metalwork indicate a complex and wealthy society.
### La Tène Culture (c. 450-1 BCE)
#### Expansion and Flourishing
- **Spread of Culture**: [[The La Tène culture]] represents the height of Celtic civilization, spreading across much of [[Europe]], including present-day [[France]], [[Britain]], [Ireland], [[Spain]], and Eastern Europe.
- **Art and Craftsmanship**: Renowned for intricate metalwork, pottery, and sculpture, reflecting a vibrant and artistic culture.
- **Society**: Tribal organization with powerful chieftains, [[the druids]] (priestly class), and warriors.
### Geographic Spread
#### Western Europe
- **[[Gaul]] (France and Belgium)**: The Celts, known as Gauls, dominated this region until the Roman conquest.
- **Britain and Ireland**: Distinctive Celtic cultures developed, known as the Insular Celts, including the Britons in Britain and the Gaels in Ireland.
#### Central and Eastern Europe
- **Central Europe**: Celtic tribes settled in regions such as [[Bohemia]], [[Hungary]], and the Balkans.
- **Eastern Expansion**: Some Celtic groups, like the Galatians, migrated as far east as [[Anatolia]] (modern [[Turkey]]).
### Interaction with Other Cultures
#### Conflict and Assimilation
- **Greek and Roman Encounters**: The Celts had both hostile and trade interactions with Greek and Roman civilizations.
- **[[Roman Conquest]]**: The Romans gradually conquered Celtic territories, integrating them into the Roman Empire and leading to the Romanization of many Celtic peoples.
### Decline and Legacy
#### Roman Influence
- **Romanization**: Under Roman rule, many aspects of Celtic culture, language, and religion were assimilated or suppressed.
- **Celtic Resistance**: Notable uprisings, such as those led by Vercingetorix in Gaul and Boudica in Britain, exemplified resistance to Roman rule.
#### Survival and Revival
- **Insular Celts**: In [[Ireland]], [[Scotland]], [[Wales]], and Brittany, Celtic languages and traditions survived more robustly, continuing to influence local cultures.
- **Modern Revival**: The 19th and 20th centuries saw a revival of interest in Celtic heritage, language, and culture, contributing to contemporary national identities in regions like Ireland and Scotland.
### Summary
The Celts were an Indo-European people who migrated into Europe around 2000 BCE, developing distinctive Hallstatt and La Tène cultures. They spread across much of Western and Central Europe, with significant presences in Gaul, Britain, and Ireland. Their rich cultural heritage, characterized by unique art, tribal society, and warrior ethos, left a lasting impact despite Roman conquest and assimilation. The legacy of the Celts continues in modern times, particularly in the Celtic nations of Ireland, Scotland, Wales, and Brittany.
