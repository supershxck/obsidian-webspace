### History of the Russian Orthodox Church

The history of the [[Russian Orthodox Church]] is deeply intertwined with the history of Russia itself, reflecting its spiritual, cultural, and political development over more than a millennium. Below is a detailed account of the Church's history, from its origins to the present day.

#### **Origins and Early History (9th–13th centuries)**
- **Kievan Rus and the Baptism of Rus'**:
  - The roots of the Russian Orthodox Church are traced back to the Christianization of Kievan Rus', a medieval Slavic state, in 988 AD. [[Prince Vladimir the Great]] of Kiev converted to Christianity and was baptized in [[Chersonesus]], in Crimea. He then made Christianity the state religion, leading to the mass baptism of the population in the Dnieper River.
  - The Church in Rus' initially fell under the jurisdiction of the [[Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople]], which appointed the first [[Metropolitan of Kiev]], Michael I, in 988.

- **Development of the Church in Kievan Rus**:
  - The Church played a central role in the cultural and political life of Kievan Rus, with the construction of the first cathedrals, such as the [[Saint Sophia Cathedral in Kiev]], and the establishment of monasticism, most notably the [[Kiev Pechersk Lavra]].
  - The 12th century saw the fragmentation of Kievan Rus into smaller principalities, weakening the centralized authority of the Church, though it remained a unifying spiritual force.

#### **The Mongol Yoke and the Rise of Moscow (13th–15th centuries)**
- **Impact of the Mongol Invasion**:
  - The Mongol invasion of Kievan Rus in the 13th century led to the devastation of many cities, including Kiev. The Church, however, survived and began to shift its center of gravity to the northeast, particularly to the city of [[Vladimir]] and later to [[Moscow]].
  - The Mongol rule (known as the "Mongol Yoke") allowed the Church a degree of autonomy, and the Church became an important spiritual and political institution, collecting tribute and acting as intermediaries between the Mongols and the Russian people.

- **Rise of the Metropolitanate of Moscow**:
  - By the late 14th century, Moscow had emerged as the most powerful principality in Rus'. In 1325, the Metropolitanate of Kiev and All Rus' moved its seat from Kiev to Moscow, establishing the city as the new spiritual center of the Russian lands.
  - In 1448, the Russian Church declared its independence from Constantinople, electing its own Metropolitan, [[Jonah of Moscow]], marking the beginning of the autocephalous Russian Orthodox Church.

#### **The Golden Age of the Russian Orthodox Church (15th–17th centuries)**
- **Formation of the Patriarchate of Moscow**:
  - In 1589, under the reign of [[Tsar Feodor I]] and the guidance of [[Boris Godunov]], the Metropolitanate of Moscow was elevated to the status of a Patriarchate. [[Job of Moscow]] became the first [[Patriarch of Moscow and All Russia]], establishing the Russian Orthodox Church as one of the most powerful autocephalous churches in the Orthodox world.
  - The elevation of the Moscow Patriarchate coincided with the final consolidation of Russian lands under Moscow's rule, further intertwining the Church with the state.

- **The Time of Troubles and the Romanov Dynasty**:
  - The early 17th century was marked by the [[Time of Troubles]], a period of political instability and foreign intervention. The Church played a key role in rallying the Russian people against invaders, and the election of [[Michael Romanov]] as Tsar in 1613 was supported by the Church, beginning the Romanov dynasty that would rule Russia until 1917.
  - The 17th century also saw internal challenges, most notably the [[Raskol]], or Schism, within the Church. In 1666, reforms initiated by Patriarch [[Nikon]] aimed at aligning Russian liturgical practices with those of the Greek Church led to a split, with the [[Old Believers]] rejecting the reforms and forming a separate religious movement.

#### **The Synodal Period (18th–19th centuries)**
- **Peter the Great's Reforms**:
  - The 18th century began with the reign of [[Peter the Great]], who sought to modernize Russia along Western lines. In 1721, Peter abolished the Patriarchate and replaced it with a [[Holy Synod]], a body of bishops under the control of a government-appointed [[Ober-Procurator]]. This marked the beginning of the Synodal Period, during which the Church was closely controlled by the state.
  - Despite this, the Church remained an integral part of Russian society, continuing its missionary work, particularly in Siberia and Alaska, and maintaining a strong influence over education and culture.

- **19th Century Challenges**:
  - The 19th century saw the Russian Church grappling with modernity and growing calls for reform. The Church played a central role in promoting Russian nationalism and supporting the [[Tsarist regime]], but it also faced internal challenges, such as the rise of the [[Old Believers]] and other dissident movements.
  - The latter half of the century witnessed the Church's involvement in the [[Pan-Slavism]] movement and its support for Orthodox Christians under Ottoman rule in the Balkans, contributing to the outbreak of the [[Russo-Turkish War]] (1877–1878).

#### **The Russian Revolution and Soviet Period (1917–1991)**
- **The Russian Revolution and Persecution**:
  - The [[Russian Revolution]] of 1917 brought an end to the Tsarist regime and ushered in a period of intense persecution of the Church by the new Bolshevik government. [[Patriarch Tikhon]], who was elected in 1917 as the first Patriarch since the abolition of the Patriarchate by Peter the Great, condemned the Bolsheviks' actions but was imprisoned and placed under house arrest.
  - The Church faced severe repression during the Soviet period, with thousands of clergy and laypeople executed or sent to labor camps, churches destroyed or repurposed, and religious practices suppressed. However, some members of the clergy and laity continued to practice their faith in secret.

- **The Church During World War II**:
  - The [[Great Patriotic War]] (World War II) saw a temporary relaxation of persecution as the Soviet government, under [[Joseph Stalin]], sought to mobilize the population against the German invasion. The Church was allowed to reopen some churches and monasteries, and [[Patriarch Sergius]] was elected in 1943, restoring the Patriarchate after a 200-year hiatus.
  - After the war, the Church continued to face restrictions, though it remained a symbol of national identity and resistance to foreign influence.

#### **Post-Soviet Revival (1991–Present)**
- **Revival and Challenges**:
  - The collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 led to a dramatic revival of the Russian Orthodox Church. Under the leadership of [[Patriarch Alexy II]] (1990–2008), the Church regained much of its former influence, with thousands of churches and monasteries reopened, and religious education and practices restored.
  - The Church has since played a central role in the spiritual and cultural life of Russia, often aligning itself with the state under the leadership of [[Vladimir Putin]].
  
- **Contemporary Issues**:
  - The Russian Orthodox Church today faces numerous challenges, including its relationship with the state, its stance on modern social issues, and its role in global Orthodoxy, particularly its strained relations with the [[Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople]] over issues like the autocephaly of the [[Orthodox Church of Ukraine]].
  - The current Patriarch, [[Kirill of Moscow]], has continued to assert the Church's influence both domestically and internationally, while also navigating the complexities of a rapidly changing world.

#### **Legacy and Impact**
The Russian Orthodox Church has had a profound impact on Russian history and culture, shaping the nation's identity and playing a key role in its political and social development. Despite periods of intense persecution and repression, the Church has remained a resilient institution, continuing to guide and inspire millions of believers both in Russia and around the world.
