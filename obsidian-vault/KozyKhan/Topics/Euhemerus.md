**Who is Euhemerus?**
Euhemerus was an ancient Greek mythographer who lived around the late 4th to early 3rd century BCE. He is most famous for his work known as "Sacred History" (or "Sacred Inscription"), though the original text has not survived and is known only through excerpts and references by later authors.

Euhemerus proposed a rationalistic theory of mythology that suggested that gods were originally just ordinary human beings who were posthumously deified through the telling and retelling of their deeds. According to Euhemerus, myths about gods are merely exaggerated accounts of historical human heroes, rulers, or kings who were honored and worshipped after their deaths due to their contributions or achievements during their lifetimes.

This approach, known as "Euhemerism," had a significant impact on the interpretation of mythology. It provided a way to understand myths not as literal accounts of supernatural events, but as exaggerated stories about real historical figures. His ideas influenced both ancient writers and later scholars during the Renaissance who sought more rational explanations for the mythologies of the past.

Despite the enduring influence of his ideas, the details of Euhemerus's own life remain sparse, and much of what we know about him comes from later sources, including the writings of the early Christian apologist Lactantius and the historian Diodorus Siculus.

Euhemerus's ideas were influential among early Christian writers, who often utilized his theory to argue against the divinity of pagan gods, portraying them instead as deified human beings. This approach helped early Christians rationalize the dismissal of pagan religions while promoting Christianity as a faith worshipping a true divine entity. Several early Church Fathers and Christian apologists referenced Euhemerus or were influenced by his ideas, including:
**Lactantius**: A Christian apologist in the early 4th century, Lactantius used Euhemerism extensively in his writings to critique pagan beliefs. In his work "Divinae Institutiones" (Divine Institutes), Lactantius argues that the gods were originally kings who were posthumously deified by their people, and he uses this argument to support the Christian position against pagan worship.
**Eusebius** of Caesarea: The historian and Bishop of Caesarea often referred to Euhemerus's theories in his works. In "Praeparatio Evangelica" (Preparation for the Gospel), Eusebius presents Euhemerism as evidence that the pagan gods were not divine but merely humans who had been overly glorified.
St. **Augustine**: Although Augustine did not focus extensively on Euhemerus, he acknowledged similar themes in his critiques of Roman religion. In "The City of God," Augustine discusses the historical origins of the Roman gods and mentions concepts akin to Euhemerism, indicating an awareness of the idea that gods could be human figures elevated to divine status.
