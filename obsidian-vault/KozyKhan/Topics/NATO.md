### The U.S. and NATO Intervention in [[Serbia]]

#### Background
- **Yugoslav Wars**: The breakup of Yugoslavia in the 1990s led to a series of conflicts known as the Yugoslav Wars. Serbia, under the leadership of Slobodan Milošević, was involved in several of these conflicts, including the Bosnian War (1992-1995) and the Kosovo War (1998-1999).
- **Kosovo Conflict**: The Kosovo War arose from tensions between the ethnic Albanian majority in Kosovo and the Serbian government. The Kosovo Liberation Army (KLA) sought independence, leading to a crackdown by Serbian forces, which resulted in significant civilian casualties and displacement.

#### NATO Intervention
- **Operation Allied Force**: In response to the humanitarian crisis and alleged war crimes, NATO launched Operation Allied Force on March 24, 1999. The campaign aimed to stop the violence in Kosovo and force the withdrawal of Serbian forces.
- **Air Campaign**: The operation consisted primarily of an aerial bombing campaign targeting Serbian military infrastructure, communications, and strategic sites. The bombing lasted 78 days, until June 10, 1999.

#### Key Events
- **Račak Massacre**: In January 1999, the massacre of ethnic Albanians in the village of Račak increased international pressure on Serbia and set the stage for NATO's intervention.
- **Rambouillet Agreement**: An attempted peace agreement in February 1999 failed, as Serbia rejected NATO's terms, which included the deployment of NATO troops in Kosovo.
- **NATO Bombing**: NATO's bombing campaign targeted various sites, including military installations, bridges, and infrastructure. Notably, the Chinese embassy in Belgrade was mistakenly bombed, causing diplomatic tensions.
- **End of Bombing**: The campaign ended with the Kumanovo Agreement on June 9, 1999. Serbian forces agreed to withdraw from Kosovo, and NATO peacekeeping forces (KFOR) were deployed.

#### Aftermath
- **UN Resolution 1244**: The UN Security Council adopted Resolution 1244, which established an international presence in Kosovo to oversee the region and ensure security.
- **Kosovo's Status**: Kosovo declared independence from Serbia in 2008, a move recognized by many countries but not by Serbia or some other international actors.
- **War Crimes Trials**: The International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) indicted and prosecuted several Serbian leaders, including Slobodan Milošević, for war crimes and crimes against humanity.

#### Impact on Serbia
- **Economic and Political Consequences**: The bombing campaign caused significant damage to Serbia's infrastructure and economy. Politically, it led to increased anti-Western sentiment and contributed to the fall of Milošević in 2000.
- **Reintegration Efforts**: Post-war Serbia faced challenges in rebuilding its economy, addressing war crimes, and integrating into European institutions.

### Conclusion
The U.S. and NATO intervention in Serbia, particularly during the Kosovo conflict, aimed to halt humanitarian abuses and enforce peace in the region. The bombing campaign was controversial but ultimately led to the withdrawal of Serbian forces from Kosovo and a significant international presence to maintain stability.
