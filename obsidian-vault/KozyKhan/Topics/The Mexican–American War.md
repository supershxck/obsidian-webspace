Below is a structured overview of **The Mexican–American War**, presented as a chapter that examines its origins, major campaigns, outcomes, and lasting impact on both nations.

---

**1. Introduction to the Mexican–American War**

  

**The Mexican–American War** (1846–1848) was a pivotal conflict between the [[United States]] and [[Mexico]] that reshaped the political and territorial landscape of North America. Fueled by expansionist ambitions and disputes over borders, the war resulted in significant territorial gains for the United States while profoundly affecting Mexico’s national identity and future development.

---

**2. Historical Background and Causes**

  

**2.1 Expansionism and Manifest Destiny**

• **Manifest Destiny:** The belief that the United States was destined to expand across the continent was a driving force behind American policies during this period. Many Americans saw westward expansion as a divine right and an opportunity for economic growth.

• **Annexation of Texas:** In 1845, the U.S. annexed Texas, which had won its independence from Mexico in 1836. However, Mexico never recognized Texan independence or its annexation, setting the stage for conflict.

  

**2.2 Border Disputes and Political Tensions**

• **Disputed Borders:** The border between Texas and Mexico was a contentious issue. The United States claimed the Rio Grande as the boundary, while Mexico maintained that the Nueces River marked the limit.

• **Diplomatic Failures:** Attempts at negotiation failed, and tensions escalated, eventually leading to military confrontations along the disputed border.

---

**3. Major Campaigns and Military Operations**

  

**3.1 Initial Engagements**

• **Outbreak of Hostilities:** In 1846, skirmishes along the Rio Grande ignited the war. U.S. troops, led by General Zachary Taylor and later by General Winfield Scott, engaged Mexican forces in a series of battles in northern Mexico.

• **Key Battles:** Early battles, such as those at Palo Alto and Resaca de la Palma, bolstered American morale and demonstrated the effectiveness of U.S. military strategies.

  

**3.2 The California and New Mexico Campaigns**

• **Expansion to the West:** U.S. forces swiftly moved to secure territories in California and New Mexico, regions rich in resources and strategic importance. The conquest of California not only disrupted Mexican control but also paved the way for future migration and settlement.

• **Naval Operations:** The U.S. Navy played a crucial role in blockading Mexican ports and supporting amphibious operations, further weakening Mexico’s defensive capabilities.

  

**3.3 The March on Mexico City**

• **General Scott’s Campaign:** In a dramatic campaign, General Winfield Scott led an amphibious assault on Veracruz and then advanced inland, capturing key cities and eventually reaching Mexico City in 1847.

• **Decisive Victory:** The capture of the capital marked a turning point, forcing the Mexican government to negotiate an end to the conflict.

---

**4. Outcomes and Consequences**

  

**4.1 Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo**

• **End of the War:** In 1848, the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo was signed, officially ending the conflict.

• **Territorial Changes:** Under the treaty, Mexico ceded approximately half of its territory to the United States, including present-day California, Nevada, Utah, Arizona, and parts of Colorado, New Mexico, and Wyoming.

• **Financial Settlement:** In exchange, the United States paid Mexico $15 million and assumed certain claims of American citizens against the Mexican government.

  

**4.2 Impact on the United States**

• **Expansion and Growth:** The acquisition of new territories spurred economic development and population growth, contributing to the United States’ emergence as a continental power.

• **Debates Over Slavery:** The vast new territories intensified debates over the expansion of slavery, setting the stage for future conflicts leading up to the American Civil War.

  

**4.3 Impact on Mexico**

• **National Trauma:** The loss of territory and the defeat in the war left a deep and lasting impact on Mexico, contributing to political instability and a sense of national humiliation.

• **Reshaping Identity:** The war forced Mexico to confront internal divisions and reform its political structures, influencing its subsequent history and development.

---

**5. Conclusion**

  

**The Mexican–American War** was a transformative conflict that reshaped North America. Driven by the ideology of Manifest Destiny and unresolved territorial disputes, the war led to dramatic shifts in boundaries and power. For the United States, the victory catalyzed westward expansion and economic growth, while for Mexico, it marked a period of loss and national re-evaluation. The legacy of the war continues to influence discussions on national identity, territorial rights, and the historical relationships between the two nations.

  

This comprehensive overview provides a solid foundation for understanding the causes, key events, and profound consequences of the Mexican–American War, highlighting its role in shaping the modern geopolitical landscape of North America.
