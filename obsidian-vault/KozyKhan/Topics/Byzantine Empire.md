The Byzantine Empire, also known as the Eastern Roman Empire, was a continuation of the Roman Empire in the eastern Mediterranean after the fall of the Western Roman Empire. Spanning over a millennium, its history is complex, marked by resilience, cultural flourishing, and eventual decline. Below is an outline of its major phases and key events:

---

### Foundation and Early Period (330–476 CE)
- **330 CE**: Emperor Constantine I establishes [[Constantinople]] (modern Istanbul) as the new capital of the Roman Empire, originally named New Rome, on the site of Byzantium. This marks the symbolic beginning of the Byzantine Empire.
- **395 CE**: The Roman Empire permanently splits into East and West after Emperor Theodosius I’s death, with the East ruled from Constantinople.
- **4th–5th Centuries**: The Eastern Empire thrives while the West weakens. Key events:
  - [[Christianity]] becomes the state religion under Theodosius I (380 CE).
  - The East avoids the worst of the barbarian invasions that plague the West.
- **476 CE**: The Western Roman Empire falls with the deposition of Romulus Augustulus, leaving the Eastern Empire as the sole Roman successor.

---

### Justinian and the Height of Power (527–602 CE)
- **527–565 CE**: Reign of Emperor Justinian I, the empire’s "golden age":
  - **Reconquest**: Justinian’s generals, like [[Belisarius]], reclaim parts of the West—North Africa, Italy, and southern Spain—from [[Vandals]], [[Ostrogoths]], and [[Visigoths]].
  - **Legal Reform**: The *Corpus Juris Civilis* (Justinian Code) codifies Roman law, influencing legal systems for centuries.
  - **Architecture**: The Hagia Sophia is built (537 CE), a masterpiece of engineering and Christian art.
  - **Plague**: The Plague of Justinian (541–542 CE) kills millions, weakening the empire’s economy and population.
- **565–602 CE**: Post-Justinian decline:
  - Overextension and financial strain from wars leave the empire vulnerable.
  - Lombard invasion (568 CE) reduces Byzantine control in Italy.

---

### Struggles and Recovery (602–867 CE)
- **602–628 CE**: War with the Persian Sassanid Empire exhausts both powers, ending with a Byzantine victory under Emperor Heraclius.
- **7th Century**: Rise of Islam:
  - Arab conquests (634–717 CE) strip Byzantium of Syria, Egypt, and North Africa.
  - Constantinople withstands sieges (674–678, 717–718 CE), aided by Greek fire.
- **8th Century**: Internal turmoil:
  - **Iconoclasm** (726–787, 814–842 CE): Emperors ban religious images, sparking civil strife and weakening unity.
  - Loss of territory to Arabs and Bulgars shrinks the empire.
- **Mid-9th Century**: Recovery begins under the Macedonian dynasty.

---

### Macedonian Renaissance and Peak (867–1071 CE)
- **867–1056 CE**: Macedonian dynasty restores Byzantine power:
  - **Military Success**: Emperors like Basil I and Basil II ("the Bulgar-Slayer") defeat Bulgars, Arabs, and Rus’, expanding into Armenia and the Balkans.
  - **Cultural Flourishing**: Art, literature, and scholarship thrive; missionaries like Cyril and Methodius spread Christianity and the Cyrillic alphabet to the Slavs.
  - **Economy**: Constantinople remains a global trade hub.
- **989 CE**: Conversion of [[Kievan Rus]]’ to [[Orthodox Christianity]] under Vladimir I strengthens Byzantine influence.
- **1025–1071 CE**: Decline sets in after Basil II’s death:
  - Dynastic instability and reliance on mercenaries weaken the military.
  - **[[Battle of Manzikert]] (1071 CE)**: Seljuk Turks defeat the Byzantines, seizing most of Anatolia.

---

### Komnenian Restoration and Crusades (1071–1204 CE)
- **1081–1185 CE**: [[Komnenos dynasty]] stabilizes the empire:
  - Alexios I Komnenos (r. 1081–1118) appeals to the West, sparking [[the First Crusade]] (1096–1099), which temporarily recovers some land.
  - Economic recovery and alliances with Crusaders bolster the empire.
- **12th Century**: Tensions with the West grow:
  - Crusaders and Byzantines clash over goals and territory.
  - Norman invasions threaten Byzantine holdings in Italy and the Balkans.
- **1204 CE**: Fourth Crusade:
  - Western Crusaders sack Constantinople, establishing the Latin Empire (1204–1261). The Byzantines fragment into successor states (e.g., Nicaea, Epirus).

---

### Late Byzantine Period and Decline (1261–1453 CE)
- **1261 CE**: Michael VIII Palaiologos recaptures Constantinople, restoring the empire under the Palaiologos dynasty.
- **13th–14th Centuries**: A weakened empire:
  - Reduced to a small state surrounded by the rising Ottoman Turks.
  - Civil wars (e.g., 1341–1347 CE) and the Black Death (1347 CE) further erode strength.
  - Loss of trade dominance to Italian city-states like Venice and Genoa.
- **15th Century**: Final collapse:
  - Ottoman sieges shrink Byzantine territory to Constantinople and a few enclaves.
  - **Fall of Constantinople (May 29, 1453)**: Sultan Mehmed II conquers the city, ending the empire. The Ottomans rename it Istanbul.

---

### Legacy
- **Cultural**: The Byzantines preserved Greek and Roman knowledge, passing it to the Renaissance West via fleeing scholars after 1453.
- **Religious**: As the center of Orthodox Christianity, they shaped Eastern Europe’s religious and cultural identity.
- **Political**: Their imperial system influenced later states, including the Ottomans and Russia ("Third Rome").
