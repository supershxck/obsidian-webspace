The concept of the "image of God" (imago Dei) is deeply rooted in theology and the teachings of the Church Fathers. This belief is primarily based on the biblical account of creation in Genesis, where it is stated that humans are made in the image and likeness of God (Genesis 1:26-27). Here are key elements of this doctrine, elaborated with references to Church Fathers:

**1. Nature of the Image**
The image of God in humans is understood to reflect certain attributes of God, although not His essence. It signifies the potential for holiness, rationality, free will, and the capacity for a relationship with God.

**St. Gregory of Nyssa:**
St. Gregory of Nyssa, in his work “On the Creation of Man”, emphasizes that the image of God in humans is primarily spiritual and intellectual, rather than physical:
"God created man to be an image of the Divine nature, making the human soul a partaker in all the beautiful and divine excellences, so that man, thus made like unto God, might display in himself the reflex of all that is characteristic of the Divine nature."

**2. Dynamic Nature of the Image**
The image of God in humanity is not static; it implies a process of becoming more like God, or theosis. This concept suggests that humans are called to grow in divine likeness through a life of virtue, prayer, and participation in the sacraments.

**St. Athanasius:**
St. Athanasius, in On the Incarnation, writes about the restoration of the divine image through Christ:
"For the Word of God became man so that we might become God. He was incarnate that we might be made divine, He manifested Himself by a body that we might receive the idea of the unseen Father, and He endured the insolence of men that we might inherit immortality."

**3. Freedom and Responsibility**
Being made in the image of God entails moral responsibility and the exercise of free will. Humans are free to choose between good and evil, reflecting God's gift of freedom.

**St. John of Damascus**:
St. John of Damascus, in “An Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith”, explains:
"Man is a living being endowed with reason and intelligence, created in the image of God, with free will, and capable of self-determination. He is subject to change and capable of choosing between good and evil."

**4. Relational Aspect**
The image of God also encompasses the relational aspect, as humans are created for communion with God and with each other. This reflects the Trinitarian nature of God, who is a communion of three Persons.

**St. Basil the Great:**
St. Basil the Great, in his work “On the Human Condition”, discusses the social and communal aspect of the divine image:
"The human being is a creature composed of body and soul, made in the image of the Creator, and capable of union with Him. By nature, humans are inclined to form societies and communities, reflecting the Trinitarian communion of the Godhead."

**5. Restoration through Christ**
The Orthodox understanding emphasizes that the image of God, though marred by sin, is restored through Jesus Christ. Christ, the perfect image of the Father, renews and elevates human nature through His incarnation, death, and resurrection.

**St. Irenaeus of Lyons:**
St. Irenaeus, in “Against Heresies”, writes about the restoration of the image:
"He (Christ) became what we are in order to enable us to become what He is. In His becoming incarnate, He has recapitulated in Himself the long history of mankind, and has obtained for us a lasting salvation, so that we might regain in Christ Jesus that which we had lost in Adam, that is, to be according to the image and likeness of God."

**Conclusion**
In Orthodox Christianity, the image of God in humans is a profound theological concept that involves a dynamic, relational, and moral dimension. It speaks to humanity's potential for divine communion, moral responsibility, and the ultimate goal of theosis—becoming like God through grace. This doctrine is richly articulated by the Church Fathers, who provide a comprehensive understanding of what it means to be created in the image of God.
