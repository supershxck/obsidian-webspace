# Is Total Depravity Biblical?

**Topic:** Is Total Depravity Biblical?
**Date:** 2024-07-03
## Introduction
- **Thesis Statement:** The concept of total depravity, while prominent in Reformed theology, is not entirely aligned with the biblical and theological perspectives of the Eastern Orthodox Church, which emphasizes human potential for good and divine-human cooperation.
## Definitions
- **Total Depravity:** The doctrine that, as a result of the Fall, every aspect of human nature is corrupted by sin, rendering humans incapable of choosing or doing good without divine intervention.
- **Theosis:** The process of becoming more like God or partaking in the divine nature.
- **Synergy:** The cooperation between divine grace and human freedom in the process of salvation.
## Background
- **Context:** The term "doctrines of grace" refers to a Calvinist or Reformed framework emphasizing total depravity, unconditional election, limited atonement, irresistible grace, and perseverance of the saints. In contrast, the Eastern Orthodox Church has a distinct theological tradition focusing on theosis, synergy, and divine-human cooperation.
## Argument Structure
### 1. Opening Statement
- **Main Points:**
  - The Eastern Orthodox Church offers a nuanced perspective on human nature post-Fall.
  - Despite the Fall, humans retain the image of God and the potential for good.
  - Divine-human cooperation (synergy) is crucial in the process of salvation.
### 2. Arguments
- **Argument 1:**
  - **Claim:** The Eastern Orthodox Church distinguishes between Ancestral Sin and Original Sin.
  - **Evidence:** [[Ancestral Sin]] refers to the fallen condition inherited from Adam and Eve, including death and a propensity towards sin, rather than guilt for their sin.
  - **Explanation:** This distinction emphasizes the inherited corruption and mortality but *not hereditary guilt*, differing from the Reformed view of total depravity.
- **Argument 2:**
  - **Claim:** Humanity retains the "image of God" despite the Fall.
  - **Evidence:** Genesis 1:26-27 states that humans are created in the image and likeness of God.
  - **Explanation:** This indelible image within humanity suggests that humans, even in their fallen state, are capable of good, challenging the notion of total depravity.
- **Argument 3:**
  - **Claim:** The Eastern Orthodox Church emphasizes healing and theosis rather than forensic justification.
  - **Evidence:** Theosis involves partaking in the divine nature and being transformed into the likeness of God (2 Peter 1:4).
  - **Explanation:** This process of healing and transformation indicates that humans can respond to and cooperate with God's grace, contrary to the idea that they are utterly depraved.

### 3. Counterarguments
- **Counterargument 1:**
  - **Objection:** The Reformed doctrine of total depravity posits that humans are entirely incapable of good without divine intervention.
  - **Rebuttal:** The Eastern Orthodox perspective maintains that the image of God within humans allows for the possibility of good and cooperation with divine grace.
- **Counterargument 2:**
  - **Objection:** The Reformed view emphasizes forensic justification, where salvation is seen as a legal transaction.
  - **Rebuttal:** The Eastern Orthodox view focuses on healing and transformation, suggesting that sin is a sickness requiring healing rather than merely a legal problem.

### 4. Conclusion
- **Restate Thesis:**
  - The concept of total depravity, while significant in Reformed theology, is not entirely supported by the biblical and theological perspectives of the Eastern Orthodox Church.
- **Summary of Main Points:**
  - The Eastern Orthodox Church distinguishes between Ancestral Sin and Original Sin.
  - Humanity retains the image of God, allowing for the potential for good.
  - Salvation involves divine-human cooperation and the process of theosis.
- **Closing Statement:**
  - The Eastern Orthodox Church offers a more optimistic view of human potential post-Fall, emphasizing the retained image of God, cooperation with divine grace, and the transformative journey towards union with God through theosis.
