**Outline of the Babylonian Language**

  

**1. Introduction to the Babylonian Language**

• **Babylonian** is a dialect of **Akkadian**, a Semitic language spoken in Mesopotamia.

• Akkadian was the **lingua franca** of the Ancient Near East, evolving from earlier **Sumerian** influences.

• Babylonian, distinct from its northern counterpart **Assyrian**, became dominant during the Old Babylonian period and persisted for centuries as a written and spoken language.

  

**2. Historical Development**

• **Old Babylonian (c. 2000–1500 BCE)**:

• Emerged as a regional dialect during the early 2nd millennium BCE.

• Used extensively during Hammurabi’s reign, notably in his **Code of Hammurabi**.

• Close to Old Akkadian but gradually diverging.

• **Middle Babylonian (c. 1500–1000 BCE)**:

• Period of Kassite rule and increased cultural exchange.

• Babylonian became a literary and diplomatic language, written in **cuneiform**.

• Standard Babylonian, a refined literary form, developed during this time for epic and religious texts (e.g., the _Epic of Gilgamesh_).

• **Neo-Babylonian (c. 1000–600 BCE)**:

• The spoken form of Babylonian continued evolving, showing influence from neighboring Aramaic.

• Used during the Chaldean dynasty (Neo-Babylonian Empire).

• **Late Babylonian (c. 600 BCE–100 CE)**:

• Marked by significant **Aramaic influence**, which gradually replaced Babylonian as the spoken language.

• Continued use in scholarly, astronomical, and ritual contexts well into the Hellenistic period.

  

**3. Script: Cuneiform**

• Written in **cuneiform**, a script adapted from Sumerian pictographs.

• The writing system was **syllabic**, using signs to represent sounds, syllables, and sometimes words.

• Required extensive training, making scribes an elite class in Babylonian society.

  

**4. Grammar and Structure**

• **Semitic Features**:

• Related to Hebrew, Aramaic, and Arabic.

• Root-based system: Most words built from **three-consonant roots**.

• **Nouns**:

• Gender: Masculine and feminine.

• Number: Singular, dual (rare), and plural.

• Case system: Nominative (-u), genitive (-i), and accusative (-a).

• **Verbs**:

• Complex conjugation system with roots modified by prefixes, suffixes, and infixes.

• Conjugated for tense (past, present, future), mood (indicative, subjunctive, imperative), and voice (active, passive).

• **Syntax**:

• Typically **subject-object-verb (SOV)** word order.

• Flexible structure, with meaning often derived from context and case endings.

  

**5. Vocabulary and Lexicon**

• Borrowed extensively from **Sumerian**, particularly for religious, legal, and scientific terminology.

• Specialized vocabulary for:

• **Astronomy and astrology**: Babylonian was central to early astronomical texts.

• **Law and governance**: The lexicon of Hammurabi’s Code reflects societal complexity.

• **Trade**: Words for commodities, weights, and measures due to Babylon’s role as a trading hub.

  

**6. Literary and Scholarly Use**

• **Religious Texts**:

• Used in hymns, prayers, and mythological epics like the _Enuma Elish_ and _Epic of Gilgamesh_.

• **Scientific Writings**:

• Babylonian became the primary language for astronomy, mathematics, and medicine.

• **Legal Texts**:

• Administrative records and legal codes, such as Hammurabi’s Code, demonstrate its use in governance.

• **Diplomatic Language**:

• Became the **diplomatic lingua franca** of the Near East during the Middle Babylonian period, used in correspondence between kingdoms (e.g., the Amarna Letters).

  

**7. Influence and Decline**

• **Aramaic Influence**:

• Aramaic, a related Semitic language, began to dominate as the spoken and administrative language by the 1st millennium BCE.

• Many Babylonian texts from the Late Babylonian period show Aramaic loanwords and bilingualism.

• **Legacy in Later Languages**:

• Babylonian contributed vocabulary and concepts to later Semitic languages, including Aramaic and Hebrew.

• Preserved through the study of cuneiform texts in modern linguistics.

  

**8. Revival and Modern Study**

• Rediscovered in the 19th century through **archaeological excavations**.

• Deciphered through the study of cuneiform inscriptions, such as the **Behistun Inscription**.

• Provides insights into Mesopotamian culture, religion, and science.
