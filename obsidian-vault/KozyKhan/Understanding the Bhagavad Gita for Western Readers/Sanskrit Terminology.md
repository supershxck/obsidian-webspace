# Sanskrit Terminology and Key Concepts in Bhagavad Gita As It Is

This glossary provides explanations of important Sanskrit terms and philosophical concepts found in the Bhagavad Gita As It Is, with special attention to how Western readers can understand these concepts in their proper context.

## Core Philosophical Concepts

### Dharma
A central concept in Hindu philosophy, and arguably the central concept in the Bhagavad Gita. Dharma is a moral code of behavior that follows from one's sacred duty to the gods, other people, and oneself. Often translated as "duty," "righteousness," or "religion," dharma represents the right way of living that maintains cosmic order. In the Bhagavad Gita, Krishna emphasizes to Arjuna that following one's dharma (in Arjuna's case, his duty as a warrior) is essential, even when it seems to conflict with other moral considerations.

### Yoga
Often translated as "discipline" or "spiritual path," yoga in the Bhagavad Gita refers to a practice of deliberate, intense devotional engagement that usually involves meditation. This concept far exceeds yoga's usual connotations in the West as a form of physical exercise. The Gita describes several types of yoga:

- **Karma Yoga**: The yoga of action or selfless service
- **Jnana Yoga**: The yoga of knowledge or wisdom
- **Bhakti Yoga**: The yoga of devotion
- **Raja Yoga**: The royal or classical yoga of meditation

### Karma
Sanskrit term for "action." In the Bhagavad Gita, karma refers to both action itself and the consequences of action. The text teaches that one should perform actions without attachment to their fruits (nishkama karma). "Yoga of action" is a translation of "karma yoga."

### Bhakti
A Sanskrit term for devotional worship, which Krishna asks of Arjuna in the Bhagavad Gita. Bhakti allows Hindus to resolve moral conflicts by turning to a higher power through disciplined, emotional rituals that establish a personal relationship with the divine. In Prabhupada's translation, bhakti is emphasized as the highest form of yoga and the most direct path to Krishna consciousness.

### Brahman
The interconnected, imperishable, unitary force of being that animates everything in the universe. Brahman is identical with atman (the individual self) and created by Krishna, who calls it his "womb." It is the impersonal aspect of the Absolute Truth, while Krishna is understood as the personal aspect in Prabhupada's translation.

### Atman
The individual self or soul that moves through samsara (the cycle of reincarnation). Atman is a component of Brahman, as its being is connected to that of all other things. Recognizing this fact is essential to achieving moksha (liberation).

### Samsara
The cycle of birth and death (also known as reincarnation or transmigration) in which the eternal self (atman) participates until it forfeits all attachment to the gunas, recognizes its unity with Brahman, and achieves moksha.

### Gunas
The three component forms or "threads" that comprise all material things: sattva, rajas, and tamas. The physical world and the bodies that a soul inhabits over time are composed of these three qualities in different proportions:

- **Sattva**: The highest and lightest of the gunas, representing truthfulness, lucidity, or purity
- **Rajas**: The guna of passion, which attaches the soul to action's consequences
- **Tamas**: The darkest and heaviest of the gunas, connected to ignorance, laziness, and neglect

### Krishna Consciousness
A central concept in Prabhupada's translation, referring to the state of awareness in which one recognizes Krishna as the Supreme Personality of Godhead and acts in devotional service to Him. Achieving Krishna consciousness is presented as the ultimate goal of spiritual practice.

## Important Sanskrit Terms

### Acintya-bheda-abheda-tattva
Fundamental truth of the Supreme Person, who is inconceivably non-different from (abheda) and different from (bheda) His potencies. This is Lord Caitanya's "simultaneously one and different" doctrine, which establishes the inconceivable, simultaneous oneness and difference of the Lord and His expansions.

### Acyuta
A name for the Supreme Personality of Godhead, meaning "infallible" or "who never falls down from His position."

### Adhokṣaja
A name for the Supreme Personality of Godhead, who is beyond material sense perception, who is not perceivable by impure material senses.

### Anartha
Unwanted desires, activities or habits that are likened to weeds hindering one's advancement in bhakti (devotional service).

### Ananya-bhakti
Exclusive devotion, unmixed with any other desire, in which one's sole motivation is to please Bhagavan (the Supreme Lord).

### Bhagavan
The Supreme Lord, complete in six opulences: beauty, wealth, strength, fame, knowledge and renunciation. Literally, 'possessor of opulence.'

### Om/Aum
A sacred syllable and mantra (chant) with various religious meanings, which is considered the highest of all sounds. Krishna encourages devotees to chant "om tat sat" ("om is the truth") during their discipline and sacrifices.

### [[Sannyasa]]
The renunciation of action; the fourth stage of life in the traditional Hindu system, in which one renounces worldly attachments.

### Tyagi
A sage who successfully abandons any attachment to the fruit of actions, a tyagi is the highest kind of human being and destined to join Brahman upon death.

## The Hindu Social System (Varna)

The Hindu caste system, or social order, consists of four categories:

### Brahmin
The highest Hindu caste, traditionally composed of priests and teachers.

### Kshatriya
The second-highest Hindu caste, traditionally composed of warriors and statesmen. Arjuna belongs to this caste.

### Vaishya
The third Hindu caste, traditionally composed of merchants, farmers, and artisans.

### Shudra
The fourth Hindu caste, traditionally composed of servants and manual laborers.

In Prabhupada's translation, he emphasizes that one becomes a member of one of the varnas not by one's birth (lineage), but by one's personal qualities (guna) and the type of work (karma) one actually performs.

## Historical and Narrative Context

### Hastinapura
The kingdom of the Bharata family, which the Pandavas and Kauravas fight over in the Mahabharata.

### Bharata
The royal family that the Pandavas and Kauravas all belong to (also often known as the Kuru family).

### Pandavas
Arjuna's side of the Bharata family, who are battling their cousins (the Kauravas) to take back Hastinapura in the set-up to the Bhagavad Gita.

### Kauravas
The side of the Bharata family that opposes Arjuna and the Pandavas. The Kauravas descend from the blind king Dhritarashtra, to whom Sanjaya narrates the Bhagavad Gita.

### Arjuna
One of the five Pandava brothers and the central human character in the Bhagavad Gita. He is a mighty warrior who faces a moral dilemma when he must fight against his own relatives. Krishna serves as his charioteer and spiritual guide.

### Vedas
The four oldest and often most authoritative Hindu scriptures, generally considered direct revelations from Brahma (the creator god) and dated to approximately 1,000 years before the Mahabharata and Bhagavad Gita.

## Concepts Emphasized in Prabhupada's Translation

### Acintya-shakti
Sri Bhagavan's inconceivable potency.

### Ananya-yoga
Bhakti unmixed with dependence on speculative knowledge, fruitive work or austerities.

### Bhagavat-prema
Love for the all-opulent Personality of Godhead.

### Bhajana
Service, especially hearing, chanting, remembering and meditating on the holy name, form, qualities and pastimes of Sri Krishna.

### Madhurya
Sweetness; in regard to bhakti, this refers to devotion inspired by Krishna's sweetness rather than His majesty (aisvarya).

### Paramatma
The Supersoul; the localized aspect of the Supreme Lord who dwells in the heart of every living entity and witnesses their activities.

## Understanding Prabhupada's Approach

A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada's translation "Bhagavad Gita As It Is" presents the text from the perspective of Gaudiya Vaishnavism, which emphasizes:

1. Krishna as the Supreme Personality of Godhead, not merely an avatar or incarnation
2. Bhakti (devotional service) as the highest spiritual path
3. The personal nature of the Absolute Truth
4. The eternal individual nature of the soul (jiva)
5. The simultaneous oneness and difference of the soul and God (acintya-bheda-abheda)

Western readers should understand that Prabhupada's translation differs from some other interpretations of the Gita in its strong emphasis on devotion to Krishna as the ultimate goal, rather than impersonal liberation or other spiritual paths. This approach is faithful to the Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition but represents one of several traditional Hindu interpretations of the text.
