# Cultural Context and Symbolism in the Bhagavad Gita As It Is

## The Bhagavad Gita Within the Mahabharata

The Bhagavad Gita is not a standalone text but rather a 700-verse section (comprising 18 chapters) of the much larger Hindu epic, the Mahabharata. Understanding this context is crucial for Western readers approaching the text.

### The Mahabharata: Scale and Significance

- The Mahabharata is one of the two major Sanskrit epics of ancient India (the other being the Ramayana)
- It is massive in scale - ten times longer than the Iliad and Odyssey combined, with over 100,000 verses
- The epic tells the story of the Kurukshetra War between two groups of cousins (the Kauravas and the Pandavas) for the throne of Hastinapura in a kingdom called Kuru
- The war lasted eighteen days, with many heroes dying on both sides
- The Mahabharata is attributed to the sage Vyasa, who is also a character within the story and is considered an expression of the god Vishnu

### The Bhagavad Gita's Position in the Epic

- The Bhagavad Gita occupies chapters 23 to 40 of Book VI of the Mahabharata
- It represents the climactic moment of the epic, occurring just before the great battle begins
- The text is often referred to simply as the "Gita" (meaning "song")
- While part of the larger epic, the Gita has become a canonical text on its own and is often read separately from the Mahabharata

## Narrative Context and Symbolism

### The Battlefield Setting (Kurukshetra)

The Bhagavad Gita takes place on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, which has both literal and symbolic significance:

- **Literal context**: The battlefield where the Pandavas and Kauravas are about to engage in a devastating war
- **Symbolic meaning**: Represents the field of dharma (righteousness) where the battle between good and evil, right and wrong takes place
- **Inner symbolism**: The battlefield can also represent the human body or mind where the constant struggle between higher and lower impulses occurs
- **Spiritual interpretation**: In Prabhupada's translation, the battlefield is presented as the place where material attachments must be overcome through spiritual knowledge

### The Chariot Symbolism

The chariot in which Arjuna and Krishna sit has rich symbolic meaning:

- **Krishna as charioteer**: Represents the divine guiding the human soul through life's journey
- **Arjuna as warrior**: Symbolizes the human soul who must fight life's battles
- **The horses**: Represent the senses that must be controlled
- **The reins**: Symbolize the mind that controls the senses
- **The chariot itself**: Represents the body, the vehicle for the soul's journey

### The Conch Shells

Before the dialogue begins, both sides blow conch shells:

- Conch shells are associated with spirituality in Hindu tradition
- The blowing of conches signifies that this is not merely a physical battle but a spiritual one
- Each hero's conch has a specific name and significance
- In Prabhupada's translation, special attention is given to Krishna's conch (Panchajanya) as representing the divine call to spiritual awakening

## Cultural Elements Essential for Western Readers

### The Hindu Concept of Time

The Bhagavad Gita presents a cyclical rather than linear view of time:

- **Yugas (Ages)**: The text references the concept of four world ages (Satya, Treta, Dvapara, and Kali)
- **The Mahabharata War**: Some traditions hold that this war marked the transition into the Kali Yuga (the current age of discord) in 3102 BCE
- **Krishna's cosmic form**: When Krishna reveals his universal form to Arjuna, it demonstrates the Hindu concept of time as cyclical and eternal
- **Prabhupada's emphasis**: In his translation, Prabhupada emphasizes the timelessness of Krishna's teachings as applicable to all ages

### The Caste System (Varna)

The Bhagavad Gita references the traditional Hindu social structure:

- The text discusses the four varnas: Brahmins (priests/teachers), Kshatriyas (warriors/rulers), Vaishyas (merchants/farmers), and Shudras (laborers)
- Arjuna is a Kshatriya, and Krishna reminds him of his duty (dharma) according to his varna
- In Prabhupada's translation, he emphasizes that one's varna is determined by qualities (guna) and actions (karma), not by birth
- This interpretation aligns with modern reformist Hindu views rather than traditional hereditary caste understanding

### The Guru-Disciple Relationship

The dialogue between Krishna and Arjuna exemplifies the sacred guru-disciple relationship in Hindu tradition:

- Krishna serves as the ultimate guru (teacher)
- Arjuna takes the position of a shishya (disciple) seeking guidance
- The relationship demonstrates the Hindu tradition of seeking spiritual knowledge through a qualified teacher
- Prabhupada's translation emphasizes this relationship as a model for spiritual seekers, positioning himself in a lineage of teachers descending from Krishna

## Symbolic Interpretations in Prabhupada's Translation

### Krishna as the Supreme Personality of Godhead

In "Bhagavad Gita As It Is," Prabhupada consistently emphasizes:

- Krishna is not merely an avatar (incarnation) but the original Supreme Personality of Godhead
- Other deities mentioned in the text are positioned as subordinate to Krishna
- Krishna's human-like form is his original, eternal form (not a temporary manifestation)
- This interpretation aligns with the Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition but differs from some other Hindu interpretations

### The Universal Form (Vishvarupa)

Chapter 11 of the Gita describes Krishna revealing his cosmic form to Arjuna:

- This terrifying, multi-armed, multi-faced form contains all beings and all time
- In Prabhupada's translation, this vision serves to establish Krishna's supreme position
- The revelation is presented as proof of Krishna's divinity rather than suggesting that the impersonal cosmic form is superior to Krishna's personal form
- After this revelation, Krishna returns to his human-like form, which Prabhupada emphasizes as Krishna's original and most intimate form

### The Metaphor of the Banyan Tree

In Chapter 15, the material world is compared to an inverted banyan tree:

- The roots are upward (representing the Supreme)
- The branches extend downward (representing the material manifestation)
- Prabhupada interprets this as representing how the material world is a reflection of the spiritual world
- The solution presented is to cut the tree with "the weapon of detachment" and seek the original source

## Changing Interpretations Through History

### Historical Interpretations

The Bhagavad Gita has been interpreted differently throughout history:

- **Traditional warrior interpretation**: Originally seen as justifying righteous warfare (dharma yuddha)
- **Colonial period**: During British rule, the text was sometimes viewed as promoting violence
- **Gandhi's interpretation**: Mahatma Gandhi reinterpreted the battlefield as an allegory for inner spiritual struggle
- **Modern global interpretation**: Now often seen as a guide to spiritual living and ethical action

### Prabhupada's Distinctive Approach

A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada's "As It Is" translation has specific characteristics:

- Emphasizes devotion (bhakti) as superior to knowledge (jnana) or action (karma)
- Presents Krishna consciousness as the ultimate goal
- Interprets the text through the lens of Gaudiya Vaishnavism
- Positions his translation as the authentic understanding, free from "personal motivation"
- Provides extensive purports (commentaries) that often exceed the length of the verses themselves

## Metaphors and Symbolism for Western Readers

### The Body as a Garment

In Chapter 2, Krishna explains that the soul changes bodies as a person changes garments:

- This metaphor helps explain the concept of reincarnation
- For Western readers, this provides a concrete image for understanding an unfamiliar concept
- Prabhupada elaborates on this metaphor to explain how the soul is distinct from the body

### The Senses as Horses

The senses are compared to horses that must be controlled:

- Uncontrolled senses lead one astray, like wild horses
- The mind is the reins that control these horses
- Intelligence is the charioteer who guides the mind
- The soul (atman) is the passenger in the chariot
- This metaphor helps Western readers understand the Hindu concept of sense control

### The Field and the Knower of the Field

In Chapter 13, the body is described as "the field" and the soul as "the knower of the field":

- The material body is a field of activities
- The soul is the consciousness that observes and experiences this field
- This agricultural metaphor helps explain the relationship between consciousness and matter
- Prabhupada uses this metaphor to explain the difference between the body, mind, and soul

## Cultural Barriers for Western Readers

### Polytheistic References

The Gita mentions various deities that may confuse monotheistically-oriented Western readers:

- References to Indra, Brahma, and other deities appear throughout the text
- Prabhupada's translation clarifies the position of these deities within a hierarchical cosmic structure
- He consistently positions Krishna as supreme above all other divine manifestations

### Cyclical vs. Linear Time

Western readers often struggle with the Hindu concept of cyclical time:

- The text references vast time scales (yugas and kalpas) unfamiliar to Western readers
- References to previous lives and future incarnations assume a cyclical view of existence
- Prabhupada's translation provides context for these concepts, explaining the four yugas and their durations

### Non-Dualistic Philosophy

The relationship between the individual soul and the supreme is complex:

- Western readers may struggle with concepts that seem paradoxical (the soul is both one with and different from the supreme)
- Prabhupada's translation emphasizes "acintya-bheda-abheda" (inconceivable oneness and difference)
- This philosophical position differs from both Western monotheism and strict monism

## Conclusion: The Gita's Universal Symbolism

Despite its specific cultural context, the Bhagavad Gita contains universal symbolic elements that transcend cultural boundaries:

- The battlefield as a metaphor for life's moral struggles
- The dialogue between human and divine representing the search for meaning
- The tension between action and renunciation reflecting universal human dilemmas
- The path to inner peace through detachment from results while performing one's duty

Prabhupada's "As It Is" translation seeks to preserve these universal elements while presenting them through the specific lens of Gaudiya Vaishnavism, offering Western readers both the cultural specificity of the Hindu tradition and the universal wisdom that has made the Bhagavad Gita a text of global significance.
