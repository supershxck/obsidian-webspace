# Tarot Cards: History and Structure

## Origins and Historical Development

The history of Tarot cards begins in [[Europe]] during the mid-15th century. According to multiple sources, Tarot decks were invented in [[Italy]] around the 1430s, specifically in regions like Ferrara, Milan, Florence, and Bologna. These early decks were initially created as playing cards for games rather than for divination purposes.

The earliest known Tarot-like cards were used to play a game called "tarocchi" (or "trionfi"), which was similar to bridge. These early decks, such as the Visconti-Sforza deck, were hand-painted and commissioned by wealthy Italian families as symbols of their status and prestige.

The original Tarot deck was formed by adding a fifth suit of 21 specially illustrated cards called "trionfi" ("triumphs") and an odd card called "il matto" ("the fool") to the existing four-suited pack of playing cards. These additional cards were allegorical illustrations that likely represented characters in medieval reenactments of Roman triumphal processions.

## Structure of the Tarot Deck

The standard modern Tarot deck consists of 78 cards divided into two main groups:

### Major Arcana (22 cards)

The Major Arcana (also known as the Trump cards) consists of 22 cards that represent significant life lessons, spiritual themes, and archetypal energies. These cards are numbered from 0 to 21, with the Fool typically being card 0 or unnumbered. The Major Arcana cards depict various forces, characters, virtues, and vices.

The 22 cards of the Major Arcana, in traditional order, are:

0. The Fool
1. The Magician (or Juggler)
2. The High Priestess (or Papess)
3. The Empress
4. The Emperor
5. The Hierophant (or Pope)
6. The Lovers
7. The Chariot
8. Justice (or Strength in some decks)
9. The Hermit
10. Wheel of Fortune
11. Strength (or Justice in some decks)
12. The Hanged Man
13. Death
14. Temperance
15. The Devil
16. The Tower (or Lightning-struck Tower)
17. The Star
18. The Moon
19. The Sun
20. Judgment
21. The World

### Minor Arcana (56 cards)

The Minor Arcana consists of 56 cards divided into four suits of 14 cards each. These suits correspond to the four elements and focus on everyday matters, personal experiences, and the challenges and opportunities encountered in daily life.

The four suits are:
- Wands (or Batons, Rods, Clubs): Associated with the element of Fire, creativity, passion, and energy
- Cups (or Chalices, Hearts): Associated with the element of Water, emotions, relationships, and intuition
- Swords (or Spades): Associated with the element of Air, intellect, communication, and conflict
- Pentacles (or Coins, Disks, Diamonds): Associated with the element of Earth, material matters, work, and finances

Each suit contains:
- Ten numbered cards (Ace through Ten)
- Four court cards: King, Queen, Knight, and Page (or Jack)

## Evolution of Tarot Interpretations

While Tarot cards were initially used for playing games, their association with divination and occult practices began to emerge in the late [[18th century]], particularly in [[France]] around 1780. French occultist Jean-Baptiste Alliette (known as Etteilla) published the first definitive guide to Tarot card reading, laying the foundation for using Tarot as a tool for spiritual and personal growth.

In the 19th and 20th centuries, various occultists and esoteric societies further developed the symbolic interpretations of Tarot cards. The [[../../Topics/Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn|Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn]], a magical order active in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, played a significant role in shaping modern Tarot interpretations, particularly through members like [[Arthur Edward Waite]] and [[Pamela Colman Smith]], who created the widely used Rider-Waite-Smith deck.

## Different Tarot Deck Traditions

Throughout history, various Tarot deck traditions have emerged, each with its own artistic style and symbolic interpretations:

1. **Tarot de Marseille**: One of the oldest and most influential Tarot patterns, originating in southern France but based on the Italian tarot. It features simple, woodcut-style illustrations and is still used for both card games and divination.

2. **Rider-Waite-Smith Deck**: Created in 1909 by Arthur Edward Waite and illustrated by Pamela Colman Smith, this is perhaps the most widely recognized Tarot deck today. It features detailed symbolic illustrations on all 78 cards (unlike earlier decks where only the Major Arcana and Court cards were illustrated).

3. **Thoth Tarot**: Designed by Aleister Crowley and painted by Lady Frieda Harris between 1938 and 1943, this deck incorporates astrological, numerological, and Qabalistic symbolism.

4. **Visconti-Sforza Deck**: One of the oldest surviving Tarot decks, commissioned by the Visconti and Sforza families of Milan in the mid-15th century. These hand-painted cards represent the luxury and artistry of Renaissance Italy.

5. **Modern and Artistic Decks**: In recent decades, countless new Tarot decks have been created, incorporating diverse cultural influences, artistic styles, and thematic elements while maintaining the traditional structure.

## Contemporary Use and Significance

In contemporary times, Tarot has experienced a resurgence in popularity. While some still use Tarot for traditional card games, most modern usage focuses on divination, self-reflection, psychological insight, and personal growth. The rich symbolism of Tarot cards provides a framework for exploring the human experience and accessing intuitive wisdom.

The interpretations and applications of Tarot have evolved to incorporate psychological perspectives, including Jungian concepts of archetypes and the collective unconscious, which will be explored in depth in subsequent sections of this research.
