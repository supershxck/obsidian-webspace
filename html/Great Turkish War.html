The Great Turkish War (1683–1699), also known as the War of the Holy League, was a major conflict between the [[Ottoman Empire]] and a coalition of European powers led by the [[Holy Roman Empire]], under the auspices of the Holy League formed by [[Pope Innocent XI]]. It marked a turning point in the centuries-long struggle between the Ottomans and Christian Europe, halting Ottoman expansion and initiating their gradual retreat from Central Europe. Here’s a detailed explanation:

Background

  • Ottoman Peak and Decline: By the late 17th century, the Ottoman Empire, under the Köprülü viziers, had regained strength after internal strife. It controlled much of Southeast Europe, including Hungary, the Balkans, and parts of Ukraine, but faced growing resistance from European states.
  • Second Siege of Vienna (1683): The war’s catalyst was the Ottoman attempt to capture [[Vienna]], capital of the Habsburg-led Holy Roman Empire. Grand Vizier Kara Mustafa Pasha led a massive army (estimated 100,000–150,000) to besiege the city in July 1683, aiming to strike a decisive blow against Christendom.
  • Holy League: Alarmed by the Ottoman advance, Pope Innocent XI organized the Holy League in 1684, uniting the Habsburg Monarchy (Austria), Poland-Lithuania, Venice, and later Russia (1686), with support from Bavaria, Saxony, and other German states.

Major Phases and Battles

  1. Battle of Vienna (September 11–12, 1683):
  2. Context: Vienna, defended by 11,000 troops under Ernst Rüdiger von Starhemberg, held out for two months against the Ottoman siege.
  3. Relief: King John III Sobieski of Poland-Lithuania led a coalition force of 70,000–80,000, including his famed Winged Hussars, joining Habsburg and German troops. On September 12, a coordinated attack from Kahlenberg Hill routed the Ottomans.
  4. Outcome: Kara Mustafa fled, losing 15,000 men and his artillery; he was executed by order of Sultan Mehmed IV. Vienna’s salvation galvanized the Holy League.
  5. Hungarian Campaign (1684–1688):
  6. Reconquest: Habsburg forces, under Charles of Lorraine and Ludwig Wilhelm of Baden (“Türkenlouis”), pushed into Ottoman Hungary.
  7. Key Battles:
    • Buda (1686): After a 78-day siege, Buda, the Ottoman stronghold since 1541, fell to the Habsburgs, ending 145 years of Turkish rule in central Hungary.
    • Mohács (1687): A decisive victory crushed Ottoman resistance, reversing the 1526 Mohács defeat and liberating much of Hungary and Transylvania.
  8. Outcome: Most of Royal Hungary was reclaimed, though eastern Hungary remained contested.
  9. Venetian Campaigns (1684–1690):
  10. Focus: Venice targeted Ottoman holdings in the Morea (Peloponnese), Dalmatia, and the Aegean.
  11. Key Gains: Under Francesco Morosini, Venice captured the Morea (1687), Athens (1687, damaging the Parthenon), and parts of Dalmatia, weakening Ottoman naval power.
  12. Polish-Lithuanian Efforts: Poland secured Podolia and parts of Ukraine, though its role diminished after early victories.
  13. Russian Entry (1686): Tsar Peter I joined the League, launching the Crimean campaigns (1687, 1689) against Ottoman vassals, the Crimean Tatars. These were less successful but tied down Ottoman resources.
  14. Final Push (1690s):
  15. Ottoman Counteroffensive: Under Grand Vizier Mustafa Köprülü, the Ottomans briefly retook Belgrade (1690), but losses mounted.
  16. [[Battle of Zenta]] (1697): Prince Eugene of Savoy’s Habsburg army annihilated an Ottoman force under Sultan Mustafa II on the Tisza River, killing 20,000–30,000 and capturing the imperial treasury.

Treaty of Karlowitz (January 26, 1699)

  • Negotiations: After Zenta, the exhausted Ottomans sued for peace. The treaty, signed in Karlowitz (modern Sremski Karlovci, Serbia), was mediated by England and the Dutch Republic.
  • Terms:
  • Habsburgs: Gained Hungary, Transylvania, Croatia, and Slavonia, leaving only the Banat of Temesvár under Ottoman control.
  • Venice: Kept the Morea, parts of Dalmatia, and Aegean islands.
  • Poland-Lithuania: Recovered Podolia and Right-Bank Ukraine.
  • Russia: Secured Azov (via the separate Treaty of Constantinople, 1700), marking its Black Sea foothold.
  • Significance: The first major treaty where the Ottomans ceded territory without compensation, signaling their decline as a European power.

Why the Holy League Won

  • Coalition Unity: Diverse allies coordinated effectively, unlike earlier fragmented resistance to the Ottomans.
  • Military Leadership: Commanders like Sobieski, Eugene, and Morosini outmaneuvered Ottoman generals, leveraging cavalry and artillery.
  • Ottoman Overreach: The Vienna siege stretched Ottoman logistics; internal dissent and corruption weakened their response.
  • Technological Edge: European armies adopted modern tactics and fortifications, while Ottoman reliance on massed infantry faltered.

Aftermath

  • Ottoman Retreat: The empire shifted to a defensive stance, losing its aura of invincibility. It retained the Balkans but faced growing pressure there in the 18th century.
  • Habsburg Ascendancy: Austria emerged as a dominant Central European power, consolidating control over Hungary and the Balkans’ northern fringe.
  • Venetian Revival: Venice briefly regained maritime influence, though it later declined.
  • Russian Rise: The war marked Russia’s entry into European geopolitics, setting the stage for further Ottoman conflicts.
  • Cultural Impact: The victory fueled European triumphalism, with art and literature (e.g., Viennese coffeehouse myths) celebrating the rollback of the “Turkish menace.”

Significance

  • Turning Point: Historians like Jeremy Black view it as the end of Ottoman expansion in Europe, reversing centuries of westward pressure since Manzikert (1071).
  • Balance of Power: It shifted momentum to Christian states, foreshadowing the Ottoman Empire’s “Long Decline” until its dissolution in 1922.
  • Legacy: The war’s memory endures in European national narratives (e.g., Poland’s “Savior of Vienna” trope) and Ottoman historiography as a moment of reckoning.
