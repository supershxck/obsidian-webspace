The Council of Ephesus was the third ecumenical council of the Christian Church, convened in 431 AD under the presidency of Cyril of Alexandria. It was held in Ephesus, a city in modern-day Turkey, to address several doctrinal disputes, most notably the nature of Christ and the title of the Virgin Mary as Theotokos, meaning "God-bearer" or "Mother of God."

The council was primarily convened to address the teachings of [[Nestorius]], the Patriarch of Constantinople. Nestorius had argued against the use of the title Theotokos for Mary, suggesting that she should instead be called Christotokos, or "Christ-bearer," to emphasize that she was the mother of Christ's human nature, not his divine nature. This teaching implied a division between the human and divine natures of Christ, suggesting that they were two separate persons rather than one.

Cyril of Alexandria and other church leaders saw Nestorius's teaching as a threat to the unity of Christ's person and to the mystery of the Incarnation. They argued that Jesus Christ was fully God and fully man in one person, and that Mary, as the mother of Christ, was rightly called Theotokos because she bore God the Word made flesh.

The Council of Ephesus declared Nestorius's teachings heretical, affirming that Jesus Christ is one person in two natures, fully divine and fully human, without confusion or separation. It upheld the title of Theotokos for Mary, affirming her role in the mystery of the Incarnation and the unity of Christ's person.

The council's decisions had a profound impact on Christian doctrine, particularly Christology, the study of the nature and work of Christ. It solidified the understanding of the hypostatic union, the union of Christ's two natures in one person, as a central tenet of Christian theology. The Council of Ephesus also deepened the devotion to Mary within the Christian tradition, emphasizing her unique role in salvation history.

The council's outcomes led to further theological disputes and contributed to the schism between the churches of the East and West, particularly in relation to the later Council of Chalcedon in 451, which further defined Christological doctrine. Nonetheless, the Council of Ephesus remains a cornerstone of orthodox Christian teaching on the nature of Christ and the role of the Virgin Mary.
