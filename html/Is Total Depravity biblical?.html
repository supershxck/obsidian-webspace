The term "doctrines of grace" typically refers to a Calvinist or Reformed theological framework emphasizing total depravity, unconditional election, limited atonement, irresistible grace, and perseverance of the saints (summarized by the acronym TULIP). These doctrines articulate a particular understanding of salvation and divine grace that is closely associated with the teachings of John Calvin and the Reformed tradition. The Eastern Orthodox Church, with its distinct theological tradition, articulates its understanding of grace and salvation differently, focusing on themes like theosis, synergy, and divine-human cooperation.

Theosis (Deification)

One of the central tenets of Eastern Orthodox soteriology (doctrine of salvation) is theosis, or deification, which is the process of becoming more like God or partaking in the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4). Theosis is a transformative process involving the entire human person, body and soul, and is made possible through the Incarnation, Crucifixion, and Resurrection of Christ. It is not an escape from humanity but the fulfillment of human destiny in union with God. This concept emphasizes the positive potential of humanity to be united with God rather than focusing primarily on human depravity.

Synergy

In contrast to the Calvinist view of irresistible grace, the Eastern Orthodox Church teaches the concept of synergy between divine grace and human freedom. Synergy refers to cooperation in which God offers his grace freely, and humans respond with their own effort. Salvation is thus seen as a collaborative process between God and the person, where both parties participate. This perspective maintains the importance of human freedom and responsibility in the process of salvation, suggesting that while God's grace is necessary and initiatory, human beings must also willingly cooperate with this grace.

Divine-Human Cooperation

Related to the concept of synergy is the broader Eastern Orthodox understanding of divine-human cooperation throughout the Christian life. This cooperation is not limited to the initial moment of salvation but extends to all aspects of Christian living, including prayer, worship, and the practice of virtues. The Eastern Orthodox view sees grace as both healing and enabling, restoring the image of God in humans and empowering them to live a life of holiness.

Sacramental Life

The Eastern Orthodox Church emphasizes the importance of the sacraments (or mysteries) as means of receiving God's grace. Baptism, Eucharist, Confession, and other sacraments are vital to the Christian life and are understood as tangible, material means through which God communicates grace to believers. These sacraments are integral to the process of theosis, providing strength and grace for the journey toward union with God.

Conclusion

While the Eastern Orthodox Church and the Reformed tradition have different emphases and theological frameworks, it's important to recognize that both traditions seek to articulate the mystery of God's grace and the marvel of salvation. The Eastern Orthodox emphasis on theosis, synergy, and divine-human cooperation offers a distinctive perspective that highlights the transformative journey of becoming like God, the importance of human freedom, and the sacramental life of the Church.

The Eastern Orthodox Church's response to the concept of total depravity, as articulated in many Reformed traditions, is nuanced and reflects its unique theological perspective. Total depravity, in the Reformed context, posits that as a result of the Fall, every part of human nature is corrupted by sin, and humans are incapable of choosing or doing good without God's divine intervention.

Eastern Orthodox Perspective on Human Nature Post-Fall:

  1. Ancestral Sin vs. Original Sin: Eastern Orthodoxy distinguishes between the Western concept of Original Sin, often associated with a hereditary guilt passed down from Adam and Eve, and what it terms "Ancestral Sin." Ancestral Sin refers to the fallen condition inherited from Adam and Eve, which includes death and a propensity towards sin, rather than guilt for their sin. Humanity inherits corruption and mortality, not the guilt of Adam and Eve's specific transgressions.

  2. Image and Likeness of God: The Eastern Orthodox Church teaches that, despite the Fall, humanity retains the "image of God" (Genesis 1:26-27). While this image is tarnished or marred by sin, it is not utterly destroyed. Thus, humans, even in their fallen state, are capable of good because of this indelible image within them. This perspective holds that there remains an inherent value and potential for goodness in humanity because of the divine image.

  3. Freedom and Cooperation (Synergy): Unlike the notion of total depravity that emphasizes human inability to choose good without divine intervention, Eastern Orthodoxy posits that humans retain free will. While deeply affected by sin, humans are still capable of cooperating with God's grace. This cooperation (synergy) is essential for salvation, where God's grace initiates and sustains the process of salvation, but human response and cooperation are also necessary.

  4. Healing, Not Just Forensic Justification: The Eastern Orthodox view of salvation focuses more on healing and restoration rather than a legal or forensic justification. It sees sin more as a sickness that affects the whole human person (body and soul) and requires healing, rather than a legal problem that necessitates a judicial solution. Christ, the Physician of our souls and bodies, comes not just to change God's attitude towards humanity but to change humanity itself, healing us from the inside out.

  5. Potential for Theosis: Eastern Orthodoxy's ultimate goal for humanity is theosis or deification, where humans partake of the divine nature through grace. This process of becoming like God or growing in union with God presupposes that humans, though fallen, are capable of receiving and responding to God's grace. The journey of theosis is seen as a path of healing, transformation, and ultimately, union with God.

In summary, while acknowledging the serious impact of sin on human nature and the world, the Eastern Orthodox Church offers a more optimistic view of human potential post-Fall than the concept of total depravity allows. It emphasizes the retained image of God within humanity, the possibility of cooperation with divine grace, and the ultimate goal of healing and union with God through theosis.
