The Ordovices were a Celtic tribe in Iron Age and Roman Britain, known for their fierce resistance to Roman conquest. Their territory was located in what is now northwest and central Wales, including Anglesey, Gwynedd, Snowdonia, and parts of Powys. The rugged, mountainous terrain of their homeland made them formidable opponents and allowed them to maintain a strong tribal identity.

Geography and Territory

• The Ordovices occupied a region characterized by mountain ranges, river valleys, and coastal areas, such as the Menai Strait near Anglesey.

• This difficult terrain provided natural defenses and made it challenging for Roman forces to subdue them.

• Their name, Ordovices, may derive from a Celtic root meaning “hammer,” possibly symbolizing their warlike nature.

Society and Economy

  1. Agriculture and Livelihood:

• The Ordovices relied on a mixed economy, combining agriculture, livestock rearing (especially sheep and cattle), and limited trade.

• Farming was challenging in their hilly region, so they likely practiced pastoralism and subsistence agriculture.

  1. Settlements:

• Their settlements were small and scattered, often located in defensible positions.

• Evidence of hillforts such as those at Pen Dinas and Tre’r Ceiri indicates that the Ordovices prioritized security and tribal unity.

  1. Craftsmanship:

• The tribe likely engaged in metalworking, crafting tools, weapons, and ornaments using local resources such as iron and bronze.

Conflict with Rome

The Ordovices are best known for their resistance to Roman conquest during the 1st century AD.

  1. Initial Resistance:

• When the Romans began their conquest of Britain in AD 43, the Ordovices joined other tribes, such as the Silures, in resisting Roman expansion into Wales.

• Their mountainous terrain made them difficult to subjugate, and they engaged in guerrilla tactics to harass Roman forces.

  1. Role of Caratacus:

• After the defeat of the Catuvellauni, the anti-Roman leader Caratacus fled west and allied with the Ordovices.

• Caratacus rallied the Ordovices to resist the Romans and led them in a significant battle, believed to have occurred in AD 50. However, they were ultimately defeated by Roman governor Ostorius Scapula, and Caratacus was captured.

  1. Destruction by Agricola:

• By the late 1st century AD, the Ordovices had largely recovered and were still resisting Roman rule.

• Around AD 77–78, during the governorship of Gnaeus Julius Agricola, the Ordovices wiped out a Roman cavalry unit stationed in their territory.

• Agricola retaliated with a brutal campaign, massacring much of the tribe and subjugating their lands. This marked the effective end of the Ordovices as an independent political entity.

Religion and Spiritual Practices

• The Ordovices, like other Celtic tribes, practiced polytheism, worshiping deities associated with nature, fertility, and war.

• Sacred sites in their territory, such as lakes, rivers, and hilltops, were likely centers of ritual activity.

• The nearby island of Anglesey (Ynys Môn) was a center of druidic activity, and the Ordovices may have had close ties to the druids based there.

Legacy

  1. Survival and Romanization:

• After their defeat, the Ordovices’ territory was integrated into the Roman province of Britannia.

• Roman forts and settlements, such as Segontium near modern Caernarfon, were established in their lands to maintain control.

• Despite Roman influence, the mountainous terrain of Wales allowed elements of Celtic culture to survive.

  1. Historical Importance:

• The Ordovices symbolize the resilience of Celtic tribes against Roman imperialism.

• Their fierce resistance contributed to the Romans’ difficulty in pacifying Wales, which remained one of the last regions of Britain to be fully subdued.

  1. Archaeological Evidence:

• Hillforts, weapons, and Roman military sites in their territory provide insight into their culture and their struggles against Rome.
