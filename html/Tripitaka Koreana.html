The Tripitaka Koreana: A Monumental Buddhist Canon

The Tripitaka Koreana (팔만대장경, 八萬大藏經) is a comprehensive collection of Buddhist scriptures engraved on over 80,000 wooden printing blocks during the Goryeo Dynasty (918–1392). Renowned for its accuracy, craftsmanship, and spiritual significance, it is one of Korea’s most treasured cultural artifacts and a UNESCO World Heritage item. The Tripitaka Koreana reflects the Goryeo Dynasty’s deep commitment to Buddhism and its enduring influence on Korean culture.

1. Overview

a. What Is the Tripitaka?

• The term Tripitaka (or “Three Baskets”) refers to the core teachings of Buddhism, divided into three main categories:

  1. Sutra (Sūtra-pitaka): Discourses of the Buddha.

  2. Vinaya (Vinaya-pitaka): Monastic rules and discipline.

  3. Abhidharma (Abhidharma-pitaka): Philosophical and doctrinal analysis.

b. What Makes the Tripitaka Koreana Unique?

• The Tripitaka Koreana is not merely a transcription of the Buddhist canon but a masterpiece of woodblock printing technology and scholarly precision.

• It contains approximately 52 million characters carved onto 81,258 wooden blocks, meticulously organized into over 6,500 volumes.

2. Historical Background

a. First Edition (Tripitaka Goryeo)

• Creation:

• The first version of the Tripitaka was commissioned in 1011 CE during the reign of King Hyeonjong.

• It was an offering to seek divine protection against Khitan invasions during the Goryeo-Khitan Wars.

• Destruction:

• The original set was destroyed in 1232 CE during the Mongol invasions, as the Mongols burned down the temple storing it.

b. Second Edition (Current Tripitaka Koreana)

• Recommissioning:

• After the destruction of the first edition, King Gojong (r. 1213–1259) ordered the creation of a new set in 1236 CE to invoke divine intervention against the ongoing Mongol invasions.

• Completion:

• The project took 16 years, completed in 1251 CE.

• The blocks were carved by teams of scholars, monks, and artisans with extraordinary precision and dedication.

3. Production and Craftsmanship

a. Material and Preparation

• Wood:

• The blocks were made from birchwood, treated to resist decay.

• Each block measures approximately 70 cm x 24 cm x 3 cm.

• Preservation Techniques:

• The wood was soaked in seawater for three years, dried in the shade, and boiled to remove sap.

• Finished blocks were carefully polished and carved to ensure durability.

b. Engraving Process

• Accuracy:

• Each block was checked multiple times by scholars to ensure no errors or omissions.

• This meticulous process has made the Tripitaka Koreana one of the most accurate versions of the Buddhist canon in existence.

• Craftsmanship:

• The carving is noted for its uniform calligraphy, reflecting the skill and dedication of Goryeo artisans.

4. Storage and Preservation

a. Haeinsa Temple

• The Tripitaka Koreana is housed at Haeinsa Temple in South Korea, a UNESCO World Heritage Site located in Gyeongsangnam-do.

• The temple’s storage facility, called the Janggyeong Panjeon, was specifically designed to preserve the wooden blocks.

b. Architectural Features

• The Janggyeong Panjeon consists of two long storage buildings and two smaller halls, built with unique architectural methods to regulate temperature and humidity.

• Features include:

• Open ventilation: To ensure airflow.

• Raised floors: To protect against moisture.

• Walls with varying thickness: To balance seasonal temperature changes.

c. State of Preservation

• Despite being over 750 years old, the wooden blocks remain remarkably well-preserved due to the sophisticated design of the storage facilities.

5. Significance

a. Religious Importance

• Offering for Protection:

• Both editions of the Tripitaka were commissioned as acts of piety to protect Korea from foreign invasions.

• Spiritual Value:

• The Tripitaka Koreana represents the Goryeo Dynasty’s profound devotion to Buddhism.

b. Scholarly and Cultural Value

• Accuracy:

• It is considered one of the most accurate and complete collections of Buddhist texts.

• Legacy:

• The project embodies Korea’s rich cultural heritage and contributions to Buddhist scholarship.

c. Technological Achievement

• The meticulous production of the Tripitaka Koreana demonstrates the advanced craftsmanship and technical expertise of Goryeo artisans.

• It predates Gutenberg’s movable-type printing press and highlights Korea’s leadership in early printing technology.

6. Influence and Legacy

a. Impact on Buddhism

• The Tripitaka Koreana remains a vital resource for Buddhist studies and rituals in Korea and beyond.

• Its texts have been used as the basis for later versions of the Buddhist canon throughout East Asia.

b. Symbol of Resilience

• The production of the Tripitaka Koreana during the Mongol invasions reflects the Korean people’s resilience and commitment to preserving their culture.

c. Modern Recognition

• UNESCO Heritage:

• The Tripitaka Koreana was designated as a UNESCO Memory of the World in 2007.

• Tourism and Scholarship:

• Haeinsa Temple attracts scholars, practitioners, and tourists from around the world, symbolizing Korea’s rich Buddhist heritage.

9. Conclusion

The Tripitaka Koreana is a monumental achievement in religious devotion, cultural heritage, and technological innovation. It stands as a testament to the dedication and resilience of the Goryeo Dynasty and remains one of the most accurate and revered collections of Buddhist scriptures in history. Its preservation at Haeinsa Temple symbolizes Korea’s enduring respect for its spiritual and cultural legacy.
