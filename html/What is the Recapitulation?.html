The recapitulation theory is a theological concept that originates from the early Church Father Irenaeus of Lyons in the 2nd century. The term "recapitulation" comes from the Latin "recapitulatio," which translates the Greek "anakephal aiosis." This concept is particularly discussed in Irenaeus's work, "Against Heresies," where he presents the idea that Christ "recapitulates" or "sums up" all of human history and experience in Himself, thus undoing the disobedience of Adam and restoring humanity to a right relationship with God.

According to the recapitulation theory, Jesus Christ, in becoming human, relives the stages of human life and history but in perfect obedience to God, thereby reversing the effects of Adam's sin. Where Adam's disobedience brought sin and death into the world, Christ's obedience brings righteousness and life. This includes not just Christ's life and teachings but His suffering, death, and resurrection, which are seen as the pivotal moment of this redemptive recapitulation.

In essence, Irenaeus sees Jesus as a new Adam who corrects Adam's mistake, embodying the perfect image of God that humans were intended to be. Through His incarnation, life, death, and resurrection, Jesus gathers all things unto Himself, reconciling the world to God and making possible the salvation of humanity.

The recapitulation theory offers a comprehensive view of salvation history, emphasizing the unity and purposefulness of God's plan from creation to redemption. It also highlights the significance of the Incarnation as the central event of human history, through which Christ not only redeems humanity but also all of creation.
