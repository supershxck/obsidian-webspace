Introduction

The concept of “real” has occupied philosophers, scientists, and mystics for centuries. While many schools of thought offer varied definitions, “real” often refers to that which has undeniable existence or manifests effects in our shared experience. Yet, with subjective consciousness playing a central role in how we interpret the world, the boundary between “real” and “perceived” can be remarkably fluid. This ambiguity appears in fields as diverse as neuroscience, [[Theology]], and even modern esoteric systems like [[Chaos Magic]].

1. Philosophical Perspectives

  1. Materialism

• Core View: The real is that which can be observed or measured objectively.

• Implication: Anything beyond physical phenomena—such as subjective experiences—can be difficult to classify as fully real.

  1. Idealism

• Core View: Reality is fundamentally mental or immaterial; the physical world might be an illusion or a construct of consciousness.

• Implication: “Realness” hinges on the consistency or coherence of mental phenomena.

  1. Phenomenology

• Core View: Prioritizes lived experience and the structures of consciousness itself.

• Implication: That which is “real” is intimately bound to what individuals experience directly, making subjective perception central.

2. Real in a Psychological Sense

  1. Cognitive Construction

• Our brains interpret sensory data to build an internal model of the world. In this view, “real” can refer to whatever consistently appears in these mental models.

• Hallucinations or dreams highlight the fragility of this distinction: they feel real subjectively but lack external confirmation.

  1. Influence of [[Imagination]]

• While imagination conjures images or ideas without direct sensory input, it can still produce effects in the mind and body (e.g., emotional reactions).

• In psychological terms, a strongly held belief—real or imagined—can alter behavior and perception in tangible ways.

3. Real in Spiritual and Esoteric Contexts

  1. [[Shamanism]]

• Shamans journey into non-ordinary realms that, while not physically measurable, yield knowledge or healing in daily life.

• Within shamanic practice, the question of “What is real?” is often replaced by “What is useful or impactful?”—a pragmatic approach also seen in [[Chaos Magic]].

  1. [[Theology]]

• Many theological traditions distinguish between temporal (physical) reality and a transcendent or divine reality.

• For believers, divine truths are “real” in a spiritual sense—even if they aren’t empirically verifiable.

  1. [[Spiritual Alchemy]]

• Alchemists historically sought to transform base metals into gold, but many interpret these processes as metaphors for internal change.

• The “real” transformation can be an inner psychological shift, even if no physical transmutation occurs in the crucible.

  1. [[Chaos Magic]]

• Adopts a flexible view of reality, treating belief as a mutable tool. If a chosen belief yields measurable results for the practitioner, it’s considered effectively “real.”

• This results-oriented stance underscores how subjective or symbolic systems can acquire real power through faith and practice.

4. Interplay Between Subjective and Objective

  1. Consensus Reality

• We often deem “real” that which multiple observers confirm. Scientific experiments, for example, rely on reproducible data to validate phenomena.

• Yet, consensus can shift with new discoveries or changing cultural frameworks—highlighting that the line between real and unreal is sometimes negotiable.

  1. Personal Reality

• An individual’s subjective experiences—emotions, thoughts, visions—carry genuine weight in shaping their worldview, regardless of outside validation.

• This tension between consensus and personal reality fuels both philosophical debates and everyday conflicts.

5. Pragmatic Definition of Real

For many thinkers, “real” is that which produces tangible consequences, whether in physical form (e.g., a tree in the forest) or in psychological/spiritual ways (e.g., a personal revelation during meditation). If it impacts behavior, emotions, or shared social structures, it can be considered “real” in a functional sense—even if it defies classic material explanation.

Conclusion

Defining “real” ultimately depends on the lens through which one views existence—materialist, idealist, spiritual, psychological, or otherwise. While scientific methods prioritize objective verification, many esoteric and spiritual systems emphasize subjective efficacy and transformation. In practice, the best working definition may be the one that balances shared verifiability with individual experience, acknowledging both the solidity of observable phenomena and the powerful role belief and consciousness play in shaping our world.
