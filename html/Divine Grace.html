What is grace? In Eastern Orthodox theology, grace is understood as the uncreated energy of God that is given to humans and all of creation for the purpose of sanctification and salvation. Grace is not created; rather, it is the very presence and action of God Himself in the world and in the life of believers. This concept distinguishes Orthodox understanding from some Western Christian views where grace might be considered a created gift bestowed by God.

Key Features of Orthodox Understanding of Grace:

  1. Uncreated and Divine: Grace is not something God creates; it is part of His own divine life. It is God acting in the world, in the Church, and within individuals.
  2. Energies of God: Grace is often described in the context of the "energies" of God. These energies are God’s own natural workings and presence that can be participated in by the faithful without them becoming part of God’s essence (which remains transcendent and inaccessible).
  3. Means of Theosis (Deification): One of the primary purposes of grace in Orthodox theology is theosis, or deification. This is the process by which humans become partakers of the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4) through union with Christ and the operation of the Holy Spirit. Grace is what enables this transformative union.
  4. Sacramental and Mystical: Grace is mediated to the faithful especially through the sacraments of the Church. Each sacrament imparts grace in a specific way that aids the believer in their spiritual journey and growth in holiness.
  5. Synergistic: In Orthodox theology, the relationship between divine grace and human freedom is synergistic, meaning that it involves cooperation between human will and divine grace. While God initiates and supports with His grace, human beings must freely respond to and cooperate with that grace to bring about spiritual growth and salvation.
  6. Transformational: Grace transforms the believer's nature and actions. It is not merely a forensic (legal) declaration of righteousness as sometimes viewed in certain Protestant theologies, but an actual impartation of divine life that changes the person.

Theological Foundations:

Orthodox theology often draws upon the writings of the Church Fathers, such as St. Gregory Palamas, who articulated distinctions between God’s essence and energies, thereby providing a framework for understanding how humans can partake of God’s life (grace) without compromising the doctrine of God’s transcendence.

In summary, grace in Eastern Orthodoxy is the uncreated energy of God, freely given to all through Christ and the Holy Spirit, enabling believers to partake of the divine nature and grow into the likeness of Christ, thus emphasizing a direct and transformative experience of God.
