St. Anselm of Canterbury: An Overview

Introduction

St. Anselm of Canterbury (1033–1109) was an Italian Benedictine monk, philosopher, theologian, and Archbishop of Canterbury. He is regarded as one of the most influential medieval thinkers, particularly in the areas of theology and philosophy. Anselm is best known for his formulation of the ontological argument for the existence of God and his contributions to scholasticism—a method of theology and philosophy that aimed to reconcile faith with reason.

Anselm’s works laid the foundation for later developments in both medieval theology and philosophy, influencing figures like Thomas Aquinas and shaping Christian intellectual traditions. He is honored as a Doctor of the Church, and his feast day is celebrated on April 21st.

1. Early Life and Background

1.1 Birth and Early Years

Anselm was born in 1033 in Aosta, a town in the northern part of Italy, which was then part of the Kingdom of Burgundy. He was born into a noble family, but his early life was marked by personal challenges, including the loss of his mother when he was young and the strained relationship with his father. Anselm was drawn to monastic life early on and entered the Benedictine monastery at Bee, in Normandy, France, at the age of 15.

• Spiritual Formation

At Bee, Anselm studied under Lanfranc, a renowned scholar and theologian. During his time in the monastery, Anselm immersed himself in theological studies, particularly in the works of Augustine and other early Christian thinkers, which deeply influenced his own theological development.

1.2 Monastic Life and Rise to Leadership

Anselm’s commitment to intellectual and spiritual life led him to rise through the ranks of the monastery, and in 1078, he became the abbot of the monastery of Bec. Under his leadership, the abbey became a center of intellectual activity, attracting students from across Europe. Anselm’s reputation as a scholar and theologian grew rapidly, and in 1093, he was appointed Archbishop of Canterbury by King William II of England.

• Political Challenges

Anselm’s tenure as Archbishop of Canterbury was marked by significant political and ecclesiastical struggles, particularly with the English monarchy. His commitment to the independence of the Church from royal influence often put him at odds with the king, and he spent time in exile during conflicts over Church authority and investiture (the practice of monarchs appointing bishops). Despite these challenges, Anselm remained a passionate advocate for the spiritual and intellectual life of the Church.

2. Anselm’s Theological Contributions

2.1 The Ontological Argument for the Existence of God

Anselm’s most famous philosophical contribution is the ontological argument for the existence of God. This argument, first presented in his work Proslogion (1077–1078), aims to demonstrate the existence of God through reason alone, based on the very concept of God.

• The Argument

Anselm defines God as “that than which nothing greater can be conceived.” He argues that if such a being exists in the mind (as an idea), it must also exist in reality because existence in reality is greater than existence in the mind alone. Therefore, the greatest conceivable being (God) must exist both in the mind and in reality.

• Criticism and Influence

The ontological argument has been the subject of much debate and criticism throughout the history of philosophy. Philosophers like Immanuel Kant and David Hume questioned its validity, while others, such as René Descartes and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, supported it. Anselm’s argument remains one of the most significant attempts to demonstrate the existence of God through pure reason.

2.2 Satisfaction Theory of Atonement

Anselm is also famous for developing the satisfaction theory of atonement, which explained how Christ’s death on the cross restores humanity’s relationship with God. This theory is most fully articulated in his work Cur Deus Homo (“Why God Became Man”), written in 1097–1098.

• Satisfaction for Sin

Anselm argued that humanity’s sin had dishonored God, and that only someone who was both fully human and fully divine could make the appropriate satisfaction for the offense. Christ’s death, in Anselm’s view, was the necessary act that satisfied God’s justice while offering mercy to humanity.

• The Impact

Anselm’s satisfaction theory of atonement significantly influenced medieval Christian thought, shaping later theological discussions on the nature of sin, atonement, and redemption. It also influenced subsequent thinkers, including Thomas Aquinas, who adapted Anselm’s ideas into the framework of Scholastic theology.

2.3 Faith Seeking Understanding (Fides Quaerens Intellectum)

Anselm is often associated with the principle of “faith seeking understanding” (in Latin, fides quaerens intellectum). This phrase encapsulates his approach to theology, which combines deep faith in the revealed truths of Christianity with the use of reason and intellect to explore and explain those truths.

• Reason and Faith

Anselm argued that faith and reason are not opposed but rather complementary. Reason, when used correctly, can help understand and articulate the truths revealed by faith. He believed that through careful theological reflection, believers could deepen their understanding of God’s nature and the mysteries of faith.

• Anselm’s Intellectual Legacy

Anselm’s approach to theology laid the foundation for later Scholasticism, particularly the work of Thomas Aquinas, who further developed the relationship between faith and reason. Anselm’s insistence on rational inquiry into divine matters shaped the intellectual tradition of the Middle Ages.

3. Anselm’s Writings and Influence

3.1 Major Works

Anselm was a prolific writer, and his works are foundational in Christian philosophy and theology. Some of his most important writings include:

• Proslogion (1077–1078): The work in which Anselm presents the ontological argument for the existence of God. It also explores the nature of God and the relationship between faith and reason.

• Cur Deus Homo (1097–1098): A treatise on the atonement and why it was necessary for God to become human in the form of Jesus Christ to restore humanity’s relationship with God.

• Monologion (1076): A work in which Anselm discusses the existence and attributes of God, arguing for the existence of a supreme being through reason.

• De Concordia Primum Principium (On the Harmony of the First Principle): A work that presents Anselm’s views on the nature of the universe and the relationship between God and creation.

Anselm’s writings deeply influenced the medieval scholastic tradition, particularly the work of Thomas Aquinas and other theologians who sought to reconcile faith and reason.

3.2 Anselm’s Influence on Later Thinkers

Anselm’s philosophical and theological works had a significant influence on Christian thought, especially in the development of Scholasticism and the understanding of the nature of God, atonement, and theology. His ontological argument continues to be studied and debated in philosophy and theology.

• Scholastics: Anselm’s contributions laid the groundwork for later Scholastics like Aquinas and Duns Scotus, who built upon Anselm’s ideas to develop more systematic and detailed theological frameworks.

• Modern Philosophy: Although the ontological argument was critiqued by later philosophers such as Immanuel Kant, it continues to influence discussions in contemporary philosophy of religion. Modern philosophers such as Alvin Plantinga have revived Anselm’s ideas in the context of modal logic.

4. Legacy and Canonization

4.1 Recognition and Canonization

St. Anselm was canonized as a saint by Pope Clement XI in 1720, recognizing his importance as a theologian and philosopher in the history of the Church. In 2000, Pope John Paul II declared Anselm to be one of the Doctors of the Church, a title given to those whose teachings have had a lasting impact on Catholic theology.

4.2 Anselm’s Influence Today

Anselm’s legacy continues to shape the fields of philosophy and theology. His ontological argument remains a central topic of study in philosophy of religion, and his works on the atonement and the nature of God have deeply influenced Christian thought. His approach to faith seeking understanding continues to resonate in Christian intellectual life, where reason is seen as a tool for deepening one’s faith and understanding of God.

5. Conclusion

St. Anselm of Canterbury was one of the greatest theologians and philosophers of the medieval Church. His groundbreaking work on the ontological argument, the nature of God, and the atonement has had a lasting impact on Christian theology and philosophy. Anselm’s emphasis on the harmony between faith and reason provided a foundation for later Scholasticism and intellectual inquiry within Christianity. His works continue to be studied and debated, and his contributions to Christian thought ensure his enduring legacy in both theological and philosophical circles.
