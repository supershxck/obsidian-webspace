Several early Church Fathers referenced the Apocrypha (or Deuterocanonical books) in their writings and considered them as part of Holy Scripture. Here are some notable examples: 1. Clement of Alexandria (c. 150-215 AD) Clement of Alexandria frequently quoted from the Apocryphal books in his works, treating them with the same authority as other canonical texts. He cited books like the Wisdom of Solomon and Sirach in his theological arguments. 2. Origen (c. 185-254 AD) Origen referenced the Apocrypha in his works and included these books in his Hexapla, a critical edition of the Old Testament. He regarded them as useful and authoritative, often quoting from Judith, Tobit, and Wisdom. 3. Athanasius of Alexandria (c. 296-373 AD) While Athanasius provided a list of canonical books in his 39th Festal Letter, which did not include the Apocrypha, he still acknowledged the usefulness of these books for edification. He often quoted from them in his theological discussions. 4. Augustine of Hippo (354-430 AD) Augustine strongly affirmed the canonicity of the Apocryphal books. At the Councils of Hippo (393 AD) and Carthage (397 AD), where he was a leading figure, the Apocryphal books were included in the list of canonical scriptures. Augustine frequently quoted from Wisdom, Sirach, Tobit, and other Deuterocanonical books in his writings. 5. Cyprian of Carthage (c. 200-258 AD) Cyprian referenced the Apocryphal books in his epistles and treatises, treating them with the same level of authority as other parts of Scripture. He often quoted from Baruch, Wisdom, and Sirach. 6. Ambrose of Milan (c. 340-397 AD) Ambrose quoted extensively from the Apocrypha in his theological works and homilies, considering them as part of the Holy Scriptures. He referenced books like Tobit, Judith, and Wisdom. 7. Jerome (c. 347-420 AD) Although Jerome was initially skeptical about the canonicity of the Apocryphal books, he eventually included them in his Latin Vulgate translation of the Bible at the request of the Church. He recognized their widespread use and value within the Christian tradition. 8. Cyril of Jerusalem (c. 313-386 AD) Cyril of Jerusalem included the Apocryphal books in his list of scriptures in his "Catechetical Lectures." He frequently quoted from these texts, especially Wisdom and Sirach.

Biblical Citations in Their Writings Clement of Alexandria: - Wisdom of Solomon: Clement often quoted Wisdom to support Christian doctrines on the nature of God and the soul. Origen: - Judith: In his "Contra Celsum," Origen references the bravery and piety of Judith as an example for Christians. Augustine: - Sirach: Augustine quotes Sirach in his work "The City of God" to illustrate points on morality and divine wisdom. Ambrose: - Tobit: Ambrose uses the story of Tobit in his ethical discussions on charity and virtue.

These early Church Fathers demonstrate the significant role the Apocryphal books played in early Christian theology and biblical interpretation. Their citations and endorsements indicate that these texts were widely read and respected within early Christian communities.

Early Church Fathers quoted a wide range of books from the Apocrypha/Deuterocanonicals and other writings that were influential in early Christian thought. Here are some additional books and the Fathers who referenced them:

Additional Apocryphal/Deuterocanonical Books Book of Tobit - Cyprian of Carthage: Referenced Tobit in his treatises on almsgiving and charity. - Ambrose of Milan: Quoted Tobit in his discussions on virtue and angelic intercession.

Book of Judith - Clement of Alexandria: Cited Judith to emphasize examples of faith and bravery. - Origen: Mentioned Judith in his works, highlighting her piety and role in delivering Israel.

Wisdom of Solomon - Clement of Alexandria: Extensively quoted Wisdom in his theological discussions on the nature of God and the soul. - Augustine of Hippo: Used Wisdom to support doctrines on divine providence and immortality.

Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) - Cyril of Jerusalem: Quoted Sirach in his "Catechetical Lectures." - Augustine of Hippo: Referenced Sirach frequently in his ethical and theological writings.

Baruch - Cyprian of Carthage: Referenced Baruch in his epistles and treatises. - Ambrose of Milan: Used Baruch in his discussions on repentance and humility.

1 Maccabees - Origen: Mentioned 1 Maccabees in his writings, using it to illustrate the historical faithfulness of the Jewish people. - Augustine of Hippo: Quoted 1 Maccabees to draw parallels between the Maccabean revolt and Christian struggles.

2 Maccabees - Clement of Alexandria: Referenced 2 Maccabees to discuss martyrdom and the resurrection. - Ambrose of Milan: Used 2 Maccabees to emphasize the theme of sacrifice and divine justice.

Additions to Daniel (Prayer of Azariah, Susanna, Bel and the Dragon) - Jerome: Included these additions in his Vulgate translation, acknowledging their traditional use. - Cyril of Jerusalem: Quoted the Prayer of Azariah and Susanna in his lectures.

Other Writings and Influential Texts Epistle of Barnabas - Clement of Alexandria: Frequently quoted the Epistle of Barnabas in his "Stromata." - Origen: Referenced Barnabas to support interpretations of Old Testament prophecy.

Shepherd of Hermas - Irenaeus of Lyons: Quoted the Shepherd of Hermas to discuss themes of repentance and church discipline. - Clement of Alexandria: Referenced the Shepherd of Hermas in his ethical teachings.

1 Clement - Irenaeus of Lyons: Quoted 1 Clement to emphasize apostolic authority and church unity. - Clement of Alexandria: Referenced 1 Clement in his discussions on humility and obedience.

Didache - Athanasius of Alexandria: Referenced the Didache in his letters, acknowledging its role in early Christian instruction. - Origen: Mentioned the Didache in his homilies, noting its importance for understanding early Christian practice.

Epistle of Polycarp to the Philippians - Irenaeus of Lyons: Quoted Polycarp's epistle to illustrate early Christian teachings on faith and morality. - Eusebius of Caesarea: Cited Polycarp in his "Ecclesiastical History" to highlight the continuity of apostolic tradition.

The early Church Fathers drew from a rich collection of texts beyond the canonical scriptures to inform their theological, ethical, and pastoral writings. These additional writings, including the Apocryphal/Deuterocanonical books and early Christian letters and teachings, were instrumental in shaping the doctrines and practices of the early Church. Their extensive use and citation by the Fathers underscore the importance and influence of these texts in the development of early Christian thought and tradition.
