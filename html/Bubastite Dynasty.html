The Bubastite Dynasty (22nd Dynasty of Egypt): The Libyan Pharaohs

The Bubastite Dynasty, also known as Egypt’s 22nd Dynasty (c. 943–720 BC), was a line of pharaohs of Libyan descent who ruled Egypt after the collapse of the New Kingdom. They were called the Bubastites because their capital was Bubastis, a city in the eastern Nile Delta.

This period was marked by:

• Foreign rule by the Meshwesh Libyans, who had settled in Egypt.

• Military conquests, including Shoshenq I’s campaign in Canaan and Israel.

• Weakening central power, leading to Egypt’s eventual fragmentation into rival kingdoms.

1. Origins: How the Libyans Took Power in Egypt

By the late 20th Dynasty (c. 1100 BC), large groups of Libyans (Meshwesh and other Berber tribes) had migrated into Egypt, some as mercenaries and settlers.

Rise of the Libyan Elite

• During the 21st Dynasty (1077–943 BC), Libyan military leaders gained power by controlling Egypt’s army.

• Shoshenq I, a powerful Libyan general, took the throne peacefully in 943 BC, founding the 22nd Dynasty.

2. Key Pharaohs of the Bubastite Dynasty

Shoshenq I (943–922 BC) – The Empire Builder

• Founder of the dynasty and the first Libyan pharaoh.

• Possibly the biblical “Shishak”, who plundered Jerusalem (1 Kings 14:25-26).

• His Karnak inscriptions boast of victories in Israel and Canaan.

• Strengthened Egyptian power and central rule.

Osorkon I (922–887 BC) – The Wealthy Pharaoh

• Son of Shoshenq I, ruled peacefully.

• Expanded temple construction, especially at Bubastis and Karnak.

• Weakened the military focus of his father’s reign.

Takelot I & Osorkon II (887–837 BC) – Internal Struggles Begin

• The empire began fragmenting, as powerful priests in Thebes challenged the pharaohs.

• Libyan tribal influence led to regional conflicts.

• Osorkon II built elaborate temples and monuments, but Egypt was becoming politically unstable.

Shoshenq III (837–798 BC) – Decline and Division

• Egypt split into two rival powers:

  1. The Delta (ruled by Bubastite pharaohs).

  2. Upper Egypt (controlled by Theban priests).

• The central authority weakened, leading to local governors acting as independent rulers.

Shoshenq V (730s BC) – End of the Dynasty

• Egypt was now fractured into small kingdoms.

• The Kushites (from Nubia) invaded Egypt, leading to the rise of the 25th Dynasty.

• By 720 BC, the Libyan dynasty had lost control, replaced by Nubian and Assyrian rulers.

3. Achievements of the Bubastite Dynasty

✅ Expanded Egyptian military campaigns, especially in Canaan under Shoshenq I.

✅ Continued Egyptian temple building, notably at Bubastis and Karnak.

✅ Introduced Libyan warrior elites into Egyptian politics and military.

However, ❌ they struggled to maintain centralized power, leading to Egypt’s political decline.

4. Legacy: The First Foreign Dynasty of Egypt

The Bubastite Dynasty was:

• The first non-Egyptian ruling family in Egypt’s history.

• A turning point in Egyptian politics, where Libyan tribal leaders ruled as pharaohs.

• A period of both military success and political fragmentation, setting the stage for Nubian and Assyrian domination.
