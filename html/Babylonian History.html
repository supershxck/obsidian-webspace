Outline of Babylonian History

1. Early Settlements and Foundation (c. 2300 BCE)

• Babylon originated as a small Akkadian-speaking town in southern Mesopotamia, near the Euphrates River.

• First mentioned during the Akkadian Empire under Sargon of Akkad (c. 2334–2154 BCE).

• Became significant during the Ur III Period (c. 2100–2000 BCE) but remained a minor city.

2. Rise of Babylon under Hammurabi (Old Babylonian Empire, c. 1792–1595 BCE)

• Hammurabi (r. 1792–1750 BCE) unified much of Mesopotamia and established Babylon as the capital of a powerful empire.

• Codification of laws: The Code of Hammurabi, one of the oldest known legal systems.

• Babylon became a cultural, political, and religious center, with the god Marduk gaining prominence.

• After Hammurabi’s death, the empire weakened, eventually falling to the Hittites in 1595 BCE.

3. Kassite Period (c. 1595–1155 BCE)

• After the Hittite sack, the Kassites, a non-native dynasty, ruled Babylon for nearly 400 years.

• Babylonian culture thrived; Kassites rebuilt temples and solidified Marduk’s supremacy.

• The Kassites preserved Babylonian traditions but eventually lost power due to internal decline and external invasions (e.g., by the Elamites).

4. Middle Babylonian Period and Assyrian Influence (c. 1155–626 BCE)

• Following the Kassites, Babylon frequently changed hands:

• Elamites briefly controlled Babylon.

• Assyrians periodically dominated the city, especially under Tiglath-Pileser III and Sargon II.

• Babylon retained cultural influence but was politically unstable.

• Notable rebellions against Assyrian rule showed Babylon’s enduring identity as a cultural and religious center.

5. Neo-Babylonian Empire (Chaldean Dynasty, c. 626–539 BCE)

• Babylon regained independence under Nabopolassar (r. 626–605 BCE), who established the Chaldean Dynasty.

• Nebuchadnezzar II (r. 605–562 BCE) expanded the empire, making it the largest in the Near East.

• Famous for constructing the Hanging Gardens of Babylon (possibly mythical) and rebuilding the Ishtar Gate and Etemenanki ziggurat.

• Conquered Jerusalem (587 BCE), leading to the Babylonian Captivity of the Jews.

• Babylon became a center of science, mathematics, and astrology.

• Decline began under weaker successors after Nebuchadnezzar’s death.

6. Fall of Babylon (539 BCE)

• Babylon fell to Cyrus the Great of Persia during the rise of the Achaemenid Empire.

• Cyrus portrayed himself as a liberator, respecting Babylonian culture and religion.

• Babylon became a province of Persia but remained a cultural hub.

7. Hellenistic Period (331–141 BCE)

• Conquered by Alexander the Great in 331 BCE. He planned to make Babylon the capital of his empire.

• After Alexander’s death (323 BCE), Babylon became part of the Seleucid Empire.

• Gradual decline as power shifted to other cities like Seleucia on the Tigris.

8. Parthian and Sassanian Periods (c. 141 BCE–7th Century CE)

• Babylon lost its political significance but remained inhabited under Parthian and later Sassanian rule.

• Its cultural and religious legacy persisted, especially through its influence on astrology and science.

9. Babylon’s Legacy

• By the early Islamic period, Babylon was largely abandoned but remained a symbol of ancient greatness.

• Its mythology, scientific contributions, and architecture (e.g., the Tower of Babel) influenced later civilizations.
