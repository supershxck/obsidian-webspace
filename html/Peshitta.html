The Peshitta: The Standard Bible of Syriac Christianity

The Peshitta (ܦܫܝܛܬܐ) is the Syriac translation of the Bible, serving as the authoritative Scripture for Syriac-speaking Christian communities in the Middle East and beyond. It is the equivalent of the Septuagint (Greek) for the Eastern Orthodox Church and the Vulgate (Latin) for the Roman Catholic Church. The name Peshitta means “simple,” “straight,” or “common”, reflecting its status as the standard version of the Bible in Syriac Christianity.

1. Historical Background

The origins of the Peshitta are somewhat debated, but scholars generally agree on the following points:

• Old Testament: The Syriac Old Testament is based on the Hebrew Bible (Masoretic Text) but shows influences from the Septuagint (LXX). It likely dates to the 2nd century BC or 1st century AD, possibly translated by Jewish communities in Edessa (modern-day Şanlıurfa, Turkey).

• New Testament: The Syriac New Testament was translated in the 2nd century AD, making it one of the earliest versions of the Christian Scriptures. Some scholars argue that certain New Testament books were originally written in Syriac rather than Greek, though this remains controversial.

• The Peshitta was widely used in the [[Church of the East]], the [[Syriac Orthodox Church]], and other Syriac Christian traditions.

2. Structure of the Peshitta

The Peshitta includes both the Old Testament and New Testament, but with some key differences from Western biblical canons:

Old Testament

• Mostly based on the Hebrew Bible (Masoretic Text) but with some Septuagint influences.

• Includes Deuterocanonical books (Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, 1 & 2 Maccabees, and additions to Daniel and Esther) in some versions.

• Psalms are often numbered differently than in Western traditions.

New Testament (Original Canon)

• The Peshitta originally lacked four books found in the Western New Testament:

• 2 Peter, 2 John, 3 John, and Revelation

These books were later added in the 6th–7th century AD when the Peshitta was expanded.

• The Syriac version of Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John sometimes contains slight textual differences from Greek manuscripts, leading to debates about whether parts of the New Testament were originally written in Syriac.

3. Connection to St. Lucian of Antioch

Some scholars believe that the Peshitta’s textual tradition—especially its New Testament—was influenced by St. Lucian of Antioch’s textual revisions in the late 3rd and early 4th centuries. Lucian’s emphasis on textual purity and correction of Greek biblical texts may have shaped the Byzantine text-type, which influenced later Syriac manuscript traditions.

4. Theological and Linguistic Importance

A Window into Aramaic-Speaking Christianity

• Syriac is a dialect of Aramaic, the language that Jesus and His disciples spoke.

• The Peshitta preserves Aramaic expressions that were later translated into Greek, giving insight into how early Christians understood Jesus’ teachings.

Differences from Greek New Testament

• Some Syriac words and phrases clarify difficult Greek passages. For example:

• In Matthew 19:24, Jesus says, “It is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle.”

• In Syriac, gamal (camel) could also mean rope, which makes more sense contextually.

• The Peshitta uses the word “Mar” (ܡܪܐ, Lord) instead of the Greek Kyrios when referring to Jesus, emphasizing Syriac-speaking Christian traditions’ Christological understanding.

5. Influence and Legacy

• The Peshitta remains the official Bible of the Syriac Orthodox Church, the Assyrian Church of the East, and the Chaldean Catholic Church.

• The Syriac Christian tradition spread as far as India, Persia, and China, carrying the Peshitta along with it.

• Eastern Syriac and Western Syriac versions later emerged due to regional dialectical differences.

6. Controversy: Was the New Testament Originally Written in Syriac?

Some Syriac Christian scholars argue for the Primacy of the Peshitta, claiming that the New Testament was originally written in Syriac/Aramaic rather than Greek. This theory is known as the Aramaic Primacy Hypothesis and is based on:

• The natural flow of Aramaic expressions that seem awkward in Greek.

• Early Syriac Christian communities’ use of Peshitta before Greek manuscripts reached them.

• The fact that Jesus and His disciples spoke Aramaic, not Greek.

However, mainstream scholarship holds that the Greek New Testament is the original, with the Peshitta being an early but secondary translation.

Conclusion: Why the Peshitta Matters

The Peshitta is one of the oldest and most influential translations of the Bible, preserving early Christian thought in the Syriac language. It bridges Jewish and Hellenistic traditions, providing unique insights into how Eastern Christianity interpreted Scripture. Even today, it remains a living tradition among Syriac Christians worldwide.
