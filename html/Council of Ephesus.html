Accomplishments of the [[Third Ecumenical Council]] (Council of Ephesus, 431 AD)

1. Condemnation of [[Nestorianism]]

  • Key Issue: The primary focus of the council was the condemnation of Nestorianism, a doctrine that emphasized a disunion between the human and divine natures of Jesus Christ.
  • Teachings of Nestorius: Nestorius, the Patriarch of Constantinople, taught that Mary should be called "Christotokos" (Christ-bearer) rather than "Theotokos" (God-bearer), implying a separation between Christ's divine and human natures.
  • Council's Decision: The council affirmed the title "Theotokos" for Mary, emphasizing the unity of Christ's natures and declaring that Jesus is one person, both fully divine and fully human.

2. Affirmation of the Nicene Creed

  • Continuation of Nicene Theology: The council reaffirmed the [[Nicene Creed]], maintaining the doctrinal continuity established by the previous councils.

3. Deposition of Nestorius

  • Removal from Office: Nestorius was deposed from his position as Patriarch of Constantinople for his teachings, which were deemed heretical by the council.

4. Canons and Church Discipline

  • Issuance of Canons: The council issued several canons related to church discipline and administration, reinforcing the hierarchical structure of the Church and addressing issues of ecclesiastical governance.

5. The Formula of Union

  • Reconciliation Efforts: In 433, two years after the council, a document known as the Formula of Union was created to reconcile differences between the Antiochene and Alexandrian theological traditions. It affirmed the unity of Christ's natures and the title "Theotokos" for Mary, aiming to heal the divisions caused by the Nestorian controversy.

Key Figures

  • Cyril of Alexandria: Played a leading role in the council, strongly opposing Nestorianism and advocating for the use of the title "Theotokos" for Mary.
  • John of Antioch: Initially opposed Cyril but later reconciled with him through the Formula of Union.
  • Emperor Theodosius II: Convened the council and provided imperial support for its decisions.

Conclusion

The Third Ecumenical Council at Ephesus was pivotal in addressing the Christological controversies of the time, particularly the teachings of Nestorius. By affirming the title "Theotokos" for Mary and emphasizing the unity of Christ's divine and human natures, the council helped to clarify and solidify orthodox Christian doctrine. The decisions made at Ephesus had a lasting impact on the theological and ecclesiastical landscape of the early Church, promoting doctrinal unity and reinforcing the authority of the Nicene Creed.
