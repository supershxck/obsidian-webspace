Dyothelitism is a pivotal Christological doctrine within Christian theology that asserts Jesus Christ possesses two distinct wills—one divine and one human—corresponding to his two natures, divine and human. This doctrine was formally established to counteract Monothelitism, which proposed that Christ has only a single will. Dyothelitism was affirmed by the Third Council of Constantinople (680–681 CE), the Sixth Ecumenical Council, and remains a cornerstone of orthodox Christian Christology today. This comprehensive overview explores Dyothelitism’s definition, historical development, key proponents, theological implications, opposition, condemnation of Monothelitism, and its enduring legacy within Christianity.

1. Definition and Overview

1.1. What is Dyothelitism?

Dyothelitism (from Greek dyo meaning “two” and thelema meaning “will”) is the theological position that Jesus Christ possesses two wills—one divine and one human. These wills are distinct yet harmonious, each corresponding to one of Christ’s two natures as defined by the Council of Chalcedon (451 CE). Dyothelitism maintains that both wills operate without conflict, confusion, division, or separation, ensuring the integrity of both Christ’s divinity and humanity.

1.2. Importance in Christian Theology

Dyothelitism is essential for preserving the doctrine of the hypostatic union—the union of Christ’s two natures in one person. By affirming two wills, Dyothelitism ensures that Jesus fully embodies both divine attributes (such as omniscience and omnipotence) and human attributes (such as suffering and moral agency), enabling him to act as a perfect mediator between God and humanity.

2. Historical Context and Origins

2.1. Early Christological Debates

Following the Council of Chalcedon in 451 CE, which affirmed that Jesus Christ is one person in two natures, significant theological tensions persisted, particularly between Chalcedonian Christians (who accepted the two natures) and Miaphysite Christians (who emphasized the unity of Christ’s nature). These tensions were compounded by political and ecclesiastical conflicts within the Byzantine Empire, leading to attempts to find theological compromises.

2.2. Emergence of Monothelitism

In the early 7th century, Monothelitism emerged as an attempt to reconcile Chalcedonian and Miaphysite positions by proposing that Christ has only one will. This doctrine aimed to preserve the Chalcedonian definition of two natures while addressing Miaphysite concerns about the unity of Christ’s person.

2.3. Development of Dyothelitism

Dyothelitism developed as a direct response to Monothelitism. Theologians and church leaders sought to reaffirm the Chalcedonian position by clearly articulating that Christ possesses two wills corresponding to his two natures. This reaffirmation was crucial for maintaining orthodox Christology and countering heretical movements within the empire.

3. Key Figures

3.1. Emperor Constantine IV (630–711 CE)

• Role: Byzantine Emperor who played a significant role in promoting Dyothelitism.

• Contributions: Convened the Third Council of Constantinople to address and ultimately condemn Monothelitism, reinforcing Dyothelitism as the orthodox position.

3.2. Patriarch Sergius I of Constantinople

• Role: Patriarch who initially supported Monothelitism.

• Contributions: His support for Monothelitism was pivotal in the development and spread of the doctrine before its eventual condemnation.

3.3. Pope Agatho (678–681 CE)

• Role: Pope who supported the Dyothelite position.

• Contributions: Endorsed the decisions of the Third Council of Constantinople, aiding in the widespread acceptance of Dyothelitism across the Christian world.

3.4. John of Damascus (c. 676–749 CE)

• Role: Influential theologian and defender of Dyothelitism.

• Contributions: Articulated and defended the Dyothelite position against Monothelite and other heretical doctrines through his writings.

4. Theological Implications

4.1. Preservation of the Hypostatic Union

Dyothelitism ensures that the hypostatic union—the union of Christ’s divine and human natures in one person—is maintained without compromising either nature. By affirming two wills, it upholds the full divinity and full humanity of Christ.

4.2. Role in Salvation

Affirming two wills in Christ underscores his capacity to fully mediate between God and humanity. The divine will ensures his role in salvation, while the human will enables him to genuinely partake in human experiences and sufferings.

4.3. Ethical and Moral Agency

By maintaining a human will, Dyothelitism affirms Christ’s genuine moral agency and responsibility. This is essential for understanding his teachings, actions, and ultimate sacrifice.

4.4. Unity and Harmony

Dyothelitism emphasizes the harmonious operation of Christ’s two wills, ensuring that his divine purpose and human experiences coalesce seamlessly, without internal conflict.

5. Opposition and Controversy

5.1. Monothelitism

• Doctrine: Proposes that Christ has only one will, typically interpreted as a divine will.

• Conflict: Seen as undermining the full humanity of Christ by denying a distinct human will, thus threatening the integrity of the hypostatic union.

5.2. Miaphysitism

• Doctrine: Emphasizes the unity of Christ’s nature, often rejecting the notion of two distinct natures.

• Conflict: While Miaphysitism focuses on the nature of Christ, its emphasis on unity can blur the distinction between divine and human aspects, conflicting with Dyothelitism’s affirmation of two wills.

5.3. Political and Ecclesiastical Tensions

• Imperial Influence: Emperors like Constantine IV sought theological solutions to maintain political unity, leading to pressure on the Church to adopt or reject specific doctrines.

• Ecclesiastical Power Struggles: Patriarchs and theologians within the Church debated and contested the validity of Monothelitism and Dyothelitism, leading to significant ecclesiastical conflicts.

6. Councils and Condemnation of Monothelitism

6.1. Third Council of Constantinople (680–681 CE)

• Convocation: Convened by Emperor Constantine IV to address ongoing theological disputes, including Monothelitism.

• Decisions:

• Condemnation of Monothelitism: Declared Monothelitism heretical, affirming that Christ possesses both a divine will and a human will.

• Affirmation of Dyothelitism: Established Dyothelitism as the orthodox position, reinforcing the Chalcedonian hypostatic union.

• Impact:

• Theological Clarity: Provided a clear theological stance against Monothelitism, ensuring the preservation of Christ’s dual wills.

• Ecclesiastical Unity: Aimed to unify the Byzantine Empire’s diverse Christian populations by settling doctrinal disputes.

6.2. Fifth Ecumenical Council

• Equivalent to: Third Council of Constantinople.

• Significance: Recognized as the Sixth Ecumenical Council in the traditional numbering sequence of Ecumenical Councils.

• Outcome: Solidified Dyothelitism and rejected all forms of Monothelitism, reinforcing the importance of two wills in Christ.

7. Spread and Influence

7.1. Byzantine Empire

Dyothelitism became the dominant Christological position within the Byzantine Empire following the Third Council of Constantinople. It was instrumental in unifying the empire’s theological stance and suppressing heretical movements.

7.2. Eastern Orthodox and Roman Catholic Churches

Both Eastern Orthodox and Roman Catholic Churches upheld Dyothelitism as part of their orthodox Christology, ensuring that the doctrine of two wills in Christ remained central to mainstream Christianity.

7.3. Theological Developments

Dyothelitism influenced subsequent theological discussions on the nature of Christ, the Trinity, and the relationship between divine and human attributes within the Godhead.

8. Decline of Monothelitism and Enduring Legacy of Dyothelitism

8.1. Suppression of Monothelitism

Following the condemnation by the Third Council of Constantinople, Monothelitism was systematically suppressed within the Byzantine Empire. Proponents faced excommunication, exile, and other ecclesiastical penalties, leading to the decline of the doctrine.

8.2. Enduring Acceptance of Dyothelitism

Dyothelitism remained firmly entrenched within orthodox Christian theology, shaping Christological teachings and ecclesiastical doctrines for centuries. It continues to be a foundational element of mainstream Christian Christology.

8.3. Influence on Later Councils

The affirmation of Dyothelitism set precedents for how Ecumenical Councils address and resolve doctrinal disputes, emphasizing the importance of clear theological definitions and ecclesiastical authority.

9. Theological Significance

9.1. Clarification of Christ’s Personhood

Dyothelitism provides a nuanced understanding of Jesus Christ’s personhood, balancing the affirmation of his full divinity and full humanity without conflating the two.

9.2. Preservation of the Hypostatic Union

By affirming two wills, Dyothelitism safeguards the integrity of the hypostatic union, ensuring that Christ’s divine and human natures coexist without compromising each other.

9.3. Mediatory Role of Christ

Dyothelitism underscores Christ’s unique role as the mediator between God and humanity, capable of fully representing both divine authority and human experience.

9.4. Ethical and Moral Implications

Affirming a human will in Christ emphasizes the genuine human experiences and moral responsibilities of Jesus, providing a model for ethical behavior and spiritual resilience.

10. Key Theological Terms

10.1. Hypostatic Union

• Definition: The theological doctrine that Jesus Christ exists in two natures—divine and human—in one person.

• Relation to Dyothelitism: Dyothelitism complements the hypostatic union by affirming that each nature has its corresponding will, ensuring the full integrity of both natures.

10.2. Monothelitism

• Definition: The belief that Jesus Christ has only one will, typically understood as a divine will.

• Contrast with Dyothelitism: Monothelitism denies the existence of a distinct human will in Christ, opposing the Dyothelite affirmation of two wills.

10.3. Dyothelitism

• Definition: The belief that Jesus Christ possesses two distinct wills—one divine and one human—corresponding to his two natures.

• Orthodox Standpoint: Affirmed by the Third Council of Constantinople as the orthodox Christological position.

10.4. Hypostasis

• Definition: In Christian theology, the individual reality of the three persons of the Trinity—the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit.

• Relation to Dyothelitism: Dyothelitism interacts with hypostatic doctrines by asserting that each hypostasis maintains its distinct will.

10.5. Council of Chalcedon

• Definition: The Fourth Ecumenical Council held in 451 CE, which defined the orthodox doctrine of Jesus Christ as one person in two natures.

• Relation to Dyothelitism: The Council of Chalcedon’s definition of the hypostatic union laid the groundwork for Dyothelitism by affirming Christ’s dual natures.

10.6. Ecumenical Council

• Definition: A formal assembly of Christian bishops convened to discuss and settle matters of Church doctrine and practice.

• Relation to Dyothelitism: The Third Council of Constantinople, an Ecumenical Council, affirmed Dyothelitism and condemned Monothelitism.

11. Conclusion

Dyothelitism stands as a crucial doctrinal affirmation within Christian theology, safeguarding the full divinity and humanity of Jesus Christ by asserting that he possesses two distinct wills corresponding to his two natures. Established by the Third Council of Constantinople, Dyothelitism effectively countered Monothelitism, reinforcing orthodox Christological positions and promoting ecclesiastical unity within the Byzantine Empire.

The doctrine’s emphasis on both wills ensures that Christ’s divine authority and human experiences coexist harmoniously, maintaining the integrity of the hypostatic union and affirming his role as the perfect mediator between God and humanity. Dyothelitism’s legacy endures in contemporary Christian theology, continuing to inform discussions on the nature of Christ, the Trinity, and the relationship between divine and human attributes.

Understanding Dyothelitism provides valuable insights into the historical development of Christian doctrines, the resolution of theological controversies, and the mechanisms through which the early Church sought to maintain unity and doctrinal purity amidst diverse and often conflicting theological perspectives.
