The Golden Bull of 1356: Constitutional Cornerstone of the Holy Roman Empire

Introduction

The Golden Bull of 1356 was a landmark imperial decree issued by Emperor Charles IV that codified the election process for the Holy Roman Emperor. It served as the constitutional foundation of the [[Holy Roman Empire]] until its dissolution in 1806, providing unprecedented political stability through clear rules of succession.

Historical Context

• Issued: January 10, 1356 at Nuremberg

• Author: Emperor Charles IV — seeking to consolidate imperial authority after decades of contested successions

• Addressed chronic rivalries among German princes and prevented dynastic conflicts

Key Provisions

|Article|Provision|Purpose| |---|---|---| |I–IV|Identification of Seven Electors|Established fixed college of electors (3 ecclesiastical, 4 secular)| |V|Election Procedures|Required majority vote in Frankfurt; coronation in Aachen| |VI|Rights of Electors|Guaranteed autonomy over territories; immunity from imperial interference| |VII|Succession Rules|Restricted candidacy to male heirs of ruling dynasties|

Political Impact

• Ended disputed imperial elections by standardizing process

• Empowered territorial princes, decentralizing imperial power

• Fostered relative peace within German lands for four centuries
