What is the theological significance of Icons?

https://ccel.org/ccel/schaff/npnf214/npnf214.xvi.i.html

“An image (είκών) corresponds to its original as an archetype; it resembles it in essential features. The term can be used even to express an identity of essence; thus, Christ is one in essence with the Father. This is affirmed in the Niceness Creed: “being  of one substance (όμοούσιος) with the Father.” The term είκών denotes that which is visible. Our Lord is the visible image of the invisible God; if we would know what the invisible God is like, we must look to that by which he has revealed himself visibly: the incarnate Christ.”

  • Paul E. Deterding on Col. 1:15 in the Concordia Commentary

(See Dyer’s Part 2 on refuting Calvinism starting at 1:00:30-1:14:20)

  • Icons should be apart of the liturgy. The reason why is because Christ, taking on human nature, which, according to the recapitulation theory, makes matter and the universes inherently good, does not make God antithetical to matter, images and forms. The Regulative Principle lacks in this area. 

  • The Hebrew liturgical religion was not iconoclastic. For example, see: the ark of the covenant, the 1st Temple, the serpent on the bronze pole (John 3), manifestations of the theophanies, the Passover imagery, the Jewish temple service. All these rituals are iconographic.

  • Is idolatry an image or an interior disposition of the mind and heart? 

  • Reality is iconographic. The Bible is iconographic. It’s “words” which are symbols, images that refer to or pick out various things and point back to the divine. 

So if the Bible can be written in a created image, a word, that refers to various divine things, then clearly images can’t be a problem. The images are not inherently wrong.

The 10 commandments do not contradict this. God won’t contradict His own word by inspiring Bezazel to make artwork for the Sanctuary. 

The Bible is full of imagery, iconographic and symbolic presentation.

  • See John 5-9, who did Abraham talk to? And who did the Patriarchs see?

Jesus says that He is the face, the icon, the image, the form, the glory of God the Father. He is the ONLY Icon of God the Father, in terms of depicting the Father’s hypostases or personhood of the Father. 

Only the Son can be imaged, not the Father or the Holy Spirit, because the Spirit is depicted through energetic manifestations (the dove & tongues of fire) and you can’t image of the divine essence or the divine nature (Acts 17:29). So the divine hypostases of the Son is imaged. 

This all helps develop nature, person, will, energy distinction, in terms of trinity theology, which determines christology, then ecclesiology, liturgy, and sacramentology, and iconography. 

All of reality is iconographic; not in the same way, not the same mode, because not everything is a Bible text. All of reality is Logos-centric; the whole created order finds its meaning in the Logos Himself, because this is the cosmic extent and scope of the Incarnation and the Redemption.

  • Ultimately, a divorcing of the physical from the spiritual and a limiting of the believers experience of God in the divine service makes the believer immature. 
  • St. John of Damascus’ books become the central texts for the 7th council.
  • God is not subject to matter; yet, by taking on a body and stepping into matter, He has made it a vessel for the divine, thereby sanctifying it. The invisible becoming visible through material body has divinized all matter, and thus matter becomes a venue through which the invisible God reveals Himself visibly.
