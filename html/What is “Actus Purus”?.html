"Actus Purus" is a term rooted in the philosophy and theology of Thomas Aquinas, describing God as "pure act" (actus purus) without potentiality, meaning that God is fully actualized and not subject to change or becoming. This concept, while central to Thomistic and certain strands of Western scholastic thought, is seen as problematic from an Orthodox Christian perspective. The reasons for this are multifaceted, involving both theological and philosophical differences.

1. Dynamic and Personal God In Orthodox Christianity, God is understood as a dynamic and personal being who interacts with His creation in a real and relational way. The concept of God as "pure act" tends to present God in more static terms, which can be perceived as limiting His personal and relational nature.

St. Gregory Palamas: St. Gregory Palamas, in his defense of the Hesychast tradition, emphasizes the dynamic and relational nature of God, especially in the context of the divine energies: "The divine essence is imparticipable, but the divine energies are participable, manifesting the dynamic presence of God in the world and in the hearts of the faithful. Through these energies, we experience the living and active God."

2. Essence-Energies Distinction A cornerstone of Orthodox theology is the distinction between God's essence (ousia) and His energies (energeia). God's essence remains incomprehensible and transcendent, while His energies are how He manifests and communicates Himself to the world. This distinction allows for a relational and experiential understanding of God that "actus purus" does not accommodate.

St. Basil the Great: St. Basil the Great, in his letter “Ad Amphilochium”, makes a clear distinction between essence and energies: "We know the essence through the energies. We do not assert that we participate in the essence, but that we participate in the energies. For this reason, we say that we are partakers of the divine nature, not by participation in the essence but by participation in the energies."

3. Immutability and Divine Freedom
While both Western and Eastern Christianity affirm God's immutability, the Orthodox understanding allows for a more nuanced view that preserves God's freedom to act and relate to creation. The concept of "pure act" might be seen as reducing God's freedom to a philosophical abstraction, rather than the living God who actively engages with His creation.

St. John of Damascus: St. John of Damascus, in his “Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith”, describes God's immutability while emphasizing His freedom: "God is without beginning, uncreated, and immutable. He is ever-existing, yet He freely acts in the world, revealing His will and love through His providence and interaction with creation."

4. Mystery and Apophatic Theology Orthodox theology strongly emphasizes the mystery of God and the limits of human knowledge in comprehending Him. The apophatic (negative) approach to theology stresses what God is not, rather than what He is, preserving the mystery and transcendence of God. The "actus purus" concept can be seen as an overly positive definition that risks diminishing the apophatic dimension of Orthodox theology.

St. Gregory of Nyssa: St. Gregory of Nyssa, in his “Life of Moses”, highlights the importance of apophatic theology: "The true knowledge and vision of God consist in this—in seeing that He is invisible, because what we seek lies beyond all knowledge, being wholly separated by the darkness of incomprehensibility."

5. Relational and Participatory Theology Orthodoxy emphasizes the relational and participatory nature of the believer's relationship with God, particularly through theosis (deification). The "actus purus" concept, by emphasizing God's unchangeability in a static sense, may be perceived as hindering the dynamic participation in the divine life that Orthodox theology upholds.

St. Athanasius: St. Athanasius, in “On the Incarnation”, speaks to the participatory nature of salvation: "The Word was made man in order that we might be made god. He was incarnate that we might participate in His divinity, experiencing the transformative and relational nature of God’s presence."

Conclusion From an Orthodox Christian perspective, the concept of "actus purus" is problematic because it can lead to a static and impersonal understanding of God that conflicts with the dynamic, relational, and participatory nature of Orthodox theology. The distinction between God's essence and energies, the emphasis on apophatic theology, and the understanding of God's immutability in relation to His freedom and interaction with creation all highlight the Orthodox critique of "actus purus." The teachings of the Church Fathers, particularly those involved in the defense of Hesychasm and the essence-energies distinction, provide a framework for understanding these theological differences and affirming the Orthodox vision of a living, dynamic, and relational God.
