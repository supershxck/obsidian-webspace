Babylonian Arithmetic

Babylonian arithmetic was highly advanced for its time and forms the foundation of many concepts still in use today. Their mathematical achievements were deeply tied to practical needs like trade, agriculture, and astronomy, as well as philosophical ideas of cosmic order.

1. Sexagesimal System (Base-60)

• Base-60 system: Unlike the decimal (base-10) system most modern cultures use, the Babylonians used a sexagesimal system (base-60).

• Numbers were expressed using combinations of cuneiform symbols.

• This system allowed for greater precision in calculations, especially for fractions and large numbers.

• The base-60 legacy remains today in the division of time (60 seconds per minute, 60 minutes per hour) and angles (360 degrees in a circle).

2. Numerical Notation

• The Babylonians used a positional system, where the value of a symbol depended on its position, similar to how modern decimal notation works.

• For example, in base-60, the number “1” in the first position represented 1, while in the second position, it represented 60.

• They lacked a true zero initially, though later texts included a placeholder symbol for clarity.

3. Arithmetic Operations

• Addition and Subtraction:

• Performed using place value, just as we do today.

• Tables of sums and differences were used to aid calculations.

• Multiplication:

• Babylonians relied on multiplication tables carved into clay tablets.

• Instead of multiplying directly, they often used reciprocal tables to simplify calculations (e.g., dividing instead of multiplying by fractions).

• Division:

• Division was achieved using tables of reciprocals, with Babylonian mathematicians calculating the inverse of numbers to perform division more efficiently.

• Their precision allowed them to work with fractions as small as 1/60.

• Fractions:

• Babylonian fractions were always expressed in base-60. For example, 1/2 was written as 30 (30/60).

• This allowed for easy calculation but required extensive memorization of reciprocal tables.

4. Advanced Mathematical Concepts

• Square Roots:

• Babylonians approximated square roots with remarkable accuracy.

• Tablets like YBC 7289 demonstrate that they knew how to compute the square root of 2 to at least six decimal places.

• Pythagorean Theorem:

• Although developed centuries before Pythagoras, Babylonian tablets show their understanding of the Pythagorean theorem.

• They applied this knowledge in geometry, particularly for land measurement and construction.

• Exponents and Powers:

• Tables for squares, cubes, and higher powers facilitated advanced calculations.

5. Applications of Arithmetic

• Astronomy:

• Arithmetic was crucial for Babylonian astronomers, who used calculations to predict celestial events like lunar eclipses and planetary movements.

• Land Measurement:

• Precise arithmetic allowed for accurate division of land, important for agriculture and taxation.

• Trade and Commerce:

• Used for pricing goods, weights, and measures, reflecting Babylon’s role as a major trading hub.

• Architecture and Engineering:

• Arithmetic was employed in construction, ensuring the stability of monumental structures like ziggurats.

6. Mathematical Texts

• Tablets as Tools:

• Clay tablets served as instructional guides, including multiplication tables, reciprocal tables, and geometric calculations.

• Notable examples:

• Plimpton 322: A Babylonian tablet containing a list of Pythagorean triples, showing their advanced understanding of right triangles.

• YBC 7289: A tablet demonstrating their precise calculation of the square root of 2.

7. Legacy

• Babylonian arithmetic influenced later civilizations, including the Greeks and Islamic scholars.

• Their base-60 system inspired aspects of modern mathematics, timekeeping, and astronomy.

• The use of tables and positional notation laid the groundwork for modern computational methods.
