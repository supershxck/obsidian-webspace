Chapter: The World Court — International Court of Justice

Introduction

The International Court of Justice (ICJ)—commonly known as the World Court—is the principal judicial organ of the [[United Nations]]. Established in 1945, it settles legal disputes between states and issues advisory opinions on questions of international law.

Origins & Historical Context

  • Founded: 1945 by the UN Charter
  • Predecessor: Permanent Court of International Justice (est. 1922)
  • Seat: Peace Palace, The Hague, Netherlands

Structure & Jurisdiction

| Feature | Description | |---------|-------------| | Composition | 15 independent judges elected to 9‑year terms by UN General Assembly and Security Council | | Jurisdiction | Contentious cases (state‑to‑state disputes) & advisory opinions (UN bodies, specialized agencies) | | Languages | English & French | | Binding Nature | Judgments binding; no direct enforcement mechanism |

Core Functions

  1. Contentious Cases: Resolve disputes on territory, maritime boundaries, diplomatic relations, treaty interpretation
  2. Advisory Opinions: Provide non‑binding legal guidance to UN organs and agencies
  3. Promote International Law: Develop jurisprudence and clarify legal principles

Notable Cases

| Case | Year | Issue | Outcome | |------|------|-------|---------| | Nicaragua v. United States | 1986 | Use of force | ICJ ruled against US intervention | | South West Africa Cases | 1966 | Mandate obligations | Dismissed due to lack of standing | | Advisory Opinion on Kosovo | 2010 | Unilateral declaration of independence | Non-binding opinion affirmed no prohibition under international law |

Psychological & Philosophical Reflection

The World Court embodies the archetype of “Judicial Equilibrium”—anchoring global order through impartial legal adjudication. It reflects humanity’s aspiration for rule‑based coexistence over power politics.
