The Armenian Orthodox Church: A Comprehensive History

1. Origins and Early History

  • Introduction of Christianity:

  • Armenia is often referred to as the "first Christian nation," having adopted Christianity as its state religion in 301 AD. The formal conversion was led by St. Gregory the Illuminator (Grigor Lusavorich), who converted the Armenian King Tiridates III (Trdat III). This event marked the founding of the Armenian Apostolic Church.

  • Early Christian Influence:

  • Christianity had already started spreading in Armenia by the first century, believed to have been introduced by the Apostles Thaddeus and Bartholomew. The church traces its apostolic lineage back to these two figures, hence the designation "Apostolic."

2. The Development of the Armenian Church

  • Council of Nicaea (325 AD):

  • The Armenian Church was represented at the First Ecumenical Council of Nicaea in 325 AD by Bishop Aristaces, son of St. Gregory the Illuminator. The council was crucial in defining Christian orthodoxy, particularly the doctrine of the Trinity.

  • The Invention of the Armenian Alphabet (405 AD):

  • A significant milestone in the history of the Armenian Church was the invention of the Armenian alphabet by St. Mesrop Mashtots in 405 AD. This enabled the translation of the Bible into Armenian, strengthening the church’s influence and fostering Armenian cultural and religious identity.

  • The Council of Chalcedon (451 AD):

  • The Council of Chalcedon, which defined the nature of Christ as both fully human and fully divine, was rejected by the Armenian Church. This rejection was due to a combination of theological disagreement and political factors, particularly the Armenian resistance to the influence of the Byzantine Empire. As a result, the Armenian Church developed independently from both Eastern Orthodoxy and Roman Catholicism, adhering to Miaphysitism, which holds that Christ has one united nature, both human and divine.

3. The Middle Ages and the Development of the Church

  • Arab and Byzantine Rule:

  • From the 7th century onwards, Armenia fell under the influence of various powers, including the Byzantine Empire and the Arab Caliphates. Despite these pressures, the Armenian Church remained a central institution, preserving the Armenian language, culture, and national identity.

  • The Armenian Kingdom of Cilicia (1080-1375):

  • During the Crusades, many Armenians moved to Cilicia (present-day Turkey), where they established the Armenian Kingdom of Cilicia. The church flourished during this period, with the Catholicosate (the office of the chief bishop) being transferred to Cilicia in 1293. The kingdom served as a bridge between East and West, and the Armenian Church engaged in diplomatic and religious interactions with both the Catholic Church and the Crusaders.

  • The Council of Sis (1307):

  • In 1307, a significant council was held in Sis, the capital of Cilicia. The council attempted to reconcile differences with the Roman Catholic Church, but the union was short-lived, primarily due to resistance from the Armenian clergy and laity who were committed to preserving their traditions.

4. The Ottoman Period and Challenges

  • Ottoman Rule:

  • After the fall of the Armenian Kingdom of Cilicia, most Armenians came under Ottoman rule. The Armenian Church, recognized by the Ottomans as a separate religious community (millet), played a crucial role in the administration and cultural life of the Armenian people.

  • The Armenian Genocide (1915-1917):

  • The Armenian Church was severely impacted by the Armenian Genocide during World War I, where 1.5 million Armenians were killed by the Ottoman government. The genocide led to the destruction of countless churches, monasteries, and cultural treasures, and the displacement of the Armenian population.

5. The Modern Era and Diaspora

  • Post-Genocide Era:

  • Following the genocide, the Armenian Church found itself fragmented, with a significant portion of its flock dispersed across the globe in the Armenian diaspora. Despite these challenges, the church continued to play a vital role in maintaining Armenian identity in communities across the Middle East, Europe, the Americas, and beyond.

  • The Armenian Apostolic Church in the Soviet Union:

  • In the early 20th century, Armenia became part of the Soviet Union, where the Armenian Church faced restrictions and persecution. Churches were closed, and religious activities were suppressed. However, the church survived, and religious life experienced a revival with the easing of Soviet policies in the late 1980s.

  • Independence and Revival:

  • After the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991, Armenia regained its independence, and the Armenian Apostolic Church experienced a resurgence. The Catholicos of All Armenians, based in the Mother See of Holy Etchmiadzin, regained its position as a spiritual leader for Armenians worldwide.

6. Organization and Structure

  • Catholicosates:

  • The Armenian Church is headed by the Catholicos of All Armenians, based at the Mother See of Holy Etchmiadzin in Armenia. There are also two other important sees: the Catholicosate of Cilicia (now based in Antelias, Lebanon) and the Patriarchates of Jerusalem and Constantinople (Istanbul).

  • Dioceses and Parishes:

  • The church is organized into dioceses, each headed by a bishop. These dioceses are spread across the Armenian homeland and the diaspora, with parishes serving the local Armenian communities.

  • Liturgy and Worship:

  • The Armenian Apostolic Church has a rich liturgical tradition, with its primary liturgical language being Classical Armenian. The Divine Liturgy (Badarak) is the central act of worship, characterized by its use of ancient hymns, elaborate rituals, and the participation of a choir.

7. Relations with Other Churches

  • Relations with Oriental Orthodox Churches:

  • The Armenian Church is part of the Oriental Orthodox communion, along with the Coptic, Syriac, Ethiopian, and Malankara Orthodox Churches. These churches share a common Miaphysite Christology and have maintained strong theological and liturgical ties.

  • Relations with the Roman Catholic Church:

  • Throughout history, the Armenian Church has had various interactions with the Roman Catholic Church. While there have been attempts at union, especially during the Crusades and the Council of Florence (1439), the Armenian Church has largely remained independent. However, ecumenical relations have improved in the modern era.

  • Relations with the Eastern Orthodox Church:

  • Although the Armenian Church is not in communion with the Eastern Orthodox Churches, there have been dialogues and cooperation on various issues. The Armenian Church’s rejection of the Council of Chalcedon remains a key theological difference.

8. Contemporary Challenges and the Future

  • Diaspora and Identity:

  • The Armenian Apostolic Church continues to play a vital role in preserving Armenian identity, particularly in the diaspora, where it serves as a cultural and religious center for Armenians spread across the world.

  • Ecumenism and Interfaith Dialogue:

  • In recent years, the Armenian Church has engaged more actively in ecumenical and interfaith dialogue, seeking to build bridges with other Christian traditions and religious communities.

  • Modernization and Tradition:

  • The church faces the challenge of balancing modernization with the preservation of its ancient traditions. Issues such as secularism, globalization, and the integration of modern technology into religious life are areas of ongoing discussion within the church.

Conclusion

The Armenian Apostolic Church, with its deep historical roots and rich traditions, has been a cornerstone of Armenian identity for nearly two millennia. It has survived numerous challenges, from invasions and persecutions to the devastation of genocide and the pressures of modernity. Today, it continues to be a living tradition, guiding and nurturing the spiritual life of Armenians around the world while preserving its unique place in the history of Christianity.
