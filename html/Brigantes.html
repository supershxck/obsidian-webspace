The Brigantes were the largest and one of the most influential Celtic tribes in Iron Age Britain, dominating much of northern England, including modern-day Yorkshire, Lancashire, and Cumbria. Their territory stretched from the east to the west coast, covering a vast and strategically important region.

Geography and Society

• The Brigantes occupied a fertile and resource-rich region, which included hills, rivers, and valleys, making it ideal for farming, herding, and trade.

• Their name derives from the Celtic root brig- meaning “high” or “elevated”, possibly reflecting their homeland’s hilly landscape or their status as a powerful tribe.

• Like other Celtic tribes, the Brigantes had a tribal hierarchy, with a ruling elite supported by warriors, artisans, and farmers.

Economy and Trade

• The Brigantes thrived on a mixed economy of farming and herding. They cultivated cereals and raised cattle and sheep.

• Their territory contained valuable natural resources, such as iron, which they worked into tools and weapons.

• Trade networks connected them to other tribes in Britain and to the Roman world. Goods like salt, amber, and metals were exchanged along these routes.

Hillforts and Settlements

• The Brigantes built hillforts, which served as defensive strongholds, political centers, and symbols of power. Notable examples include:

• Stanwick Hillfort (near Darlington): A massive site that was likely a tribal capital.

• Ingleborough (in Yorkshire): A prominent hillfort that overlooked key trade routes.

• These sites were often fortified with earthworks, timber palisades, and ditches, reflecting the tribe’s need for security and their sophisticated engineering skills.

Religious Practices

• The Brigantes, like other Celts, were polytheistic, worshipping deities associated with nature, war, and fertility.

• Sacred sites, including springs, rivers, and groves, played a key role in their religious life. The goddess Brigantia, possibly the tribe’s namesake, was a significant deity, associated with fertility, protection, and sovereignty.

Interaction with Rome

The Brigantes’ interactions with Rome were complex and marked by alliances, betrayals, and resistance.

  1. Initial Contact:

• The Romans first encountered the Brigantes during the invasion of Britain in AD 43.

• Early on, the Brigantes adopted a policy of alliance with Rome under their queen Cartimandua, who became a client ruler.

  1. Cartimandua’s Reign:

• Cartimandua is one of the most notable figures in Brigantian history. She ruled the tribe with the support of the Romans.

• She secured her position by handing over Caratacus, the leader of anti-Roman resistance in southern Britain, to the Romans in AD 51.

• However, her reign was marked by internal strife. She divorced her husband Venutius, who later led a rebellion against her and the Romans.

  1. Venutius’s Revolt:

• Venutius, a Brigantian nobleman, opposed Roman influence and rallied anti-Roman factions.

• After overthrowing Cartimandua, he led a full-scale rebellion against Roman control, prompting military campaigns by the Roman governor Quintus Petillius Cerialis to subdue the Brigantes.

  1. Roman Conquest:

• The Brigantes were eventually defeated by the Romans during a series of campaigns in the late 1st century AD.

• By AD 71, the Romans had established the fortress of Eboracum (York) as their northern base, marking the full subjugation of Brigantian territory.

Legacy

  1. Archaeological Discoveries:

• Artifacts like metalwork, weapons, and jewelry showcase the Brigantes’ craftsmanship and cultural identity.

• Sites like Stanwick Hillfort have revealed evidence of extensive trade and complex social structures.

  1. Brigantia, the Goddess:

• The Brigantes’ cultural legacy includes Brigantia, a goddess who may have symbolized the tribe’s unity and spirit. She was later syncretized with Roman deities like Minerva during Roman occupation.

  1. Historical Impact:

• The Brigantes were one of the most influential tribes in northern Britain, and their resistance delayed Roman expansion into Scotland.

• Their story reflects the complexity of tribal politics in Iron Age Britain and the challenges faced by Rome in securing the northern frontier.
