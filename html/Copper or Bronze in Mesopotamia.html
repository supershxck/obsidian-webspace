Copper was a significant metal in the ancient Middle Eastern civilizations, including Babylon, Egypt, and ancient Israel. Its importance was not only practical but also symbolic, reflecting various cultural and religious values.

Babylon

In ancient Mesopotamia, including Babylon, copper was extensively used for a variety of practical purposes, including tools, weapons, and jewelry. Copper's prevalence in daily life made it a symbol of strength and durability. In the context of mythology and religion, metals often had associations with specific gods or divine attributes. For example, the extensive use of copper in the region is reflected in the myths surrounding gods like Enki (Ea), a god of water, knowledge, mischief, crafts, and creation, who was often depicted as a bringer and master of civilization, including its technological aspects.

Egypt

Copper in ancient Egypt had a profound importance both in practical and symbolic terms. Egypt had rich copper mines in the Sinai Peninsula, making it a crucial resource for Egyptian metallurgy. Copper was used to make tools, ornaments, and importantly, ritual objects used in temples and funerary practices. Symbolically, copper and its alloy bronze were associated with eternal durability. This eternal aspect was crucial in Egyptian culture, which heavily focused on the afterlife and immortality. The Egyptians also attributed magical properties to copper. It was often used in amulets and in the composition of mirrors used in religious and funerary rituals, likely reflecting qualities of purity and protection. The use of copper in the creation of statuary and in temple ritual items may also reflect its perceived sacred properties.

Israel

In ancient Israel, copper held significant utilitarian and symbolic importance. The Old Testament mentions copper numerous times, highlighting its use in various religious and daily contexts. One of the most notable references to copper is in the construction of the Temple of Solomon, where copper was used extensively for decorative purposes and for making the "Brazen Sea" or "Molten Sea" - a large basin for ritual washing. Copper, as a material used in the construction of the Temple, symbolizes strength and permanence but also has connotations of purification and sanctity due to its use in ritual objects and settings. The biblical narrative of the copper serpent (Nehushtan), which Moses was commanded to make and set on a pole so that anyone bitten by a snake could look at it and be healed, indicates a symbolic dimension of healing and divine protection associated with copper.

Conclusion

Throughout the Middle Eastern ancient civilizations, copper represented a blend of practical utility and deep symbolic significance tied to strength, durability, purity, and sacredness. Its role in mythological, religious, and daily life contexts underpins its value across various cultures in the region.
