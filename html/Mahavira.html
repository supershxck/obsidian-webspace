Mahavira: The Founder of Jainism in Its Present Form

Mahavira (599–527 BCE, traditionally), also known as Vardhamana, was the 24th and last Tirthankara (spiritual teacher) of [[Jainism]]. His teachings form the cornerstone of Jain philosophy, emphasizing nonviolence, self-discipline, and spiritual liberation. Though Jainism predates him, Mahavira is credited with systematizing and revitalizing its doctrines, making them accessible and practical for his followers.

I. Early Life of Mahavira

1. Birth and Family

• Name: Vardhamana, meaning “one who grows” or “one who brings prosperity.”

• Birthplace: Kundagrama, near Vaishali in present-day Bihar, India.

• Parents:

• Siddhartha: A nobleman and head of the Natha clan.

• Trishala (Priyakarini): A devout woman of the Licchavi clan, who is said to have had auspicious dreams foretelling Mahavira’s greatness.

• Social Status: Born into a Kshatriya (warrior class) family, Mahavira had a privileged upbringing.

2. Renunciation

• At the age of 30, after experiencing the impermanence of worldly pleasures, he renounced his royal life.

• He left behind his family and possessions to pursue spiritual enlightenment.

II. Spiritual Quest

1. Austerities and Meditation

• Mahavira practiced extreme asceticism, subjecting himself to rigorous self-discipline and penance.

• Spent 12 years meditating and enduring hardships, including wandering naked and fasting for extended periods.

• Practiced Ahimsa (nonviolence) to the highest degree, taking care not to harm even the smallest life forms.

2. Kevala Jnana (Omniscience)

• At the age of 42, Mahavira attained Kevala Jnana, the highest state of knowledge and enlightenment, while meditating under a sal tree near the Rijupalika River.

• With this realization, he became a Jina (conqueror of desires and senses), giving rise to the term Jain for his followers.

III. Teachings and Philosophy

1. Core Principles of Jainism

Mahavira emphasized five central vows (Panch Mahavrata) that form the foundation of Jain ethical practice:

  1. Ahimsa (Nonviolence):

• Avoidance of harm to any living being, whether in thought, word, or action.

  1. Satya (Truthfulness):

• Always speaking the truth, avoiding deceit and falsehood.

  1. Asteya (Non-stealing):

• Not taking anything that is not freely given.

  1. Brahmacharya (Celibacy):

• Renouncing sensual pleasures and maintaining control over desires.

  1. Aparigraha (Non-possession):

• Detachment from material possessions and wealth.

2. Doctrine of Non-Absolutism (Anekantavada)

• Mahavira taught the principle of Anekantavada, which asserts that truth and reality are multi-faceted and cannot be fully understood from a single perspective.

• Encourages tolerance and respect for diverse viewpoints.

3. Karma and Liberation

• Mahavira elaborated on the concept of karma as subtle matter that binds the soul due to passions and attachments.

• Liberation (Moksha) is achieved through self-discipline, right conduct, and the shedding of karma, leading to eternal bliss.

4. Equality and Simplicity

• Advocated equality among all living beings, transcending caste, gender, and social distinctions.

• Encouraged a simple and ethical life to reduce harm and promote spiritual progress.

IV. Life as a Teacher and Preacher

1. Establishment of Jain Sangha (Community)

• Mahavira organized his followers into a structured community:

• Monks (Sadhus)

• Nuns (Sadhvis)

• Laymen (Shravakas)

• Laywomen (Shravikas)

2. Spread of Teachings

• Traveled extensively across the Gangetic Plain (modern-day Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, and Bengal) for over 30 years, spreading his teachings in Prakrit, the common language of the people.

V. Death and Liberation

1. Nirvana

• Mahavira attained Nirvana (liberation from the cycle of birth and death) at the age of 72 in 527 BCE at Pavapuri, near Patna in modern-day Bihar.

• His passing is commemorated as Diwali in Jainism, symbolizing the light of wisdom.

VI. Key Contributions to Jainism

1. Systematization of Jain Doctrine

• Mahavira formalized the principles of Jainism, creating a cohesive philosophy based on nonviolence, asceticism, and spiritual liberation.

2. Promotion of Vernacular Language

• Used Prakrit instead of Sanskrit, making his teachings accessible to the common people.

3. Ethical and Environmental Awareness

• His emphasis on nonviolence and respect for all forms of life continues to inspire Jain dietary practices (e.g., vegetarianism) and environmental consciousness.

VII. Legacy and Influence

1. Formation of Jain Communities

• After Mahavira’s death, Jainism spread widely, particularly in western and southern India, becoming a major religious tradition.

2. Sacred Texts

• Mahavira’s teachings were orally transmitted and later compiled in the Agamas by his disciples.

• Different Jain sects, such as Svetambaras and Digambaras, interpret his teachings slightly differently, but both honor Mahavira as the central figure.

3. Philosophical Impact

• Mahavira’s teachings on nonviolence and karma influenced Indian philosophical traditions and leaders like Mahatma Gandhi, who adopted Ahimsa as a core principle of his political and social philosophy.

IX. Modern Relevance

• Environmentalism:

• Mahavira’s principles of nonviolence and minimalism resonate with modern environmental and sustainability movements.

• Ethical Living:

• His teachings encourage ethical consumerism and vegetarianism, aligning with contemporary ethical and health-conscious lifestyles.

• Global Impact:

• Though Jainism remains a relatively small religion (about 4–6 million adherents globally), its emphasis on nonviolence has had a profound impact on global leaders, philosophies, and movements.

X. Conclusion

Mahavira’s life and teachings continue to inspire millions around the world with their timeless message of peace, compassion, and self-realization. As the 24th Tirthankara, he not only redefined Jainism but also left a legacy that transcends religious boundaries, promoting harmony with all living beings and the natural world. His philosophy remains relevant in addressing modern issues like environmental degradation, violence, and inequality.
