The Wattasids (Arabic: الوطاسيون, al-waṭṭāsīyūn) were a ruling dynasty of Morocco that governed from 1472 to 1554. They were of Zenata Berber descent, like their predecessors, the Marinid dynasty, with whom they were closely related. The Wattasids emerged from a power struggle within the Marinid realm, eventually seizing control after a popular revolt in Fez in 1465 killed the last Marinid sultan, Abu Muhammad Abd al-Haqq. This revolt followed al-Haqq’s massacre of many Wattasids in 1459, leaving Abu Abd Allah al-Sheikh Muhammad ibn Yahya as a key survivor who became the first Wattasid sultan.

Historical Context

The Wattasids originated as a prominent family in the eastern Rif region, near present-day Nador, where they had been autonomous governors since the late 13th century. They rose to prominence under the Marinids by serving as viziers—high-ranking officials who often wielded more power than the sultans themselves. This influence allowed them to orchestrate a takeover when the Marinid dynasty weakened amid internal strife and external pressures, such as Portuguese and Spanish incursions during the late Reconquista period.

Rule and Challenges

The Wattasids ruled primarily over northern Morocco, with their capital in Fez, establishing what’s often called the Kingdom of Fez or Wattasid Sultanate. Their authority never extended fully to the south, which fragmented into various principalities until the rise of the Saadi dynasty. Their reign was marked by a policy of conciliation toward European powers, notably Portugal, which had begun occupying Moroccan coastal cities like Safi and Agadir. This approach, including a 1485 treaty with Spain to avoid conflict in the Alboran Sea, contrasted sharply with the Marinids’ resistance and earned the Wattasids a reputation as ineffective against foreign encroachment.

Decline and Fall

By the 16th century, the Saadi dynasty, based in southern Morocco, gained momentum. The Saadis capitalized on public discontent with Wattasid rule, portraying themselves as defenders against colonial powers after victories like the 1537 defeat of the Portuguese at Agadir. The Wattasids’ fate was sealed at the Battle of Tadla in 1554, where the Saadis decisively overthrew them. The last Wattasid sultan, Ali Abu Hassun, briefly retook Fez but was killed by the Saadis, and surviving Wattasids were murdered by pirates while fleeing by ship.

Legacy

The Wattasids are often seen as a transitional dynasty, bridging the Marinid and Saadi eras during a time of Moroccan decline. They failed to unify the country or halt European expansion, and their rule saw little progress in improving conditions post-Reconquista. However, their period coincided with cultural shifts, including the arrival of Jewish and Muslim refugees from Spain after 1492, some of whom became influential in their court. Their coinage, like rare gold coins and silver dirhams, followed earlier Almohad standards, reflecting continuity in economic traditions.

In essence, the Wattasids were a Berber dynasty whose brief rule reflected Morocco’s struggles with internal division and external threats, ultimately paving the way for the more assertive Saadi dynasty.
