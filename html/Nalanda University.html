Nalanda University: A Jewel of Ancient Indian Education

Nalanda University, located in present-day Bihar, India, was one of the world’s oldest and most renowned centers of higher learning. It functioned as a Buddhist monastic university between the 5th and 12th centuries CE, attracting students and scholars from across Asia and beyond. Nalanda became a symbol of India’s intellectual, cultural, and spiritual achievements.

I. Historical Background

1. Foundation

• Established: Around 5th century CE during the reign of Gupta Emperor Kumaragupta I (c. 415–455 CE).

• Purpose: Initially a Buddhist monastic center, it grew into a multidisciplinary university, offering education in religion, philosophy, medicine, astronomy, mathematics, and more.

2. Name and Location

• The name Nalanda likely derives from Na-Alam-Da, meaning “insatiable in giving” in Sanskrit, symbolizing the quest for knowledge.

• Located near Rajagriha (modern Rajgir), Nalanda was strategically situated near ancient trade routes, facilitating its global connections.

II. Structure and Facilities

1. Campus Layout

• The university spanned a vast area, with a complex of brick buildings, including:

• Lecture Halls

• Meditation Halls

• Libraries (referred to as Dharmaganja, the “Treasury of Truth”)

• Monasteries (Viharas) for monks and scholars.

• The campus was surrounded by gardens, ponds, and stupas.

2. The Libraries

• Nalanda’s library, one of the largest in the ancient world, consisted of three multi-storied buildings:

• Ratnasagara (Ocean of Jewels)

• Ratnadadhi (Sea of Jewels)

• Ratnaranjaka (Delight of Jewels)

• Contained thousands of manuscripts covering diverse subjects like Buddhist scriptures, philosophy, medicine, and astronomy.

• Manuscripts were preserved on palm leaves, birch bark, and other materials.

3. Accommodation

• The university housed around 10,000 students and 2,000 faculty members, offering residential facilities for both.

III. Academic System

1. Curriculum

• Subjects taught included:

• Buddhist Philosophy: Mahayana and Theravada traditions.

• Hindu Scriptures and Philosophy: Vedas, Upanishads, and Samkhya philosophy.

• Medicine and Ayurveda

• Mathematics and Astronomy

• Logic (Nyaya)

• Languages: Sanskrit, Pali, and foreign languages like Greek and Persian.

• Fine Arts and Architecture.

2. Admission

• Admission was highly competitive, requiring potential students to pass rigorous oral examinations administered by faculty.

3. Pedagogy

• Emphasized debate, discussion, and interactive learning.

• Combined practical application with theoretical studies.

4. International Students

• Attracted students and scholars from:

• China (e.g., Xuanzang and Yijing, who documented their studies at Nalanda).

• Tibet, Korea, Japan, Mongolia, Southeast Asia, and beyond.

IV. Prominent Scholars and Visitors

1. Xuanzang (Hiuen Tsang)

• A Chinese Buddhist monk who studied at Nalanda in the 7th century CE.

• Stayed for 12 years, documenting its thriving academic life in his travelogue Great Tang Records on the Western Regions.

2. Yijing

• Another Chinese scholar who spent a decade at Nalanda.

• Translated numerous Buddhist texts from Sanskrit to Chinese, enhancing cross-cultural knowledge exchange.

3. Aryabhata

• The renowned mathematician and astronomer is believed to have been associated with Nalanda.

4. Nagarjuna

• The Buddhist philosopher and founder of the Madhyamaka (Middle Way) school of thought, who influenced Nalanda’s teachings.

V. Patronage and Support

1. Gupta Dynasty

• Nalanda flourished under the Gupta emperors, who provided funding and resources for its expansion.

2. Harsha (606–647 CE)

• Emperor Harsha of Kanauj was a major patron, providing substantial financial aid to support scholars and infrastructure.

3. Pala Dynasty (8th–12th Century CE)

• The Palas, who were staunch Buddhists, elevated Nalanda to its zenith, supporting its growth as an international center for Buddhist studies.

VI. Decline and Destruction

1. Islamic Invasions

• In 1193 CE, Nalanda was sacked and destroyed by Bakhtiyar Khilji, a Turkic general of the Delhi Sultanate.

• The libraries were burned, reportedly smoldering for months due to the vast number of manuscripts.

2. Loss of Patronage

• Decline in Buddhist patronage and the rise of Hindu and Islamic influences contributed to the university’s gradual decline.

3. Aftermath

• The destruction of Nalanda marked a significant loss to global knowledge, as countless texts and centuries of scholarship were destroyed.

VII. Legacy and Modern Revival

1. Academic Influence

• Nalanda’s pedagogical and cultural contributions influenced the development of other ancient institutions like Vikramashila and Takshashila.

• Inspired the establishment of Buddhist centers across China, Tibet, and Southeast Asia.

2. Archaeological Discoveries

• Excavations in the 19th and 20th centuries revealed:

• Monastery remains, temples, and inscriptions.

• Artifacts, including Buddhist sculptures and inscriptions in Brahmi and Devanagari scripts.

3. Modern Nalanda University

• In 2014, the Nalanda University was re-established in Rajgir, Bihar, as an international university focusing on interdisciplinary studies, inspired by the ancient institution’s legacy.

IX. Conclusion

Nalanda University was a beacon of learning, attracting scholars from across the ancient world. Its emphasis on scholarly rigor, cross-cultural exchange, and interdisciplinary studies made it a precursor to modern universities. Though it was destroyed, its legacy endures as a symbol of India’s rich intellectual and cultural heritage, inspiring contemporary efforts to revive its spirit of learning.
