Benjamin Harrison (1833–1901): The Centennial President and Trust Buster

Introduction

Benjamin Harrison was the 23rd President of the United States (1889–1893), grandson of President William Henry Harrison. His administration is noted for its vigorous antitrust enforcement, expansion of federal authority, and significant legislative achievements that shaped late‑19th century America.

Early Life & Political Rise

• Born: August 20, 1833 in North Bend, Ohio

• Education: Miami University (Ohio), graduated 1852; studied law

• Civil War Service: Brigadier General in Union Army

• Political Career: U.S. Senator from Indiana (1881–1887)

Presidency (1889–1893)

|Issue|Action|Outcome| |---|---|---| |Antitrust Enforcement|Signed Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)|First federal law curbing monopolies; landmark case United States v. E.C. Knight Co. (1895)| |Tariff Policy|McKinley Tariff (1890)|Raised duties to protect American industry; contributed to public backlash| |Federal Spending|Expanded pensions for Civil War veterans|Federal budget tripled, earning nickname “Billion-Dollar Congress”| |Civil Rights|Supported federal voting rights enforcement|Limited success amid Southern resistance| |Admission of States|Admitted six new states (ND, SD, MT, WA, ID, WY)|Expanded Union and facilitated Western development|

Legacy & Long‑Term Influence

|Domain|Impact| |---|---| |Antitrust Law|Sherman Act foundation for modern competition policy| |Federal Government Role|Cemented precedent for active legislative agenda and federal oversight| |Electoral Politics|Lost re‑election in 1892 amid economic downturn, illustrating volatility of tariff politics|

Key Vocabulary

• Sherman Antitrust Act (1890): Landmark law prohibiting monopolistic practices

• McKinley Tariff: High protective tariff raising average rates to 49%

• Billion-Dollar Congress: First Congress to appropriate over $1 billion

• Trust Busting: Government action to break up corporate monopolies
