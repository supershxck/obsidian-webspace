The practice of asking for the intercession of saints is more explicitly developed in the tradition and teaching of the church, particularly within Roman Catholicism and Eastern Orthodoxy, rather than being directly outlined in the Bible. However, several biblical principles and passages are cited to support the practice and to argue for its consistency with Christian belief

The concept of the communion of saints suggests a spiritual solidarity between Christians on earth and those who have passed into the afterlife. This idea is supported by passages that speak to the unity of the Church, such as Hebrews 12:1, which describes a "great cloud of witnesses" surrounding and supporting the faithful, and the understanding that the Body of Christ transcends earthly life (1 Corinthians 12:12-27).

The practice of intercessory prayer is well established in Scripture, with numerous instances of individuals praying on behalf of others (e.g., Abraham praying for Abimelech in Genesis 20:17, Moses interceding for the Israelites in Exodus 32:11-14, and Job's intercession for his friends in Job 42:8-10). The New Testament also encourages believers to pray for one another (James 5:16). The logic extends that if believers are encouraged to pray for one another on earth, then the saints, who are alive in Christ and perfected in holiness, can also pray for us.

While 1 Timothy 2:5 affirms Jesus as the "one mediator between God and men," the context is about Christ's unique role in bringing reconciliation between God and humanity through His sacrificial death and resurrection. Asking saints to pray for us does not negate Christ's mediating role but is seen as participating in the communion He established, where all members of the Church support one another, including through prayer.

Revelation 5:8 and 8:3-4 describe the saints and angels in heaven offering the prayers of the saints on earth before God. This imagery supports the idea that those in heaven are aware of and can present the prayers of those on earth to God.

Protestant critiques of the practice often argue that the Bible does not explicitly endorse praying to saints and that our prayers should be directed to God alone through Jesus Christ. They emphasize the priesthood of all believers (1 Peter 2:9) and direct access to God through Christ (Hebrews 4:14-16).

The theological foundation for seeking the intercession of saints is rooted in the broader Christian understanding of the Church as comprising both the Church Militant (Christians on earth) and the Church Triumphant (the saints in heaven). This communion reflects a shared participation in the life of Christ, in which the faithful on earth can ask their brothers and sisters in heaven to pray to God on their behalf, just as they would ask fellow believers on earth to pray for them.

The practice of seeking the intercession of saints, therefore, while not explicitly addressed in the decrees of the Seventh Ecumenical Council, fits within the council's affirmation of the saints' ongoing presence and role within the life of the Church. The veneration of icons, as sanctioned by the council, often includes prayers asking for the saints' intercession, reflecting a continuity of faith and prayer that bridges the earthly and the heavenly realms.

In contrast, the Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox Churches, as well as some other Christian traditions, uphold the practice of asking saints to intercede with God on their behalf, seeing it not as a form of worship (which is due to God alone) but as a way of seeking the prayers of the whole body of Christ, including those who are with God in heaven. These traditions point to the communion of saints and emphasize that asking for a saint's intercession is fundamentally different from worshiping them, arguing that it is a misunderstanding to view this practice as violating the First Commandment. Each of these perspectives is rooted in different theological understandings and interpretations of Scripture and tradition. The Apostles' Creed, as a foundational statement of faith, does not directly address these later theological developments and debates.
