Poseidon (Ποσειδῶν) is one of the major deities of the ancient [[Greek pantheon]] and is best known as the god of the sea, earthquakes, storms, and horses. Below is an overview of his mythology, domains, and cultural significance:

1. Family and Origins

• Lineage: Poseidon is a son of the Titans [[Cronus]] and [[Rhea]], making him a brother to [[Zeus]] (king of the gods), [[Hades]] (god of the underworld), [[Hera]], [[Demeter]], and [[Hestia]].

• Olympian Rank: After the defeat of the Titans, the three brothers—[[Zeus]], Poseidon, and [[Hades]]—drew lots to divide the cosmos. Poseidon was given dominion over the sea.

2. Domains and Powers

  1. God of the Sea: Poseidon commands the oceans, wielding immense power over storms, waves, and sea creatures. Sailors and fishermen prayed to him for safe voyages and bountiful catches.

  2. Earthquakes: He is often referred to as the “Earth-Shaker” because of his ability to cause earthquakes by striking the ground with his trident.

  3. Horses: Poseidon is associated with the creation and protection of horses. In some myths, he is credited with fashioning the first horse out of sea foam.

3. Iconography and Symbols

• Trident: A three-pronged spear that symbolizes his sovereignty over the waters and power to shake the earth.

• Chariot Drawn by Hippocamps: Often depicted riding a chariot pulled by hippocamps (mythical sea-horse creatures), underscoring his lordship of the seas.

• Sea Creatures: Dolphins and fish frequently accompany him in art, highlighting his maritime domain.

4. Myths and Offspring

  1. Marriage to Amphitrite: Poseidon’s consort is Amphitrite, a sea nymph (one of the Nereids), who bears him [[Triton]], often depicted as a merman.

  2. Other Children: He fathers numerous heroes and beings, including the Cyclops Polyphemus and, in some tales, the horse Arion.

  3. Rivalries and Feuds: Poseidon competes with Athena for the patronage of Athens; Athena wins by gifting the olive tree, whereas Poseidon’s briny spring fails to impress the Athenian populace.

5. Worship and Cult

• Temples and Sanctuaries: Major sanctuaries to Poseidon stood at coastal or island locations, such as the Temple of Poseidon at Sounion (Attica), reflecting the importance of maritime activities in Greek life.

• Sacrifices and Festivals: Greeks offered sacrifices and held festivals to honor Poseidon, seeking his favor for safe sea travel and protection against earthquakes.

6. Cultural Impact

• Vital for Sea-Faring Greeks: Given the maritime nature of ancient Greece, Poseidon’s wrath or blessing meant the difference between disaster and prosperity.

• Symbol of Power and Unpredictability: He embodies the volatile nature of the sea and tectonic forces—respected and feared for his capacity to shape landscapes and sea routes.

Conclusion

Poseidon is a central figure in Greek mythology, wielding supreme authority over the seas, the shifting earth, and even the realm of horses. His presence is felt in coastal temples, stormy waters, and the foundations of Greek society itself, highlighting the Greeks’ deep reverence for the forces of nature he personifies.
