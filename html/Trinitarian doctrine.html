The doctrine of the Trinity, which describes God as one being in three persons (the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit), was developed and established over several centuries in early Christianity through debates, councils, and theological discourse.

The formal and more explicit articulation of the Trinitarian doctrine was established in the early ecumenical councils:

  1. [[First Council of Nicaea]] (325 A.D.): This council was convened to address the Arian controversy, which questioned the divinity of Jesus Christ. The Nicene Creed, formulated at this council, affirmed the full divinity of the Son, stating that He is "of the same essence" (homoousios) with the Father.

  2. [[First Council of Constantinople]] (381 A.D.): This council expanded the Nicene Creed to include a more detailed description of the Holy Spirit, affirming His divinity and procession from the Father, thus further solidifying the Trinitarian doctrine.

The term "Trinity" itself was used before these councils to describe God, but it was these councils, particularly through the Nicene Creed and its later expansions, that the orthodox understanding of the Trinity as one God in three persons was formally established and affirmed as central to Christian faith.
