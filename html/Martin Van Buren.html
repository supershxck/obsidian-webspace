Martin Van Buren (1782–1862): Founder of the Modern Democratic Party

Introduction

Martin Van Buren was the eighth President of the United States (1837–1841) and the first American‑born president. As architect of the Jacksonian Democratic Party, he professionalized political organization and championed the principle of party loyalty — laying the groundwork for America’s two‑party system.

Early Life & Rise in Politics

• Born: December 5, 1782 in Kinderhook, New York — of Dutch ancestry

• Legal Training: Admitted to the bar (1803); practiced law in Albany

• Political Apprenticeship: Clerk of New York State Senate; U.S. Senator (1821–1828); Governor of New York (1829)

• Jacksonian Alliance: Served as Secretary of State (1829–1831) and Vice President (1833–1837) under [[Andrew Jackson]]

Key Vocabulary

• Jacksonian Democracy: Political movement expanding suffrage for white men and emphasizing popular rule

• Independent Treasury: Federal system holding government funds separate from private banks

• Spoils System: Rewarding political supporters with public office

• National Convention: Party assembly to nominate presidential candidate
