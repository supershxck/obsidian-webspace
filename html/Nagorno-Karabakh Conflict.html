Chapter: The Nagorno‑Karabakh Conflict — Ethno‑Territorial Struggle at the Crossroads

Introduction

The Nagorno‑Karabakh conflict is a decades‑long ethno‑territorial dispute between Armenia and Azerbaijan over the predominantly ethnic Armenian enclave of Nagorno‑Karabakh (known in Azerbaijan as Qarabağ). Rooted in competing national narratives, Soviet legacy borders, and regional power dynamics, it has produced two full‑scale wars, periodic flare‑ups, and profound humanitarian consequences.

Historical Context

• Pre‑Soviet Era: Multi‑ethnic Caucasus region; Armenians and Azeris coexisted under shifting Persian and Ottoman rule.

• Soviet Delimitation (1923): Stalin assigned Nagorno‑Karabakh as an autonomous oblast within the Azerbaijan SSR despite its Armenian majority—seeding future tensions.

• Late Soviet Unrest (1988–1991): Rising Armenian nationalism and demands to transfer Nagorno‑Karabakh to Armenia triggered violent clashes.

Phases of the Conflict

|Phase|Timeline|Key Events|Outcome| |---|---|---|---| |First War|1988–1994|Ethnic violence; full war; Armenian forces capture Nagorno‑Karabakh + seven surrounding districts|1994 ceasefire; Armenian de facto control| |Frozen Conflict|1994–2020|Sporadic skirmishes; failed peace talks (Minsk Group)|Stalemate; humanitarian toll| |Second War|Sep–Nov 2020|Azerbaijan retakes territories via drone warfare|Russia‑brokered ceasefire; Azerbaijan regains ~70% occupied lands| |Post‑2020 Tensions|2021–Present|Border incidents; humanitarian blockade; Russian peacekeepers|Fragile status quo; renewed negotiations underway|

Root Causes & Drivers

  1. Ethnonational Identity: Competing historical narratives and territorial claims

  2. Soviet Legacy Borders: Administrative boundaries divorced from demographic realities

  3. Geopolitics: Turkey’s support for Azerbaijan; Russia’s balancing act; Iran’s cautious neutrality

  4. Resource & Strategic Interests: Control of transit corridors and energy routes

Strategic SWOT Analysis

|Strengths|Weaknesses|Opportunities|Threats| |---|---|---|---| |Azerbaijan’s military modernization|Armenia’s economic fragility|Multilateral mediation|Renewed full‑scale war| |Diaspora funding (Armenia)|Refugee/displacement crisis|Confidence‑building measures|Extremist radicalization| |Russian peacekeeping presence|Humanitarian abuses|Economic reconstruction|Regional power competition|

Psychological & Philosophical Reflection

The conflict exemplifies the “Tragic Cycle” archetype—historical grievances perpetuating violence across generations. Psychologically, collective trauma fuels intractability; philosophically, it challenges concepts of justice, sovereignty, and reconciliation.

Humanitarian Impact

• Casualties: >30,000 killed (both wars)

• Displacement: ~100,000 ethnic Armenians fled territories regained by Azerbaijan; 600,000 internally displaced Azeris since first war

• Cultural Loss: Damage to heritage sites; contested narratives of history

Current Status (March 2025)

• Territorial Control: Azerbaijan controls Nagorno‑Karabakh proper and adjacent districts; remaining Armenian population under Russian peacekeepers

• Diplomacy: Ongoing OSCE Minsk Group talks; bilateral negotiations intermittently mediated by Russia and EU

• Humanitarian Concerns: Blockade of Lachin corridor, access restrictions to basic services, rising civilian insecurity

Actionable Frameworks

1️⃣ Conflict Resolution Canvas

• Stakeholders: Armenia, Azerbaijan, Karabakh Armenians, Russia, Turkey, EU

• Core Issues: Territorial sovereignty, security guarantees, return of displaced persons, cultural heritage protection

• Phased Roadmap: Immediate ceasefire enforcement → confidence-building measures (prisoner exchanges, demining) → formal peace treaty → joint economic zones

The Nagorno-Karabakh Conflict: A Struggle Over Identity and Territory

Introduction: A Long-Standing Dispute

The Nagorno-Karabakh Conflict is a complex and protracted territorial and ethnic dispute primarily between Armenia and Azerbaijan over the region of Nagorno-Karabakh. Characterized by a mix of historical grievances, national identity, and geopolitical interests, the conflict has spanned several decades, resulting in intermittent warfare, displacement, and ongoing tension.

Historical Background

• Ethnic and Historical Roots:

Nagorno-Karabakh, a mountainous region within Azerbaijan’s borders, has been predominantly populated by ethnic Armenians for centuries. Historical claims and cultural ties contribute to Armenia’s sense of connection to the area, while Azerbaijan maintains its sovereignty over the territory.

• Soviet Era Policies:

During the Soviet period, Nagorno-Karabakh was established as an autonomous oblast within the Azerbaijan SSR. Soviet policies, which often suppressed ethnic tensions, temporarily muted underlying conflicts. However, the administrative decision also sowed the seeds of future disputes by placing a largely Armenian-populated region under Azerbaijani control.

The Escalation of the Conflict

• Late 1980s and the Dissolution of the Soviet Union:

As the Soviet Union began to disintegrate in the late 1980s, nationalist sentiments resurfaced. Armenians in Nagorno-Karabakh demanded unification with Armenia, triggering violent clashes between Armenian and Azerbaijani communities.

• Full-Scale War (1991–1994):

Following the collapse of the Soviet Union, the conflict escalated into a full-scale war. Armenian forces gained control over Nagorno-Karabakh and adjacent Azerbaijani territories, leading to significant casualties, widespread displacement, and a humanitarian crisis. A ceasefire was brokered in 1994, leaving Nagorno-Karabakh and surrounding areas under de facto Armenian control but remaining internationally recognized as part of Azerbaijan.

International Involvement and Geopolitical Dynamics

• Diplomatic Efforts and Mediation:

Various international bodies, including the Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe (OSCE) Minsk Group, have attempted to mediate a peaceful resolution. Despite numerous negotiations, a lasting settlement has proven elusive.

• Geopolitical Interests:

The conflict is further complicated by the strategic interests of regional and global powers. The involvement of neighboring countries and alliances adds layers of complexity, influencing both military support and diplomatic initiatives.

Recent Developments and the Current Situation

• Renewed Violence:

The conflict has seen periodic escalations, most notably in 2020, when a brief but intense war erupted, resulting in significant territorial shifts and casualties on both sides. A new ceasefire, brokered by Russia, has temporarily halted active hostilities but has not resolved the underlying issues.

• Ongoing Tensions:

Despite periods of calm, the Nagorno-Karabakh region remains a flashpoint. Sporadic clashes and military posturing continue to disrupt any long-term peace, with both Armenia and Azerbaijan firmly holding onto their historical, cultural, and political claims.

Conclusion: A Conflict Seeking Resolution

The Nagorno-Karabakh Conflict encapsulates the challenges of reconciling historical grievances with contemporary geopolitical realities. It is a dispute rooted in ethnic identity, territorial integrity, and the legacy of Soviet administrative decisions. While international efforts continue to push for a comprehensive peace agreement, the deep-seated nature of the conflict ensures that its resolution remains one of the most challenging issues in the South Caucasus.

This exploration of the Nagorno-Karabakh Conflict provides an in-depth overview of its historical origins, key phases of escalation, and the ongoing challenges to peace—inviting reflection on the complex interplay of identity, sovereignty, and regional geopolitics.
