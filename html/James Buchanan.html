James Buchanan (1791–1868): The President Who Failed to Prevent Civil War

Introduction

James Buchanan was the 15th President of the United States (1857–1861). His tenure is widely criticized for his inability to resolve escalating sectional tensions over slavery, setting the stage for the [[American Civil War]].

Early Life & Political Career

• Born: April 23, 1791 in Cove Gap, Pennsylvania

• Education: Dickinson College graduate (1809); studied law

• Political Rise: U.S. Representative (1821–1831), Senator (1834–1845), Secretary of State under President Polk (1845–1849), Minister to the United Kingdom (1853–1856)

Presidency (1857–1861)

|Issue|Action|Outcome| |---|---|---| |Dred Scott Decision|Supported Supreme Court ruling denying citizenship to African Americans|Intensified Northern outrage; legitimized slavery expansion| |Lecompton Constitution|Backed pro‑slavery Kansas state constitution despite popular opposition|Deepened sectional division; split Democratic Party| |Secession Crisis|Declared secession illegal but believed federal government powerless to stop it|Failed to build consensus or prepare for conflict|

Key Vocabulary

• Dred Scott Decision (1857): Supreme Court ruling denying African American citizenship

• Lecompton Constitution: Pro‑slavery Kansas constitution rejected by free‑state majority

• Secession: Formal withdrawal of states from the Union

• Popular Sovereignty: Doctrine letting territories decide slavery status
