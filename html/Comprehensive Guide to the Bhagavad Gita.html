A Comprehensive Guide to the Bhagavad Gita As It Is for Western Readers

Introduction

The Bhagavad Gita, often referred to simply as "the Gita," stands as one of the most influential spiritual texts in world literature. For Western readers approaching this ancient Sanskrit text, particularly through A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada's translation titled "Bhagavad Gita As It Is," there are numerous cultural, philosophical, and linguistic elements that may present challenges to full comprehension. This guide aims to bridge those gaps, providing Western readers with the context and understanding needed to approach the text with greater clarity and insight.

Prabhupada's translation, first published in complete form in 1972, has become one of the most widely distributed versions of the Bhagavad Gita in the world. As the founder of the International Society for Krishna Consciousness (ISKCON), Prabhupada brought a distinctly devotional perspective to his translation and commentary, presenting the text through the lens of Gaudiya Vaishnavism. While this provides valuable insight into one traditional interpretation of the text, Western readers benefit from understanding both the strengths and limitations of this approach.

This guide will explore the historical and cultural context of the Bhagavad Gita, explain key Sanskrit concepts and terminology, examine the narrative structure and symbolism, address translation challenges, and provide a chapter-by-chapter breakdown to facilitate deeper understanding. By approaching the text with this broader context, Western readers can engage more meaningfully with the philosophical and spiritual teachings contained within this timeless work.

Historical and Cultural Background

Origins and Dating

The Bhagavad Gita ("Song of the Lord") is a 700-verse Sanskrit text that appears as a section of the Mahabharata, one of India's great epic poems. Scholars generally date the composition of the Gita to between the 5th and 2nd centuries BCE, though traditional Hindu belief often attributes a much earlier date.

The text takes the form of a dialogue between Prince Arjuna and his charioteer Krishna, who is revealed to be an avatar (incarnation) of the god Vishnu. This conversation occurs on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, just before the start of a great war between two related families, the Pandavas (Arjuna's family) and the Kauravas.

A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada and ISKCON

Abhay Charanaravinda Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada (1896-1977) was born in Calcutta, India. After a career as a pharmaceutical businessman, he took formal initiation into the Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition in 1933. In 1959, he took the vows of sannyasa (renounced order), and in 1965, at the age of 69, he traveled to the United States to spread Krishna consciousness in the West.

In 1966, Prabhupada founded the International Society for Krishna Consciousness (ISKCON), commonly known as the Hare Krishna movement. His translation and commentary on the Bhagavad Gita, titled "Bhagavad Gita As It Is," was first published in a complete edition in 1972 and has since been translated into over 60 languages, with millions of copies distributed worldwide.

Prabhupada's approach to translating the Gita was distinctive in several ways:

  1. He presented the text from the perspective of Gaudiya Vaishnavism, emphasizing devotion to Krishna as the Supreme Personality of Godhead
  2. He included the original Sanskrit text, transliteration, word-for-word translation, and flowing English translation
  3. He provided extensive commentary (purports) explaining the verses from his theological perspective
  4. He claimed to present the text "as it is," suggesting his interpretation was the authentic understanding of the text without "personal motivation"

The Bhagavad Gita in Context

To fully appreciate the Bhagavad Gita, it's important to understand its place within the larger Mahabharata epic and Hindu tradition:

  1. The Mahabharata Context: The Gita occurs in the Bhishma Parva (Book 6) of the Mahabharata, just before the great battle begins. Understanding the relationships between the characters and the events leading to the war provides essential context for the dialogue.

  2. Hindu Philosophical Traditions: The Gita synthesizes various philosophical systems of ancient India, including Samkhya, Yoga, Vedanta, and aspects of the Upanishads. It presents a unified vision that incorporates paths of knowledge (jnana), action (karma), and devotion (bhakti).

  3. Historical Impact: Throughout Indian history, the Gita has been interpreted in numerous ways by different philosophical and religious traditions. Prabhupada's interpretation represents one important tradition among many.

Key Sanskrit Concepts and Terminology

Understanding key Sanskrit terms is essential for grasping the philosophical concepts in the Bhagavad Gita. Below is a glossary of important terms that appear frequently in the text:

Philosophical Concepts

  • Atman: The eternal self or soul, distinct from the physical body and mind.
  • Brahman: The ultimate reality, the absolute or universal consciousness in Vedantic philosophy.
  • Dharma: Righteousness, duty, cosmic order; one's ethical and moral obligations according to one's nature and place in society.
  • Karma: Action and its consequences; the universal law of cause and effect.
  • Maya: The illusory nature of the material world; the power that veils the true nature of reality.
  • Moksha: Liberation from the cycle of rebirth and death; spiritual freedom.
  • Samsara: The cycle of birth, death, and rebirth; the world of suffering and transience.

Spiritual Practices

  • Bhakti: Devotional service or loving devotion to God.
  • Dhyana: Meditation, contemplation.
  • Jnana: The path of knowledge or wisdom.
  • Karma Yoga: The yoga of selfless action without attachment to results.
  • Yoga: Union with the divine; various spiritual disciplines leading to such union.

Metaphysical Terms

  • Gunas: The three qualities of material nature: sattva (goodness, purity), rajas (passion, activity), and tamas (ignorance, inertia).
  • Prakriti: Material nature; the manifest world of matter and energy.
  • Purusha: The cosmic spirit or consciousness; the observer of prakriti.

Divine Beings and Concepts

  • Avatar: Divine incarnation, particularly of Vishnu.
  • Bhagavan: The Supreme Lord possessing all opulences.
  • Ishvara: The Supreme Controller, God.
  • Krishna: In the Gita, the charioteer who reveals himself as the Supreme Personality of Godhead.
  • Paramatma: The Supersoul, the localized aspect of God present in the heart of every living entity.

Social and Ethical Concepts

  • Ahimsa: Non-violence in thought, word, and deed.
  • Varnashrama: The traditional Hindu social system of four varnas (social classes) and four ashramas (stages of life).
  • Yajna: Sacrifice, ritual worship.

Cultural Context and Symbolism

The Battlefield Setting (Kurukshetra)

The Bhagavad Gita takes place on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, which has both literal and symbolic significance:

  1. Literal Context: Kurukshetra is the field where the Pandavas and Kauravas are about to engage in a devastating war.

  2. Symbolic Meaning: The battlefield represents the field of dharma (righteousness) where the battle between good and evil, right and wrong takes place.

  3. Inner Symbolism: For many interpreters, including Mahatma Gandhi, the battlefield represents the human body or mind where the constant struggle between higher and lower impulses occurs.

  4. Spiritual Interpretation: In Prabhupada's translation, the battlefield is presented as the place where material attachments must be overcome through spiritual knowledge.

Key Symbolic Elements

  1. The Chariot: Krishna as charioteer represents the divine guiding the human soul (Arjuna) through life's journey. The horses symbolize the senses, the reins represent the mind, and the chariot itself represents the body.

  2. Arjuna's Bow (Gandiva): Represents the strength and determination needed to fight life's battles.

  3. The Conch Shells: Blown before the battle, they represent the divine call to spiritual awakening. Each hero's conch has a specific name and significance.

  4. The Cosmic Form (Vishvarupa): Krishna's revelation of his universal form in Chapter 11 symbolizes the all-encompassing nature of the divine, containing all of existence within it.

Cultural Elements Essential for Western Readers

  1. Cyclical vs. Linear Time: Unlike the Western linear conception of time, the Gita presents a cyclical view with vast cosmic cycles (yugas). This affects how concepts like creation, destruction, and eternity are understood.

  2. The Guru-Disciple Relationship: The dialogue between Krishna and Arjuna exemplifies the sacred guru-disciple relationship in Hindu tradition, where spiritual knowledge is transmitted directly from teacher to student.

  3. Non-Dualistic Philosophy: The relationship between the individual soul and the supreme is complex and may seem paradoxical to Western readers accustomed to strict distinctions between creator and creation.

  4. Dharma and Social Duty: The concept of dharma (duty according to one's nature and position) is central to the Gita's ethical framework and differs from Western individualistic ethics.

Translation Challenges for Western Readers

Sanskrit to English Translation Nuances

Translating the Bhagavad Gita from Sanskrit to English presents several challenges:

  1. Untranslatable Concepts: Many Sanskrit terms have no direct English equivalents and carry complex meanings that cannot be fully captured in a single English word.

  2. Grammatical Differences: Sanskrit has a complex grammatical structure that allows for nuanced meanings through its case system, compound words, and verb forms that don't exist in English.

  3. Poetic Structure: The Gita is written in poetic verse with specific meter and rhythm that is difficult to preserve in translation while maintaining the literal meaning.

  4. Cultural Context: Many concepts in the Gita are embedded in cultural and historical contexts that are unfamiliar to Western readers.

Prabhupada's Translation Approach

Prabhupada's approach to translating the Bhagavad Gita has several distinctive characteristics:

  1. Word-for-Word Translation: He provides a word-for-word translation from Sanskrit to English, allowing readers to see the original structure of the text.

  2. Verse Translation: Following the word-for-word translation, he provides a more readable verse translation.

  3. Extensive Commentary (Purports): The majority of "Bhagavad Gita As It Is" consists of Prabhupada's explanations and interpretations of the verses.

  4. Gaudiya Vaishnava Lens: Prabhupada explicitly translates and interprets the Gita through the theological framework of Gaudiya Vaishnavism, emphasizing devotion to Krishna as the Supreme Personality of Godhead.

  5. Claim to Authenticity: The title "As It Is" reflects Prabhupada's claim that his translation presents the Gita without "adulteration" or "personal motivation."

Comparison with Other Translations

To understand the distinctive features of Prabhupada's translation, it's helpful to compare it with other popular English translations:

  1. Eknath Easwaran's Translation: Known for its accessibility and readability, with a focus on making the text approachable for Western readers.

  2. Stephen Mitchell's Translation: Known for its poetic quality and literary merit, prioritizing readability and poetic flow over literal accuracy.

  3. Georg Feuerstein's Translation: More scholarly and academic, with extensive linguistic notes and detailed philological and historical commentary.

Strategies for Western Readers

To navigate these translation challenges, Western readers can adopt several strategies:

  1. Compare Multiple Translations: Reading several translations side by side can provide a more nuanced understanding of difficult passages.

  2. Distinguish Translation from Commentary: Be aware of where the translator's interpretation begins and the original text ends.

  3. Learn Key Sanskrit Terms: Familiarize yourself with untranslatable Sanskrit terms rather than relying solely on English approximations.

  4. Contextual Reading: Understand the Gita in its historical and cultural context, as well as its place within the larger Mahabharata.

Chapter-by-Chapter Breakdown

Chapter 1: Arjuna's Dilemma (Arjuna-Vishada Yoga)

Summary: The first chapter sets the scene on the battlefield of Kurukshetra. Arjuna, seeing his relatives, teachers, and friends on the opposing side, is overcome with grief and moral dilemma. He questions the righteousness of fighting and killing his own kinsmen and expresses his desire to withdraw from battle.

Key Concepts: - Moral dilemma (dharma-sankata) - Compassion and attachment - The setting of the battlefield as both literal and metaphorical

Western Reader Notes: - This chapter establishes the narrative framework for the philosophical teachings that follow - Arjuna's dilemma represents universal ethical questions about duty versus compassion - Prabhupada's commentary emphasizes that Arjuna's compassion is misplaced sentimentality rather than true spiritual understanding

Chapter 2: Transcendental Knowledge (Sankhya Yoga)

Summary: Krishna begins his teachings by explaining the immortality of the soul (atman) and the temporary nature of the body. He introduces the concepts of dharma (duty) and explains that Arjuna, as a warrior, must fulfill his duty without attachment to the results. This chapter introduces key philosophical concepts that will be developed throughout the text.

Key Concepts: - Immortality of the soul (atman) - Reincarnation - Duty (dharma) according to one's nature - Equanimity in pleasure and pain - Introduction to Karma Yoga (the path of action)

Western Reader Notes: - This chapter contains the famous verse about the soul changing bodies "as a person puts on new garments" - The concept of duty (dharma) differs from Western notions of universal ethics - Prabhupada emphasizes the distinction between the eternal soul and the temporary body

Chapter 3: Karma Yoga (The Path of Action)

Summary: Krishna explains the importance of performing one's duties without attachment to the fruits of action. He clarifies that renunciation does not mean inaction but rather action without selfish desire. This chapter emphasizes that everyone must work according to their nature, but without being bound by the results.

Key Concepts: - Karma Yoga (selfless action) - The cycle of sacrifice and prosperity - Action vs. inaction - Desire as the enemy of wisdom

Western Reader Notes: - The concept of detached action may seem paradoxical to Western readers - Prabhupada's commentary emphasizes that all action should be performed as service to Krishna - The social duty described here is based on the varnashrama system (traditional Hindu social structure)

Chapter 4: Transcendental Knowledge (Jnana Yoga)

Summary: Krishna reveals his divine nature and explains the purpose of his incarnations: to reestablish dharma whenever there is a decline in righteousness. He describes different types of sacrifices and emphasizes that the highest wisdom comes from approaching a spiritual master who has realized the truth.

Key Concepts: - Divine incarnations (avatars) - Various types of sacrifice - The role of the spiritual master - Knowledge as the purifier

Western Reader Notes: - Prabhupada strongly emphasizes Krishna's divinity in this chapter - The concept of cyclical time and recurring divine incarnations differs from linear Western religious narratives - The guru-disciple relationship is presented as essential for spiritual advancement

Chapter 5: Karma-Sannyasa Yoga (Action and Renunciation)

Summary: Krishna explains that both the path of action (karma yoga) and the path of renunciation (sannyasa) lead to the same goal, but karma yoga is generally more practical. The truly wise see action in inaction and inaction in action, understanding that the self neither acts nor causes action.

Key Concepts: - Unity of action and renunciation - Self-control and inner peace - Seeing the divine in all beings - Liberation through detachment

Western Reader Notes: - The apparent contradiction between action and renunciation is resolved through detachment - Prabhupada's commentary emphasizes that Krishna consciousness is the perfect state of renunciation - The concept of seeing the same divine essence in all beings (a brahmin, a cow, an elephant, a dog, or a dog-eater) may challenge Western hierarchical thinking

Chapter 6: Dhyana Yoga (The Path of Meditation)

Summary: Krishna describes the practice of meditation, explaining the proper posture, mental attitude, and focus. He emphasizes that the mind is both the friend and enemy of the self, depending on whether it is controlled or uncontrolled. The chapter concludes with the assertion that the yogi is greater than the ascetic, the scholar, or the worker.

Key Concepts: - Meditation techniques - Control of the mind - Moderation in all activities - The nature of the perfect yogi

Western Reader Notes: - This chapter contains practical instructions for meditation that influenced modern yoga practices - Prabhupada interprets the meditation described here as ultimately leading to Krishna consciousness - The balanced lifestyle recommended (neither too much nor too little sleep, food, or work) offers practical wisdom

Chapter 7: Knowledge of the Absolute (Jnana-Vijnana Yoga)

Summary: Krishna describes his divine manifestations and explains how he is the source of everything. He discusses his two natures (material and spiritual) and explains why most people cannot recognize his supreme form. The chapter introduces the concept of surrender to the divine.

Key Concepts: - Material and spiritual natures of the divine - The divine as the source of everything - Four types of people who approach God - Four types of people who don't approach God

Western Reader Notes: - Prabhupada's commentary strongly emphasizes Krishna as the supreme God above all other deities - The concept of God having different energies or natures may differ from Western monotheistic conceptions - The categorization of devotees and non-devotees reflects the devotional perspective of Gaudiya Vaishnavism

Chapter 8: Attaining the Supreme (Aksara-Brahma Yoga)

Summary: Krishna explains what happens at the time of death and how one's consciousness at that moment determines one's next destination. He describes the paths of light and darkness (the solar and lunar paths) and emphasizes that those who remember him at the time of death will come to him.

Key Concepts: - The importance of one's final thoughts at death - Cosmic time cycles (yugas and kalpas) - The paths of return and non-return - Constant remembrance of the divine

Western Reader Notes: - The vast time scales mentioned (billions of years) may seem fantastical to Western readers but reflect Hindu cosmology - Prabhupada emphasizes the practice of constantly chanting Krishna's names to ensure remembrance at death - The concept that one's thoughts at death determine one's destination has parallels in some Western mystical traditions

Chapter 9: The Most Confidential Knowledge (Raja-Vidya Yoga)

Summary: Krishna reveals the most confidential knowledge: his relationship with the material world and how he remains unattached while sustaining everything. He emphasizes that even those considered lowborn can attain the supreme destination through devotion. This chapter strongly emphasizes the path of devotion (bhakti).

Key Concepts: - God's relationship with the material world - The universality of spiritual opportunity - The supreme value of devotion - The divine as the ultimate recipient of all offerings

Western Reader Notes: - Prabhupada's commentary on this chapter strongly emphasizes exclusive devotion to Krishna - The democratic spiritual vision (that anyone regardless of birth can attain God) contrasts with traditional caste-based restrictions - The emphasis on devotion rather than ritual correctness has parallels in Western devotional traditions

Chapter 10: The Opulence of the Absolute (Vibhuti Yoga)

Summary: Krishna describes his divine manifestations in the world, explaining that he is the source of all material and spiritual worlds. He lists his principal manifestations, showing how the divine is present in the most excellent of every category of existence.

Key Concepts: - Divine manifestations in the world - God as the source of all qualities and abilities - The universe as a fraction of divine energy

Western Reader Notes: - This chapter provides a way of seeing the divine in everyday excellence - Prabhupada's commentary connects the Sanskrit concepts to their Western equivalents where possible - The list of manifestations includes many references to Indian mythology and culture that may be unfamiliar to Western readers

Chapter 11: The Universal Form (Vishvarupa-Darshana Yoga)

Summary: At Arjuna's request, Krishna reveals his cosmic form (Vishvarupa), which contains all beings, all worlds, and all time. Arjuna is overwhelmed by this terrifying and magnificent vision, seeing the simultaneous creation and destruction of the universe. He begs Krishna to return to his four-armed and then human-like forms.

Key Concepts: - The cosmic form of God - Divine omnipresence - The terrifying aspect of the divine - Time as the destroyer

Western Reader Notes: - This chapter's description of the cosmic form has influenced Hindu iconography and art - Prabhupada emphasizes that this vision confirms Krishna's supreme position - The vision of God as simultaneously creator and destroyer may challenge Western conceptions of divine benevolence

Chapter 12: Devotional Service (Bhakti Yoga)

Summary: Arjuna asks which path is better: devotion to the personal form of God or meditation on the impersonal absolute. Krishna responds that devotion to the personal form is easier and more direct, though both paths lead to him. He describes the qualities of the ideal devotee.

Key Concepts: - Personal vs. impersonal approaches to God - The superiority of devotion (according to Krishna) - Qualities of the ideal devotee - Gradual spiritual advancement

Western Reader Notes: - Prabhupada's commentary strongly favors the personal approach to God over the impersonal - The qualities of the ideal devotee (free from hatred, compassionate, without ego) have universal spiritual appeal - The emphasis on devotion rather than philosophical speculation may resonate with Western devotional traditions

Chapter 13: Nature, the Enjoyer, and Consciousness (Ksetra-Ksetrajna Vibhaga Yoga)

Summary: Krishna explains the difference between the body (the field) and the knower of the body (the knower of the field). He describes what constitutes knowledge and the nature of the Supreme. This chapter contains some of the most philosophical content in the Gita.

Key Concepts: - The body as the field of activities - The soul as the knower of the field - God as the ultimate knower of all fields - The constituents of true knowledge

Western Reader Notes: - The field metaphor helps explain the relationship between consciousness and matter - Prabhupada's commentary connects these concepts to Sankhya philosophy - The list of qualities constituting knowledge (humility, nonviolence, tolerance, etc.) provides practical ethical guidance

Chapter 14: The Three Modes of Material Nature (Gunatraya-Vibhaga Yoga)

Summary: Krishna explains the three modes (gunas) of material nature: goodness (sattva), passion (rajas), and ignorance (tamas). He describes how these modes affect the embodied soul and how one can transcend them to attain liberation.

Key Concepts: - The three modes of nature (gunas) - How the modes bind the soul - Characteristics of people influenced by each mode - Transcending the modes

Western Reader Notes: - The concept of the three gunas provides a psychological framework different from Western models - Prabhupada's commentary emphasizes that Krishna consciousness is beyond all material modes - The description of how different modes influence behavior, food preferences, and worship has practical applications

Chapter 15: The Yoga of the Supreme Person (Purushottama Yoga)

Summary: Krishna describes the material world as an inverted banyan tree with roots upward and branches downward. He explains that one must cut this tree with the weapon of detachment and seek the supreme abode. He describes the living entities as eternal fragments of himself and explains his three manifestations.

Key Concepts: - The inverted tree metaphor for material existence - The soul as a fragment of God - The three manifestations of the divine (Brahman, Paramatma, Bhagavan) - The supreme person beyond the perishable and imperishable

Western Reader Notes: - Prabhupada interprets this chapter as establishing Krishna as the Supreme Personality of Godhead above all other conceptions - The inverted tree metaphor may be unfamiliar but provides a powerful image of material existence - The concept of the soul as a fragment of God differs from Western notions of the created soul

Chapter 16: The Divine and Demoniac Natures (Daivasura-Sampad-Vibhaga Yoga)

Summary: Krishna describes the qualities of those with divine nature and those with demoniac nature. He explains how demoniac qualities lead to bondage and suffering, while divine qualities lead to liberation. This chapter provides clear ethical guidance.

Key Concepts: - Divine qualities (fearlessness, purity, charity, etc.) - Demoniac qualities (pride, anger, harshness, etc.) - The consequences of demoniac nature - The three gates to hell: lust, anger, and greed

Western Reader Notes: - Prabhupada's commentary on this chapter is particularly strong in its condemnation of materialistic civilization - The ethical framework presented has both similarities and differences with Western moral traditions - The concept of "demoniac" nature refers to qualities rather than specific beings or people

Chapter 17: The Divisions of Faith (Shraddhatraya-Vibhaga Yoga)

Summary: Krishna explains how faith, worship, food, austerity, and charity are divided according to the three modes of nature. He emphasizes that actions performed without faith in the scriptures are fruitless and explains the meaning of OM TAT SAT, the threefold designation of Brahman.

Key Concepts: - Faith according to the three modes - Food preferences in different modes - Austerity of body, speech, and mind - The meaning of OM TAT SAT

Western Reader Notes: - The classification of foods may be of particular interest to those following vegetarian diets - Prabhupada's commentary emphasizes that activities should be performed according to scriptural injunctions - The concept that one's faith is determined by one's nature provides insight into religious diversity

Chapter 18: Conclusion—The Perfection of Renunciation (Moksha-Sannyasa Yoga)

Summary: In this final and longest chapter, Krishna summarizes the key teachings of the Gita. He explains different types of renunciation, the factors involved in action, knowledge according to the three modes, and the duties of the four social orders. He concludes by emphasizing complete surrender to him as the highest path.

Key Concepts: - Different types of renunciation - The five factors of action - Knowledge, action, and actors in the three modes - The duties of the four social orders - Complete surrender to God

Western Reader Notes: - Prabhupada's commentary on this chapter strongly emphasizes surrender to Krishna as the ultimate conclusion of the Gita - The description of duties according to the four social orders (varna) reflects traditional Hindu social structure - The final instruction to "abandon all varieties of religion and just surrender unto Me" is interpreted by Prabhupada as the essence of the entire Gita

Thematic Exploration

The Three Paths to Realization

The Bhagavad Gita presents three main spiritual paths, which are not mutually exclusive but rather complementary approaches to self-realization:

  1. Karma Yoga (The Path of Action):
  2. Performing one's duties without attachment to results
  3. Acting as an offering to the divine
  4. Maintaining equanimity in success and failure

  5. Western parallel: "Do your duty" ethics, virtue ethics

  6. Jnana Yoga (The Path of Knowledge):

  7. Discriminating between the eternal and the temporary
  8. Realizing the true nature of the self beyond the body and mind
  9. Cultivating wisdom through study and contemplation

  10. Western parallel: Philosophical inquiry, contemplative traditions

  11. Bhakti Yoga (The Path of Devotion):

  12. Cultivating loving devotion to the divine
  13. Surrendering one's will to God's will
  14. Engaging in devotional practices like prayer, chanting, and worship
  15. Western parallel: Devotional Christianity, Sufism

In Prabhupada's interpretation, while all three paths are valid, bhakti yoga is presented as the highest and most direct path, particularly appropriate for the current age (Kali Yuga).

The Concept of Dharma

Dharma is a central concept in the Bhagavad Gita that can be challenging for Western readers to fully grasp:

  1. Multiple Meanings:
  2. Cosmic order or law
  3. Righteousness or virtue
  4. Duty according to one's nature and position

  5. Religious or spiritual path

  6. Contextual Application:

  7. Arjuna's immediate dharma as a warrior (kshatriya) is to fight
  8. Universal dharma includes qualities like truthfulness and nonviolence

  9. Dharma varies according to one's nature (svabhava) and position (varnashrama)

  10. Dharma vs. Western Ethics:

  11. Contextual rather than universal
  12. Based on cosmic order rather than human reasoning
  13. Emphasizes duty to society rather than individual rights

Prabhupada's commentary emphasizes that the highest dharma is devotional service to Krishna, which transcends all other duties.

The Nature of the Self

The Bhagavad Gita presents a view of the self that differs significantly from common Western conceptions:

  1. The Eternal Soul (Atman):
  2. Not created but eternal
  3. Neither born nor dies
  4. Unchanging amidst bodily changes

  5. A fragment of the divine

  6. The Body-Mind Complex:

  7. Temporary vehicle for the soul
  8. Composed of material elements
  9. Subject to the three modes of nature

  10. The "field" in which the soul operates

  11. The Relationship with the Divine:

  12. In Prabhupada's interpretation, the soul is eternally individual but qualitatively one with God
  13. The concept of "acintya-bheda-abheda" (inconceivable oneness and difference)
  14. The soul's natural position is service to God

This understanding challenges both Western materialistic views (that consciousness is merely a product of brain activity) and some Western religious views (that the soul is created at birth).

The Concept of Time

The Bhagavad Gita presents a view of time that differs dramatically from Western linear conceptions:

  1. Cosmic Cycles:
  2. Four yugas (ages) in each cycle: Satya, Treta, Dvapara, and Kali
  3. Each complete cycle (mahayuga) lasts 4.32 million years
  4. 1,000 mahayugas form one day of Brahma (kalpa)

  5. Repeated creation and dissolution of the universe

  6. Time as a Manifestation of the Divine:

  7. Krishna declares, "I am time, the destroyer of all"
  8. Time as an aspect of God's power

  9. Time as both creator and destroyer

  10. Transcending Time:

  11. The soul exists beyond time
  12. Liberation means freedom from the cycle of time
  13. The divine realm exists in eternal present

This cyclical view of time provides context for concepts like reincarnation and the periodic appearance of avatars.

Practical Application for Western Readers

Approaching the Text

For Western readers approaching the Bhagavad Gita As It Is, here are some practical suggestions:

  1. Read Multiple Translations: Compare Prabhupada's translation with others like Eknath Easwaran's or Stephen Mitchell's to gain different perspectives.

  2. Distinguish Translation from Commentary: Be aware that Prabhupada's purports represent his interpretation through the lens of Gaudiya Vaishnavism.

  3. Start with Key Chapters: If the entire text seems overwhelming, begin with Chapters 1, 2, and 12, which provide a good introduction to the narrative and key teachings.

  4. Keep a Glossary Handy: Refer to the glossary of Sanskrit terms provided in this guide when encountering unfamiliar concepts.

  5. Read Contextually: Remember that the Gita is part of the larger Mahabharata epic and reflects the cultural and historical context of ancient India.

Applying the Teachings

The Bhagavad Gita offers practical wisdom that can be applied regardless of one's cultural or religious background:

  1. Mindful Action: Practice performing your duties with full attention while remaining detached from the results.

  2. Equanimity: Cultivate evenness of mind in success and failure, pleasure and pain.

  3. Self-Knowledge: Reflect on the distinction between your eternal self and your temporary body and mind.

  4. Moderation: Follow the middle path in all activities—eating, sleeping, recreation, and work.

  5. Devotional Attitude: Approach your work and relationships with a spirit of service rather than self-interest.

Navigating Cultural Differences

When encountering aspects of the text that reflect cultural values different from Western norms:

  1. Historical Context: Remember that the text reflects the social structures and gender roles of ancient India.

  2. Universal Principles: Look for the universal principles underlying culturally specific expressions.

  3. Metaphorical Reading: Consider whether passages about warfare, social hierarchy, or gender roles can be understood metaphorically.

  4. Critical Engagement: It's possible to deeply value the spiritual wisdom of the text while critically engaging with aspects that may conflict with contemporary values.

Conclusion

The Bhagavad Gita As It Is offers Western readers a profound spiritual and philosophical perspective that can enrich their understanding of themselves and their relationship with the divine. By approaching the text with awareness of its cultural context, key concepts, and translation challenges, readers can engage more deeply with its timeless wisdom.

Prabhupada's translation and commentary provide valuable insight into one traditional interpretation of the Gita through the lens of Gaudiya Vaishnavism. While recognizing the devotional perspective that shapes his approach, readers can appreciate the depth of spiritual insight he brings to the text.

Whether approached as a religious scripture, a philosophical treatise, or a guide to ethical living, the Bhagavad Gita continues to speak to seekers across cultural boundaries. Its exploration of duty, knowledge, devotion, and the nature of reality offers wisdom that transcends its historical origins, inviting readers into a dialogue that has continued for over two millennia.

As Krishna tells Arjuna in the final chapter, "Reflect on this knowledge I have taught you, and then do as you wish." This invitation to thoughtful engagement rather than blind acceptance makes the Gita particularly valuable for contemporary readers seeking to integrate ancient wisdom with modern life.
