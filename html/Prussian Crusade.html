The Prussian Crusade (1230–1283): The Christianization and Conquest of the Old Prussians

The Prussian Crusade (1230–1283) was a military campaign led by the Teutonic Knights to convert the pagan Old Prussians to Christianity and incorporate their lands into Christendom. It was part of the larger Northern Crusades, which targeted pagan Baltic and Slavic peoples.

This brutal war resulted in the destruction of Prussian tribal society, the settlement of German, Polish, and other Christian colonists, and the formation of the State of the Teutonic Order, which later evolved into Prussia.

1. The Old Prussians: Who Were They?

Before the crusade, the Old Prussians were a pagan Baltic people living in what is now modern-day northern Poland, Kaliningrad (Russia), and Lithuania.

• Spoke a Baltic language, related to Lithuanian and Latvian.

• Practiced pagan religion, worshiping gods like Perkūnas (thunder god).

• Lived in tribal societies, resisting foreign rule.

• Were raided by Christian Poles and Germans, who saw them as heathens.

2. Why Did the Prussian Crusade Happen?

The crusade was driven by a mix of religion, politics, and territorial expansion.

A. The Pope and the Northern Crusades

• The Catholic Church wanted to Christianize the pagan Balts, just as they had done with Slavs and Germans.

• Pope Innocent III (1198–1216) called for a crusade against the Prussians, similar to the Crusades in the Holy Land.

B. Polish and German Interests

• Duke Konrad of Masovia (Poland) had fought the Prussians for years but failed to defeat them.

• In 1226, Konrad invited the Teutonic Knights to conquer Prussia in exchange for land.

• In 1230, the Pope officially authorized the Teutonic Order to wage a holy war.

3. The Teutonic Knights and the Conquest of Prussia

The Teutonic Order, a militarized Catholic monastic order, led the Prussian Crusade. They had already fought in the Holy Land and Hungary before focusing on Prussia.

A. Early Campaigns (1230–1249)

• The Knights built castles along the Vistula River.

• Launched brutal attacks on Prussian tribes, forcing many to convert or be killed.

• In 1242, the Prussians allied with the pagan Lithuanians, but the Knights crushed the rebellion.

• The Treaty of Christburg (1249) forced many Prussians to accept Christianity.

B. The Great Prussian Uprising (1260–1274)

• Inspired by Lithuania’s victory over the Knights at Durbe (1260), the Prussians revolted.

• Led by figures like Herkus Monte, the Prussians waged guerrilla warfare, destroying Teutonic castles.

• The Knights responded with extreme brutality, executing captured Prussian leaders.

• By 1274, the rebellion was crushed, and most Prussians were either slaughtered or forced to assimilate.

C. Final Conquest (1274–1283)

• The last free Prussian tribes were subdued by 1283.

• Survivors were converted, enslaved, or driven into exile.

• The Teutonic Order fully controlled Prussia, setting up a Christian knightly state.

4. Aftermath: The End of the Old Prussians

• German settlers moved into Prussia, bringing Catholicism and feudalism.

• The Old Prussian language and culture declined, disappearing by the 17th century.

• The Teutonic Order ruled Prussia for centuries, eventually becoming the Duchy of Prussia, a foundation for modern Germany.

5. Why the Prussian Crusade Matters

✅ Erased an entire pagan culture, similar to the Crusades in the Holy Land.

✅ Led to the rise of Prussia, which later became a European superpower.

✅ Shaped the Baltic region, influencing Polish, German, and Lithuanian history.
