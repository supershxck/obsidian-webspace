Who Was Pythagoras?

Pythagoras (c. 570–495 BCE) was a Greek philosopher and mathematician best known for his contributions to geometry, particularly the Pythagorean Theorem, which relates the sides of a right triangle. His ideas extended beyond mathematics, influencing fields like philosophy, music theory, and cosmology.

Pythagoras the Philosopher

Pythagoras founded a religious movement known as the Pythagorean Brotherhood, which combined philosophical, scientific, and spiritual teachings. This group of followers, mostly in Croton (modern-day southern Italy), practiced a strict lifestyle that included dietary rules (e.g., vegetarianism), the belief in the immortality of the soul, and a deep focus on numbers as the key to understanding the universe. They also embraced metempsychosis (the transmigration of souls), believing that souls could be reborn into different bodies.

Pythagoras the Mathematician

Pythagoras’s mathematical work had a lasting impact. His theorem states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides. This formula, written as a² + b² = c², is still fundamental in geometry today.

He and his followers were also interested in the relationship between numbers and the natural world. They believed that mathematical harmony was the key to understanding the cosmos. For example, they discovered the relationship between musical intervals and numerical ratios, which laid the groundwork for the development of music theory.

Influence and Legacy

Pythagoras’s influence extended well beyond his time. His ideas were central to the development of Platonic thought and later Neoplatonism, and his mystical approach to mathematics inspired thinkers in both the ancient and modern worlds. His philosophy encouraged the belief that order and harmony governed the universe, an idea that permeated Western thought for centuries.

Many of the teachings attributed to Pythagoras were passed down through his followers, so much of what is known about him comes from later philosophers. As a result, there is some debate about which ideas were truly his and which were later additions. Nonetheless, his legacy as a thinker who sought to unite the mathematical, philosophical, and mystical realms remains strong.
