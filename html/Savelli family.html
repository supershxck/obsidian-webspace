The Savelli Family

  • A Roman noble family prominent in the 13th century, known for two popes and several cardinals and military leaders.

  • Rivals with families like the Colonna and Orsini, involved in Guelf-Ghibelline struggles.

  • Left a legacy with palaces like Palazzo Savelli in Albano Laziale; main branch ended in 1712.

Overview
The Savelli family was a significant Roman aristocratic family that rose to prominence in the 13th century. They are best known for producing two popes, Honorious III and Honorious IV, and had a notable presence in the Church and military, with several cardinals and condottieri. They were deeply involved in the political rivalries of Rome, often clashing with families like the Colonna and Orsini. Their architectural contributions include palaces like Palazzo Savelli in Albano Laziale, and their influence waned after the main branch ended in 1712. Interestingly, while some sources claim they produced four popes, including Benedict II and Gregory II, modern historians confirm only two, making their historical claims a point of intrigue.

Historical Context
Originating in medieval Rome, the Savelli family took their name from the castle of Sabellum near Albano, previously owned by the counts of Tusculum. They were part of the Black nobility, supporting the papacy, and played a key role in Roman politics, sometimes aligning with the Guelfs and at other times supporting the Colonna against the papacy in the Guelf-Ghibelline struggles.

Legacy and Modern Status
Their legacy lives on through historical records and buildings like Palazzo Savelli, but the main family line ended in 1712, leaving no prominent noble descendants today, though the name persists in modern contexts like businesses.

Historical Development

The Savelli family's origins are traditionally linked to the castle of Sabellum, near Albano, which had belonged to the counts of Tusculum before passing to the Savelli. Early modern genealogies, such as the unpublished manuscript "eulogistic treatise" compiled by Onofrio Panvinio, drew connections to Pope Benedict II, a possible but undocumentable connection, and even to the cognomen Sabellius of Antiquity. However, modern historiography confirms no such links, and their prominence began in the 13th century as a rich and influential Roman aristocratic family. They rose to power in rivalry with the first generation of great Roman families, including the Colonna, Orsini, Caetani, and Annibaldi, later being overshadowed by the emergence of the Chigi, Borghese, Barberini, Doria Pamphili, and Sforza Cesarini families.

The family held the lordship of Palombara Sabina and was deeply involved in the Guelf-Ghibelline struggles, sometimes aligning with the Guelfs but generally supporting the Colonna against the papacy. Their political influence waned by the 17th century, with the main branch becoming extinct in 1712 with Giulio Savelli.

Cultural and Architectural Legacy

The Savelli were significant patrons of architecture, with notable contributions including Palazzo Savelli in Albano Laziale, initially built as a fortress in the Middle Ages and later expanded to include ruins of the Pretoria Gate and the Church of Saint Peter Apostle. In 1368, they acquired the Theater of Marcellus in Rome, building a palace there, replaced in 1523–27 by a more splendid one. Their coat of arms can be seen over a wall of the church of Santa Maria in Aracoeli in Rome, reflecting their wealth and status.

Claims of Additional Popes

Some sources, including the Savelli family stores' website, claim the family produced four popes: Benedict II, Gregory II, Honorius III, and Honorius IV. However, modern historians dispute the connections to Benedict II and Gregory II, noting these claims started in the 15th century and are unattested in contemporary documents. For instance, Pope Benedict II's possible Savelli connection is mentioned but not certain (Pope Benedict II - Wikipedia), and Pope Gregory II is described as an alleged collateral ancestor, likely unreliable (Pope Gregory II - Wikipedia).

Modern Context and Legacy

Today, the direct male lines of the Savelli family are extinct, with the main branch ending in 1712. However, their name persists in modern contexts, such as Savelli Technologies and Savelli Religious, a family-owned Vatican gift shop since 1898. Their legacy is evident in historical studies of Roman politics and architecture, with buildings like Palazzo Savelli serving as reminders of their influence.
