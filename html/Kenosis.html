Kenosis is a theological concept found in Christian doctrine, primarily based on a passage from the Epistle to the Philippians (2:7), where St. Paul writes that [[Jesus Christ]] "emptied himself" (Greek: ἐκένωσεν, ekenōsen) by becoming human and taking on the form of a servant. The term "kenosis" itself comes from this Greek verb, meaning "to empty."

In Christian theology, kenosis refers to the self-emptying of Jesus' own will and becoming entirely receptive to God's divine will. It is about the Son of God, existing in the form of God, voluntarily relinquishing his divine privileges and adopting the condition of a servant, being made in human likeness, and thus fully participating in human vulnerability and suffering.

Kenosis has profound theological and christological significance, as it touches on the mystery of the Incarnation—God becoming man. It is a model of humility and self-sacrifice for Christians, illustrating how one should live a life of service, humility, and obedience to God. Furthermore, kenotic theology explores the nature of Jesus as both fully God and fully man, focusing on how these two natures coexist within Him.

In broader Christian thought, kenosis also influences the understanding of Christian ethics and spiritual life, advocating for a self-emptying love towards others, inspired by Jesus' example. It encourages believers to set aside their own desires and interests in service and love for others, embodying the sacrificial nature of Jesus’ teachings and actions.
