Nowruz is the Persian New Year, celebrated on the spring equinox, which usually falls on March 20 or 21 each year. It marks the beginning of the new year in the Persian calendar and the arrival of spring. Nowruz has been celebrated for over 3,000 years and is deeply rooted in the traditions of the Zoroastrian religion. It is observed by millions of people across the globe, particularly in countries and regions with Persian cultural influence, such as:

  • Iran
  • Afghanistan
  • Tajikistan
  • Uzbekistan
  • Kazakhstan
  • Kyrgyzstan
  • Azerbaijan
  • Turkey
  • Iraqi Kurdistan
  • Parts of India and Pakistan

Key Traditions of Nowruz

  1. Haft-Seen Table: A symbolic table setting with seven items starting with the letter "S" (in Persian), each representing a different hope or blessing for the new year. Common items include:
  2. Sabzeh (sprouted wheat or lentils) for rebirth.
  3. Samanu (sweet pudding) for wealth.
  4. Senjed (dried fruit) for love.
  5. Seer (garlic) for health.
  6. Seeb (apple) for beauty.
  7. Somāq (sumac) for sunrise and new beginnings.

  8. Serkeh (vinegar) for patience and age.

  9. Spring Cleaning (Khaneh Tekani): Families clean their homes thoroughly to prepare for the new year.

  10. Visiting Family and Friends: People visit loved ones, exchange gifts, and share festive meals.

  11. Chaharshanbe Suri: A fire-jumping festival celebrated on the eve of the last Wednesday before Nowruz, symbolizing the purification of the soul.

  12. Sizdah Bedar: On the 13th day of the new year, people go outdoors to picnic and celebrate nature, marking the end of the Nowruz festivities.

Nowruz was inscribed on the UNESCO Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2009, recognizing its cultural significance and the values of peace, solidarity, and renewal it promotes.
