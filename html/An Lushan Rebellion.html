The An Lushan Rebellion (755–763 CE): A Turning Point in Chinese History

The An Lushan Rebellion was one of the most significant and devastating events in [[Chinese history]]. It occurred during the [[Tang Dynasty]] (618–907 CE) and marked a turning point in the dynasty’s fortunes, resulting in massive social, political, and economic upheaval. Led by An Lushan, a military governor (jiedushi), the rebellion was both a reflection of internal weaknesses within the Tang government and a catalyst for long-term changes in Chinese society.

1. Background and Causes

a. Tang Dynasty Prosperity and Challenges

• Golden Age: By the mid-8th century, the Tang Dynasty had reached its zenith, with flourishing trade, cultural achievements, and strong central governance.

• Decentralization: Despite its successes, the Tang government increasingly relied on regional military governors (jiedushi) to maintain border security and manage provinces.

• Corruption and Nepotism: Corruption within the imperial court and the favoritism shown to certain officials undermined administrative efficiency and public trust.

b. Role of An Lushan

• An Lushan’s Background:

• An Lushan was a Sogdian-Turkic general who served as a military governor of three strategic regions in the north: Fanyang, Pinglu, and Hedong.

• Known for his charisma, military skill, and close relationship with Emperor Xuanzong and his favored concubine Yang Guifei.

• Ambitions and Grievances:

• An Lushan grew resentful of rivals at court, particularly Yang Guozhong, a relative of Yang Guifei who held significant power.

• He leveraged his control over a large and well-trained army to challenge the central government.

c. Economic and Social Discontent

• Taxation and Inequality:

• Heavy taxation and unequal land distribution created widespread discontent among the peasantry and provincial elites.

• Ethnic Tensions:

• The Tang Empire’s cosmopolitan policies led to ethnic diversity, but also friction between the Han Chinese majority and non-Han elites like An Lushan.

2. The Rebellion

a. Outbreak (755 CE)

• Declaration of Rebellion: In December 755, An Lushan declared himself emperor of a new dynasty, the Yan Dynasty, and marched south with his forces.

• Initial Successes:

• Captured Luoyang, the eastern capital of the Tang Dynasty, and made it his base of operations.

• Advanced on Chang’an, the Tang capital, forcing Emperor Xuanzong to flee to Sichuan.

b. Key Events

• Battle of Tong Pass (756 CE):

• An Lushan’s forces won a decisive victory near the vital Tong Pass, solidifying his control over central China.

• Assassination of An Lushan (757 CE):

• An Lushan was assassinated by his own son, An Qingxu, due to internal power struggles within the rebel camp.

• Tang Counteroffensive:

• The Tang court, under Emperor Suzong (Xuanzong’s successor), allied with foreign powers, including the Uyghur Khaganate, to launch a counteroffensive.

• In 757 CE, Tang forces recaptured Chang’an and Luoyang.

• Rebel Resurgence under Shi Siming (757–763 CE):

• After An Qingxu’s death, another rebel leader, Shi Siming, revitalized the rebellion but was ultimately defeated.

c. Suppression of the Rebellion (763 CE)

• The rebellion officially ended in 763, but lingering unrest persisted for years, leaving China politically fragmented and economically devastated.

3. Consequences and Impact

a. Death and Destruction

• Massive Casualties:

• The rebellion caused widespread destruction and loss of life, with an estimated 36 million deaths, making it one of the deadliest conflicts in history.

• Famine and Displacement:

• Large-scale famines and mass migrations devastated the population and agricultural economy.

b. Weakening of the Tang Dynasty

• Decline of Central Authority:

• The rebellion exposed the weaknesses of the Tang government and marked the beginning of its decline.

• The dynasty increasingly relied on regional military governors (jiedushi), who gained greater autonomy and sometimes defied imperial authority.

• Economic Consequences:

• The rebellion disrupted trade along the Silk Road and other major routes, diminishing China’s international influence.

• The tax system broke down, forcing the Tang to rely on irregular levies and contributions from warlords.

c. Social and Cultural Shifts

• Cosmopolitanism Decline:

• The Tang court became more insular and less tolerant of foreign influences following the rebellion, reversing earlier cosmopolitan policies.

• Rural Focus:

• The destruction of cities during the rebellion led to a shift in focus toward rural administration and agriculture.

d. Rise of Regional Powers

• The rebellion empowered the jiedushi, who became de facto rulers of their regions, further decentralizing the empire and sowing the seeds for the Tang’s eventual collapse in 907 CE.

4. Legacy

a. Historical Memory

• The An Lushan Rebellion is remembered as one of the most catastrophic events in Chinese history, often compared to the later Taiping Rebellion (1850–1864).

• It is frequently cited as an example of how internal weaknesses and unchecked military power can destabilize a centralized state.

b. Cultural Depictions

• The rebellion has inspired numerous works of literature, art, and theater, often focusing on its key figures and dramatic events.

• Yang Guifei: The story of Emperor Xuanzong and his concubine Yang Guifei has been romanticized in poems and operas, symbolizing the decadence and downfall of the Tang court.

• Example: The famous poem Changhen Ge (“The Song of Everlasting Sorrow”) by Bai Juyi.

c. Lessons for Governance

• The rebellion highlighted the dangers of excessive reliance on regional military leaders and the importance of a strong central government.

• It also underscored the need for fair taxation and economic policies to maintain social stability.

5. Timeline of Key Events

Year Event

755 An Lushan declares rebellion and establishes the Yan Dynasty.

756 An Lushan captures Luoyang and Chang’an; Emperor Xuanzong flees to Sichuan.

757 An Lushan is assassinated; Tang forces, with Uyghur allies, recapture Chang’an and Luoyang.

759 Shi Siming revives the rebellion and retakes Luoyang.

763 Rebellion officially ends with the death of Shi Siming’s son, Shi Chaoyi.

6. Conclusion

The An Lushan Rebellion was not only a military uprising but also a pivotal event that reshaped the political, social, and cultural landscape of China. While the Tang Dynasty survived the rebellion, it never fully recovered its former glory, and the events of the rebellion foreshadowed the fragmentation and decentralization that would dominate Chinese history in the centuries to come. The rebellion’s legacy serves as a powerful reminder of the fragility of even the most prosperous empires in the face of internal strife and mismanagement.
