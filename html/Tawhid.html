Tawhid (توحيد) – The Oneness of God in Islam

1. What is Tawhid?

Tawhid (توحيد) is the central doctrine of Islam, meaning “oneness” or “absolute unity” of Allah (God). It is the foundation of Islamic monotheism and the most essential belief in Islam.

In simple terms, Tawhid means that there is only One God, who has no partners, no equals, and no divisions.

The Shahada (Islamic declaration of faith) is based on Tawhid:

“La ilaha illa Allah, Muhammadun Rasul Allah.”

“There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is His Messenger.”

Tawhid is the first and most fundamental article of faith, and denying it is considered shirk (polytheism), which is the gravest sin in Islam.

2. Three Categories of Tawhid

Islamic scholars traditionally divide Tawhid into three aspects:

1. Tawhid al-Rububiyyah (توحيد الربوبية) – Oneness of Lordship

• Allah alone is the Creator, Sustainer, and Ruler of the Universe.

• No one shares in His power over creation.

• Everything in existence depends on Him.

• Example from the Qur’an:

“Allah is the Creator of all things, and He is, over all things, Disposer of affairs.”

(Qur’an 39:62)

2. Tawhid al-Uluhiyyah (توحيد الألوهية) – Oneness of Worship

• Only Allah should be worshiped.

• No prayers, sacrifices, or devotion should be directed to anything else.

• Any act of worshiping saints, idols, or other beings is shirk (polytheism).

• Example from the Qur’an:

“And We sent not before you any messenger except that We revealed to him that, ‘There is no deity except Me, so worship Me.’”

(Qur’an 21:25)

3. Tawhid al-Asma’ wa’l-Sifat (توحيد الأسماء والصفات) – Oneness of Allah’s Names and Attributes

• Allah has unique names and attributes (Asma’ wa’l-Sifat).

• His attributes cannot be compared to anything in creation.

• He is Al-Rahman (The Most Merciful), Al-Alim (The All-Knowing), Al-Qadir (The All-Powerful), etc.

• Example from the Qur’an:

“There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the Hearing, the Seeing.”

(Qur’an 42:11)

3. Tawhid vs. Shirk (Polytheism)

Tawhid is the foundation of pure monotheism, while shirk (شرك) is its opposite—associating partners with God.

Examples of Shirk (Polytheism)

• Worshiping idols, saints, or prophets.

• Believing that someone other than Allah controls the universe.

• Seeking intercession through supernatural beings.

• Calling upon the dead or spirits for help.

• Believing that Allah has a son, daughter, or partner.

Qur’anic Warnings Against Shirk

• Shirk is the only unforgivable sin if one dies without repentance.

“Indeed, Allah does not forgive associating others with Him, but He forgives what is less than that for whom He wills.”

(Qur’an 4:48)

• The Qur’an criticizes those who call upon others besides Allah.

“Do not invoke with Allah another deity. There is no deity except Him.”

(Qur’an 28:88)

4. Tawhid in Islamic Practice

Tawhid influences every aspect of a Muslim’s life:

  1. Salah (Prayer) – Worship is directed only to Allah.

  2. Zakat (Charity) – Giving wealth for the sake of Allah.

  3. Fasting (Ramadan) – A test of devotion to Allah.

  4. Hajj (Pilgrimage) – Visiting the Kaaba, the symbol of monotheism.

Even in daily actions, Muslims say Bismillah (“In the name of Allah”) to affirm Tawhid.

5. Mystical (Sufi) Understanding of Tawhid

• Sufis (Islamic mystics) interpret Tawhid as the realization of divine unity within oneself.

• The famous Sufi Mansur Al-Hallaj declared, “Ana al-Haqq” (I am the Truth), which some understood as a deep realization of Tawhid.

• Sufi teachings emphasize that only Allah truly exists, and all creation is a manifestation of His will (Wahdat al-Wujud – Unity of Being).

Conclusion: The Essence of Tawhid

• Tawhid is the foundation of Islamic belief.

• It means that Allah is the sole Creator, the only one worthy of worship, and unique in His attributes.

• Shirk (polytheism) is the greatest sin in Islam.

• Muslim life is structured around affirming Tawhid in thought, worship, and actions.
