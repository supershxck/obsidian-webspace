Background Research on Bhagavad Gita As It Is

Historical Context of the Bhagavad Gita

The Bhagavad Gita ("Song of God") is a 700-verse Hindu scripture that is part of the Indian epic Mahabharata. It is structured as a dialogue between Prince Arjuna and his guide and charioteer Lord Krishna on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, where Arjuna is faced with the moral dilemma of whether to fight his relatives in a righteous war.

The text is dated to approximately the 2nd century BCE, though some scholars place it earlier or later. It represents a synthesis of various philosophical ideas and is considered one of the most important texts in Hindu philosophy and spirituality.

A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada

Abhay Charanaravinda Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada (1896-1977) was a spiritual teacher, philosopher, and the founder of the International Society for Krishna Consciousness (ISKCON), commonly known as the Hare Krishna movement.

Key biographical details: - Born: September 1, 1896 in Calcutta, India - Died: November 14, 1977 (aged 81) in Vrindavan, India - Birth name: Abhay Charan De - Education: Scottish Churches College, University of Calcutta - Spiritual mission: To spread Krishna consciousness worldwide

Prabhupada came to the United States in 1965 at the age of 69, traveling on a cargo ship with very few possessions. He began chanting Hare Krishna in a park in New York City and giving classes on the Bhagavad Gita. In 1966, he established the International Society for Krishna Consciousness (ISKCON), which now has centers around the world.

Publication History of "Bhagavad Gita As It Is"

"Bhagavad Gita As It Is" is Prabhupada's translation and commentary on the Bhagavad Gita. The publication history includes:

  1. First published in 1968 by Macmillan Publishers as an abridged edition (approximately 400 pages)
  2. The original manuscript was over 1000 pages
  3. In 1972, Macmillan published the "Complete Edition" after the success of the abridged version
  4. After Prabhupada's death in 1977, the Bhaktivedanta Book Trust (BBT) published a "Revised and Enlarged" posthumous edition in 1983, containing at least one thousand substantial changes
  5. The changes in the 1983 edition were justified as being "closer to the original manuscript" but have been controversial among Prabhupada's followers
  6. In 2001, Krishna Books Inc (KBI), licensed by the BBT, reprinted the 1972 "Complete Edition," making both the "Complete" and "Revised and Enlarged" editions available

Significance of "As It Is" Translation

The title "Bhagavad Gita As It Is" reflects Prabhupada's approach to translation and commentary:

  1. Prabhupada claimed to present the Gita without "the slightest taint of adulteration or personal motivation"
  2. The translation follows the Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition, whose followers regard the Bhagavad Gita as the essence of Vedic knowledge and the Upanishads
  3. For each verse, the complete editions include:
  4. The Devanagari script
  5. A Latin transliteration
  6. Word-for-word Sanskrit-English meanings
  7. English translation

  8. Extensive commentary

  9. The commentary is based on various Gaudiya Vaishnava works, including:

  10. Ramanuja Bhasya (in Sanskrit)
  11. Sarartha-varsini-tika by Visvanatha Chakravarti Thakura
  12. Gita-bhusana-tika by Baladeva Vidyabhushana
  13. Bhaktivinode Thakur's Bengali commentaries

Philosophical Approach

Prabhupada's translation emphasizes:

  1. A path of devotion (bhakti) toward Krishna as the Supreme Personality of Godhead
  2. The view that Krishna is not an incarnation but the cause of all causes and source of all incarnations
  3. The loving service of the transcendental personality of the Lord
  4. The concept that achieving Krishna consciousness and realizing that all life is a manifestation of Krishna's eternal energy will release an individual from the cycles of reincarnation

Global Impact and Distribution

"Bhagavad Gita As It Is" has had significant global reach:

  1. It is sold worldwide through the efforts of Hare Krishna members in public places
  2. It has been published in nearly sixty languages
  3. Some abridged editions include prefaces by poet Allen Ginsberg and theologian Thomas Merton
  4. The 1972 Macmillan "Complete Edition" includes a foreword by Professor Edward C. Dimock, Jr. from the University of Chicago
  5. The book has faced some controversies, including a legal challenge in Russia in 2011 (which was dismissed)

Social and Cultural Teachings

The book suggests a way of life for the contemporary Western world:

  1. It describes ideal human society as divided into four varnas (brahmana - intellectuals, kshatriya - administrators, vaishya - merchants, shudra - workers)
  2. Prabhupada supports the view that one becomes a member of a varna not by birth but by personal qualities and the type of work one performs
  3. It advocates special protection for brahmanas, elders, women, children, and cows
  4. It encourages a lacto-vegetarian diet and presents agriculture as the ideal economic basis of society
  5. It concludes that society should be "Krishna conscious"—enlightened by devotion to Krishna

This translation and commentary has become one of the most influential presentations of the Bhagavad Gita for Western audiences and continues to be a primary source for those interested in Krishna consciousness and Hindu philosophy.
