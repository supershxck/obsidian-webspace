The Arab Revolt (1936–1939): Palestine’s Uprising Against British Rule and Zionist Immigration

The Arab Revolt (1936–1939), also known as the Great Palestinian Revolt, was a pivotal nationalist uprising in British Mandate Palestine, driven primarily by Arab Palestinian resistance against British colonial authority and the rapid expansion of Jewish immigration facilitated by the Zionist movement.

1. Historical Context and Causes

By the mid-1930s, Palestine was under British administration following the collapse of the Ottoman Empire after World War I. Key factors leading to the revolt included:

• Increasing Jewish Immigration:

Significant Jewish immigration, encouraged by Zionist aims for a Jewish homeland, triggered fears among Palestinian Arabs about displacement, land loss, and demographic shifts.

• British Mandate Policies:

British support for the establishment of a Jewish “national home,” formalized by the [[Balfour Declaration]] (1917), intensified Arab discontent.

• Economic Strains and Land Disputes:

Rapid Zionist settlement increased pressure on Arab land and resources, fueling grievances, economic hardship, and resentment among Palestinians.

2. Phases and Progression of the Revolt

The revolt occurred in two primary phases:

First Phase (April–October 1936): General Strike and Protests

• Began with the formation of the Arab Higher Committee, led by influential Palestinian leaders including [[Hajj Amin al-Husseini]], Grand Mufti of Jerusalem.

• Featured a widespread general strike across Palestine, peaceful demonstrations, boycotts, and civil disobedience aiming to pressure Britain to halt Jewish immigration and establish Arab self-rule.

• Initially largely peaceful, but quickly escalated into violent confrontations between Arabs, Jews, and British security forces.

Interlude: Peel Commission (1937)

• British authorities attempted to resolve the conflict through the Peel Commission, proposing to partition Palestine into separate Jewish and Arab states.

• Palestinian Arab leadership strongly rejected partition, fueling greater unrest.

Second Phase (September 1937–1939): Armed Rebellion

• Marked by intensified guerrilla warfare, sabotage, attacks on British military targets, and escalating violence against Zionist settlements.

• Rebels attacked British infrastructure, settlements, transportation, and administration centers, effectively turning the revolt into a widespread anti-colonial insurrection.

3. Role of Mohammad Amin al-Husseini

[[Mohammad Amin al-Husseini]], the Grand Mufti of Jerusalem (1921–1948), played a pivotal role:

• Political and Spiritual Leadership:

As head of the Arab Higher Committee, he provided organizational leadership and rallied broad Arab support, using his religious authority to legitimize the revolt.

• Mobilization and Ideology:

Promoted Arab nationalism, anti-Zionism, and independence from British control, framing the revolt as a nationalist and religious duty.

4. British Suppression and Consequences

The British authorities responded harshly:

• Deployed over 20,000 British troops, using collective punishments, curfews, mass arrests, home demolitions, and execution of rebels.

• The revolt resulted in severe casualties: approximately 5,000 Arabs killed, thousands wounded, and significant displacement.

• Britain issued the restrictive 1939 White Paper, limiting Jewish immigration and land purchases, signaling a temporary concession to Arab demands but intensifying tensions with Zionist leaders.

5. Legacy and Historical Impact

The Arab Revolt profoundly shaped Palestinian nationalism and regional dynamics:

• Deepened Arab-Jewish divisions, shaping narratives that persist into modern-day Israeli-Palestinian conflict.

• Fostered the development of distinct Palestinian national identity, solidarity, and consciousness.

• Militarized political opposition, laying the foundations for subsequent conflicts, including the 1948 Arab-Israeli War.

4. Symbolic and Historical Legacy

The revolt symbolizes resistance, nationalism, and self-determination, reflecting broader anti-colonial struggles worldwide:

• Commemorated in Palestinian cultural memory as a foundational moment of national resistance and identity.

• Serves as a historical reminder of the complexities and painful legacy of colonial-era conflicts and their enduring modern implications.

Conclusion: The Arab Revolt—A Turning Point

The Arab Revolt (1936–1939) remains a defining historical event symbolizing resistance, national awakening, and collective Palestinian identity. Its legacy continues to influence contemporary geopolitics, reminding the world of the complexities and enduring impact of historical colonial decisions, resistance movements, and unresolved aspirations for self-determination in the region.
