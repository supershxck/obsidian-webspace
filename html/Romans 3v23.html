In Romans 3:23, St. Paul is saying that all — both Jews and Gentiles — have sinned and fallen short of the glory of God.

He is not saying that every individual person has sinned. Indeed there are many who have not committed any sins, such as innocent unborn infants, for instance.

The context of Romans 3 flows from the context of Psalm 13 (14), which is quoted extensively in the text. This Psalm focuses specifically on fools — those who say there is no God, and live as if God does not exist. Among such people, there is no one holy; there are not any "righteous savages". 

Romans 3 is focusing on Jews and Gentiles, and the widespread sinfulness of both groups of people. This passage is not focused on the innocent unborn, or righteous Job, or Mary who is full of grace.

If we turn our attention to Mary herself, in the Magnificat, she calls God her "savior", which means there was something she needed to be saved from. Because Adam sinned, we all die, including Mary. Whether she sinned or not, she would have to face death, and she had no power to overcome it on her own.

By defeating death, the power of Christ's resurrection brings life to all of us — including Mary — who otherwise would have no opportunity to escape the grave.
